'''Sir David Roy Lidington''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|KCB|CBE}} (born 30 June 1956) is a British politician who was the [[Member of Parliament (United Kingdom)|Member of Parliament]] (MP) for [[Aylesbury (UK Parliament constituency)|Aylesbury]] from [[1992 United Kingdom general election|1992]] until [[2019 United Kingdom general election|2019]]. A member of the [[Conservative Party (UK)|Conservative Party]], he served as [[Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster]] and [[Minister for the Cabinet Office]] from 2018 to 2019<ref>{{cite news |date=8 January 2018 |url=https://uk.reuters.com/article/uk-britain-politics-lidington/may-appoints-david-lidington-as-cabinet-office-minister-mays-office-idUKKBN1EX1D5 |title=May appoints David Lidington as cabinet office minister – May's office |publisher=[[Reuters]] |accessdate=8 January 2018 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180108153445/https://uk.reuters.com/article/uk-britain-politics-lidington/may-appoints-david-lidington-as-cabinet-office-minister-mays-office-idUKKBN1EX1D5 |archivedate=8 January 2018 |url-status=live}}</ref> and was frequently described as being [[Theresa May]]'s ''de facto'' Deputy Prime Minister.<ref name="ex-sturg" /><ref name="ex-NI" />

Between 2010 and 2016, he served as [[Minister of State for Europe]] holding the position for the entirety of [[David Cameron]]'s premiership, a longer period than any of his predecessors. Theresa May appointed him to the cabinet for the first time in June 2016, where he held a number of roles including [[Leader of the House of Commons]], [[Lord Chancellor]] and [[Secretary of State for Justice]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.number10.gov.uk/news/topstorynews/2010/05/her-majestys-government-49840 |title=Her Majesty's Government |date=13 May 2010|accessdate=13 November 2010 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100515034600/http://www.number10.gov.uk/news/topstorynews/2010/05/her-majestys-government-49840 |archivedate=15 May 2010 }}</ref> He resigned from the government on 24 July 2019, in anticipation of the appointment of [[Boris Johnson]] as [[British Prime Minister]]. He did not seek reelection in the [[2019 United Kingdom general election|2019 general election]].<ref name=":0">{{cite web|url=https://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/aylesbury-mp-sir-david-lidington-to-step-down-at-next-general-election-1-9124085|title=Aylesbury MP Sir David Lidington to step down at next general election|last=Bamford|first=Thomas|date=30 October 2019|website=Bucks Herald|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191030122617/https://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/aylesbury-mp-sir-david-lidington-to-step-down-at-next-general-election-1-9124085|archivedate=30 October 2019|accessdate=30 October 2019}}</ref>

==Early life and career==
Born in [[Lambeth]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.freebmd.org.uk/cgi/information.pl?cite=1vJF0gDk0g6mxHhfKzTtWA&scan=1|title=Index entry|accessdate=1 February 2018|work=FreeBMD|publisher=ONS}}</ref> Lidington was educated at [[Merchant Taylors' School, Northwood#Merchant Taylors' Prep School|Merchant Taylors' Prep School]] and [[Haberdashers' Aske's Boys' School]]. He read Modern History at [[Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge]].<ref name="auto">{{cite web |url=https://www.gov.uk/government/people/david-lidington|title=The Rt Hon David Lidington MP – GOV.UK |website=www.gov.uk |accessdate=22 June 2017 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170509163738/https://www.gov.uk/government/people/david-lidington |archivedate=9 May 2017 |url-status=live}}</ref> His PhD was entitled ''The enforcement of the penal statutes at the [[court of the Exchequer]] c. 1558 – c. 1576''.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/?id=SpkHGwAACAAJ|title=The Enforcement of the Penal Statutes at the Court of the Exchequer C.1558-c.1576 |last=Lidington |first=David Roy|date=1988|publisher=University of Cambridge}}</ref> While at Cambridge, he was chairman of [[Cambridge University Conservative Association]] and Deputy President of the [[Cambridge University Students' Union]].<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.cam.ac.uk/news/a-new-home-for-cambridge-university-students-union|title=A new home for Cambridge University Students' Union |date=1 October 2007 |publisher=University of Cambridge |accessdate=22 June 2017 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180109181319/https://www.cam.ac.uk/news/a-new-home-for-cambridge-university-students-union|archivedate=9 January 2018|url-status=live}}</ref> He was the Captain of the [[Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge]] team that won the 1979 series of ''[[University Challenge]].'' The team also won the 2002 ''University Challenge – Reunited'' "champion of champions" series for the show's 40th anniversary.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/pressoffice/pressreleases/stories/2002/08_august/13/university_challenge_reunited.shtml|title=BBC – Press Office – University Challenge Reunited final|publisher=BBC|accessdate=22 June 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171204001127/http://www.bbc.co.uk/pressoffice/pressreleases/stories/2002/08_august/13/university_challenge_reunited.shtml|archivedate=4 December 2017|url-status=live}}</ref>

Lidington's early employment included posts with [[BP]] and the [[Rio Tinto Group]] before being appointed in 1987 as [[Special advisers in the United Kingdom|special adviser]] to the then Home Secretary [[Douglas Hurd]]. He moved to the [[Foreign and Commonwealth Office]] in 1989 when Hurd was appointed Foreign Secretary.<ref name="auto" />

In the [[1987 United Kingdom general election|1987 general election]], Lidington stood unsuccessfully in the [[Vauxhall (UK Parliament constituency)|Vauxhall constituency]].

==Parliamentary career==
===From 1992 to 2010===
Lidington was selected as the Conservative candidate for the safe seat of [[Aylesbury (UK Parliament constituency)|Aylesbury]] in December 1990. He became the constituency's member of parliament at the [[1992 United Kingdom general election|1992 general election]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.parliament.uk/biographies/commons/david-lidington/15|title=Rt Hon David Lidington MP|website=UK Parliament|accessdate=22 June 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170630132328/http://www.parliament.uk/biographies/commons/david-lidington/15|archivedate=30 June 2017|url-status=live}}</ref>

At Westminster, Lidington previously participated in the Education Select Committee and Conservative Backbench Home Affairs Committee. In 1994, he successfully promoted a Private Members Bill which became the Chiropractors Act 1994.

Lidington first joined the Conservative front bench team in August 1994, when he became [[Parliamentary Private Secretary]] to Home Secretary [[Michael Howard]]. In June 1997, with the Conservatives in opposition, he became [[Parliamentary Private Secretary]] to Leader of the Opposition [[William Hague]]. Two years later, in June 1999, he was promoted to become Shadow Home Affairs Minister (deputy to [[Ann Widdecombe]]). In September 2001, Lidington was promoted to become Shadow [[Financial Secretary to the Treasury]].<ref name="ACCA">{{cite web|url=https://www.aylesburyconservatives.com/person/rt-hon-david-lidington|title=Rt Hon David Lidington|website=Aylesbury Constituency Conservative Association|accessdate=22 June 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160109120344/http://www.aylesburyconservatives.com/person/rt-hon-david-lidington|archivedate=9 January 2016|url-status=live}}</ref>

====Shadow Cabinet====
Lidington became a member of the [[Official Opposition Shadow Cabinet (United Kingdom)|Shadow Cabinet]] in May 2002, replacing [[Ann Winterton]] as [[Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food|Shadow Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food]] (later [[Shadow Secretary of State for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs]]) after she resigned. When [[Michael Howard]] [[2003 Conservative Party  leadership election|was elected]] Conservative Party leader in November 2003, Lidington became [[Shadow Secretary of State for Northern Ireland]], but was not included as a member of the Shadow Cabinet.<ref name="ACCA"/>

In May 2005, Howard enlarged the Shadow Cabinet, granting Lidington the right to attend it again. He continued to serve as the Shadow Secretary of State for Northern Ireland under [[David Cameron]]. On 2 July 2007, was appointed as a Shadow Minister of State for Foreign Affairs.<ref name="ACCA"/>

====Expenses (2009)====
In May 2009, ''[[The Daily Telegraph]]'' revealed Lidington had claimed nearly £1,300 for his dry cleaning and had also claimed for toothpaste, shower gel, body spray and vitamin supplements on his second home allowance.<ref name=Gammell>{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/newstopics/mps-expenses/5364110/MPs-expenses-David-Lidington-is-Mr-Clean-but-you-pay-for-his-soap.html |title=MPs' expenses: David Lidington is Mr Clean (but you pay for his soap) |first=Caroline |last=Gammell |work=[[The Daily Telegraph]] |date=22 May 2009 |accessdate=7 June 2009 |url-status=live |archivedate=25 May 2009 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090525073142/http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/newstopics/mps-expenses/5364110/MPs-expenses-David-Lidington-is-Mr-Clean-but-you-pay-for-his-soap.html }}</ref> Lidington repaid the claims.<ref name=Gammell/>

Lidington was also criticised by local newspaper the ''[[Bucks Herald]]'' for claiming £115,891 in expenses in one year, almost double his salary.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/MPs-Expenses-The-price-of.5271666.jp |title=MPs Expenses: The price of democracy in Aylesbury Vale |work=[[The Bucks Herald]] |accessdate=9 April 2010 |url-status=live |archivedate=25 May 2009 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090525073142/http://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/mps_expenses_the_price_of_democracy_in_aylesbury_vale_1_613622 }}</ref>

===Since the 2010 general election===
Following the [[2010 United Kingdom general election|2010 general election]], Lidington was appointed [[Minister for Europe]].<ref name="ACCA"/> In August 2016 following the resignation of [[David Cameron]], Lidington was appointed a CBE in the [[2016 Prime Minister's Resignation Honours]] for his services to the government as European minister.

In November 2013, Lidington was criticised in an editorial of the local newspaper the ''[[Bucks Herald]]'' after he abstained on votes on the [[HS2]] rail project which will run through his constituency.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/more-news/editor-s-comment-time-fast-approaching-for-mr-lidington-to-stand-up-and-be-counted-over-hs2-1-5655994 |title=Editor's comment: Time fast approaching for Mr Lidington to stand up and be counted over HS2 |work=The Bucks Herald |accessdate=9 November 2013 |url-status=live |archivedate=9 November 2013 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20131109125345/http://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/more-news/editor-s-comment-time-fast-approaching-for-mr-lidington-to-stand-up-and-be-counted-over-hs2-1-5655994 }}</ref>

On 25 November 2016, when he was serving as [[Leader of the House of Commons]], Lidington deputised for Prime Minister [[Theresa May]] at [[Prime Minister's Questions|PMQs]]<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2016/nov/25/commons-leader-david-lidington-to-take-on-pmqs-for-the-first-time|title=Commons leader David Lidington to take on PMQs for the first time|first=Peter|last=Walker|date=25 November 2016|accessdate=30 December 2017|work=The Guardian|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171230225944/https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2016/nov/25/commons-leader-david-lidington-to-take-on-pmqs-for-the-first-time|archivedate=30 December 2017|url-status=live}}</ref> questioned first-hand by the [[Shadow Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs|Shadow Foreign Secretary]], [[Emily Thornberry]] who also deputised, as per custom, for [[Jeremy Corbyn]] on the day.

Under Prime Minister [[Theresa May]], Lidington was appointed [[Leader of the House of Commons]] and [[Lord President of the Council]]. This was a position he held till 11 June 2017, when he was promoted to Justice Secretary and Lord Chancellor.<ref name="auto"/> His appointment was criticised due to his record on LGBT rights, having opposed scrapping the ban on 'promotion of homosexuality' in schools, as well as civil partnerships.<ref name="Independent 12 June 2017">{{cite news|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/david-lidington-lgbt-rights-gay-marriage-theresa-may-justice-secretary-a7786751.html|title=Theresa May appoints Justice Secretary opposed to LGBT rights who said 'marriage is for procreation of children'|work=The Independent|date=12 June 2017|accessdate=22 June 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170621005606/https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/david-lidington-lgbt-rights-gay-marriage-theresa-may-justice-secretary-a7786751.html|archivedate=21 June 2017|url-status=live}}</ref> During the debate on the legalisation of same-sex marriage he argued that "marriage was for the procreation of children" and that the "definition of marriage should not be changed without an extremely compelling case for doing so". He later said that he regretted voting against civil partnerships.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/aylesbury-mp-david-lidington-explains-why-he-voted-against-gay-marriage-but-admits-he-was-wrong-about-civil-partnerships-in-the-past-1-4764699|title=Aylesbury MP David Lidington explains why he voted against gay marriage|accessdate=24 October 2018|work=Bucks Herald|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181024192118/https://www.bucksherald.co.uk/news/aylesbury-mp-david-lidington-explains-why-he-voted-against-gay-marriage-but-admits-he-was-wrong-about-civil-partnerships-in-the-past-1-4764699|archivedate=24 October 2018|url-status=live}}</ref>

On 8 January 2018, during a [[2018 British cabinet reshuffle|Cabinet reshuffle]], Lidington became the [[Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster]] and [[Minister for the Cabinet Office]]. Several media outlets subsequently referred to Lidington as [[Theresa May]]'s de facto Deputy Prime Minister and a candidate for her succession.<ref name="ex-sturg">{{cite web|url=https://www.express.co.uk/news/politics/1046659/brexit-news-david-Lidington-nicola-sturgeon-back-up-deal-scotland|title=David Lidington urges Sturgeon to back Brexit deal for good of Scotland|author=<!--Staff writer(s); no by-line.-->|date=17 November 2018|website=Express.co.uk|accessdate=12 December 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181215223410/https://www.express.co.uk/news/politics/1046659/brexit-news-david-Lidington-nicola-sturgeon-back-up-deal-scotland|archivedate=15 December 2018|url-status=live}}</ref><ref name="ex-NI">{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/politics/2018/11/29/david-lidington-pledges-britain-would-follow-northern-ireland/|title=David Lidington pledges Britain would follow Northern Ireland single market rules for backstop duration|last=Johnson|first=Simon|date=29 November 2018|work=The Telegraph|accessdate=12 December 2018|issn=0307-1235|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181215221952/https://www.telegraph.co.uk/politics/2018/11/29/david-lidington-pledges-britain-would-follow-northern-ireland/|archivedate=15 December 2018|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>[https://www.politicshome.com/news/uk/political-parties/conservative-party/news/102752/theresa-may-facing-cabinet-plot-replace-her Theresa May facing cabinet plot replace her] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190324100948/https://www.politicshome.com/news/uk/political-parties/conservative-party/news/102752/theresa-may-facing-cabinet-plot-replace-her |date=24 March 2019 }}, PoliticsHome, 24 March 2019.</ref> Despite this, Lidington said that he had 'no wish' to become Prime minister, stating that [[Theresa May]] was 'doing a fantastic job'.<ref>{{cite web|title=David Lidington: 'I don't think that I've any wish to take over from the PM'|date=24 March 2019|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Fy6e9qdEOHM|accessdate=9 April 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190409103411/https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Fy6e9qdEOHM|archivedate=9 April 2019|url-status=live}}{{better source|date=April 2019}}</ref> On 24 July 2019, Lidington resigned as Cabinet Office Minister & Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster. On 10 September, Lidington was appointed a [[Knight Commander of the Order of the Bath]] in [[2019 Prime Minister's Resignation Honours|Theresa May's resignation honours]] "for political and public service".<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.bucksfreepress.co.uk/news/17892133.aylesbury-mp-david-lidington-given-knighthood-theresa-may-39-s-resignation-honours-list/|title=Aylesbury MP David Lidington given knighthood in Theresa May's resignation honours list|work=Bucks Free Press|accessdate=10 September 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190927030043/https://www.bucksfreepress.co.uk/news/17892133.aylesbury-mp-david-lidington-given-knighthood-theresa-may-39-s-resignation-honours-list/|archivedate=27 September 2019|url-status=live}}</ref>

During the [[2019 Conservative Party leadership election|2019 Conservative leadership election]], Lidington backed [[Matt Hancock]].<ref>{{Cite news|title=Matt Hancock has no Brexit 'baggage'|language=en-GB|work=BBC News|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/av/uk-politics-48589891|access-date=2020-09-15}}</ref> At the final stage of the leadership election, he supported [[Jeremy Hunt]] over [[Boris Johnson]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=Healey|first=Derek|title=EXCLUSIVE: Deputy PM David Lidington backs Jeremy Hunt for leader and says no-deal Brexit would put pressure on union|url=https://www.thecourier.co.uk/fp/news/politics/scottish-politics/921064/exclusive-deputy-pm-david-lidington-backs-jeremy-hunt-for-leader-but-says-no-deal-brexit-would-put-pressure-on-union/|access-date=2020-09-15|website=The Courier|language=en-GB}}</ref>

Writing in his local newspaper, the ''[[Bucks Herald]]'', on 30 October 2019, Lidington said he was not planning to seek re-election at the [[2019 United Kingdom general election|next general election]].<ref name=":0" /> Lidington officially stepped down as the MP for Aylesbury on 6 November 2019.{{citation needed|date=November 2019}}

==Personal life==
Lidington and his wife Helen<ref name="bucks">{{cite web|url=http://www.speen-cofe.bucks.sch.uk/803-2/|website=speen-cofe.bucks.sch.uk|title=Browser Unsupported|accessdate=27 March 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190327150052/http://www.speen-cofe.bucks.sch.uk/803-2/|archivedate=27 March 2019|url-status=live}}</ref><ref name="aylesburyconservatives">{{cite web|url=https://www.aylesburyconservatives.com/news/david-lidingtons-20-years-aylesbury-mp|title=David Lidington&#039;s 20 years as Aylesbury MP|website=Aylesbury|accessdate=27 March 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190327161224/https://www.aylesburyconservatives.com/news/david-lidingtons-20-years-aylesbury-mp|archivedate=27 March 2019|url-status=live}}</ref> have four sons.<ref name="auto" /> He was raised as a [[Congregationalist]] but is now an [[Anglican]].<ref name="Independent 12 June 2017"/>
