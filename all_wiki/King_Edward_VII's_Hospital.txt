'''King Edward VII's Hospital''' (formal name: '''King Edward VII's Hospital Sister Agnes''') is a charity-registered [[private hospital]] in [[Marylebone]], west [[London]].

==History==
===Early history===
[[File:King Edward VII Hospital Sister Agnes.jpg|thumb|left|Buildings on the corner of Devonshire Street and Beaumont Street.]]
The hospital was established in 1899 at the suggestion of the Prince of Wales (later [[Edward VII|King Edward VII]]). [[Agnes Keyser]], a mistress of the Prince,<ref name="Royal choice">{{cite news | url=https://www.theguardian.com/uk/2012/dec/07/king-edward-vii-hospital | title=King Edward VII hospital – the royal choice | work=[[The Guardian]] | date=7 December 2012 | accessdate=11 December 2012 | author=Meikle, James | location=London}}</ref> and her sister Fanny used their house at 17 [[Grosvenor Crescent]] to help sick and wounded British Army officers who had returned from the [[Second Boer War]].<ref name="Times51204" /> King Edward VII became the hospital's first patron.<ref name="heritage">[http://www.kingedwardvii.co.uk/about-king-edward-vii/ ''King Edward VII's Hospital - About us''] Re-linked 2016-01-29</ref> In 1904 it officially became King Edward VII's Hospital for Officers.<ref name="Times51204" />

===20th century===
During the [[World War One|First World War]], the hospital was at 9 Grosvenor Gardens, where officers would be nursed; the young novelist [[Stuart Cloete]] was one of them,<ref>Cloete, Stuart (1972) ''A Victorian Son, an autobiography, 1897-1922''.</ref> as was the future British Prime Minister, [[Harold Macmillan]], who underwent a series of long operations followed by recuperation there from 1916–18, from serious wounds sustained in conflict during the [[Battle of the Somme]] in 1916.<ref>''Supermac''. Author: D.R. Thorpe. Publisher: Chatto & Windus. Published: 9 September 2010. Retrieved: 1 February 2014.</ref> In 1930, the hospital was awarded a [[Royal Charter]]<ref>[http://apps.charitycommission.gov.uk/Showcharity/RegisterOfCharities/CharityFramework.aspx?RegisteredCharityNumber=208944&SubsidiaryNumber=0 The Charity Commission: ''King Edward VII's Hospital Sister Agnes - Governing document''] Re-linked 2016-01-29</ref> "''to operate an acute Hospital where serving and retired officers of the Services and their spouses can be treated at preferential rates.''"<ref name=Report>[http://apps.charitycommission.gov.uk/Accounts/Ends44/0000208944_AC_20150331_E_C.pdf ''King Edward VII's Hospital - Annual Report 2014-2015, page 8''] Linked 2016-01-29</ref>

In 1941 the interior of the building was badly damaged by bombing, and Sister Agnes died from natural causes.<ref name="Times51204" /> In 1948 the hospital moved to Beaumont Street.<ref name="heritage" /> It was officially opened on 15 October by [[Mary of Teck|Queen Mary]].<ref name="Times51204">{{Cite newspaper The Times |articlename=Hospital For Service Officers - New Premises Opened by Queen Mary |section=Reviews |day_of_week=Saturday |date=16 October 1948 |page_numbers=6 |issue=51204 }}</ref>
 
In 1962, the hospital became a [[Charitable organization#United Kingdom|registered charity]].<ref>[http://apps.charitycommission.gov.uk/Showcharity/RegisterOfCharities/CharityFramework.aspx?RegisteredCharityNumber=208944&SubsidiaryNumber=0 The Charity Commission: ''King Edward VII's Hospital Sister Agnes - Registration history''] Linked 2016-01-29</ref>

===21st century===
In 2000 the hospital and charity changed its formal name to King Edward VII's Hospital Sister Agnes. Originally a hospital for officers, today it is a private hospital which supports the treatment of all ranks of former servicemen, as well as the general public.<ref name="heritage" /> Through the hospital's Sister Agnes Benevolent Fund, active or retired personnel in the British armed services, as well as their spouses, can receive a means tested grant that can cover up to 100% of their hospital fees.<ref name=Report/>
 
In recent years, the hospital has been used by various members of the [[British Royal Family]]. Previous royal patients at the hospital include [[Elizabeth II|Queen Elizabeth II]], [[Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother]], [[Princess Margaret, Countess of Snowdon]], [[Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh]], [[Catherine, Duchess of Cambridge]], and [[Charles, Prince of Wales]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.itv.com/news/london/2012-12-03/hospital-kate-admitted-to-has-long-association-with-royals/|title=Kate admitted to hospital with royal connections|publisher=[[ITV Network|ITV]]|date=4 December 2012|accessdate=4 December 2012}}</ref>
 
In February 2002, [[Princess Margaret, Countess of Snowdon|Princess Margaret]] died at the age of 71 at the hospital, after suffering a stroke.<ref name="Scotland-Tribute">{{cite web|url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/uk_news/scotland/1810838.stm|title=Scots sorrow at death of princess|publisher=BBC|date=9 February 2002|accessdate=27 May 2018}}</ref>
 
In December 2013 it was announced that the hospital had received a donation of £30 million from [[Michael Uren]].<ref>{{cite news|title=A gift fit for a Queen|url=http://www.hsj.co.uk/comment/blogs/a-gift-fit-for-a-queen/5065893.blog|accessdate=7 December 2013|newspaper=Health Service Journal|date=4 December 2013}}</ref>
 
In October 2014 Zambian President [[Michael Sata]] died at the age of 77 at the hospital, after receiving treatment for an undisclosed illness.<ref>{{cite news|title=Zambian President Sata death: White interim leader appointed|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-29818212|accessdate=29 October 2014|newspaper=BBC|date=29 October 2014}}</ref>
 
It generates revenue per bed of £347,000 a year.<ref>{{cite news|title=Rich overseas patients help private hospitals beat recession|url=http://www.ft.com/cms/s/0/c354cf36-dd2d-11e4-a772-00144feab7de.html#axzz3jivM6Rqr|accessdate=24 August 2015|publisher=Financial Times|date=12 April 2015}}</ref>

==Royal hoax call and death of Jacintha Saldanha==
{{main|Suicide of Jacintha Saldanha}}
 
In December 2012, the hospital received international media attention when [[Catherine, Duchess of Cambridge]] was admitted, suffering from [[hyperemesis gravidarum]]. While the Duchess was staying at the hospital, two [[Disc jockey|DJs]] from the Australian radio station [[2Day FM]] made a hoax telephone call to the hospital, pretending to be Queen Elizabeth II  and Prince Charles.<ref name="Post hospital">{{cite news | url=http://news.nationalpost.com/2012/12/07/jacintha-saldanha-who-committed-suicide-after-kate-middleton-radio-hoax-was-an-excellent-nurse/ | title=Jacintha Saldanha, who died in suspected suicide after Kate Middleton radio hoax, was an ‘excellent nurse’ | work=[[National Post]] | location= Toronto|date=7 December 2012 | accessdate=7 December 2012 }}</ref> They managed to obtain confidential information about the Duchess and her treatment from a nurse at the hospital. The call was recorded and broadcast after receiving approval from the station management. The hospital apologised, and said that privacy and security protocols would be reviewed.<ref name="Royal pregnancy">{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-20610197 | title=Royal pregnancy: Hoax call fools Duchess of Cambridge hospital | work=[[BBC News]] | date=5 December 2012 | accessdate=11 December 2012}}</ref> Two days after the broadcast, nurse Jacintha Saldanha, who had worked just over four years at the hospital and had passed on the hoax call to the other nurse in the Duchess's private ward, was found dead. The [[Metropolitan Police]] described it as an "unexplained death".<ref>{{cite news|title=Duchess of Cambridge prank call nurse found dead in suspected suicide|url=https://www.standard.co.uk/news/london/duchess-of-cambridge-prank-call-nurse-found-dead-in-suspected-suicide-8393236.html|work=[[Evening Standard]]|location= London|date=7 December 2012|accessdate= 7 December 2012}}</ref><ref name="independent hall 2012-12-07">{{cite news | url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/home-news/nurse-who-took-prank-phone-call-at-duchess-of-cambridges-hospital-is-found-dead-in-suspected-suicide-8393289.html | title=Nurse who took prank phone call at Duchess of Cambridge's hospital is found dead in suspected suicide | work=[[The Independent]] | location= London|date=7 December 2012 | accessdate= 7 December 2012 | author=Hall, John}}</ref><ref name="MSN News">{{cite news | url=http://news.msn.com/world/duchess-of-cambridge-prank-call-nurse-dies | title=Duchess of Cambridge prank call nurse dies | work=MSN News | date=8 December 2012 | accessdate=11 December 2012 | author1=Vinograd, Cassandra | author2=Kirka, Danica | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121210120211/http://news.msn.com/world/duchess-of-cambridge-prank-call-nurse-dies | archive-date=10 December 2012 | url-status=dead }}</ref> On 14 December, ''[[The Guardian]]'' reported that it understood  that the third of the notes left by Saldanha 'addressed her employers, the hospital, and contained criticism of staff there.' <ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2012/dec/13/jacintha-saldanha-suicide-notes|title=Jacintha Saldanha suicide notes|publisher= The Guardian|date= 14 December 2012|accessdate=29 June 2014}}</ref>
