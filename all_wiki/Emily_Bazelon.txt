'''Emily Bazelon''' (born March 4, 1971) is an [[United States|American]] journalist. She is a staff writer for ''[[The New York Times Magazine]],'' a senior research fellow at [[Yale Law School]], and co-host of the ''[[Slate (magazine)|Slate]]'' podcast [[Slate Political Gabfest|''Political Gabfest'']]. She is a former senior editor of ''Slate''. Her work as a writer focuses on law, women, and family issues.  She has written two national bestsellers published by [[Penguin Random House]]: ''Sticks and Stones: Defeating the Culture of Bullying and Rediscovering the Power of Character and Empathy'' (2013) and ''Charged: The New Movement to Transform American Prosecution and End Mass Incarceration'' (2019).<ref>{{Cite book|last=Bazelon, Emily|title=Sticks and stones : defeating the culture of bullying and rediscovering the power of character and empathy|year=2014|isbn=978-0-8129-8263-3|edition=Random House Trade paperback|location=New York|lccn=2012022773|oclc=855848064|ol=OL25355894M}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{cite web |last1=Bazelon |first1=Emily |title=Charged: The New Movement to Transform American Prosecution and End Mass Incarceration |url=https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/548313/charged-by-emily-bazelon/ |website=Charged: The New Movement to Transform American Prosecution and End Mass Incarceration |publisher=Penguin Random House |accessdate=25 February 2020}}</ref>  ''Charged'' won the 2020 [[Los Angeles Times]] [[Los Angeles Times Book Prize|Book Prize]] in the Current Interest category, and the 2020 Silver Gavel Award from the American Bar Association.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Pineda |first1=Dorany |title=2020 Silver Gavel Award from the American Bar Association |url=https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/books/story/2020-04-17/2019-times-book-prizes-winners-announced |accessdate=28 May 2020 |work=Los Angeles Times |publisher=Los Angeles Times |date=April 17, 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=American Bar Association names 2020 Silver Gavel Awards for Media and the Arts (press release, May 20, 2020) |url=https://www.americanbar.org/news/abanews/aba-news-archives/2020/05/american-bar-association-names-2020-silver-gavel-awards-for-medi/ |website=American Bar Association |publisher=American Bar Association |accessdate=28 May 2020}}</ref> It was also the runner up for the [[J. Anthony Lukas Book Prize]] from [[Columbia University]] and the [[Nieman Foundation]], and a finalist for the [[Helen Bernstein Book Award for Excellence in Journalism]] from the [[New York Public Library]].<ref>{{cite web |title=Winners and finalists of the 2020 Lukas Prize Project Awards announced |url=https://nieman.harvard.edu/news/2020/03/winners-of-the-2020-j-anthony-lukas-prize-project-awards/#:~:text=Finalist%3A%20Emily%20Bazelon%2C%20a%20staff,different%20and%20profoundly%20better%20future. |website=The Nieman Foundation |publisher=The President and Fellows of Harvard College |accessdate=28 May 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |last1=Fowler |first1=Ian |title=2020 Bernstein Awards Finalist Spotlight: 'Charged' by Emily Bazelon |url=https://www.nypl.org/blog/2020/04/01/2020-bernstein-awards-finalist-spotlight-charged-emily-bazelon |website=New York Public Library |publisher=The New York Public Library |accessdate=28 May 2020}}</ref>

==Early life and education==

Bazelon was born on March 4, 1971<ref name="Birthday">{{cite web|url=http://www.slate.com/articles/life/family/2012/04/what_s_your_earliest_memory_how_old_were_you_.html|title=What's Your Earliest Memory?|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|date=April 12, 2012|work=[[Slate (magazine)|Slate]]|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.politico.com/story/2019/03/04/playbook-birthday-emily-bazelon-1200727|title=BIRTHDAY OF THE DAY: Emily Bazelon, staff writer for the NYT Magazine, co-host of the 'Slate Political Gabfest' and Truman Capote fellow at Yale Law School|last=Staff|first=Politico|website=POLITICO|language=en|access-date=2019-04-19|date=March 4, 2019}}</ref> and grew up in Philadelphia. Her father was an attorney and her mother was a psychiatrist.<ref name="azj">{{cite news|last1=Wilensky|first1=Sheila|title=Social, legal facets of bullying topic for author, Yale law grad|url=https://azjewishpost.com/2013/social-legal-facets-of-bullying-topic-for-author-yale-law-grad/|accessdate=August 22, 2017|work=Arizona Jewish Post|date=September 12, 2013}}</ref> She attended [[Germantown Friends School]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.germantownfriends.org/news/article/index.aspx?linkid=618&moduleid=261|title=Germantown Friends: News » The Ninny State: The Danger of Overprotecting Your Kids from Technology|work=germantownfriends.org|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}{{dead link|date=July 2020|bot=medic}}{{cbignore|bot=medic}}</ref> where she was on the tennis team.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Wartenberg|first1=Steve|title=Stenstrom wins PIAA District 1 championship|url=https://www.newspapers.com/image/169213873/?terms=Emily%2BBazelon|accessdate=August 22, 2017|work=[[The Philadelphia Inquirer]]|url-access=subscription |date=November 1, 1988}}</ref> She has three sisters: Jill Bazelon, who founded an organization that provides financial literacy classes free of charge to low-income high school students and individuals in several cities; [[Lara Bazelon]], an associate professor at the [[University of San Francisco School of Law]] and prominent advocate for overturning wrongful convictions; and [[Dana Bazelon]], Senior Policy Counsel to [[Larry Krasner]], the Philadelphia district attorney.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Hill|first1=Miriam|title=Let's talk about bullies|url=http://www.philly.com/philly/blogs/raising_philadelphia/bazelon.html|accessdate=August 22, 2017|work=Philly.com|date=February 28, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Heller|first1=Karen|title=Classes in financial literacy open eyes, doors|url=https://www.newspapers.com/image/199696125/?terms=jill%2BBazelon|accessdate=August 22, 2017|work=[[The Philadelphia Inquirer]]|url-access=subscription |date=April 11, 2012|page=A02}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.usfca.edu/law/faculty/lara-bazelon|title=Lara Bazelon - Associate Professor of Law and Director of the Criminal Juvenile Justice Clinic and the Racial Justice Clinic|access-date = 14 January 2019|date=2016-05-26}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=D'Onofrio|first1=Michael|title= D.A. makes way for people to clear records|url=http://www.phillytrib.com/news/d-a-makes-way-for-people-to-clear-records/article_b83eed3a-9cf7-5bd5-a64b-0ca0caf99d9f.html|accessdate=January 14, 2019|work=The Philadelphia Tribune|date=May 4, 2018}}</ref> Her family is Jewish but not exceptionally religious; she said in an interview, "I was raised to see Judaism in terms of ethical precepts."<ref name="azj"/><ref>{{Cite web|title= Emily Bazelon  |website=[[Jewish Women's Archive]]|url= https://jwa.org/people/bazelon-emily }}</ref>

Bazelon is the granddaughter of [[David L. Bazelon]], formerly a judge on the [[United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit]],<ref name=inbrief>[http://www.bazelon.org/about/inbrief/summer03.pdf ''In Brief''] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081128134354/http://www.bazelon.org//about/inbrief/summer03.pdf |date=2008-11-28 }}, Summer 2003, Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law.</ref> and [[cousin|second cousin twice removed]] of feminist [[Betty Friedan]].<ref>{{cite news |last=Bazelon|first=Emily|title=Shopping With Betty|url=http://www.slate.com/id/2135515/|work=Slate|date=February 5, 2006|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref>

Bazelon graduated from [[Yale College]] in 1993, where she was managing editor of ''[[The New Journal]]''. She received her [[Juris Doctor|J.D.]] from [[Yale Law School]] in 2000 and was an editor of the ''[[Yale Law Journal]]''.<ref name=yal/> In 2004, she was made a Soros Justice Media Fellow by [[Open Society Foundations]].<ref>{{cite press release |author=<!--Staff writer(s); no by-line.--> |title=OSI Awards More Than $1.5 Million Nationwide to Winners of 2004 Soros Justice Fellowships |url=https://www.opensocietyfoundations.org/newsroom/osi-awards-more-15-million-nationwide-winners-2004-soros-justice-fellowships |agency=Open Society Foundations |date=2004-01-19 |access-date=2019-10-20}}</ref> She held the Dorot Fellowship in Israel from 1993–94.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dorot.org/dorotfellows|title=Dorot Fellows|work=dorot.org|accessdate=June 15, 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140710090555/http://www.dorot.org/dorotfellows|archive-date=July 10, 2014|url-status=dead}}</ref> After law school she worked as a [[law clerk]] for Judge [[Kermit Lipez]] of the [[United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit]].

==Journalism career==
Bazelon is a writer for ''[[The New York Times Magazine]]'' and former senior editor of ''[[Slate (magazine)|Slate]]''.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://investors.nytco.com/press/press-releases/press-release-details/2014/Emily-Bazelon-Joins-The-New-York-Times-Magazine/default.aspx|title=Emily Bazelon joins New York Times|date=September 2, 2014|work=The New York Times|author=New York Times Press Release|accessdate=May 19, 2017}}</ref><ref name=yal>{{cite web|url=http://www.law.yale.edu/faculty/EBazelon.htm|title=Emily Bazelon}}</ref> She has written on subjects such as voting rights,<ref name=Bazelon>[http://www.legalaffairs.org/issues/January-February-2004/feature_bazelon_janfeb04.msp The Big Kozinski], ''[[Legal Affairs]]'', Emily Bazelon, February 2005. Retrieved August 5, 2018.</ref> the ''[[Hamdan v. Rumsfeld]]'' [[Guantanamo detainee]] [[due process]] trial<ref>{{cite news|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=http://www.slate.com/id/2138750|title=Invisible Men : Did Lindsey Graham and Jon Kyl mislead the Supreme Court?|publisher=Slate|date=March 27, 2006|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> and the alleged [[Abortion and mental health|post-abortion syndrome]].<ref name=syn>{{cite news|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2007/01/21/magazine/21abortion.t.html|title=Is There a Post-Abortion Syndrome?|work=The New York Times|date=January 21, 2007|accessdate=January 17, 2009}}</ref> Her work as a writer focuses on law, women, and family issues.<ref name=yal/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.politico.com/blogs/media/2014/09/ny-times-magazine-hires-emily-bazelon-194728.html|title=N.Y. Times Magazine hires Emily Bazelon|last=Gold|first=Hades|work=POLITICO|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref>

Before joining ''Slate'', Bazelon was a senior editor of ''[[Legal Affairs]]''.<ref name="contribs">[http://www.slate.com/id/117517/ List of ''Slate'' contributors]  {{webarchive|url=https://www.webcitation.org/5zaOym6OE?url=http://www.slate.com/id/117517/ |date=June 20, 2011 |accessdate=May 20, 2017 }}</ref> Her writing has also appeared in ''[[The Atlantic]]'', ''[[Mother Jones (magazine)|Mother Jones]]'', ''[[The Washington Post]]'', ''[[The Boston Globe]]'', ''[[The New Republic]]'', and other publications.<ref name=contribs/>

Bazelon is also a senior research scholar in Law and [[Truman Capote]] Fellow for Creative Writing and Law at Yale Law School.<ref name=yal/> Bazelon is affiliated with the Law and Media Program of Yale Law School.<ref>{{cite news|title=Spotlight on LAMP|url=http://blogs.law.yale.edu/blogs/admissions/archive/2008/11/18/spotlight-on-lamp.aspx|publisher=Yale Law School|date=November 18, 2008|accessdate=January 18, 2009|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090125044808/http://blogs.law.yale.edu/blogs/admissions/archive/2008/11/18/spotlight-on-lamp.aspx|archive-date=January 25, 2009}}</ref>

Between 2012–14, Bazelon made eight appearances on ''[[The Colbert Report]]'' on [[Comedy Central]] to discuss [[Supreme Court of the United States|Supreme Court]] and anti-bullying issues.<ref>{{cite news|title=Emily Bazelon: Reforming Health-Care Reform|url=http://www.cc.com/video-clips/fe3wjm/the-colbert-report-reforming-health-care-reform---emily-bazelon|accessdate=August 22, 2017|work=The Colbert Report|publisher=Comedy Central|date=November 13, 2014}}</ref>

===Writing on legalization of prostitution===
In 2016, Bazelon wrote an article in ''The New York Times'' on the [[legalization of prostitution]], discussing the [[decriminalization]] of johns, pimps, and brothel owners as a means to protect sex workers.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/05/08/magazine/should-prostitution-be-a-crime.html|title=Should Prostitution Be a Crime?|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|date=May 5, 2016|work=The New York Times|accessdate=May 19, 2017}}</ref> She has been accused of distorting facts to push a narrative under the guise of neutral reporting.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.feministcurrent.com/2016/05/12/unethical-practices-produce-new-york-times-sex-work-story/|title=Unethical practices produce New York Times' 'sex work' story|last=Murphy|first=Megan|date=May 12, 2016|work=Feminist Current|accessdate=October 19, 2019}}</ref>

===Writing on bullying===
Bazelon wrote a series on bullying and cyberbullying for ''Slate'', called "Bull-E".<ref>{{cite news|title=Bull-E: The new world of online cruelty.|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=http://www.slate.com/id/2242320/year/2010/landing/1/|publisher=Slate|date=January 26, 2010|accessdate=April 13, 2011|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110119080052/http://www.slate.com/id/2242320/year/2010/landing/1/|archive-date=January 19, 2011}}</ref> She was nominated for the 2011 Michael Kelly Award<ref>{{cite news|title=Michael Kelly Award finalists named|last=Romenesko|first=Jim|url=http://www.poynter.org/latest-news/romenesko/127093/michael-kelly-award-finalists-named/|publisher=The Poynter Institute|date=April 7, 2011|accessdate=April 13, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110414072756/http://www.poynter.org/latest-news/romenesko/127093/michael-kelly-award-finalists-named/|archive-date=April 14, 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref> for her story "What Really Happened to Phoebe Prince?"<ref>{{cite news|title=What Really Happened to Phoebe Prince?|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=http://www.slate.com/id/2260952/entry/2260953/|work=Slate|date=July 20, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> The three-part article is about the [[suicide of Phoebe Prince]], a 15-year-old girl who committed suicide in [[South Hadley, Massachusetts]], in January 2010, and the decision by the local prosecutor to bring criminal charges against six teenagers in connection with this death. The [[Michael Kelly (editor)#The Michael Kelly Award|Michael Kelly Award]], sponsored by the Atlantic Media Co., "honors a writer or editor whose work exemplifies a quality that animated Michael Kelly's own career: the fearless pursuit and expression of truth."<ref>{{cite web|title=The Michael Kelly Award|url=http://www.kellyaward.com/|publisher=The Atlantic Media Co.|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> Bazelon's series also sparked heated reaction<ref>{{cite news|title=Revelations Stir New Debate Over Phoebe Prince Suicide|last=Lohr|first=David|url=http://www.aolnews.com/2010/07/23/revelations-stir-new-debate-over-pheobe-prince-suicide/|publisher=[[archive.is]]|work=AOL News|date=July 23, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://archive.today/20120722144602/http://www.aolnews.com/2010/07/23/revelations-stir-new-debate-over-pheobe-prince-suicide/|archive-date=July 22, 2012}}</ref> and a response from district attorney Elizabeth Scheibel,<ref>{{cite news|title=Blaming the Victim|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=http://www.doublex.com/blog/xxfactor/blaming-victim|publisher=Slate|date=July 22, 2010|accessdate=April 13, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110414224424/http://www.doublex.com/blog/xxfactor/blaming-victim|archive-date=April 14, 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref> who brought the charges against the six teenagers.

Bazelon authored a book about bullying and school climate published by Random House, titled ''Sticks and Stones: Defeating the Culture of Bullying and Rediscovering the Power of Character and Empathy''.<ref>{{cite news|title=Emily Bazelon Lands Book Deal for Bullying Investigation|last=Boog|first=Jason |url=http://www.mediabistro.com/galleycat/slate-bullying-book-deal_b16506|publisher=Media Bistro GalleyCat Blog|date=November 10, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017 |url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101114214408/http://www.mediabistro.com/galleycat/slate-bullying-book-deal_b16506|archive-date=2010-11-14}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Schwartz|first1=John|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/03/11/books/sticks-and-stones-by-emily-bazelon.html|title=Words That Hurt and Kill: Lessons for Society From Bullying and Its Psychic Toll|date=March 10, 2013|work=The New York Times|accessdate=August 22, 2017}}</ref> It received a front page ''[[The New York Times Book Review]]'' review, which called the book "intelligent" and "rigorous", and described the author as "nonjudgmental in a generous rather than simply neutral way," and "a compassionate champion for justice in the domain of childhood’s essential unfairness."<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/03/03/books/review/sticks-and-stones-emily-bazelons-book-on-bullying.html|work=The New York Times|last=Solomon|first=Andrew|title=The Brutal Years|date=February 28, 2013|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> In ''[[The Wall Street Journal]]'', Meghan Cox Gurdon called ''Sticks and Stones'' a "humane and closely reported exploration of the way that hurtful power relationships play out in the contemporary public-school setting".<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB10001424127887323495104578313050784258938|title=The Cruelty of Youth|last=Gurdon|first=Meghan Cox|date=2013-02-22|work=Wall Street Journal|access-date=2018-10-02|language=en-US|issn=0099-9660}}</ref>

===Writing on abortion===
Bazelon has reported critically on the [[pro-life]] movement and opponents of legal abortion, including "pro-life feminists"<ref>[http://motherjones.com/politics/2007/01/suffragette-city "Suffragette City"], E. Bazelon, ''[[Mother Jones (magazine)|Mother Jones]]'', Jan.-Feb. 2007.</ref> and proponents of the concept of post-abortion syndrome,<ref name=syn/> while being supportive of abortion providers<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2010/07/18/magazine/18abortion-t.html|title=The New Abortion Providers|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|work=[[The New York Times Magazine]]|date=July 14, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> and [[pro-choice]] [[United States federal judge|federal judges]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.slate.com/id/2250581/|title=Defining Radical Down|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|work=Slate|date=April 13, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> She has described [[crisis pregnancy center]]s as being "all about bait-and-switch" and "falsely maligning" the abortion procedure.<ref>[http://www.slate.com/id/2236707/pagenum/all/#p2 "Sign Them Up"], E. Bazelon, ''Slate'', Nov. 25, 2009.</ref><ref>[http://douthat.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/12/03/the-politics-of-pregnancy-counseling/ "The Politics of Pregnancy Counseling"], [[Ross Douthat|R. Douthat]], ''New York Times Opinion'' blog, Dec. 3, 2009.</ref> Bazelon has discussed her support for legal abortion on the ''Double X'' blog.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.doublex.com/blog/xxfactor/feminist-establishment-rejects-mama-grizzlies|title=The Feminist Establishment Rejects the Mama Grizzlies|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|work=Double X|date=August 19, 2010|accessdate=May 20, 2017|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100909014243/http://www.doublex.com/blog/xxfactor/feminist-establishment-rejects-mama-grizzlies|archive-date=September 9, 2010}}</ref>

===Writing on criminal justice===
In 2018 and 2019, Bazelon published a number of articles on criminal justice reform.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/09/26/magazine/ex-felons-voting-rights-florida.html|title=Will Florida's Ex-Felons Finally Regain the Right to Vote?|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|work=[[The New York Times Magazine]]|date=September 26, 2018|accessdate=January 14, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/12/11/opinion/how-local-prosecutors-can-reform-their-justice-systems.html|title=There's a Wave of New Prosecutors. And They Mean Justice.|last1=Bazelon|first1=Emily|last2=Krinsky|first2=Miriam|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=December 11, 2018|accessdate=January 14, 2019}}</ref>  Her book ''Charged'' focuses on the role of prosecutors, the history of "tough on crime" politics in elections for that office, and the new generation of reformist prosecutors.<ref name=":0" />

===Ruth Bader Ginsburg interview controversy===
In July 2009, the ''New York Times Magazine'' published Bazelon's interview with [[Supreme Court of the United States|U.S. Supreme Court]] [[Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States|Justice]] [[Ruth Bader Ginsburg]]. Discussing her view of ''[[Roe v. Wade]]'' in 1973, Ginsburg commented, "Frankly I had thought that at the time Roe was decided, there was concern about population growth and particularly growth in populations that we don't want to have too many of. So that Roe was going to be then set up for Medicaid funding for abortion."<ref name=gins>{{cite news|last=Bazelon|first=Emily|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/07/12/magazine/12ginsburg-t.html?_r=1|title=The Place of Women on the Court|work=The New York Times Magazine|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref>

Bazelon did not ask any follow-up question to what some interpreted as Ginsburg endorsing a [[eugenics]]-based rationale for legalized abortion, i.e., as a remedy for "populations that we don't want to have too many of."<ref name="gers">{{cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2009/07/16/AR2009071603485.html?hpid=opinionsbox1|title=Ruth Bader Ginsburg's Comments on Abortion in the New York Times|last=Gerson|first=Michael|date=July 17, 2009|work=Washington Post|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref> Bazelon was criticized by some conservative commentators for not doing so.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.jewishworldreview.com/cols/jonah071509.php3|title=Ruth Bader Ginsburg and a Question of Eugenics|last=Goldberg|first=Jonah|work=Jewish World Review|date=July 15, 2009|accessdate=May 20, 2017}}</ref><ref name=baze>{{cite news|url=http://www.politicsdaily.com/2009/07/17/why-emily-bazelon-didnt-follow-up-on-ginsburgs-offensive-abort/2|title=Why Emily Bazelon Didn't Follow Up on Ginsburg's Abortion Comment|last=Henneberger|first=Melinda|work=Politics Daily|publisher=archive.is|date=July 17, 2009|accessdate=May 20, 2017|url-status=bot: unknown|archive-url=https://archive.today/20130201003059/http://www.politicsdaily.com/2009/07/17/why-emily-bazelon-didnt-follow-up-on-ginsburgs-offensive-abort/2|archive-date=February 1, 2013}}</ref> Bazelon responded to the criticism, stating that she is "imperfect" and did not ask a follow-up question because she believed that Ginsburg's use of "we" had referred to "some people at the time, not [Ginsburg] herself or a group that she feels a part of."<ref name="baze" />

The interview was cited in the [[United States House of Representatives]]' Committee Report in support of the Prenatal Nondiscrimination Act of 2012.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.congress.gov/congressional-report/112th-congress/house-report/496/1|title=H. Rept. 112-496 - PRENATAL NONDISCRIMINATION ACT (PRENDA) OF 2012|website=www.congress.gov|access-date=2020-03-04|quote=\123\Emily Bazelon, The Place of Women on the Court, New York Times Magazine}}</ref>
