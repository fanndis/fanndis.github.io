{{main|2004 Wimbledon Championships}}
[[Maria Sharapova]] defeated the two-time defending champion [[Serena Williams]] in the final, 6–1, 6–4 to win the Ladies' Singles tennis title at the 2004 Wimbledon Championships.<ref name="Barrett">{{cite book|last=Barrett|first=John|title=Wimbledon: The Official History|date=2014|publisher=Vision Sports Publishing|isbn=9-781909-534230|edition=4th}}</ref> This victory was hailed by the media as "the most stunning upset in memory".<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A25495-2004Jul3.html|title=Sharapova Wins Wimbledon After Improbable Journey|work=The Washington Post |author=Liz Clarke|date=April 7, 2004|accessdate=June 20, 2010}}</ref>

With this win, Sharapova, who was to become a future world No. 1, entered the top ten for the first time in her career. She also became the third-youngest woman to win Wimbledon (behind [[Lottie Dod]] and [[Martina Hingis]])<ref>{{cite web|url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/sport1/hi/tennis/3860923.stm|title=Sharapova storms to Wimbledon glory|date=3 July 2004|work=[[BBC Sport]]|accessdate=3 July 2017}}</ref> and the second Russian woman (after [[Anastasia Myskina]] won the [[2004 French Open – Women's Singles|French Open earlier that year]]) to win a Grand Slam title.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2004/jul/04/wimbledon2004.wimbledon4|title=Sensational Sharapova is a Russian revelation|date=4 July 2004|work=[[The Guardian]]|accessdate=3 July 2017}}</ref>

Serena Williams was attempting to become the first player to win the women's singles tournament three consecutive times since [[Steffi Graf]] was champion in [[1991 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|1991]], [[1992 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|1992]] and [[1993 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|1993]].

[[Venus Williams]], who had appeared in the previous four Wimbledon finals, winning in 2000 and 2001, suffered her earliest exit from Wimbledon since her debut, being upset in the second round by [[Karolina Šprem]].<ref>{{cite web |last1=Cheese |first1=Caroline |title=Venus makes shock exit |url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/sport1/hi/tennis/3837841.stm|website=news.bbc.co.uk |publisher=[[BBC]]|accessdate=2 June 2018|date=24 June 2004}}</ref>

This was also the last Grand Slam singles tournament that former world No. 1 [[Martina Navratilova]] competed in; having been awarded a wild card, she won her first round match and became, at age 47, the oldest player in the [[History of tennis#Open era|Open Era]] to win a main draw match at Wimbledon and the lowest ranked play (no. 1,001) to win at Wimbledon , except [[Barbara Schwartz (tennis)|Barbara Schwartz]] who was unranked in 2001.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2004/jun/22/wimbledon2004.wimbledon3|title= Navratilova rolls back the years in quick time |date=22 June 2004|work=[[The Guardian]]|accessdate=3 July 2017}}</ref> <ref>Harman, Neil (2004). ''The Official Wimbledon Annual 2004''. 2 Puddle Dock, London: Hazleton Publishing Ltd. {{ISBN|1-903135 39 7}}.</ref>
