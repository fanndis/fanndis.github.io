'''Marc Porat''' is a tech [[entrepreneur]] and [[angel investor]]. He is founder of six companies including [[General Magic]].  In the early 2000s, Porat was a member of a high-profile wave of tech executives who founded cleantech companies.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Silicon Valley tech leaders are reinventing themselves for a cleantech revolution|url = http://www.mercurynews.com/green-energy/ci_14225614|accessdate = 2015-08-30}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.greentechmedia.com/articles/read/green-building-entrepreneur-build-green-or-face-catastrophe|title=Green Building Entrepreneur: Build Green or Face Catastrophe|access-date=2016-09-23}}</ref> He launched three companies in the built environment: Serious Materials, Zeta Communities, and CalStar Cement and was a member of the U.S. China Green Energy Council.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://ucgef.org/en/bio/marc-porat|title=Porat, Marc  {{!}} US-China Green Energy Council|website=ucgef.org|access-date=2016-09-23}}</ref>

== Early career ==
Porat was educated at [[Columbia College (New York)|Columbia College]], where he graduated in 1972.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Biographies of Silicon Valley protagonists|url=https://www.scaruffi.com/svhistory/silicon/porat.html|website=www.scaruffi.com|access-date=2020-05-27}}</ref>

He then continued his graduate studies at [[Stanford University]] and authored a pivotal work entitled [[information economy|The Information Economy]]<ref>{{cite book |last = Porat|first = Mark Uri|title = The Information Economy: Definition and Measurement|date = May 1977|location = Washington, DC|publisher = [[United States Department of Commerce]]|url = http://eric.ed.gov/ERICWebPortal/custom/portlets/recordDetails/detailmini.jsp?_nfpb=true&_&ERICExtSearch_SearchValue_0=ED142205&ERICExtSearch_SearchType_0=no&accno=ED142205|oclc = 5184933}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Porat|first=Marc Uri|date=|title=The Information Economy: Definition and Measurement|url=http://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED142205|journal=|language=en|volume=}}</ref> as his doctoral thesis in which he predicted the transition from a manufacturing-based U.S. economy to one based on information. Porat is credited with first identifying the U.S. as an "information society."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?isbn=1136687416|title=The Information Society: Economic, Social, and Structural Issues|last=Salvaggio|first=Jerry|date=|website=books.google.com|publisher=|access-date=2016-09-22}}</ref> Later, his nephew Aaron Hurst defined and wrote about the "Purpose Economy" and credited Porat with the inspiration for predicting the rise of a new economy.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.fastcoexist.com/3028636/how-do-you-create-a-new-economy-based-on-purpose-and-meaningful-relationships|title=How Do You Create A New Economy Based On Purpose And Meaningful Relationships?|date=2014-04-07|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-22}}</ref>

After Stanford, Porat worked for the U.S. Department of Commerce and then served as a program director at the [[Aspen Institute]] and was later appointed Executive Director, Washington Activities of the Aspen Institute Program on Communications and Society.<ref>{{Cite web|url = http://hourofthetime.com/1-LF/aspeninstituteannualmeeting1978.pdf|title = Aspen Institute Annual Meeting 1978|date = |accessdate = |website = |publisher = |last = |first = }}</ref>  While at   Aspen, Porat produced the film The Information Society<ref>{{Cite web|title = The information society (on YouTube)|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=waQy2WKHiwY}}</ref><ref>{{Citation|title = The information society|url = http://www.worldcat.org/title/information-society/oclc/8392652|date = 1980-01-01|accessdate = 2015-08-30|language = English}}</ref> for PBS.

After leaving the Aspen Institute, Porat co-founded Private Satellite Network (PSN).<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1891&dat=19880624&id=VmUfAAAAIBAJ&sjid=vNQEAAAAIBAJ&pg=2557,2976456&hl=en|title=Private TV Networks Flourishing as Satellite Technology Advances|last=|first=|date=June 24, 1988|website=news.google.com|publisher=Gadsden Times - Google News Archive Search|access-date=2016-09-22}}</ref> The company was a direct broadcast satellite innovator that built and operated television and data networks for Fortune 500 companies and governments. The firm  pioneered the use of small aperture rooftop antennas for videoconferencing. The company was sold and Porat joined [[Apple Inc.|Apple Computer]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://lowendmac.com/2006/growing-apple-with-the-macintosh-the-sculley-years/|title=Growing Apple with the Macintosh: The Sculley Years|date=2006-02-22|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-22}}</ref>

== General Magic ==
Porat co-founded [[General Magic]] in 1990 with [[Andy Hertzfeld]] and [[Bill Atkinson]] from the original Mac team. The company built an early handheld communications device called [[Magic Link]]. Referred to then as a “personal intelligent communicator,” it was the precursor to the smart phone [[Personal digital assistant|PDA]].<ref>{{Cite news|url = |title = Marc Porat: Philosopher of the Shared Vision|last = Businessweek|first = |date = |work = |access-date = |via = }}</ref> The company also pioneered "intelligent agents."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.wired.com/1995/01/porat/|title=I Want|last=Kline|first=David|date=1995|website=www.wired.com|publisher=|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-22}}</ref><nowiki/>

Porat served as CEO from 1990 to 1996 and took the company public in 1995 at a valuation of $834M. The stock doubled on the first day.<ref>{{Cite news|title = COMPANY NEWS; General Magic Stock Surges on First Trading Day|url = https://www.nytimes.com/1995/02/11/business/company-news-general-magic-stock-surges-on-first-trading-day.html|newspaper = The New York Times|date = 1995-02-11|access-date = 2015-08-30|issn = 0362-4331|first = John|last = Markoff}}</ref>

== The Built Environment ==
In 2002, Porat co-founded Serious Materials, a company manufacturing high-efficiency windows and drywall.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Serious Materials - MIT Technology Review|url = http://www2.technologyreview.com/tr50/seriousmaterials/|accessdate = 2015-08-30|first = MIT Technology|last = Review}}</ref> He then founded CalStar Products in 2007, a firm recovering energy from industrial waste streams. He  also co-founded Zeta Communities (ZETA) in 2007,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.builderonline.com/building/operations/startup-builders-make-waves-in-recessions-wake_o|title=Startup Builders Make Waves in Recession's Wake|date=2011-09-09|access-date=2016-09-23}}</ref> a firm  designing and manufacturings net-zero energy multifamily housing that won the Green Builder Home of the Year Award.<ref>{{Cite web|title = ZETA Communities wins green award for affordable, zero-energy townhome|url = http://content.usatoday.com/communities/greenhouse/post/2009/12/zeta-communities-wins-2009-green-award-for-zero-energy-oakland-ca-townhome/1#.VeKHj9NViko|accessdate = 2015-08-30}}</ref>

== Personal Life ==
Porat was born to Frieda and Dan Porat in [[Israel]] and grew up in [[England]]<ref>{{cite web |title=Marc Porat on Information Economy |url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZYuzWvF60Kg |accessdate=17 September 2020}}</ref>. He and his family moved to the [[United States]], where his father worked at [[Harvard University]] and [[Stanford University]]'s [[SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=D'Onfro|first=Jillian|title=The incredible rise of Ruth Porat, CFO at one of the most valuable companies in the world|url=https://www.businessinsider.com/ruth-porat-google-alphabet-biography-2016-7|access-date=2020-07-27|website=Business Insider}}</ref> Porat's sister, [[Ruth Porat]], is the current CFO of [[Google]] and former CFO of [[Morgan Stanley]], and was named by ''[[Forbes]]'' as the 19th most powerful woman in 2019.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Bergen|first=Mark|date=2015-07-15|title=Wall Street Is Downright Giddy for the Debut of Ruth Porat, Google's New CFO|url=https://www.vox.com/2015/7/15/11614746/wall-street-is-downright-giddy-for-the-debut-of-ruth-porat-googles|access-date=2020-07-27|website=Vox|language=en}}</ref>
