'''Emilio Botín-Sanz de Sautuola García de los Ríos,''' ''[[iure uxoris]]'' '''[[Marquess of O'Shea]]''' (1 October 1934 – 10 September 2014) was a Spanish banker.<ref name=bbc>[https://www.bbc.com/news/business-29138310 Santander's chairman "Emilio Botin dies."] BBC News. Retrieved 9 October 2014.</ref> He was the executive chairman of Spain's [[Grupo Santander]]. In 1993 his bank absorbed Banco Español de Crédito ([[Banesto]]), and in 1999 it merged with Banco Central Hispano creating [[Banco Santander|Banco Santander Central Hispano]] (BSCH), which became Spain's largest bank, of which he was co-president with Central Hispano's José María Amusategui, until Amusategui retired in 2002. In 2004, BSCH acquired the British bank [[Abbey (bank)|Abbey National]], making BSCH the second largest bank in Europe by market capitalisation. He was known for his obsession with growth and performance as well as regularly visiting branches.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/02_24/b3787634.htm |title=BW Online &#124; June 17, 2002 &#124; Emilio Botín |work=businessweek.com |year=2011 |accessdate=31 March 2011 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20110601110648/http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/02_24/b3787634.htm |archivedate=1 June 2011 }}</ref>

==Early life==
Botín was born in [[Santander, Cantabria|Santander]], [[Cantabria]], on the northern coast of Spain, the eldest of two sons of [[Emilio Botín (1903–1993)|Emilio Botín-Sanz de Sautuola López]] , born on 18 January 1903, and Ana María García de los Ríos y Caller.<ref>http://www.geneall.net/H/per_page.php?id=327221 Emilio Botín in a Portuguese Genealogical site</ref> After attending as a [[boarding school|boarding student]] the [[Society of Jesus|Jesuit]] school of [[Colegio de la Inmaculada (Gijón)|Colegio de la Inmaculada]], in [[Gijón]], he studied law and economics at the [[University of Deusto]] in [[Bilbao]]. 

==Career==
In 1986 Botín, then aged 52, took over from his father as chairman of Santander, one of many banks that existed in Spain at the time. Botín was no newcomer to the banking world. His father, grandfather and great-grandfather were all bankers.{{citation needed|date=January 2011}}

In 2005, ''[[Forbes (magazine)|Forbes]]'' put Emilio Botín's net worth at [[United States dollar|$]]1.7 billion.{{Citation needed|date=June 2009}} Botín's eldest daughter, [[Ana Patricia Botín]], was the president of [[Banesto]] from 2002 to November 2010 and was the CEO of [[Santander UK]] from December 2010 until Emilio's death upon which she was elected his successor as executive chairman of Grupo Santander.

During his chairmanship, Banco Santander was named 2012 'Best bank in the world', the third time that the bank had received this award over the previous seven years.<ref name="noticiasbancarias.com">{{cite news| url=http://noticiasbancarias.com/bancos/06/07/2012/banco-santander-elegido-mejor-banco-del-mundo-por-euromoney/16126.html | work= Noticias Bancarias | title=Banco Santander, elegido mejor banco del mundo por Euromoney| date=6 July 2012}}</ref>

==Secret bank accounts==
Botín and his family held undisclosed bank accounts in Switzerland since 1937. Those accounts were discovered by the Spanish tax authorities in 2010. Botín and his family voluntarily settled the case, paying a bill of €200 million. In 2012, Spain's High Court dropped a tax evasion probe for these issues, stating that Botín and his family had satisfied Spanish tax authorities with the €200 million settlement.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ft.com/intl/cms/s/0/23a87a82-a40f-11e1-8878-00144feabdc0.html#axzz2KZinTALV|title=Spain drops Botín family tax evasion probe|work=Financial Times|accessdate=10 October 2014}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Botín was married to the [[Paloma O'Shea|Marchioness of O'Shea]],<!-- when did they marry? --> and they had six children. His daughter Carmen was married to golfer [[Seve Ballesteros]] from 1988 to 2004.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/sport/golf/2393501/Ballesteros-troubles-grow-with-divorce.html|title=Lewine Mair, ¨Ballesteros Troubles Grow With Divorce¨, ''The Telegraph'' (29 December 2004)|date=29 December 2004|work=Telegraph.co.uk|accessdate=10 October 2014}}</ref>

He died on 9 September 2014, of a heart attack in [[Madrid]]. Botín was survived by his six children and nineteen grandchildren.

Botin's eldest daughter [[Ana Patricia Botín]], previously head of Santander's British business, was appointed as chairman after his death.<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.santander.com/csgs/Satellite/CFWCSancomQP01/en_GB/Corporate/Press-Room/2014/09/10/Ana-Botin-unanimously-appointed-to-chair-the-board-of-Banco-Santander-.html|title=Ana Botín, unanimously appointed to chair the board of Banco Santander| date=10 September 2014}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2011/09/21/business/global/spain-examines-emilio-botins-hidden-swiss-account.html|title=Spain Examines Long Hidden Swiss Account|last=Jr|first=Landon Thomas|date=2011-09-20|work=The New York Times|access-date=2018-01-02|last2=Minder|first2=Raphael|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>
