'''Teresópolis''' ({{IPA-pt|teɾeˈzɔpolis}}, {{IPA-pt|tɛɾeˈzɔpɔliɕ|}}, {{IPA-pt|tɛɾeˈzɔpuliɕ|}}, {{IPA-pt|teɾeˈzɔpuliɕ|}}) is a [[Brazil]]ian municipality located in the [[States of Brazil|state]] of [[Rio de Janeiro (state)|Rio de Janeiro]], in a mountainous region known as ''Região Serrana''. The [[Serra dos Órgãos National Park]] lies partly within the city limits. The city is known as the home of the [[Brazil national football team]], since it hosts [[Brazilian Football Confederation|CBF]]'s [[Granja Comary (CBF)|training ground]] at [[Granja Comary]].<ref>http://www.teresopolison.com/cbf.php</ref>

==History==
[[File:Fundacao de Teresópolis.jpg|thumb|left|Founding of Teresopolis by then Governor of Rio de Janeiro, [[Francisco Portela]] on July 6, 1890.]]
Before the arrival of the Portuguese to the area where Teresópolis lies today, in the 16th century, it was inhabited by [[Indigenous peoples in Brazil|indigenous brazilians]]. In the following centuries, Portuguese started buying land there. The region was also occupied by a [[quilombo]], formed by runaway slaves coming from sugar cane plantations near [[Rio de Janeiro]].

In 1821, English citizen George March (born and raised in Portugal) established a farm there, which later became the most important settlement along the way between the court, in Rio de Janeiro, and the territory of Gerais (nowadays, the state of [[Minas Gerais]]), which led to the great improvement of agriculture and cattle raising.

The Brazilian imperial family was much impressed by the natural beauty and the climate of the region, which developed slowly so that in 1855 the settlement became a village that was named Freguesia de Santo Antonio de Paquequer.

The further development of the village was due to the traders that came from Minas Gerais in the way to Rio de Janeiro, and used the region as a resting stop. Finally, on July 6, 1891, the village became a municipality that was named Teresópolis ("city of Teresa"), after [[Teresa Cristina of the Two Sicilies|Empress Teresa Cristina]], wife of [[Pedro II of Brazil|Emperor Pedro II]].

==Geography==

According to the 2010 Brazilian Census, the city has a population of 163,746 within a land area of 770,6 km2. The Serra dos Órgãos National Park is in the vicinity. Its predominant vegetation belongs to the Atlantic Forest type. The city is surrounded by forests and by well-known summits or peaks, because of which the city is known as the national capital of mountaineering. The most famous peaks are:
* Peak of Pedra do Sino – {{convert|2263|m}}
* Peak of Pedra do Açu – {{convert|2230|m}}
* Peak of Agulha do Diabo – {{convert|2020|m}}
* Peak of Nariz do Frade – {{convert|1919|m}}
* Peak of Dedo de Deus – {{convert|1651|m}}
* Peak of Pedra da Ermitage – {{convert|1485|m}}
* Peak of Dedo de Nossa Senhora – {{convert|1320|m}}
Of these, Dedo de Deus ("God's finger") is the most famous.

The municipality contains the {{convert|4397|ha}} [[Montanhas de Teresópolis Municipal Nature Park]], created in 2009 to protect a large area of [[Atlantic Forest]].<ref>{{citation|ref={{harvid|Parque Natural Municipal Montanhas de Teresópolis – Prefeitura}}|language=Portuguese

==Climate==

Teresópolis features a [[subtropical highland climate]] ([[Köppen climate classification]]: '''Cfb'''), with relatively cold and dry winters and mild and wet summers. The annual average temperature is 16&nbsp;°C/60.8&nbsp;°F.

Teresópolis has one of the mildest climates in [[Brazil]]<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.365diasviajando.com/os-encantos-de-teresopolis/|title=Os encantos de Teresópolis |date=14 April 2009 |work=Geórgia Honório| publisher=365 dias viajando|accessdate=5 November 2011}}</ref> and this is one reason why the area has been chosen as [[Brazilian Football Confederation]]'s training headquarters, hosting the [[Brazil national football team]], located in [[Granja Comary]].

==Tragedy==
The uncontrolled growth of Teresópolis and other cities of the mountainous region near Rio de Janeiro led to the construction of a great number of houses in mountainous terrain or on the banks of rivers. This fact, together with a 24-hour rainfall that exceeded what was expected for the entire month, caused [[January 2011 Rio de Janeiro floods and mudslides|a series of floods and mudslides in January 2011]]. More than 400 people died and thousands lost their houses in the event, which is considered the worst weather-related tragedy in Brazilian history. .<ref>{{cite news|title=UPDATE 5-At least 257 died as rains pummeled this Brazilian region|author=Stuart Grudgings|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/idUSN1220035420110112|newspaper=[[Reuters]]|date=January 12, 2011|accessdate=January 13, 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title= Brazil Landslides Death Toll Rises|author= Tom Phillips|url= https://www.theguardian.com/world/2011/jan/13/brazil-landslides-death-toll-rises1|date= January 14, 2011|accessdate= January 14, 2011|newspaper= [[The Guardian]]}}</ref>
