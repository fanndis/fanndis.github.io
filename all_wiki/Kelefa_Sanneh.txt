'''Kelefa T. Sanneh''' (born 1975) is an American [[journalist]] and [[music criticism|music critic]]. From 2000 to 2008, he wrote for ''[[The New York Times]]'', covering the [[rock and roll]], [[hip hop music|hip-hop]], and [[pop music]] scenes.<ref>[http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/people/s/kelefa_sanneh/index.html Kelefa Sanneh | Articles], ''The New York Times''.</ref> Since 2008 he has been a staff writer for ''[[The New Yorker]]''.<ref>[http://www.newyorker.com/contributors/kelefa-sanneh "Contributors | Kalefa Sanneh"], ''The New Yorker''.</ref>

==Early life==
Sanneh was born in [[Birmingham]], [[West Midlands (county)|West Midlands]], [[England]], and spent his early years in [[Ghana]] and [[Scotland]], before his family moved to [[Massachusetts]] in 1981, then to [[Connecticut]] in 1989.<ref name="New Yorker">{{cite web|author=|title=Contributors: Kelefa Sanneh|url=http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/bios/kelefa_sanneh/search?contributorName=kelefa%20sanneh|work=The New Yorker|date=|accessdate=April 16, 2009}}</ref><ref name="Christianity Today">{{cite web|last=Bonk|first=Jonathan J.|title=The Defender of the Good News: Questioning Lamin Sanneh|work=Christianity Today|url=http://www.christianitytoday.com/ct/2003/october/35.112.html|volume=47|issue=10|date=October 1, 2003}}</ref> His father, [[Lamin Sanneh]], was born in [[Janjanbureh, The Gambia|Janjanbureh]], [[Gambia]], and was D. Willis James Professor of Missions and World Christianity and professor of history at [[Yale Divinity School]].<ref name="Christianity Today"/> Kelefa's mother, Sandra, is a [[white South African]] linguist who teaches the [[isiZulu]] language at [[Yale University|Yale]].<ref>{{cite web|last=Micner|first=Tamara|title=Zulu program clicks with small group of students|url=http://www.yaleherald.com/article.php?Article=4889|work=The Yale Herald|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20061129044005/http://www.yaleherald.com/article.php?Article=4889|archivedate=November 29, 2006|date=October 6, 2006}}</ref>

Sanneh graduated from [[Harvard University]] in 1997 with a degree in literature.<ref name=Harvard>{{cite web |title=Welcome from the Director of Studies |url=http://isites.harvard.edu/icb/icb.do?keyword=k64196&tabgroupid=icb.tabgroup92294 |publisher=Harvard University Department of Comparative Literature |archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5xsTk2j4I?url=http://isites.harvard.edu/icb/icb.do?keyword=k64196&tabgroupid=icb.tabgroup92294 |archivedate=April 12, 2011 |url-status=dead }}</ref>  While at Harvard he worked for ''[[Transition Magazine]]'' and served as rock director for [[WHRB]]'s [[Record Hospital]]. Sanneh played bass in the Harvard bands Hypertrophie Shitstraw, MOPAR, Fear of Reprisal and TacTic, as well as a [[Devo]] cover band that included members of [[Fat Day]], Gerty Farish, [[Bishop Allen]] and [[Lavender Diamond]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Incipient Roadkill|url=http://www.thecrimson.com/article.aspx?ref=233680|work=The Harvard Crimson|date=March 24, 1994}}</ref> Sanneh's thesis paper, ''The Black Galactic: Toward A Greater African America'', combined interests in music, literature and culture in writing about [[The Nation of Islam]] and the [[Sun Ra Arkestra]] as efforts to transcend oppression in the [[African-American]] experience with desires to travel into [[outer space]].<ref name="Harvard Lit Dept">{{cite web|title=Lit Alumni|url=http://isites.harvard.edu/icb/icb.do?keyword=k64196&pageid=icb.page306181&pageContentId=icb.pagecontent861594&view=monthly_totals|archive-url=https://archive.today/20121214190409/http://isites.harvard.edu/icb/icb.do?keyword=k64196&pageid=icb.page306181&pageContentId=icb.pagecontent861594&view=monthly_totals|url-status=dead|archive-date=14 December 2012|work=Department of Comparative Literature|publisher=Harvard University|accessdate=15 March 2012}}</ref><ref name="Google Book: Black Galactic">{{cite book|last=Sanneh|first=Kelefa|title=The Black Galactic: Towards a Greater African America|url=https://books.google.com/books/about/The_black_galactic.html?id=5GQ3IAAACAAJ|publisher=Harvard University|accessdate=15 March 2012}}</ref>

==Career==
Sanneh garnered considerable publicity for an article he wrote in the October 31, 2004, edition of ''[[The New York Times]]'' titled "The Rap against Rockism".<ref>James Houston, [http://www.dolphin.upenn.edu/fcpaper/0507.pdf "Rockism of Ages"], ''First Call'', Vol. V, No. 7, November 15, 2004.</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.thefader.com/2015/10/05/poptimism-kelefa-sanneh-interview|title=Poptimism's Unlikely Reign|last=Ducker|first=Eric|date=October 5, 2015|work=The Fader|access-date=2017-10-07|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.slate.com/articles/arts/music_box/2006/05/the_perils_of_poptimism.html|title=The Perils of Poptimism|last=Rosen|first=Jody|date=2006-05-09|work=Slate|access-date=2017-10-07|language=en-US|issn=1091-2339}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.popmatters.com/column/196122-no-apologies-a-critique-of-the-rockist-v.-poptimist-paradigm/|title=No Apologies: A Critique of the Rockist v. Poptimist Paradigm|last=Loss|first=Robert|date=August 10, 2015|work=PopMatters|access-date=2017-10-07}}</ref> The article brought to light to the general public a debate among American and British music critics about [[rockism]], a term Sanneh defined to mean "idolizing the authentic old legend (or underground hero) while mocking the latest pop star; lionizing punk while barely tolerating disco; loving the live show and hating the music video; extolling the growling performer while hating the lip-syncher."<ref name=":0">{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2004/10/31/arts/music/31sann.html|title=The Rap Against Rockism|last=Sanneh|first=Kelefa|date=October 31, 2004|work=The New York Times}}</ref> In the essay, Sanneh further asks music listeners to "stop pretending that serious rock songs will last forever, as if anything could, and that shiny pop songs are inherently disposable, as if that were necessarily a bad thing. [[Van Morrison]]'s '[[Into the Music]]' was released the same year as the [[Sugarhill Gang]]'s '[[Rapper's Delight]]'; which do you hear more often?"<ref name=":0" />

Before covering music for the ''Times'', Sanneh was the deputy editor of ''Transition'', a journal of race and culture, based at the [[W. E. B. Du Bois Institute|W. E. B. Du Bois Institute for African and African American Research]], at Harvard University. His writing has also appeared in ''[[The Source (magazine)|The Source]]''; ''[[Rolling Stone]]''; ''[[Blender (magazine)|Blender]]''; ''[[The Village Voice]]''; ''[[Man's World (magazine)|Man's World]]'' ("India's classiest men's magazine"); ''[[Da Capo Press|Da Capo Best Music Writing]]'' in 2002, 2005, and 2007; and newspapers around the world.

Sanneh wrote the "Project Trinity," which appeared in ''The New Yorker's'' April 7, 2008, edition, to give context to the controversial comments of Reverend [[Jeremiah Wright]], who was [[Barack Obama]]'s pastor. The article provides a historical context of the [[Trinity United Church of Christ]], Obama's church, and to Wright, the former pastor of Trinity.

In 2008, he left ''The New York Times'' to join ''[[The New Yorker]]'' as a staff writer.<ref>{{cite web|last=Koblin |first=John |title=Kelefa Sanneh, Ariel Levy Join New Yorker |url=http://www.observer.com/2008/kelefa-sanneh-ariel-levy-join-i-new-yorker-i |work=New York Observer |accessdate=April 11, 2011 |date=March 4, 2008 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20091204102801/http://www.observer.com/2008/kelefa-sanneh-ariel-levy-join-i-new-yorker-i |archivedate=December 4, 2009 }}</ref> As of 2009, Sanneh lived in [[Brooklyn]].<ref name="New Yorker"/>
