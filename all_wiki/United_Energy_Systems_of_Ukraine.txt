'''United Energy Systems of Ukraine''', (UESU) ({{lang-uk|Єдині енергетичні системи України, ЄЕСУ}}), was a natural gas trading company in [[Ukraine]]. In the years 1995 and 1996, it was the largest natural gas importer in Ukraine. The company was affected by a series of financial irregularities leading to criminal charges against the principals and closure of the company in 2009. 

== Origin ==
[[File:Yulia Tymoshenko November 2009-3cropped.jpg|thumb|upright=1.2|Yulia Tymoshenko]] 
In 1989, [[Yulia Tymoshenko]] founded a family cooperative in [[Dnipropetrovsk]]. In 1991, Tymoshenko, her husband, [[Oleksandr Tymoshenko|Oleksandr]], and Olexandr Gravetsas created the Ukrainian Petrol Company, a vendor of gasoline to farmers in [[Dnipropetrovsk Oblast]]. In 1995, the company was reorganized into the United Energy Systems of Ukraine.<ref>Varfolomeyev O. [http://www.jamestown.org/single/?no_cache=1&tx_ttnews%5Btt_news%5D=20189&tx_ttnews%5BbackPid%5D=220 "Will Yulia Tymoshenko be Ukraine's first woman prime minister?"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20061125034223/http://www.jamestown.org/publications_details.php?volume_id=5&issue_id=249&article_id=2820 |date=25 November 2006 }} ''Prism'' [[The Jamestown Foundation]] 4(3) 6 February 1998 Accessed 4 February 2007.</ref> 

UESU bought gas from RAO Gazprom (Russia). However, UESU was largely held by a Turkish company, United Energy International Limited (UEIL) and the perhaps inflated payments of Ukraine gas customers was filtered through a number of international locations (allowing a distribution of profits (hundreds of millions of dollars) amongst the energy [[Ukrainian oligarchs|oligarchs]] involved, before Russia was paid.<ref>[https://books.google.com/books?id=HEZYAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA2921&lpg=PA2921&dq=uesu&source=bl&ots=5lpvamXiXU&sig=9qyw2Z6996pMWOkEseJXmlSOndU&hl=en&sa=X&ei=0OZTU6y3FYrPkAX1yoDYDg&ved=0CDQQ6AEwAzgU#v=onepage&q=uesu&f=false "American International Law Cases Fourth Series: 2009."] Oceana editorial board. Oxford University Press, 2011 p.2906 {{ISBN|0199758921}}, 9780199758920.</ref>

== Growth and decline ==
In 1997, UESU formed a consortium that was responsible for supplies of natural gas to Ukraine from Russia.<ref>[https://books.google.com/books?id=udlwTxw8FkYC&pg=PA26 "Staff Country Report Ukraine."] [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]] 1997 p.26</ref><ref> Kuziod T. [https://books.google.com/books?id=tRnMeZcgry8C&pg=RA1-PA61 "Ukraine: State and Nation Building."] ''[[Routledge]]'' 1998 p61 {{ISBN|0415171954}}</ref> By 1996 and 1997, UESU was the biggest gas trading company in Ukraine and had branched out into other areas.<ref name="Aslund (2009)"/><ref name="Wilson (2006)"/> In early 1997, it controlled several banks, had stakes in [[metallurgy]] and machine building companies and in two airports; participated in [[Bulgaria|Bulgarian]] and [[Turkey|Turkish]] pipelines; and controlled several local and national newspapers.<ref name="Rutland (1998)">Rutland P. [https://books.google.com/books?id=uHHWDreGPZsC&pg=PA173 "The Challenge of Integration."] [[M. E. Sharpe]] 1998 p.173 - 174 {{ISBN|0765603594}}.</ref> It controlled 80% of Ukraine's natural gas supply and accounted for an estimated eights of Ukraine's GDP.<ref name="Pond (2002)">Pond E. [https://books.google.com/books?id=fHl1Y-OYyMYC&pg=PA146&dq=%22United+Energy%22+Ukraine&hl=nl&sa=X&ei=cpz5UJfvN8il0AWfy4GwDg&ved=0CFQQ6AEwBjgK#v=onepage&q=%22United%20Energy%22%20Ukraine&f=false "The Rebirth of Europe."] ''[[Brookings Institution Press]]'' 2002 Second edition p.147 {{ISBN|0815771592}}.</ref> 

The decline of UESU related to shifts in power in Ukraine, leaving the principals exposed to criminal charges and the company exposed to heavy taxation.<ref name="Wilson (2006)"/>

== Potential conflicts of interest ==
[[Pavlo Lazarenko]], [[Vice Prime Minister of Ukraine]] in 1995, was a part owner of UESU. He allocated gasoline quotas to private companies, giving priority to [[Itera]] and UESU. <ref name="Aslund (2009)"> Åslund A. [https://books.google.com/books?id=uHHWDreGPZsC&pg=PA173 "How Ukraine Became a Market Economy and Democracy."] [[Peterson Institute for International Economics]] 2009 p.173 - 174. {{ISBN|0-88132-427-2}}</ref><ref name="Rutland (1998)"/> Lazarenko resigned in 1997.<ref name="Wilson (2006)">Wilson A. [https://books.google.com/books?id=LooNAwAAQBAJ&pg=PA20&lpg=PA20&dq=uesu&source=bl&ots=UrlDtZ1d_W&sig=4d8zTEUUqOe3osvHfmCT5YKGEDM&hl=en&sa=X&ei=0OZTU6y3FYrPkAX1yoDYDg&ved=0CDEQ6AEwAjgU#v=onepage&q=uesu&f=false "Ukraine's Orange Revolution."] Yale University Press, 2006 p.20 {{ISBN|0300143915}}, 9780300143911.</ref>

In December 1996, Yulia Tymoshenko was elected to the [[parliament of Ukraine]] and resigned from the presidency of UESU. [[Hennady Tymoshenko]], her father in law, became UESU president.<ref name="Rutland (1998)"/> Tymochenko was Minister of Fuel and Energy (1999 - 2001) and later, prime minister (2005 and 2007 - 2010). In July 1997, Lazarenko, the [[Prime Minister of Ukraine|Prime Minister]], resigned <ref name="Rutland (1998)"/> In September 1997, UESU was accused of [[Dumping (pricing policy)|dumping]].<ref name="Rutland (1998)"/> and at the end of 1997, UESU was excluded from the natural gas market of Ukraine.<ref name="Wilson (2006)"/>

== Charges against principals ==
=== Lazarenko ===
In 1999, Lazarenko was arrested in the US and charged with corruption and money laundering. Between 2000 and 2004, Yulia Tymoshenko's ties to Lazarenko were investigated.<ref>Zarakhovich Y. [http://www.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,1022561,00.html "Ukraine's Iron Lady."] ''Time'' 30 January 2005 Accessed 26 May 2010.</ref> UESU was dissolved in 2009. {{citation needed|date=October 2011}}

=== Olexandr Tymoshenko  and Valery Falkovych ===
In August 2000, Ukrainian police arrested Olexandr Tymoshenko and Valery Falkovych (first deputy general director). Tymoshenko was charged with [[Embezzlement|embezzling]] US$800,000 in public funds through the export of rolled metal to Asia during the 1990s.<ref>[http://www.ce-review.org/00/29/ukrainenews29.html "Central Europe Review – Ukrainian News Review."] Ce review Ukrainian news website.</ref> On 9 August 2001, following a decision of the Kyiv Sviatoshynsky District Court of the Kyiv region, Tymoshenko and Falkovich were released from custody and all charges against them were dropped. This decision was supported by the Court of Appeal of Kyiv region and the [[Supreme Court of Ukraine]].{{citation needed|date=January 2013}} On 30 April 2002, the Kyiv Svyatoshinsky local court of the Kyiv region dismissed all criminal charges against Oleksandr Tymoshenko and admitted that the actions of the Prosecutor General of Ukraine were illegal.{{citation needed|date=January 2013}}

In early 2012, Oleksandr Tymoshenko received political asylum in the [[Czech Republic]].<ref>[https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-europe-16439321 "Husband of Ukraine's Tymoshenko wins Czech asylum."] ''[[BBC]]'' 6 January 2012 Accessed 6 January 2012.</ref><ref>[http://uk.reuters.com/article/2012/01/06/uk-ukraine-tymoshenko-czech-idUKTRE8050Q520120106?feedType=RSS&feedName=worldNews "Czechs give asylum to spouse of Ukraine's Tymoshenko."] ''[[Reuters]]'' 6 January 2012.</ref>

=== Yulia Tymoshenko ===
Yulia Tymoshenko was arrested on 13 February 2001 and released on 27 March 2001. She was charged with forgery and gas smuggling.<ref>[https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/worldnews/europe/ukraine/8597402/Yulia-Tymoshenko-Ukrains-Iron-Lady.html "Yulia Tymoshenko: Ukrain's 'Iron Lady'."] ''[[The Daily Telegraph]]'' 24 June 2011 Accessed 25 June 2011.</ref> In May 2004, Kyiv's Appeal Court cancelled the lodging of criminal proceedings against her. <ref>[http://www2.pravda.com.ua/en/news/2003/4/9/2782.htm "Court closes Tymoshenkos cases."] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070930221152/http://www2.pravda.com.ua/en/news/2003/4/9/2782.htm |date=30 September 2007 }} Ukrayinska Pravda Dead URL October 2010.</ref> In December 2005, the Russian Prosecutor General's Office closed all criminal cases against Tymoshenko.<ref>[http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/europe/4283844.stm "Ukraine ex-PM warrant cancelled."] ''BBC News'' 26 September 2005 Accessed 26 May 2010.</ref>

=== Mykola Syvulskyi ===
Syvulskyi, a former deputy minister of finance was arrested and charged in September 1998. It was alleged he had illegally siphoned funds from [[Ukrgazprom]] (predessor of [[Naftogaz]]) to UESU.

== Further charges ==
In July 2011, the [[Ukrainian security service]] opened a criminal investigation into the embezzlement of $405&nbsp;million by UESU in 1997.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/108074/ "Tymoshenko faces yet another investigation."] ''[[Kyiv Post]]'' 5 July 2011.</ref> 

In April 2012, Tymoshenko faced five charges: organising  and attempting to appropriate a large sum of public funds in 1997 and 1998 through receiving [[value added tax]] (VAT) (Part 3 of Article 27, Part 5 of Article 191 of the Criminal Code of Ukraine); tax evasion by the UESU Corporation (Part 3 of Article 27, Part 3 of Article 212 of the Criminal Code); the non-payment of income tax (Part 3 of Article 212 of the Criminal Code); and, committing a crime via official forgery (Part 3 of Article 27, Part 2 of Article 366 of the Criminal Code).<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/ukraine/trial-of-tymoshenko-in-uesu-case-postponed-until-december-27-333145.html "Trial of Tymoshenko in UESU case postponed until December 27."] Kyiv Post news website 6 December 2013. Accessed 21 April 2014.</ref>

Kyivsky District Court of [[Kharkiv]] closed the case on 28 February 2014,
:"The court has ruled that criminal proceedings on charges against Yulia Volodymyrivna Tymoshenko regarding crimes committed under Part 3, Article 27, Part 5, Article 191, Part 2, Article 15, Part 3, Article 212, and Part 2, Article 366 of the criminal Code have been closed close due to the prosecutor's dropping of the charges." (Kostiantyn Sadovsky, judge.) <ref>[http://en.interfax.com.ua/news/general/193370.html "Kharkiv court drops charges against Tymoshenko in UESU case."] [[Interfax-Ukraine]] 28 February 2014.</ref>
