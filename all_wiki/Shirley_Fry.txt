'''Shirley June Fry Irvin''' (née '''Fry'''; born June 30, 1927) is a former world No. 1 [[tennis]] player from the United States. During her career, which lasted from the early 1940s until the mid-1950s, she won the singles title at all four Grand Slam events as well as 13 doubles titles. As of 2020, Fry Irvin is the longest surviving female Grand Slam singles champion.

==Biography==
Fry was raised in [[Akron, Ohio]] and started playing tennis competitively at age nine.<ref name="sentinel2000">{{cite web|author1=David Whitley|title=Orlando's Wimbledon Champion|url=http://articles.orlandosentinel.com/2000-09-08/sports/0009080343_1_irvin-shirley-fry-grand-slam|website=[[Orlando Sentinel]]|date=September 8, 2000}}</ref><ref name="engaged"/> She was educated at [[Rollins College]] in [[Winter Park, Florida]] where she graduated in 1949.<ref name="sentinel2000"/><ref name="itahof"/>

Fry is one of 10 women{{efn|name=grandslam|along with [[Maureen Connolly]], [[Margaret Court]], [[Steffi Graf]], [[Doris Hart]], [[Billie Jean King]], [[Martina Navratilova]], [[Chris Evert]], [[Maria Sharapova]], and [[Serena Williams]].}} to have won each [[Grand Slam (tennis)|Grand Slam]] [[Singles (tennis)|singles]] tournament at least once during her career. She is also one of seven women (with Hart, Court, Navratilova, [[Pam Shriver]], Serena Williams, and [[Venus Williams]]) to have won all four Grand Slam [[Doubles (tennis)|doubles]] tournaments. At the [[U.S. National tennis championship|U.S. National Championship]] (precursor of the [[U.S. Open (tennis)|U.S. Open]]) in 1942, Irvin reached the singles quarterfinals at the age of 15. At [[The Championships, Wimbledon|Wimbledon]] in [[1953 Wimbledon Championships|1953]], Fry and Hart lost only four games during the entire women's doubles tournament and won three matches without losing a game, including the semifinals and finals, the latter over Connolly and [[Julia Sampson]]. Fry won the last three Grand Slam singles tournaments she entered, including wins over [[Althea Gibson]] in the Wimbledon quarterfinal and U.S. Championship final in 1956 and the [[Australian Open|Australian Championships]] final in 1957.

Fry was ranked in the world top 10 in 1946 and 1948 and from 1950 through 1955 (no rankings issued from 1940 through 1945), and No. 1 in 1956.<ref>{{cite book |author=Collins, Bud |title=The Bud Collins History of Tennis: An Authoritative Encyclopedia and Record Book |publisher=New Chapter Press |location=New York, N.Y |year=2008 |pages=695, 702–3 |isbn=0-942257-41-3 |oclc= |doi= |accessdate=}}</ref> The [[United States Tennis Association|United States Lawn Tennis Association]] ranked her in the U.S. top 10 from 1944 through 1955 and No. 1 in 1956.<ref>{{cite book |author=United States Tennis Association |title=1988 Official USTA Tennis Yearbook |publisher=H.O. Zimman, Inc. |location=Lynn, Massachusetts |year=1988 |isbn= |oclc= |doi= |accessdate= |page=261}}</ref>  She was inducted into the [[International Tennis Hall of Fame]] in 1970.

From 1951 through 1956 she participated in the [[Wightman Cup]], the women's team competition between Great Britain and the United States, and contributed to the U.S. victory during each of these editions with the exception of [[1954 Wightman Cup|1954]], when her final doubles rubber was not played. She compiled a 10-2 W/L record.<ref name="itahof"/>

Fry married Karl Irvin in Australia, in February 1957 after which she retired from top-level tennis. The couple had four children.<ref name="engaged">{{cite news|title=Shirley Fry engaged; to wed in February|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=888&dat=19561204&id=ZiNVAAAAIBAJ&sjid=bIADAAAAIBAJ&pg=7042,1873525|work=[[St. Petersburg Times]]|date=December 4, 1956|page=22|via=[[Google News Archive]]}}</ref><ref name="itahof">{{cite web|title=Women's Collegiate Tennis Hall of Fame|url=http://itahalloffame.org/inductees/shirley-fry-irvin|website=Intercollegiate Tennis Association (ITA)}}</ref>
