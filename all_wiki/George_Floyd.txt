'''George Perry Floyd Jr.''' (October 14, 1973 – May 25, 2020) was an [[African Americans|African American]] man [[Killing of George Floyd|killed during an arrest]] after a store clerk alleged he had passed a counterfeit $20 bill in [[Minneapolis, Minnesota|Minneapolis]]. A white police officer named [[Derek Chauvin]] knelt on Floyd's neck for a period initially reported to be [[8 minutes and 46 seconds]].{{notetag|name=7:46 not 8:46|The initial criminal complaint gave the duration as 8:46, which came to be often cited by protesters and the media. Born in [[Fayetteville, North Carolina|Fayetteville]], [[North Carolina]], Floyd grew up in [[Houston]], [[Texas]], playing football and basketball throughout high school and college. He was a [[hip hop]] artist and served as a mentor in his religious community. Between 1997 and 2005, he was convicted of eight crimes. He served four years in prison after accepting a [[plea bargain]] for a 2007 [[Robbery|aggravated robbery]] in a home invasion.<ref name="snopes" /> In 2014, he moved to the Minneapolis area, residing in the nearby suburb of [[St. Louis Park, Minnesota|St. Louis Park]], and worked as a truck driver and [[Bouncer (doorman)|bouncer]]. In 2020, he lost his security job during the [[COVID-19 pandemic in Minnesota|COVID-19 pandemic]].

== Early life and education ==
Floyd was born on October 14, 1973, in Fayetteville, North Carolina to George Perry and Larcenia "Cissy" Jones Floyd.<ref name=BBC/><ref name=ChicagoTribune/>
He had four siblings.<ref name=HuffPost/><ref name=USAToday/><ref name=WaPo/>

Floyd's parents separated and, when he was two, his mother moved with the children to the [[Cuney Homes]] public housing,<ref name="NewYorkTimes" /><ref name="CNN-levenson" /><ref name="henaoCHITRIB11june2020">{{Cite web|last1=Henao|first1=Luis Andres|last2=Merchant|first2=Nomaan|last3=Lozano|first3=Juan|last4=Geller|first4=Adam|date=11 June 2020|title=A long look at the complicated life of George Floyd|url=https://www.chicagotribune.com/nation-world/ct-nw-life-of-george-floyd-biography-20200611-cxmlynpyvjczpbe6izfduzwv54-story.html|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200617102312/https://www.chicagotribune.com/nation-world/ct-nw-life-of-george-floyd-biography-20200611-cxmlynpyvjczpbe6izfduzwv54-story.html|archive-date=June 17, 2020|access-date=2020-06-12|website=chicagotribune.com}}</ref> known as Bricks, in Houston's [[Third Ward, Houston|Third Ward]], a historic [[African-American neighborhood|black neighborhood]] and one of the poorest areas of the city.<ref name="BBC" /><ref name="ChicagoTribune" /><ref name="NewYorkTimes" /> Floyd was called Perry as a child, but also Big Floyd; being over {{convert|6|ft|cm|0|spell=in}} tall in middle school, he saw sports as a vehicle for improving his life.<ref name="NewYorkTimes" />

Floyd graduated from [[Yates High School]] in 1993. While there, he was co-captain of the [[basketball]] team playing as a [[Power forward (basketball)|power forward]]. He was also on the [[American football|football]] team as a [[tight end]], and in 1992, his team went to the Texas state championships.<ref name=BBC/><ref name=NewYorkTimes/><ref name="CNN-levenson"/><ref name=USAToday/>

Between 1997 and 2005, Floyd served eight prison terms on various charges, including drug possession, theft, and trespass.<ref name="snopes" /><ref name=NewYorkTimes/><ref name=USAToday/> In 1997, at age 23, Floyd was arrested when police caught him delivering less than one gram of cocaine to another person, for which he was sentenced to six months in prison. The following year, Floyd was arrested twice for theft, receiving a sentence of 10 months for one count and 10 days for the other. In 2001, Floyd was arrested and sentenced to 15 days in jail for failing to provide his name, address, or birth date to a police officer. Between 2002 and 2005, he was arrested four more times: twice for drug possession and once for delivering, in each case less than a gram of cocaine, and once for criminal trespassing. He was sentenced to a total of about 30 months in jail for those four crimes. In 2009, he was sentenced to five years in prison for armed robbery in a home invasion<ref>{{Cite web|last=Toone|first=Stephanie|date=June 3, 2020|title=George Floyd, man killed in Minneapolis police encounter, had started new life in Minnesota|url=https://www.ajc.com/news/victim-police-encounter-had-started-new-life-minnesota/rMmT2wipeQFNnsypmh6oBL/|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200528221125/https://www.ajc.com/news/victim-police-encounter-had-started-new-life-minnesota/rMmT2wipeQFNnsypmh6oBL/|archive-date=May 28, 2020|access-date=June 4, 2020|website=[[The Atlanta Journal-Constitution]]|language=English}}</ref><ref name=walters_05292020>{{cite news|last=Walters|first=Joanna|date=May 29, 2020|title=An athlete, a father, a 'beautiful spirit': George Floyd in his friends' words|work=[[The Guardian]]|url=https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/may/29/george-floyd-who-was-he-his-friends-words|accessdate=June 4, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200603221534/https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/may/29/george-floyd-who-was-he-his-friends-words|archive-date=June 3, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref> and was paroled in January 2013.<ref name=TexasMonthly/>

By June 6, murals had been created in many cities, including [[Manchester]], Dallas, Miami, [[Idlib]], Los Angeles, Nairobi, [[Oakland, California|Oakland]], [[Strombeek-Bever]], Berlin, [[Pensacola, Florida|Pensacola]], and [[La Mesa, California|La Mesa]].<ref>{{Cite web|last1=Steele|first1=Austin|last2=Almond|first2=Kyle|date=June 6, 2020|title=George Floyd murals are popping up all over the world|url=https://www.cnn.com/2020/06/06/world/gallery/george-floyd-murals-trnd/index.html|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200624010959/https://www.cnn.com/2020/06/06/world/gallery/george-floyd-murals-trnd/|archive-date=June 24, 2020|access-date=June 6, 2020|website=CNN}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.miamiherald.com/news/local/community/miami-dade/article243231636.html |first=April |last=Rubin |date=June 5, 2020 |work=Miami Herald |title='Positive revolution.' Miami artist paints mural in Overtown of Floyd, Taylor, Kaepernick |access-date=June 6, 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200606213345/https://www.miamiherald.com/news/local/community/miami-dade/article243231636.html |archive-date=June 6, 2020 |url-status=live }}</ref>

A bill proposed by US Representative [[Sheila Jackson Lee]], the [[George Floyd Law Enforcement Trust and Integrity Act]], was designed to reduce police brutality and establish national policing standards and accreditations.<ref name="ABC">{{Cite web|last=Siegel|first=Benjamin|date=June 4, 2020|title=House Democrats introduce policing reform bill named for George Floyd|url=https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/house-democrats-introduce-policing-reform-bill-named-george/story?id=71069101|access-date=2020-06-06|work=ABC News|language=en|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200611135124/https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/house-democrats-introduce-policing-reform-bill-named-george/story?id=71069101|archive-date=June 11, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Kim|first1=Caitlyn|date=June 4, 2020|title=Congress Is Eyeing Legislative Solutions To Police Brutality, Including Jason Crow|language=en|work=Colorado Public Radio|url=https://www.cpr.org/2020/06/04/congress-is-eyeing-legislative-solutions-to-police-brutality-including-jason-crow/|accessdate=June 6, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200605192310/https://www.cpr.org/2020/06/04/congress-is-eyeing-legislative-solutions-to-police-brutality-including-jason-crow/|archive-date=June 5, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref>

The length of time that Chauvin was initially believed to have had his knee on Floyd's neck, [[eight minutes 46 seconds]], was widely commemorated as a "moment of silence" to honor Floyd.<ref>{{cite news |first1=William |last1=Cummings |first2=Ledyard |last2=King |first3=Christal |last3=Hayes |url=https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2020/06/08/democrats-gather-silence-honor-george-floyd-ahmaud-arbery/3155884001/|title=Democrats unveil sweeping police reform bill, honor George Floyd with 8 minutes, 46 seconds of silence|date=June 8, 2020|work=USA Today|accessdate=June 12, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200612024021/https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2020/06/08/democrats-gather-silence-honor-george-floyd-ahmaud-arbery/3155884001/|archive-date=June 12, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.marketwatch.com/story/new-york-stock-exchange-observes-moment-of-silence-in-honor-of-george-floyds-funeral-2020-06-09|title=New York Stock Exchange observe 8 minutes and 46 seconds of silence in honor of George Floyd's memory|last=DeCambre|first=Mark|date=June 9, 2020|work=Market Watch|accessdate=June 12, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200611054537/https://www.marketwatch.com/story/new-york-stock-exchange-observes-moment-of-silence-in-honor-of-george-floyds-funeral-2020-06-09|archive-date=June 11, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref>{{refn|name=7:46 not 8:46|group=note}}

Floyd's death was featured prominently in ''[[The Economist]]'', with the magazine running an [[obituary]], multiple articles, and numerous reader letters, ultimately making the legacy of his death its June 13 cover story.<ref>{{Cite news|title=How George Floyd's death reverberates around the world|work=The Economist|url=https://www.economist.com/international/2020/06/08/how-george-floyds-death-reverberates-around-the-world|access-date=2020-07-12|issn=0013-0613}}</ref> It wrote that his legacy "[is] the rich [[George Floyd protests#Impact and effects|promise of social reform]]."<ref name=TheEconomist/>

On September 18, 2020, the Minneapolis City Council approved designating the section of Chicago Avenue between 37th and 39th Streets as [[George Floyd memorial site|George Perry Floyd Jr. Place]], with a marker at the intersection with 38th Street where the incident took place. The intersection had been the location of a makeshift memorial that emerged the day after his death.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/minneapolis-to-name-stretch-of-chicago-avenue-for-george-floyd/ar-BB19bMl5|title=Minneapolis to name stretch of Chicago Avenue for George Floyd|last=Navratil|first=Liz|work=[[Star Tribune]]|date=September 19, 2020|accessdate=September 20, 2020}}</ref>

On 6 October 2020, [[Amnesty International]] delivered a letter with one million signatures from around the world to the US Attorney General [[William Barr]] to demand justice for George Floyd. The human rights advocacy group demanded that the police officers involved in the killing of George Floyd be held accountable.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2020/10/usa-amnesty-international-delivers-one-million-signatures-calling-for-justice-for-george-floyd/|title=USA: Amnesty International delivers one million signatures calling for justice for George Floyd|accessdate=6 October 2020|website=Amnesty International}}</ref>
