The '''Ukrainian parliamentary election of 2012''' took place on 28 October 2012.<ref name=dateset>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/95986/ Parliament mulls Feb. 3 vote to amend Constitution], [[Kyiv Post]] (January 31, 2011)<br />[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/96077/ Parliament sets parliamentary elections for October 2012, presidential elections for March 2015], [[Kyiv Post]] (February 1, 2011)<br />[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/96081/ Ukraine sets parliamentary vote for October 2012], [[Kyiv Post]] (February 1, 2011)<br />[http://en.rian.ru/exsoviet/20100125/157671733.html Early parliamentary elections may take place in May - Ukraine's Yanukovych], [[RIA Novosti]] (January 25, 2010)</ref> Because of various reasons, including the "impossibility of announcing election results" various [[by-elections]] have taken place since.<ref name=IUfinalresults12112012/><ref name=100prUKNWelect/><ref name=repeat7112012/><ref name="now 7 troubled constituencies after 2012 Ukrainian elections"/> Hence, several constituencies have been left unrepresented at various times.<ref name=IUfinalresults12112012/><ref name="Baloha and Dombrovsky mandates 3-7-13"/><ref name=BBCCAMP/><ref name="5 troubled constituencies after 2012 Ukrainian elections"/>

Unlike the two previous elections, this election used a mixed voting system (50% under party lists and 50% under [[majority|simple-majority]] [[constituencies]])<ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111IFU/> with a 5% [[election threshold]] and the participation of blocs of political parties was not allowed any more.<ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/117151/ Parliament passes law on parliamentary elections], [[Kyiv Post]] (17 November 2011)</ref> The method of 50/50 mixed elections was used previously in 1998 and 2002.<ref>[http://www.interfax.com.ua/eng/main/126521/ Ukrainian communists to seek return to proportional electoral system], [[Interfax-Ukraine]] (8 November 2012)</ref>

The [[election campaign]] was limited to 90 days.<ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111IFU/> Every [[Ukrainian citizenship|citizen of Ukraine]] 18 years of age or older<ref>{{in lang|uk}} [http://www.pravda.com.ua/articles/2012/04/9/6962399/ Перший крок до зриву виборів], [[Ukrayinska Pravda]] (9 April 2012)</ref> was able to vote in 33,540 polling stations in [[Ukraine]] and 116 foreign polling stations in [[Ukrainian diaspora|77 countries]].<ref name="116fpsSvobwin">[http://nrcu.gov.ua/en/148/514221/ After counting all ballots at 116 foreign polling stations "Svoboda" wins in parliamentary elections in Ukraine] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121202094554/http://nrcu.gov.ua/en/148/514221/ |date=2012-12-02 }}, [[National Radio Company of Ukraine]] (29 October 2012)<br />[http://www.itar-tass.com/en/c154/557300.html No violations reported at Ukraine’s overseas polling stations], [[ITAR-TASS]] (28 October 2012)</ref><ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/125903/ Central Election Commission forms 33,540 polling stations in Ukraine and 114 abroad], [[Kyiv Post]] (12 April 2012)</ref>

The [[Party of Regions]] won the largest number of seats while [[All-Ukrainian Union "Fatherland"|Fatherland]] (with several parties together as an "umbrella" party) came second.<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/> The election was also noted for the rise of the far-right party [[All-Ukrainian Union "Svoboda"|Svoboda]], which came in fourth.<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/> The new (on the national scene) party [[Ukrainian Democratic Alliance for Reform|UDAR]] also enjoyed noticeable great success with its third place in the election.<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/> The [[Communist Party of Ukraine]] almost tripled its numbers of voters but because of the mixed election system used in the election it only won five more seats compared with the [[2007 Ukrainian parliamentary election|previous election]].<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/> Because of this mixed system three small parties and 43 unaffiliated politicians also made it into parliament.<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/>

The new parliament was appointed and started its tasks on 12 December 2012 – six weeks after the elections.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/parliament-of-sixth-convocation-ends-its-work-317221.html Parliament of sixth convocation ends its work], [[Kyiv Post]] (6 December 2012)</ref><ref>[http://ukrainianweek.com/Politics/60928 You Scratch My Back, and I’ll Scratch Yours], [[The Ukrainian Week]] (26 September 2012)</ref> This was the last national Ukrainian election [[Crimea]] participated in prior to the [[annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation]].

===2012 election date set===
On February 1, 2011 the Verkhovna Rada set the election date for October 28, 2012.<ref name=dateset/><ref name="UKPravda1feb11"/> Several deputies whose votes were registered that day have stated they could not have taken part in voting because they were not in [[Kyiv]] (where the [[Verkhovna Rada building]] is located) on February 1, 2011.<ref name="UKPravda1feb11"/><ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/96146/ Tymoshenko faction deputy denies voting to extend parliament term], [[Kyiv Post]] (February 2, 2011)</ref> Voting by [[Member of Parliament|MP]]s in the place of absent MPs of the Verkhovna Rada is prohibited by law.<ref name="UKPravda1feb11">{{in lang|uk}} [http://www.pravda.com.ua/articles/2011/02/2/5870436/ Янукович отримав контрольний пакет у парламенті], [[Ukrayinska Pravda]] (February 2, 2011)</ref> On July 27, 2012 the [[Central Election Commission of Ukraine]] announced that campaigning for the elections would commence on July 30.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ria.ru/world/20120727/710576223.html |title=ЦИК Украины объявил о старте избирательной кампании по выборам в Раду &#124; РИА Новости |publisher=Ria.ru |access-date=2012-11-07}}</ref>

sought to re-instate a [[parallel voting]] system – used in the [[1998 Ukrainian parliamentary election|1998]] and [[2002 Ukrainian parliamentary election|2002]] elections<ref name="Ukrelectionsystem199820021994">[https://books.google.com/books?id=uD-9tyD2KrMC&pg=PA93&dq=2006+ukrainian+elections+district&hl=en&ei=rSZcTqadM42cOpHD8ZIM&sa=X&oi=book_result&ct=result&resnum=3&ved=0CDIQ6AEwAjgK#v=onepage&q=2006%20ukrainian%20elections%20district&f=false 225 local single-member districts elected (in one round)<ref>[http://ukrainianweek.com/Politics/64047 The Distorted Will of the People], [[The Ukrainian Week]] (5 November 2012)</ref> by a [[first-past-the-post electoral system]] (candidate with the highest vote total wins); and the remaining 225 parliamentary seats being elected nationwide on a proportional party-list system<ref name="DraftlawUkrelectionVC62011"/> with a 5% support threshold; and excluding [[political blocs]] from all elections.<ref name="DraftlawUkrelectionKP62011">[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/opinion/op_ed/detail/107435/ People First: The latest in the watch on Ukrainian democracy], [[Kyiv Post]] (June 24, 2011)</ref> The option "Vote against all" was also made defunct in the proposal<ref name="DraftlawUkrelectionKP62011"/><ref name="Law for POR?">[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/114020/ Experts: Proposed the voters would have voted "against everybody" during the elections).<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/poll-17-of-ukrainians-would-vote-against-everybody-during-elections-316701.html Poll:17% of Ukrainians would vote 'against everybody' during elections], [[Kyiv Post]] (29 November 2012)</ref> The opposition and Ukrainian analysts accused the [[Party of Regions]] of "rewriting the law so that the president could secure a majority in the next Verkhovna Rada."<ref name="Law for POR?"/><ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/113338/ Opposition pushes for immediate system.<ref>[http://www.interfax.com.ua/eng/main/81418/ Venice Commission advises Ukraine against returning to mixed electoral system], [[Interfax-Ukraine]] (October 4, 2011)</ref> Nevertheless, on 17 November 2011 the Ukrainian Parliament approved an election law almost identical to the June 2011 proposed Draft Law.<ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111IFU>{{in lang|uk}} [http://www.interfax.com.ua/ukr/main/85997/ ВР ухвалила закон про вибори народних депутатів], [[Interfax Ukraine]] (17 November 2011)</ref><ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111/> This new law satisfied the major opposition parties [[All-Ukrainian Union "Fatherland"|Batkivschyna]] and [[Front for Change (Ukraine)|Front for Change]]; but was condemned by the core party of [[Our Ukraine–People's on party lists or through self-nomination.<ref name=newUKRelectionlawof171111IFU/> On 8 December 2011 [[President of Ukraine|President]] [[Viktor Yanukovych]] signed the new election law.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/118418/ Yanukovych signs law on parliamentary elections], [[Kyiv Post]] (8 December 2011)</ref> Since then several parties merged with other parties.<ref>{{in lang|uk}} [http://www.pravda.com.ua/news/2011/12/18/6846740/ "Наша Україна" й УНП почали об’єднання з Дніпропетровська], [[Ukrayinska Pravda]] (18 December The possibility to be simultaneously be nominated on a nationwide party list and in a single mandate constituency also was declared unconstitutional by the [[Constitutional Court of Ukraine]] on 10 April 2012.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/125768/ Court bans simultaneous running for parliament in single-member districts and under party lists], [[Kyiv Post]] (10 April 2012)<br />[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/121370/ CEC head suggests ban on running for Rada independently and on party lists simultaneously], [[Kyiv Post]] (28 January 2012)</ref>

===Repeat elections in 5 constituencies===
The [[Central Election Commission of Ukraine]] adopted a resolution on November 5 recognizing the impossibility of announcing election results in five single-seat constituencies (electoral districts 94, 132, 194, 197 and 223); it also recognized the need to hold repeat elections in these constituencies and asked parliament to take a decision on holding repeat elections in these constituencies.<ref name=repeat7112012/> On November 6 the [[Verkhovna Rada]] adopted a resolution that proposed repeat elections.<ref name=repeat7112012/> On 8 November the Central Election Commission stated that the Verkhovna Rada should thus make a respective law for this and the [[Cabinet of Ministers of Ukraine]] should determine the amount of funding for these elections.<ref name=newelecUkr8112012/>

On 29 December 2012, the [[Constitutional Court of Ukraine]] received a query from 54 Verkhovna Rada members concerning procedures for the five repeat elections.<ref name="IUrepelect3313"/> As of 21 March 2013 the Constitutional Court has not opened a case on this issue.<ref name="IUrepelect3313"/> The current Verkhovna Rada parliamentary majority refused to consider the scheduling of repeated elections in the five constituencies before the court issues its ruling on this issue.<ref name="IUrepelect3313">[http://en.interfax.com.ua/news/press-conference/145621.html CIS-EMO mission says parliamentary elections in Ukraine legally incomplete], [[Interfax-Ukraine]] (3 March 2013)</ref>

On 5 September 2013 the Verkhovna Rada set the date of (all, see below) 7 re-elections to 15 December 2013.<ref name=UWre5913/>

==Campaign==
[[Andriy Klyuyev]] was the chief campaign manager for the Party of Regions.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.osw.waw.pl/en/publikacje/analyses/2012-02-22/a-reshuffle-ukrainian-government-element-preparations-election|title=A reshuffle in the Ukrainian government as an element of preparations for the election|last=Olszański|first=Tadeusz A.|work=[[Centre for Eastern Studies|Centre for Eastern Studies (OSW)]]|date=22 February 2012|access-date=24 September 2018}}</ref> The [[Party of Regions]]' campaign focused heavily on promoting its record as the ruling party, contrasting the "stability" of the (then current) [[Azarov Government]] with "chaos" during the [[Second Tymoshenko Government]] in 2007–10 (which it referred to as: "the chaos and ruins of 5 years of [[Orange Revolution|orange]] leadership",.<ref>[http://www.partyofregions.org.ua/en/program Draft Campaign Program of the Party of Regions], [[Party of Regions]] (2012)<br />[http://www.wespeaknews.com/world/the-upcoming-parliamentary-elections-in-ukraine-summary-106098.html The upcoming parliamentary elections in Ukraine &#91;Summary&#93;] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121026120142/http://www.wespeaknews.com/world/the-upcoming-parliamentary-elections-in-ukraine-summary-106098.html |date=2012-10-26 }}, [[WSN]] (23 October 2012)</ref><ref name=BBCCAMP/> It advocated a "balanced" approach to developing [[Russia–Ukraine relations|relations with Russia]] [[Foreign relations of Ukraine#Western relations|and the West]], saying neither should be given priority over the other.<ref name=BBCCAMP/>

[[All-Ukrainian Union "Fatherland"|Fatherland]] tried to paint the election as a battle of good against evil and pledged to [[impeach]] [[President of Ukraine|President]] [[Viktor Yanukovych]].<ref name=BBCCAMP/> The party stated it advocated "European values" and promised to reverse the Azarov Government policy of [[Legislation on languages in Ukraine|raising the status of the Russian language]].<ref name=BBCCAMP/>

[[UDAR]] avoided sensitive and polarising subjects and focused instead on popular topics, such as more empowerment to ordinary Ukrainians and a ruthless campaign against [[Corruption in Ukraine|corruption]], the indifference of the authorities, the lack of [[local governance in Ukraine|local governance]], inequality and poverty.<ref name=BBCCAMP/><ref name=SvobBBC/>

[[All-Ukrainian Union "Svoboda"|Svoboda]] softened their rhetoric in the campaign but nevertheless promised to shake up the country's political status quo.<ref name=SvobBBC/><ref name=kpderad>{{cite news|last=Gorchinskaya|first=Katya|title=Svoboda tames radicals to get into parliament|url=http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/svoboda-tames-radicals-to-get-into-parliament-316584.html|access-date=22 November 2012|newspaper=[[Kyiv Post]]}}</ref>

One of the biggest spenders of the campaign was the party [[Ukraine – Forward!]].<ref name=UkrweekNK>[http://ukrainianweek.com/Politics/52030 A Royal Gift to the Government], [[The Ukrainian Week]] (5 June 2012)</ref><ref name=2012Benjamins>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/korolevska-recruits-acting-sports-stars-for-campaign.html Korolevska recruits acting, sports stars for campaign], [[Kyiv Post]] (Aug. 2, 2012)</ref> One of their election billboards claimed that “an average wage of [[Euro|EUR]]€1,000 and a pension of €500” was realistic for Ukraine (the monthly average wage was €300 at the time).<ref>[https://www.economist.com/blogs/easternapproaches/2012/10/ukraine%E2%80%99s-parliamentary-elections?fb_ref=activity Footballers and other candidates], [[The Economist]] (8 October 2012)</ref>

Many candidates in single-seat constituencies tended to focus on local issues, often distancing themselves from party agendas.<ref name=BBCCAMP/>

Overall the [[election program]]s of the major parties bore many similarities; all pledged reforms to spur [[Economy of Ukraine|economic]] growth, higher wages, pensions and other benefits, better [[education in Ukraine|education]] and [[Health in Ukraine|medical care]].<ref name=BBCCAMP/>

Two weeks before the (28 October) election UDAR withdrew 26 of its candidates running in [[single-member constituencies]] in favour of Fatherland candidates and they withdrew 26 parliamentary candidates in favor of UDAR in an attempt to maximise votes for the opposition.<ref>[http://www.euractiv.com/europes-east/main-opposition-forces-tandem-ah-news-515430 Opposition parties join forces ahead of Ukrainian election], [[EurActiv]] (16 October 2012)</ref>

===Costs===
Political parties spent more than [[United States dollar|US$]]75 million on the election campaign in multi-member constituencies (according to the parties' official reports).<ref name=UkrelecmoneyKP161112>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/parties-spend-over-hr-600-million-on-elections-according-to-report-316205.html Parties spend over Hr 600 million on elections, according to report], [[Kyiv Post]] (16 November 2012)</ref> The [[Party of Regions]] spent about US$27 million, [[All-Ukrainian Union "Fatherland"|Fatherland]] more than $13 million, [[UDAR]] more than $4 million, the [[Communist Party of Ukraine]] $9 million, [[Our Ukraine (political party)|Our Ukraine]] $8 million and [[Ukraine – Forward!]] $7.6 million.<ref name=UkrelecmoneyKP161112/> [[All-Ukrainian Union "Svoboda"|Svoboda]] claimed it had spent US$3 million on the campaign.<ref name=NMUkrofF/> The [[Ukraine of the Future]] did not spend anything on campaigning yet still managed to take the 15th spot amongst the 21 parties who participated in the nationwide list with 0.18% of the votes.<ref name=NMUkrofF>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/media-parties-report-to-cec-on-election-costs-316136.html Media:Parties report to CEC on election costs], [[Kyiv Post]] (15 November 2012)</ref>

Denys Kovrizhenko of the International Foundation for Electoral Systems – Ukraine stated the sum of money spend could be up to 10 times more than what parties report afterwards.<ref name=CUEKP81212/> According to [[OPORA]] “In general, candidates spend about three times more than they officially report to spend”.<ref name=CUEKP81212>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/campaigns-file-dodgy-spending-declarations-317271.html Campaigns file dodgy spending declarations], [[Kyiv Post]] (8 December 2012)</ref> Political scientist Artem Bidenko estimated other figures; he believed that the Party of Regions had spent around $850 million, Ukraine – Forward some $150 million, and the election campaigns of the rest of the political parties $350 million, while candidates in majority constituencies had spent some $900 million on the election campaign.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/candidates-spent-25-billion-on-election-campaign-says-expert-315462.html Candidates spent $2.5 billion on election campaign, says expert], [[Kyiv Post]] (2 November 2012)</ref> About half of the single-constituency candidates submitted reports about their campaign spending.<ref name=CUEKP81212/>

In October 2008 Ukrainian experts estimated that a small political party who wants to win seats in parliament would spend up to US$30 million on the campaign and large political parties would spend up to $100 million.<ref name="more money"/> Political analyst Pavlo Bulhak stated then that a party's election budget will be spent on advertising on television, [[Corruption in Ukraine|bribing]] voters, organizing rallies and [[Campaign advertising|party propaganda]].<ref name="more money">{{cite news|url=http://www.ukranews.com/eng/article/157497.html|title=Experts Estimate Costs Of Party's Or Bloc's Campaign In Snap Rada Elections At USD 25-100 Million|publisher=Ukrainian News Agency|date=22 October 2008|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081024063950/http://www.ukranews.com/eng/article/157497.html|archive-date=24 October 2008|url-status = dead|df=dmy-all}}</ref>

===Forming of new government===
On 9 December [[President of Ukraine|President]] [[Viktor Yanukovych]] nominated [[Mykola Azarov]] for a new term as [[Prime Minister of Ukraine|Prime Minister]].<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/ukraine/ukraine-leader-picks-prime-minister-azarov-for-new-term-in-post-317367.html Yanukovych picks Azarov for new term as prime minister (updated)], [[Kyiv Post]] (9 December 2012)</ref> This nomination was approved by parliament on 13 December 2012.<ref name=PMAzarovA>[https://www.reuters.com/article/2012/12/13/ukraine-pm-idUSL5E8ND8QN20121213 Ukraine parliament approves Azarov as prime minister], [[Reuters]] (13 December 2012)</ref> 252 deputies of the 450 deputies supported the nomination; the whole factions of [[Party of Regions]] (210 deputies) and [[Communist Party of Ukraine|Communist Party]] (32 deputies) and ten independent deputies.<ref>{{in lang|uk}} [http://www.pravda.com.ua/news/2012/12/13/6979650/ АЗАРОВ МАЙЖЕ СТАВ ЗНОВУ ПРЕМ'ЄРОМ ''Azarov ALMOST BECAME PRIME AGAIN''], [[Ukrayinska Pravda]] (13 December 2012)</ref>

The [[second Azarov Government]] was appointed by Yanukovych on 24 December 2012.<ref name=USMG241212>[http://www.unian.info/news/543116-yanukovych-appoints-new-staff-of-cabinet-of-ministers-of-ukraine.html President of Ukraine has appointed new staff of the Cabinet of Ministers of Ukraine] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20131211161007/http://www.unian.info/news/543116-yanukovych-appoints-new-staff-of-cabinet-of-ministers-of-ukraine.html |date=2013-12-11 }}, [[UNIAN]] (24 December 2012)</ref>

==Reactions==
[[All-Ukrainian Union "Fatherland"|Fatherland]], [[UDAR]] and [[All-Ukrainian Union "Svoboda"|Svoboda]] stated 12 November they did not recognize the results and will challenge them in local and international courts.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/ukraine/ukraine-opposition-protests-election-results-316008.html Ukraine opposition protests election results], [[Kyiv Post]] (1 November 2012)</ref> In a joint statement the three parties vowed to work towards the [[impeachment]] of [[President of Ukraine|President]] [[Viktor Yanukovych]], the resignation of the [[Azarov Government]] and [[General Prosecutor of Ukraine|chief prosecutor]] [[Viktor Pshonka]], and the [[Yulia Tymoshenko#2011 trial and imprisonment|release from jail]] of [[Yulia Tymoshenko]] "and other [[political prisoner]]s".<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/ukraine/ukraines-opposition-grudgingly-accepts-election-results-316003.html Ukraine's opposition grudgingly accepts election results], [[Kyiv Post]] (12 November 2012)</ref><ref>[http://www.euronews.com/2012/11/13/ukraine-s-united-opposition-threatens-to-impeach-president/ Ukraine’s United Opposition threatens to impeach president], [[Euronews]] (13 November 2012)</ref>

[[Prime Minister of Ukraine|Prime Minister]] [[Mykola Azarov]] and President Yanukovych praised the elections; Azarov claimed “neither democratic norms nor legal democratic procedures were in fact violated” and according to Yanukovych the elections "took place well from the technical viewpoint".<ref>[http://en.rian.ru/news/20121107/177264926.html Ukrainian PM Denies Ballot Rigging in Parliamentary Polls], [[RIA Novosti]] (7 November 2012)<br />[http://en.rian.ru/world/20121029/177028584.html Ukrainian President Praises Parliamentary Elections], [[RIA Novosti]] (29 October 2012)</ref>

Party leader [[Petro Symonenko]] of the [[Communist Party of Ukraine]] believed on 8 November that the new parliament could not work better than the present one, as "there will be a confrontation between the financial, political and clan groups who got seats in the new parliament."<ref name=Symsaid8112012>[http://www.interfax.com.ua/eng/main/126533/ Ukrainian communists not to join other political forces in new parliament, says Symonenko], [[Interfax-Ukraine]] (8 November 2012)</ref> He also stated then that his party will not form any coalition with other groups in the new parliament.<ref name=Symsaid8112012/>

[[Civil movement "Chesno"]] stated on 5 December 2012 that 331 out of the 450 deputies elected on 28 October fell short of its criteria for honesty; according to "Chesno"'s parameters, 114 of them violated the rights and freedoms of citizens, 30 earlier changed their political position while working in parliament or on local councils, 233 had been involved in corrupt practices, 185 had nontransparent incomes and expenses, 156 did not personally take part in voting in previous parliaments, and 101 had shirked work in parliament.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/only-two-deputies-meet-criteria-of-honesty-of-chesno-movement-311172.html Chesno movement says 331 newly elected parliamentaries cheated earlier], [[Kyiv Post]] (5 December 2012)</ref>

[[Party of Regions]] politician [[Sergei Tigipko]] stated in December 2012 "the parliamentary elections showed that politics in Ukraine is becoming more ideological".<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/tigipko-not-planning-to-work-in-azarov-government-318042.html Tigipko not planning to work in Azarov government], [[Kyiv Post]] (22 December 2012)</ref>

==International observers==
On election day (28 October) there were 3,500 accredited foreign observers.<ref name=SvobBBC/> The observers from the [[European Academy for Elections Observation]] (most of whom where [[European Parliament]] members), stated it was "a good election, not perfect but clearly acceptable",<ref name=BBCObs2910/> and that it was "in compliance with democratic norms".<ref name=BBCObs2910/> On 29 October the [[Organisation for Security and Co-operation in Europe]] (OSCE) (who had monitored the election with 600 observers)<ref name=SvobBBC/> stated in a preliminary report<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/osceodihr-to-issue-final-report-on-rada-elections-in-2-months-316039.html OSCE/ODIHR to issue final report on Rada elections in 2 months], [[Kyiv Post]] (13 November 2012)</ref> that "certain aspects of the pre-election period constituted a step backwards compared with recent national elections" and that the election was marred by "the abuse of power and the excessive role of money".<ref name="CESOlszańskiUKel12"/><ref name=BBCObs2910/> It complained of "a lack of a level playing field, caused primarily by the abuse of [[administrative resources]], lack of transparency of campaign and party financing, and lack of balanced media coverage".<ref name=BBCObs2910/> This contrasted sharply with the international observers' conclusions on Ukraine's [[2010 Ukrainian presidential election|February 2010 presidential election]], judged then to have been transparent, unbiased and an "impressive display" of democracy.<ref name=BBCObs2910>[https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-europe-20120888 Ukraine election 'reversed democracy', OSCE says], [[BBC News]] (29 October 2012)</ref>

Ten thousand foreign observers where expected to observe the elections.<ref name=KP87P/> Some 100 long term observers from [[Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe|OSCE]] member states arrived in Ukraine starting from the middle of September 2012, followed by 600 short-term observers who will arrive a week before the elections to monitor the election process at voting stations.<ref name=KP87P/>

[[Poland]] is to send observers to Ukraine to monitor the elections, [[Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Poland)|Polish Foreign Minister]] [[Radosław Sikorski]] stated on 8 February 2012.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/nation/detail/122056 Polish observers to come to parliamentary elections in Ukraine], [[Kyiv Post]] (8 February 2012)</ref> [[German–Ukrainian relations|German Ambassador to Ukraine]] {{Interlanguage link|Hans-Jürgen Heimsoeth|de}} stated "[[Germany]] is planning to send a numerous group of official supervisors" on 13 March 2012.<ref>[http://www.kyivpost.com/news/politics/detail/124191/ Central Election Commission: Germany to send supervisors for elections in Ukraine], [[Kyiv Post]] (13 March 2012)</ref>

The total number of registered observers on October 9, was 1053 persons.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ua.rian.ru/elections_2012/20121009/336032833.html|archive-url=https://archive.today/20130417185559/http://www.ua.rian.ru/elections_2012/20121009/336032833.html|url-status = dead|archive-date=2013-04-17|title=РИА Новости-Украина: ЦИК зарегистрировал еще 81 международного наблюдателя за выборами}}</ref> The largest mission of international observers from [[CIS-EMO]] was 197 people.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.cvk.gov.ua/pls/vnd2012/wp041?PT001F01=900|title=Центральна виборча комісія України - WWW відображення ІАС "Вибори народних депутатів України 2012"|website=www.cvk.gov.ua|access-date=2012-10-11|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121014212503/http://www.cvk.gov.ua/pls/vnd2012/wp041?PT001F01=900|archive-date=2012-10-14|url-status = dead}}</ref>

On 2 October 2012 [[CIS-EMO]] observers presented the Interim report of the CIS-EMO Election Monitoring Mission.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.kommersant.ru/|title=Издательский дом Коммерсантъ}}</ref> The report, in particular, noted that "The majority of detected violations are connected not with a political struggle of party lists but with the struggle of single-seat candidates". An impression that “antidemocratic power” clash with “democratic opposition” imposed by European and world society has a very relative nature that, as a rule, doesn’t distinct the real situation. In nowadays Ukrainian “peripheral capitalism” model such classes as “power” and “opposition” are conventionality. When the “Power Elite” is unconsolidated and disconnected and there is an open internal war between leading financial-industrial groups and corporations of Ukraine to get leverage of real state authority, all existing political parties only play the role of institutionalized political framework of realization of oligarchs’ economic interests.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.cis-emo.net/en/news/interim-report-cis-emo-election-monitoring-mission|title=Interim report of the CIS-EMO Election Monitoring Mission|website=www.cis-emo.net}}</ref>

On 5 October 2012 the [[CIS-EMO]] report was presented at the annual meeting of the [[Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights|OSCE Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rbc.ua/rus/vyboru/show/doklad-cis-emo-vybory-v-ukraine-predstavlyayut-ne-stolknovenie-05102012095300|title=Доклад CIS-EMO: Выборы в Украине представляют не столкновение мировоззрений, а борьбу бизнес-элит|last=Rbc.ua}}</ref> Shortly before the presentation of [[CIS-EMO]] interim report web-site of [[CIS-EMO]] had been subjected to a massive DDoS-attack.<ref>[http://www.interfax.com.ua/rus/elections-2012/120009/ В преддверии оглашения доклада CIS-EMO по Украине сайт организации подвергся DDoS-атаке]</ref> The report was published on the official website of the OSCE in English and Ukrainian<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.osce.org/odihr/91152|title=Interim report of the CIS-EMO Election Monitoring Mission|access-date=2012-10-27|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121105090837/http://www.osce.org/odihr/91152|archive-date=2012-11-05|url-status = dead}}</ref> and also in Russian.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.osce.org/node/95091|title=Interim Report of the CIS-EMO Election Monitoring Mission - ОБСЕ|website=www.osce.org}}</ref>

The ENEMO (European Network of Election Monitoring Organizations) mission for the 2012 parliamentary elections in Ukraine began its work on 23 July 2012 with the arrival of four Core Team members. ENEMO is the first international election observation mission registered for the Parliamentary Elections 2012 by the Central Election Commission (CEC). 35 LTOs (long-term observers) arrived to Kyiv on 5 August 2012 and were deployed throughout Ukraine. Long-term observer teams cover one or two oblasts of Ukraine. On E-day, October 28, ENEMO deployed 43 STO (Short-term observer) teams throughout all oblasts of Ukraine.

==Factions in parliament after elections==
According to the amendment to parliamentary regulations adopted in November 2012, the smallest faction of parliament can be formed out a party with the smallest number of deputies elected by a party list and a single constituency vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://w1.c1.rada.gov.ua/pls/radan_gs09/ns_golos?g_id=30844|title=Voting for the Verkhovna Rada regulations amendment|access-date=2012-11-22|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121203023638/http://w1.c1.rada.gov.ua/pls/radan_gs09/ns_golos?g_id=30844|archive-date=2012-12-03|url-status = dead}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://iportal.rada.gov.ua/meeting/stenogr/show/4094.html|title=Офіційний портал Верховної Ради України|website=iportal.rada.gov.ua}}</ref> That amendment to regulations can also be interpreted as "either or" meaning that the smallest faction can be formed either based on party list or a single constituency election. In that case the smaller parties' deputies that were elected to the parliament will be able to form factions of their own, making it more challenging to form a coalition in the Ukrainian parliament.<ref>[http://blogs.pravda.com.ua/authors/leschenko/50ae3e29a9cd7/ Політичний цирк: кнопкодави попалися на своїх звичках (Political circus: the "button-pushers" got caught on its habits)]. [[Ukrayinska Pravda]].</ref>

On 27 November 2012 Party of Regions parliamentary leader [[Oleksandr Yefremov]] claimed that 223 members of the Verkhovna Rada had already expressed their desire to work in his party's fraction; according to earlier press reports 38 of the 43 unaffiliated politicians elected into parliament would join the Party of Regions faction.<ref name="seatsVR121212KPUW">[http://www.kyivpost.com/content/politics/yefremov-regions-party-faction-already-has-223-members-316850.html Yefremov: Regions Party faction already has 223 members], [[Kyiv Post]] (28 November 2012 2012)<br />[https://archive.today/20130416103410/http://www.osw.waw.pl/en/notatka/a-difficult-victory-party-regions A difficult victory for the Party of Regions], [[Centre for Eastern Studies]] (31 October 2012)</ref>
