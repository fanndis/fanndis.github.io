The '''''Charleston Mercury''''' was a [[secession]]ist newspaper in [[Charleston, South Carolina]], founded by [[Henry L. Pinckney]] in 1819. He was its sole editor for fifteen years. It ceased publication with the [[Union Army]] occupation of Charleston. After the [[American Civil War]], publication resumed in November 1866 before the paper closed permanently two years later in 1868.<ref name=Encylcopedia>Heidler, David Stephen, and Jeanne T. Heidler (eds.) (2002). ''[https://books.google.com/books?id=SdrYv7S60fgC&pg=PA407#v=twopage&q=&f=false Encyclopedia of the American Civil War: A Political, Social, and Military History]''. ==History==
During the American Civil War, the paper was "strongly [[secession]]ist", calling upon South Carolinian men to take up arms to defend the South.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=fjiZ9oIhsnsC&pg=PT25&dq=%22Charleston+mercury%22+%22issue+before+the%22&hl=en&sa=X&ved=0CCkQ6AEwAmoVChMIwbjhnKDOxwIVSjg-Ch0b2w3d#v=onepage&q=%22Charleston%20mercury%22%20%22issue%20before%20the%22&f=false|date=November 3, 1860|title=Charleston Mercury}}</ref> Its owner, [[Robert Barnwell Rhett]], had two [[plantations in the American South|plantation]]s and 190 slaves.<ref>Henshaw, Tom (December 18, 1960). "[https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=ZZdJAAAAIBAJ&sjid=dQwNAAAAIBAJ&pg=3798,3556997&dq=charleston+mercury&hl=en The Secession of South Carolina]". ''[[The Post and Courier|The News and Courier]]'' (Charleston, South Carolina). p. 18.</ref> The paper was critical of Davis and Confederate generals, in contrast with its pro-Davis competitor the ''[[Charleston Courier]]''.<ref name=Encylcopedia/>

[[Humorist]] [[George William Bagby]] was a [[Richmond, Virginia]] correspondent of the ''Charleston Mercury'' during the Civil War era and "covered the politics of the war and made a reputation for Hermes, his pen name, as a fearless writer who would criticize Confederate General [[Robert E. Lee]] as easily as Confederate President [[Jefferson Davis]]".

==Modern paper==
{{Notability|date=February 2020}}

An online newspaper with the same name was created in March 2000 by Charles W. Waring III and a group of investors. It covered the [[First inauguration of George W. Bush|inauguration of George W. Bush]] in January 2001, and covered the ''Taste of the South''{{clarify |date=February 2020 |reason= What is the Taste of the South?}} in May of the same year. In October 2002, the [[Evening Post Industries|Evening Post Publishing Company]] purchased 70 percent of the paper and a print edition was started. From that point until March 2013, the paper published every two weeks; since then, the paper has published a monthly edition under the ownership of Holy City Productions, LLC, an independent media firm owned by Waring and a group of investors. The "Charleston Mercury" newspaper reaches roughly 50,000 readers in the greater Charleston area. (Sources: [https://www.charlestonmercury.com/ www.charlestonmercury.com]){{Citation needed|date=February 2020}}

[[Tim Hames]], former writer for [[The Times]] of London, wrote for the ''Charleston Mercury'' and ''[[The Tablet]].''
