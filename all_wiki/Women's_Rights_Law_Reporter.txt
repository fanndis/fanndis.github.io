The '''''Women's Rights Law Reporter''''' is a [[Law review|journal of legal scholarship]] published by an independent student group at [[Rutgers School of Law—Newark]]. The journal was founded in 1970 by Rutgers law students working with [[Ruth Bader Ginsburg]] and Professor Nadine H. Taub. It was the first law journal in the U.S. to focus exclusively on women's rights.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://pegasus.rutgers.edu/~wrlr/index.html|title=About the Reporter |access-date=June 29, 2008|url-status=dead|work=Women's Rights Law Reporter|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080708192947/http://pegasus.rutgers.edu/~wrlr/index.html|archivedate=July 8, 2008|quote=Founded in 1970 by now-Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg and feminist activists, legal workers, and law students{{nbs}}...}}</ref> Professor Taub (now deceased) 
remained the faculty advisor of the journal through the 1980s and 1990s. The journal provides a forum for exploring law and public policy relating to [[women’s rights]] and [[gender]].  The journal is published quarterly.

== History and focus ==
Ann Marie Boylan, a recent graduate of [[Rutgers Law School|Rutgers School of Law]], had been trying to establish a [[Feminism|feminist]] journal.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://barnard.edu/headlines/birth-womens-rights-law-reporter|title=The Birth of the Women’s Rights Law Reporter|last=Langer|first=Elizabeth|date=30 November 2010|website=Barnard College|language=en|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180525125706/https://barnard.edu/headlines/birth-womens-rights-law-reporter|archive-date=25 May 2018|access-date=2018-06-02}}</ref> Boylan had created one issue of the ''Women's Rights Law Reporter'' (WRLR), published out of her apartment in [[Newark, New Jersey|Newark]] in 1970.<ref name=":0" /> She shared the idea for the journal with a women's group in Newark who wanted to help the publication become successful and it was suggested that they turn to Rutgers Law School.<ref name=":0" /> The women in the group met with the dean, James Paul, who said they would need to find a faculty advisor, find office space in the school and raise their own funds.<ref name=":0" /> [[Ruth Bader Ginsburg]] agreed to become the faculty advisor and space for WRLR was found in an old building.<ref name=":0" /> WRLR was moved onto Rutgers' campus in 1972 and became formally associated with Rutgers in 1974.<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|url=https://www.bustle.com/p/7-things-you-didnt-know-about-ruth-bader-ginsburg-44311|title=7 Things You Didn't Know About Ruth Bader Ginsburg…|last=Thorpe|first=J.R.|date=15 March 2017|work=Bustle|access-date=2018-06-02|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170710171439/https://www.bustle.com/p/7-things-you-didnt-know-about-ruth-bader-ginsburg-44311|archive-date=10 July 2017|language=en}}</ref> Professor Nadine H. Taub was its faculty advisor for many years.

On its founding advisory board were [[Arthur Kinoy]], [[Pauli Murray]], [[Eleanor Holmes Norton]], Ruth Bader Ginsburg,<ref name=":0" /> Margot Champagne, Mary Eastwood, [[Riane Eisler]], Ann Freedman, Jo Ann Evans Gardner, Janice Goodman, Renee Hanover, Bernice Handler, Diane B. Schulder, [[Faith Seidenberg]], and Nancy Stearns.
