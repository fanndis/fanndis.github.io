'''Wellington Koo''' ({{zh|first=t|t=顧立雄|p=Gù Lìxióng}}; born 31 October 1958) is a Taiwanese lawyer and politician. During his legal career, Koo represented several politicians. His own political career began with an unsuccessful campaign for the Taipei mayoralty in 2013. In 2016, he was elected a legislator at large representing the [[Democratic Progressive Party]]. Koo left the [[Legislative Yuan]] to lead the [[Ill-gotten Party Assets Settlement Committee]]. In 2017, he became chairman of the [[Financial Supervisory Commission (Taiwan)|Financial Supervisory Commission]]. Koo was appointed Secretary-General of the [[National Security Council (Taiwan)|National Security Council]] in 2020.

==Early life==
Koo was born in Taipei in 1958, to [[Mainland Chinese|Mainlander]] parents originally from [[Shanghai]]. He attended [[National Taiwan University]] before earning a master's degree in public service law from [[New York University]]. Koo passed the Taiwanese bar exam in 1983, and began teaching law at [[Chinese Culture University]] in 1993, a job he held until 2003.<ref name="tt2004">{{cite news|last1=Chuang|first1=Jimmy|title=Koos's legal experience helping DPP|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2004/12/05/2003213817|accessdate=15 February 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=5 December 2004}}</ref>

==Political career==
In September 2013,<ref name="tn"/> Koo announced his intent to run for the mayoralty of Taipei as a member of the Democratic Progressive Party.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Wang|first1=Chris|title=Sean Lien has to address 'princeling' status: DPP|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2014/02/25/2003584305|accessdate=15 February 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=25 February 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Wang|first1=Chris|title=Non-KMT Taipei mayoral hopeful debates set for TV|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2014/03/03/2003584765|accessdate=15 February 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=3 March 2014}}</ref> A primary held in May 2014, after the Sunflower Movement, was won by [[Pasuya Yao]], and Koo dropped out of the race.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Wang|first1=Chris|title=Pasuya Yao wins DPP's first-stage Taipei primary|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2014/05/15/2003590394|accessdate=15 February 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=15 May 2014}}</ref> Yao later dropped out of the race, endorsing  [[Ko Wen-je]], who won the [[2014 Taiwan local elections|mayoral election]] as an independent candidate.

Koo, then the director of the Judicial Reform Foundation, was selected for the Democratic Progressive Party's proportional representation ballot in November 2015.<ref>{{cite Times|date=25 November 2015}}</ref> Listed forth on the ballot during the [[2016 Taiwanese general election|2016 legislative election]], he won a seat in the [[Legislative Yuan]].<ref>{{cite 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=12 December 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Chen|first1=Yu-fu|title=President's rights record slammed|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2016/01/31/2003638489|accessdate=15 February 2016|work=Taipei Times|date=31 January 2016}}</ref> In his time as legislator, he called for the establishment of a government commission on human rights.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Gerber|first1=Abraham|title=Groups call for national human rights Legislative Yuan to take the appointment, and was succeeded in office by [[Julian Kuo]].<ref>{{cite news|last1=Tzou|first1=Jiing-wen|last2=Chin|first2=Jonathan|title=Koo to head new assets Koo was appointed to the chairmanship of the [[Financial Supervisory Commission (Taiwan)|Financial Supervisory Commission]] in September 2017, succeeding [[Lee Ruey-tsang]] on the same day that [[William Lai]] replaced [[Lin Chuan]] as premier.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Chen|first1=Wei-han|title=Lai to replace two Cabinet ministers, retain all others|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2017/09/08/2003678033|accessdate=10 September 2017|work=Taipei Times|date=8 September 2017}}</ref> Koo stated shortly before taking control of the FSC that he sought to implement a "differentiated management style" in which financial institutions that ranked higher would be allowed more regulatory freedom to innovate within the financial services sector, and those institutions that did less well would be granted less latitude.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Lin|first1=Sean|title=Koo vows 'differentiated' style|url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2017/09/07/2003677965|accessdate=10 September 2017|work=Taipei Times|date=7 September 2017}}</ref> Koo left the Financial Supervisory Commission in May 2020 and became the secretary-general of the [[National Security Council (Taiwan)|National Security Council]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=Wang |first1=Flor |last2=Wang |first2=Cheng-chung |last3=Wen |first3=Kuei-hsiang |title=Culture minister quits, six others to keep posts in new Cabinet |url=https://focustaiwan.tw/politics/202005150013 |accessdate=15 May 2020 |agency=Central News Agency |date=15 May 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=Yeh |first1=Su-ping |last2=Yu |first2=Matt |last3=Lim |first3=Emerson |title=New NSC chief positive to U.S.-Taiwan security cooperation: expert |url=https://focustaiwan.tw/politics/202005200011 |accessdate=20 May 2020 |agency=Central News Agency |date=20 May 2020}}</ref>
