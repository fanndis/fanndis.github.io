'''Jennifer 8. Lee''' ([[Chinese language|Chinese]] name: {{lang|zh-Hant|李競}};<ref name="Simplified Chinese">{{cite web|last=Lee|first=Jennifer 8.|title=Someone added my Chinese name to my Wikipedia entry in simplified :( form |date=March 17, 2008 |url=http://www.fortunecookiechronicles.com/blog/2008/03/17/someone-added-my-chinese-name-to-my-wikipedia-entry/|work=The Fortune Cookie Chronicles|accessdate=March 10, 2014}}</ref> [[pinyin]]: ''{{lang|zh-Latn-pinyin|Lǐ Jìng}}''; [[Pe̍h-ōe-jī|POJ]]: ''{{lang|nan-Latn|Lí Kēng}}'') (born March 15, 1976) is an [[United States|American]] [[journalist]] who previously worked for ''[[The New York Times]]''.<ref name=buyout/> She is also the co-founder and president of the literary studio [[Plympton, Inc.|Plympton]],<ref name=Plympton /> as well as a producer on ''[[The Search for General Tso]]'', which premiered at the 2014 [[Tribeca Film Festival]].<ref>{{cite web|last1=Foundas|first1=Scott|title=Tribeca Film Review: ‘The Search for General Tso’|url=https://variety.com/2014/film/reviews/tribeca-film-review-the-search-for-general-tso-1201161639/|website=Variety|accessdate=June 16, 2014}}</ref>

Lee is a vice-chair of the [[Unicode Emoji Subcommittee]],<ref>{{Cite web | title=Unicode Emoji | url=http://unicode.org/emoji/#Unicode_Emoji_Subcommittee | publisher=Unicode Consortium | date=October 27, 2017 | accessdate=November 3, 2017 }}</ref> which is responsible for making recommendations relating to [[emoji]] to the [[Unicode Technical Committee]]. Inspired by the universality of the dumpling across cultures and cuisines (e.g., [[jiaozi]] in China, [[ravioli]] in Italy, [[pierogi]] in Poland, [[empanadas]] in Argentina), she helped to make the dumpling emoji a candidate.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Warzel|first1=Charlie|title=One Woman’s Bizarre, Delightful Quest To Change Emojis Forever|url=https://www.buzzfeed.com/charliewarzel/there-will-be-dumplings|website=Buzzfeed|accessdate=17 October 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=Kar|first1=Ian|title=Dumplings and fortune cookies: Your emoji are about to get even more diverse|url=http://qz.com/613227/your-emojis-are-going-to-get-even-more-asian/|website=Quartz|accessdate=17 October 2016}}</ref> She also co-authored the proposal for a hijab emoji.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Farber|first1=Madeline|title=Unicode Is Considering a Hijab Emoji|url=http://fortune.com/2016/09/15/muslim-teen-hijab-emoji/|website=Fortune.com|accessdate=17 October 2016}}</ref>

==Early life and education ==
Lee was born on March 15, 1976, in [[New York City]], to immigrants from [[Kinmen]], [[Taiwan]], an island off the coast of [[mainland China|China]]'s [[Fujian province]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://tv.nytimes.com/learning/students/ask_reporters/Jennifer_Lee.html |title=Ask a Reporter: Jennifer 8. Lee |work=[[New York Times]] |access-date=May 7, 2011 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110628065620/http://tv.nytimes.com/learning/students/ask_reporters/Jennifer_Lee.html |archive-date=June 28, 2011 }}</ref><ref name="book">{{cite book |title= The Fortune Cookie Chronicles |last= Lee |first= Jennifer 8. |year= 2008 |publisher= Twelve Books |location= New York, NY |isbn= 0-446-69897-0 |url= https://archive.org/details/fortunecookiechr00leej |oclc= 225870250 |url-access= registration }}</ref> Lee was not given a middle name at birth so she chose "8." when she was a teenager.<ref>{{cite news| last = Horne | first = Jim| publisher = [[The New York Times]] | title = Lucky Number 8 | url= http://wordplay.blogs.nytimes.com/2008/11/22/fortune/| accessdate = January 2, 2009 | date=November 22, 2008}}</ref><ref name="Simplified Chinese"/><ref>{{cite news |last=Lee |first=Jennifer 8. |title=Yes, 8 is my middle name. |newspaper=[[Boston Globe]] |date=August 8, 1996 |page=E1 |url=https://www.quora.com/Jennifer-8-Lee/Why-does-Jennifer-8-Lee-have-a-numerical-middle-initial |via=quora.com }}</ref> In [[China|Chinese]] culture, the number eight [[Numbers in Chinese culture#Eight|symbolizes prosperity and good luck]]. She graduated from [[Hunter College High School]] in [[Manhattan]] in 1994. Lee graduated from [[Harvard College]] in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts]] in 1999 with a degree in [[applied mathematics]] and [[economics]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Lee featured in Harvard Magazine {{!}} Harvard John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences|url=https://www.seas.harvard.edu/news/2019/03/lee-featured-harvard-magazine|access-date=2020-12-05|website=www.seas.harvard.edu}}</ref>

==Career==
While a student at Harvard, Lee was the vice president of ''[[The Harvard Crimson]]'' student newspaper.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.thecrimson.com/static/staff.html |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/19981205081638/http://www.thecrimson.com/static/staff.html |url-status=dead |archive-date=December 5, 1998 |title=The Harvard Crimson Online: Staff |work=The Harvard Crimson |accessdate=February 18, 2020 }}</ref> She interned at ''[[The Washington Post]]'', ''[[The Wall Street Journal]]'', ''[[The Boston Globe]]'', ''[[Newsday]]'', and ''The New York Times'' during college. She joined the ''Times'' in 2001, one and a half years after graduating from Harvard.{{Citation needed|date=November 2019}}

Published in 2008, Lee wrote a book about the history of [[American Chinese cuisine|Chinese food in the United States]] and around the world, titled ''The Fortune Cookie Chronicles'',<ref name="book"/> documenting the process on her blog. Warner Books editor Jonathan Karp struck a deal with Lee to write a book about "how Chinese food is more all-American than apple pie."<ref name=womenofchina>[http://www.womenofchina.cn/Profiles/Writers/206808.jsp "Jennifer 8. Lee Attracts Americans with Chinese Food"], October 13, 2008. Source: Xinhua/Translated by womenofchina.cn {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081017201004/http://www.womenofchina.cn/Profiles/Writers/206808.jsp |date=October 17, 2008 }}</ref> She appeared on ''[[The Colbert Report]]'' to promote the book.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.cc.com/video-clips/86yukf/the-colbert-report-jennifer-8--lee |title=Jennifer 8. Lee |work=The Colbert Report |date=March 4, 2008 |via=cc.com }}</ref> The book was #26 on [[the New York Times Best Seller list]].<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/03/30/books/bestseller/0330besthardnonfiction.html |title=Best Sellers, Hardcover Nonfiction |date=March 30, 2008 |work=[[The New York Times Best Seller list]] }}</ref>

In December 2009, Lee accepted a [[buyout]] from ''The New York Times''.<ref name=buyout>{{cite web|url=http://www.observer.com/2009/media/jennifer-8-lee-taking-times-buyout |title=Jennifer 8. Lee Taking Times Buyout |publisher=[[New York Observer]] |date=December 9, 2009 |access-date=June 11, 2012 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120119132317/http://www.observer.com/2009/media/jennifer-8-lee-taking-times-buyout |archive-date=January 19, 2012 }}</ref>

Lee attempted to popularize the term "[[wikt:man date|man date]]" in a 2005 ''New York Times'' article, which subsequently inspired the 2009 film ''[[I Love You, Man]]'' starring [[Paul Rudd]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://observer.com/2006/08/itimesi-goes-hollywood-gives-content-work-to-beverly-hills-group/|title=Times Goes Hollywood: Gives Content Work to Beverly Hills Group|website=New York Observer|last1=Liu|first1=Jonathan|accessdate=June 17, 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.reverseshot.com/article/i_love_you_man|title=I Love You, Man|website=Reverse Shot|last1=Ventura|first1=Elbert|accessdate=June 16, 2014}}</ref>

Lee has served on the advisory panel for the [[John S. and James L. Knight Foundation]]'s "News Challenge", and has assisted the whistleblowing site [[WikiLeaks]], dealing with the press and with social networking sites.<ref name=yahoo>[https://news.yahoo.com/s/ynews/20100617/ts_ynews/ynews_ts2677_3 WikiLeaks questions why it was rejected for Knight grant], Yahoo! News, June 17, 2010 {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100927213302/http://news.yahoo.com/s/ynews/20100617/ts_ynews/ynews_ts2677_3 |date=September 27, 2010 }}</ref> She helped the organization with its April 2010 release of a video showing the [[July 12, 2007, Baghdad airstrike]].<ref>Clint Hendler: [https://www.cjr.org/the_kicker/wikileaks_releases_video_showi.php WikiLeaks Releases Video Showing Death of Reuters Staff] [[Columbia Journalism Review]] April 5, 2010</ref> Lee serves on the Board of Directors of the [[Center for Public Integrity]],<ref>{{cite web|title=Board of Directors|url=http://www.iwatchnews.org/about/our-people/board-of-directors|publisher=[[Center for Public Integrity]]|access-date=June 10, 2012|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120701143630/http://www.iwatchnews.org/about/our-people/board-of-directors|archive-date=July 1, 2012|df=mdy-all}}</ref> the Advisory Board of the [[Nieman Foundation]],<ref name=Nieman>{{cite web|title=About The Foundation {{!}} Advisory Board|url=http://www.nieman.harvard.edu/NiemanFoundation/AboutTheFoundation/AdvisoryBoard.aspx|publisher=[[The Nieman Foundation for Journalism]]|accessdate=October 26, 2012}}</ref> and the [[Asian American Writers' Workshop]].<ref name=AAWW>{{cite web|title=Read the Margins – About|url=http://aaww.org/about/#.UIsShsXLSSp|work=Read the Margins|publisher=Asian American Writers' Workshop|accessdate=October 26, 2012}}</ref> She is also an advisor to [[Upworthy]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.paleycenter.org/nbt-upworthy|publisher=The Paley Center for Media|accessdate=March 18, 2014|title=Upworthy &#124; The Paley Center for Media}}</ref>

In 2011, Lee and fellow writer [[Yael Goldstein Love]] founded a literary studio named Plympton, Inc.<ref name=Plympton>{{cite web|title=Our Team {{!}} Plympton|url=http://plympton.com/about/our-team/|accessdate=October 26, 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121111202628/http://plympton.com/about/our-team/|archive-date=November 11, 2012|url-status=dead}}</ref> The studio focuses on publishing [[serialized fiction]] for digital platforms.<ref name=Denison>{{cite news|last=Denison|first=D.C.|title=Boston literary start-up lands Amazon deal|url=http://bostonglobe.com/business/2012/09/07/boston-literary-start-lands-amazon-deal/szOcQBJfumV9pTthmd2HgI/story.html|accessdate=October 26, 2012|newspaper=[[The Boston Globe]]|date=September 8, 2012}}</ref> Investors include [[Reddit]] co-founder [[Alexis Ohanian]], [[Y Combinator (company)|Y Combinator]] partner Garry Tan, [[Delicious (website)|Delicious]] founder [[Joshua Schachter]], Hipmunk founder [[Adam Goldstein (author)|Adam Goldstein]], [[Inkling (company)|Inkling]] founder Matt MacInnis, [[Columbia Law School|Columbia Law]] Professor [[Tim Wu]] (author of “The Master Switch”), [[Quora]] co-founder [[Charlie Cheever]], and [[Tony Hsieh]]’s Vegas Tech Fund.<ref>{{cite news|last=Ha|first=Anthony|title=Aiming To Fit Fiction Into Busy Schedules, Rooster Is An iPhone App For Serialized Novels|url=https://techcrunch.com/2014/03/10/rooster-serialized-fiction/|accessdate=March 18, 2014|newspaper=TechCrunch|date=March 10, 2014}}</ref> Its first series launched in September 2012 as part of the [[47North#Kindle Serials|Kindle Serials]] program.<ref name=Bosman>{{cite news|last=Bosman|first=Julie|title=E-Books Expand Their Potential With Serialized 2012}}</ref> Its app [[Rooster (application)|Rooster]], launched in March 2014, is a mobile reading service for [[iOS7]].<ref>{{cite news|last=McMurtrie|first=John|title=S.F. company launches Rooster, a new mobile reading service|url=http://blog.sfgate.com/bookmarks/2014/03/12/s-f-company-launches-rooster-a-new-mobile-reading-service/|accessdate=March 17, 2014|newspaper=SFGate|date=March 12, 2014}}</ref>

In 2012, Lee created [[NewsDiffs]], a website that archives article revisions from ''[[The New York Times]]'', [[CNN]], ''[[Politico]]'', ''[[The Washington Post]]'', and the [[BBC]], with two brothers who were programmers, [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology|MIT]] graduate student Eric Price and Tddium employee Greg Price.<ref name="Brisbane2012-06-30">{{cite news|last=Brisbane |first=Arthur S. |author-link=Arthur S. Brisbane |date=June 30, 2012 |title=Insider's View of Changes, From Outside |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2012/07/01/opinion/sunday/article-changes-are-shown-in-a-tool-created-by-outsiders.html |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |access-date=June 18, 2015 |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/6ZN08oV82?url=http://www.nytimes.com/2012/07/01/opinion/sunday/article-changes-are-shown-in-a-tool-created-by-outsiders.html?_r=0 |archive-date=June 18, 2015 |url-status=dead }}</ref><ref name="Silverman2012-06-18">{{cite news|last=Silverman |first=Craig |date=June 18, 2012 |title=NewsDiffs tracks changes to New York Times, CNN |url=http://www.poynter.org/news/mediawire/177515/newsdiffs-tracks-changes-to-new-york-times-cnn-content/ |publisher=[[Poynter Institute]] |access-date=June 18, 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150618050130/http://www.poynter.org/news/mediawire/177515/newsdiffs-tracks-changes-to-new-york-times-cnn-content/ |archive-date=June 18, 2015 |url-status=dead }}</ref><ref name="Goldenberg2013-02-04">{{cite news|last=Goldenberg |first=Kira |date=February 4, 2013 |title=Tracking the NYT's evolving Koch obit: NewsDiffs reveals the newspaper's multiple revisions, resulting in a surge of traffic |url=https://www.cjr.org/behind_the_news/koch_newsdiffs.php |newspaper=[[Columbia Journalism Review]] |access-date=June 18, 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150618050354/https://www.cjr.org/behind_the_news/koch_newsdiffs.php |archive-date=June 18, 2015 |url-status=dead }}</ref> They built the website in 38 hours (including sleep) during the June 16–17, 2012, Knight-Mozilla-M.I.T. hackathon at the [[MIT Media Lab]].<ref name="Brisbane2012-06-30"/>
