The '''''Washington Monthly''''' is a bimonthly{{efn|name=bimonthly|as in once every two months}} [[nonprofit]] [[magazine]] of [[United States]] [[politics]] and [[government]] that is based in [[Washington, D.C.]] The magazine is known for its annual ranking of American colleges and universities, which serves as an alternative to the ''[[Forbes]]'' and ''[[U.S. News & World Report]]'' rankings.

==History==
The magazine was founded on February 19, 1969 by [[Charles Peters]], who wrote the "Tilting at Windmills" column in each issue until 2014.<ref name="Monthly01022014">{{cite news|first=Charles|last=Peters|url=http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/magazine/january_february_2014/tilting_at_windmills/why_bad_news_should_always_tri048348.php?page=all|title=Why bad news should always trickle up ... Polyester and merlot ... The hippest fund-raiser in New York|work=Washington Monthly|issue= Jan–Feb 2014|access-date=November 9, 2014}}</ref> [[Paul Glastris]], former speechwriter for [[Bill Clinton]], has been ''Washington Monthly'''s editor-in-chief since 2001. In 2008, the magazine switched from a monthly to a bimonthly publication schedule, citing high publication costs.

Past staff editors of the magazine include [[Jonathan Alter]], [[Taylor Branch]], [[James Fallows]], [[Joshua Green (journalist)|Joshua Green]], [[David Ignatius]], [[Mickey Kaus]], [[Nicholas Lemann]], [[Suzannah Lessard]], [[Jon Meacham]], [[Timothy Noah]], [[Joe Nocera]], and [[Steven Waldman]].<ref name="NYT042202">{{cite news|first=David|last=Carr|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2002/04/22/business/media-new-life-for-washington-watchdog.html|title=New Life for Washington Watchdog|work=The New York Times|date=April 22, 2002|access-date=June 13, 2012}}</ref>

In 2008, the liberal watchdog and advocacy group [[Common Cause]] considered acquiring ''Washington Monthly'', but the deal fell apart.<ref name="WashingtonPost">{{cite news |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/story/2008/02/19/ST2008021901252.html|title= Common Cause, Washington Monthly Explore a Common Future |work=The Washington Post|date= February 19, 2008| access-date=April 30, 2010|first=Jeffrey H.|last=Birnbaum}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Calderone|first1=Michael|title=Washington Monthly not merging with Common Cause|url=http://www.politico.com/blogs/michaelcalderone/0508/Washington_Monthly_not_merging_with_Common_Cause.html|access-date=December 7, 2015|publisher=Politico|date=May 27, 2008}}</ref>

==Contents and viewpoint==
The politics of ''Washington Monthly'' are often considered [[Centre-left politics|center-left]].<ref name=PoliticsUnspun>{{cite web|title=Media Bias|url=http://politicsunspun.com/?page_id=165|publisher=Politics Unspun|year=2016|access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref><ref name=NYMag122415>{{cite news|first=Ed|last=Kilgore |url=http://nymag.com/daily/intelligencer/2015/12/america-really-moving-left.html#|title=Is America Really Moving Left?|work=New York Magazine|date=December 24, 2015|access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref><ref name=Forbes091406>{{cite news|first=Rich|last=Karlgaard |url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/digitalrules/2006/09/14/republicans-for-divided-government/#73b80f0b3ddf |title=Republicans For Divided Government|work=Forbes|date=September 14, 2006 |access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref> Founder Charles Peters refers to himself as a [[New Deal]] Democrat and advocates the use of government to address social problems. His columns also frequently emphasized the importance of a vigilant "[[fourth estate]]" in keeping government honest.

''Washington Monthly'' features a continuing [[weblog|blog]]; "Political Animal" was written principally by [[Kevin Drum]] for several years, with frequent guest contributions by ''Washington Monthly's'' current and alumni editors. In 2008, [[Steve Benen]] took over as lead blogger; in 2012, he was succeeded by Ed Kilgore.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/political-animal/2012_01/and_thats_a_wrap034999.php|title=And that's a wrap|magazine=Washington Monthly|date=January 2012|access-date=January 31, 2012}}</ref> Kilgore left the magazine in 2015.<ref name="WaMo112015">{{cite news|first=Paul|last=Glastris|url=http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/political-animal-a/2015_11/ed_kilgore_some_going_away_tho058723.php|title=Ed Kilgore: Some Going Away Thoughts|work=Washington Monthly|date=November 20, 2015|access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref>

In addition to "Political Animal," the magazine's website also hosts "Ten Miles Square," a general blog featuring posts from staff and political scientists, which debuted in 2011,<ref>{{cite magazine|url=http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/ten-miles-square/2011/04/welcome_to_ten_miles_square_the_new_washington_monthly_blog028267.php|title=Welcome to the New Washingtonmonthly.com|magazine=Washington Monthly|date=April 2011|access-date=November 4, 2011}}</ref> and "College Guide," a blog about higher education, which the magazine began offering in 2009.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/college_guide/blog/welcome.php|title=Welcome|magazine=Washington Monthly|date=September 2009|access-date=November 4, 2011}}</ref>

==College rankings==
''Washington Monthly'''s annual [[college and university rankings]],<ref>[http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/college_guide/ Washington Monthly's Annual College Guide]</ref> a deliberate alternative college guide to ''[[U.S. News & World Report]]'' and ''[[Forbes]] College Rankings'' among domestic publications, began as a research report in 2005. It was introduced as an official set of rankings in the September 2006 issue.<ref>[http://www.washingtonmonthly.com/features/2006/0609.collegeguide.html "The ''Washington Monthly's'' Annual College Guide"]</ref>

Its "National Universities Rankings", most recently published in 2016, began as a research report in 2005, with rankings appearing in the September 2006 issue. ''Washington Monthly ''rates schools "based on their contribution to the public good in three broad categories: Social Mobility (recruiting and graduating low-income students), Research (producing cutting-edge scholarship and PhDs), and Service (encouraging students to give something back to their country)."<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|title=2019 National University Rankings|url=https://washingtonmonthly.com/2019college-guide/national|access-date=June 20, 2020|website=The Washington Monthly}}</ref>

==Funding==
The ''Washington Monthly'' receives financial support from the [[Lumina Foundation]] to provide coverage of post-secondary education-related issues.<ref name=lumina>{{cite web|title=Strategic Media Partners: Washington Monthly Corporation|url=http://www.luminafoundation.org/luminagrants/washington_monthly_corporation_grant_9079/| publisher=Lumina Foundation|year=2014|access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref> The magazine has also received funding from the [[Schumann Center for Media and Democracy]],<ref>{{cite news|last1=Hagey|first1=Keach|title=Liberal journalism's fickle godfather|url=http://www.politico.com/story/2011/07/liberal-journalisms-fickle-godfather-058180|access-date=December 7, 2015|publisher=Politico|date=July 1, 2011}}</ref> the [[Carnegie Corporation of New York]],<ref name=carnegie>{{cite web|title=Grants Database: Washington Monthly Corporation| url=https://www.carnegie.org/grants/grants-database/grantee/washington-monthly-corporation/#!/grants/grants-database/grant/33702.0/|work=Carnegie Corporation of New York|year=2016|access-date=February 6, 2016}}</ref> and individual supporters, including [[Warren Buffett]] and [[Markos Kounalakis]].<ref name="NYT042202"/>
