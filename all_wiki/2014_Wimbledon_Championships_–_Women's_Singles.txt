{{main|2014 Wimbledon Championships}}

[[2011 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|2011]] champion [[Petra Kvitová]] defeated [[Eugenie Bouchard]] in the final in straight sets, 6–3, 6–0 to win the Ladies' Singles tennis title at the 2014 Wimbledon Championships.<ref name="Barrett">{{cite book|last=Barrett|first=John|title=Wimbledon: The Official History|date=2014|publisher=Vision Sports Publishing|isbn=9-781909-534230|edition=4th}}</ref> The final lasted only 55 minutes, the fifth-shortest women's singles final in Wimbledon history in terms of time elapsed. Bouchard had not lost a set prior to the final and was the betting favorite to win.<ref name=final>{{cite news|title=Petra Kvitova thrashes Eugenie Bouchard in final|url=https://www.bbc.com/sport/0/tennis/28177057|date=5 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|first=Piers|last=Newbery|work=BBC Sport|publisher=BBC}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Petra Kvitova inspired by 'fan' Navratilova|url=https://www.bbc.com/sport/0/tennis/28178902|date=5 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|first=Piers|last=Newbery|work=BBC Sport|publisher=BBC}}</ref> [[Marion Bartoli]] was the reigning champion, but retired from professional tennis in August 2013.<ref>{{cite news|title=Wimbledon champion Marion Bartoli retires from tennis|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/sport/tennis/10243997/Wimbledon-champion-Marion-Bartoli-retires-from-tennis.html|date=15 August 2013|accessdate=6 July 2014|newspaper=The Daily Telegraph}}</ref> Kvitová dropped only one set the entire tournament, to [[Venus Williams]] in the third round.

Like the previous major, the 2014 Wimbledon Championships was marked by two big upsets. The top two seeds – [[Serena Williams]] and [[Li Na]] – both lost in the third round. This marked the first time in the [[History of tennis#Open era|Open era]] that neither of the top two seeds reached the fourth round of Wimbledon. Five-time Wimbledon champion Williams' defeat to 25th-seeded [[Alizé Cornet]] equalled her earliest exit from the tournament (she lost at the same stage in [[1998 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|1998]] and [[2005 Wimbledon Championships – Women's Singles|2005]]).<ref>{{cite news|title=A Most Unusual Upset for Serena|url=http://espn.go.com/espnw/news-commentary/article/11149941/espnw-top-seeded-serena-williams-stunned-france-alize-cornet-wimbledon-third-round|date=28 June 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|first=Melissa|last=Isaacson|work=ESPNW|publisher=ESPN Internet Ventures}}</ref> Li fell to unseeded [[Barbora Strýcová|Barbora Záhlavová-Strýcová]], which would be Li's final professional tennis match before she announced her retirement almost three months later.<ref>{{cite news|title=More major malaise for Li Na|url=http://espn.go.com/tennis/wimbledon14/story/_/id/11144932/wimbledon-more-major-malaise-li-na|date=28 June 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|first=Greg|last=Garber|work=ESPN|publisher=ESPN Internet Ventures}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2014/09/19/sports/tennis/chinas-li-na-set-to-retire-from-tennis.html |title=Li Na, Two-Time Major Winner, Is Retiring With Knee Woes |newspaper=The New York Times |first=Ben |last=Rothenberg |date=18 September 2014 |accessdate=19 September 2014 }}</ref>

==The final==
Sixth-seeded Kvitová defeated her compatriot and 23rd-seeded [[Lucie Šafářová]], 7–6<sup>(8–6)</sup>, 6–1 in the first semifinal, while 13th-seeded [[Eugenie Bouchard]] defeated third-seeded [[Simona Halep]], 7–6<sup>(7–5)</sup>, 6–2 in the other.<ref>{{cite news|title=Petra Kvitova beats Lucie Safarova in semi-final|url=https://www.bbc.com/sport/0/tennis/28148500|date=3 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|first=Piers|last=Newbery|work=BBC Sport|publisher=BBC}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.bbc.com/sport/0/tennis/28150860 | title=Halep injured in Wimbledon semi | accessdate=20 May 2015}}</ref> Bouchard, playing in only her sixth grand slam tournament, advanced to the final without losing a set, becoming the first Canadian tennis player to reach the singles final of a grand slam.<ref name=final/><ref name=WTA>{{cite web|title=Kvitova Re-conquers Wimbledon|url=http://www.wtatennis.com/news/article/3963603/title/kvitova-re-conquers-wimbledon|date=5 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|publisher=WTA Tour}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Eugenie Bouchard reaches Wimbledon final|url=http://www.cbc.ca/sports/tennis/eugenie-bouchard-reaches-wimbledon-final-1.2694990|date=3 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|work=CBC Sports|publisher=CBC}}</ref> The title match was the first grand slam final contested between two players born in the 1990s.<ref name=final/> In the first set, Kvitová broke in the third game and broke again in the seventh to establish a 5–2 lead. With Kvitová serving for the set, Bouchard broke back, but Kvitová did the same in the following game to take the first set 6–3. The second set saw Kvitová lose only three points on serve as she [[Bagel (tennis)|bagelled]] Bouchard 6–0.<ref name=final/> Only 10 other Wimbledon women singles champions lost "fewer games in the final than Kvitova did".<ref>{{cite news|title=Petra Kvitova wins Wimbledon title|url=http://espn.go.com/tennis/wimbledon14/story/_/id/11178235/petra-kvitova-wins-second-wimbledon-title|date=5 July 2014|accessdate=6 July 2014|work=ESPN|publisher=ESPN Internet Ventures|agency=Associated Press}}</ref>

The victory gave Kvitová her second Wimbledon title and second [[List of Grand Slam women's singles champions|Grand Slam title]] overall.<ref name=final/> After the tournament, Bouchard improved to a career-high World No. 7 in the WTA rankings, surpassing [[Carling Bassett-Seguso]]'s record of being the highest-ranked Canadian woman of all-time, while Kvitová moved up to World No. 4.<ref name=WTA/>
