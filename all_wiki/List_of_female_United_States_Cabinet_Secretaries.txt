The [[Cabinet of the United States]] has had 32&nbsp;female officers. No woman held a Cabinet position before the ratification of the [[Nineteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution]] in 1920, which prohibits states and the federal government from denying any citizen the [[Suffrage|right to vote]] because of that citizen's sex.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.archives.gov/exhibits/charters/constitution_amendments_11-27.html |title=The Constitution of the United States: Amendments 11–27 |publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]] |accessdate=November 14, 2008}}</ref>

[[Frances Perkins]] was the first woman to serve in the Cabinet; she was appointed [[United States Secretary of Labor|Secretary of Labor]] in 1933 by [[President of the United States|President]] [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]].<ref name="perkins_a">{{cite web |url=https://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=FA0916FF3D5F147A93C7A8178ED85F418685F9 |title=Frances Perkins, The First Woman In Cabinet, Is Dead |date=May 15, 1965 |work=[[The New York Times]] |accessdate=November 9, 2008}}</ref><ref name="perkins_b">{{cite web |url=http://www.thenation.com/article/roosevelt-appoint-first-ever-female-cabinet-member |title=Roosevelt to Appoint First-Ever Female Cabinet Member |last=Villard |first=Oswald G. |date=December 8, 2008 |work=[[The Nation]] |accessdate=February 22, 2011}}</ref> [[Oveta Culp Hobby]] became the second woman to serve in the Cabinet,<ref name="hobby">{{cite web |url=https://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=990CE3D71531F934A2575BC0A963958260 |title=Oveta Culp Hobby, Founder of the WACs And First Secretary of Health, Dies at 90 |first=James |last=Barron |date=August 17, 1995 |work=[[The New York Times]] |accessdate= November 14, 2008}}</ref> when she was named head of the then newly formed [[United States Department of Health, Education, and Welfare|Department of Health, Education, and Welfare]] in 1953.<ref name="HHS">{{cite web |url=https://www.hhs.gov/about/hhshist.html |title=Historical Highlights |publisher=[[United States Department of Health and Human Services]] |accessdate=November 14, 2008}}</ref> This [[United States federal executive departments|department]] was subdivided into the departments of [[United States Department of Education|Education]] and [[United States Department of Health and Human Services|Health and Human Services]] in 1979.<ref name="HHS"/> [[Patricia Roberts Harris]], who was Secretary of Health, Education, and Welfare before the department split and had earlier served as [[United States Secretary of Housing and Urban Development|Secretary of Housing and Urban Development]] in 1977, became the first female Secretary of Health and Human Services in 1979. Harris was also the first [[African Americans|African American]] woman to serve in the Cabinet.<ref name="harris">{{cite web |url=http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/255927/Patricia-Roberts-Harris |title=Patricia Roberts Harris |work=[[Encyclopædia Britannica]] |accessdate=November 14, 2008}}</ref>

Former [[North Carolina]] [[United States Senate|Senator]] [[Elizabeth Dole]] is the first woman to have served in two different Cabinet positions in two different administrations. She was appointed by President [[Ronald Reagan]] as [[United States Secretary of Transportation|Secretary of Transportation]] in 1983 and was the Secretary of Labor during the tenure of [[George H. W. Bush]]—Reagan's successor.<ref name="dole">{{cite web |url=http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=d000601 |title=Dole, Elizabeth Hanford, (1936–) |publisher=[[United States Congress]] |accessdate=January 13, 2009}}</ref> [[Czechoslovakia]]-born [[Madeleine Albright]] became the first foreign-born woman to serve in the Cabinet when she was appointed [[United States Secretary of State|Secretary of State]] in 1997.{{ref label|Note1|a|a}}<ref name="albright">{{cite web |url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/americas/931993.stm |title=Albright: Lift 'Foreign' Presidents Bar |date=September 19, 2000 |work=[[BBC News]] |accessdate=November 14, 2008}}</ref> Her appointment also made her the highest-ranking female Cabinet member at that time.{{ref label|Note2|b|b}}<ref name="albright"/> [[Condoleezza Rice]] was appointed Secretary of State in 2005 and thus became the highest-ranking woman in the [[United States presidential line of succession]] in history.<ref name="rice">{{cite web |url=https://georgewbush-whitehouse.archives.gov/government/rice-bio.html |title=Condoleezza Rice |publisher=[[White House]] |accessdate=November 14, 2008}}</ref> In 2006, [[Nancy Pelosi]] replaced Rice as the highest-ranking woman in line when she was elected [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|Speaker of the House]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=P000197 |title=Pelosi, Nancy, (1940–) |publisher=[[United States Congress]] |accessdate=November 16, 2008}}</ref><ref name="succession"/> In 2021, Vice President-elect [[Kamala Harris]] will replace Pelosi as the highest-ranking woman in the line of succession when she becomes [[Vice President of the United States|Vice President]].<ref name="VP">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/11/07/us/politics/kamala-harris.html|last=Schaff|first=Erin|date=November 7, 2020|access-date=November 24, 2020|newspaper=New York Times}}</ref>

In 2009, President [[Barack Obama]] named four women to the Cabinet—[[List of governors of Arizona|Arizona Governor]] [[Janet Napolitano]] as [[United States Secretary of Homeland Security|Secretary of Homeland Security]], former [[List of first ladies of the United States|First Lady]] and [[New York (state)|New York]] Senator [[Hillary Clinton]] as Secretary of State, [[California]] [[United States House of Representatives|Representative]] [[Hilda Solis]] as Secretary of Labor and [[List of governors of Kansas|Kansas Governor]] [[Kathleen Sebelius]] as Secretary of Health and Human Services.<ref name="napolitano">{{cite web |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/21/us/politics/20web-inaug2.html?pagewanted=2 |title=Obama Is Sworn In as the 44th President |work=[[The New York Times]] |last=Hulse |first=Carl |page=2 |date=January 20, 2009 |accessdate=January 21, 2009}}</ref><ref name="clinton">{{cite web |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/22/us/politics/22holder.html?hp |title=Clinton Is Approved, But Vote on Holder Is Delayed |last=Stout |first=David |date=January 21, 2009 |work=[[The New York Times]] |accessdate=January 21, 2009}}</ref><ref name="solis">{{cite web 2009}}</ref><ref name="sebelius">{{cite web |url =http://thecaucus.blogs.nytimes.com/2009/04/28/senate-confirms-sebelius-to-hhs-secretary/ |title=Senate Confirms Sebelius as Health Secretary |last=Pear |first=Robert |date=April 28, 2009 |work=[[The New York Times]] |accessdate=April 28, 2009}}</ref> Clinton became the only [[First Lady of the United States|First Lady]] to serve in the Cabinet and the third female Secretary of State.<ref name="clinton"/> Napolitano became the first female Secretary of Homeland Security.<ref name="napolitano"/> Obama appointed eight women to Cabinet positions, the most of any presidency, surpassing [[George W. Bush]]'s record of six.

The Department of Labor has had the most female Secretaries with seven.<ref name="women">{{cite web|url=http://www.cawp.rutgers.edu/fast_facts/levels_of_office/documents/prescabinet.pdf |title=Women Appointed to Presidential Cabinets |date=June 6, 2014 |publisher=Center for American Women and Politics, [[Eagleton Institute of Politics]], [[Rutgers University|Rutgers, The State University of New Jersey]] |format=PDF |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/6KVJEPnTT?url=http://www.cawp.rutgers.edu/fast_facts/levels_of_office/documents/prescabinet.pdf |archive-date=October 20, 2013 |url-status=dead |accessdate=February 23, 2017 }}</ref> The Department of Health and Human Services has had five; the departments of [[United States Department of State|State]], [[United States Department of Transportation|Transportation]], [[United States Department of Commerce|Commerce]] and [[United States Department of Education|Education]] have had three; the departments of [[United States Department of Housing and Urban Development|Housing and Urban Development]], [[United States Department of Justice|Justice]], the [[United States Department of the Interior|Interior]] and [[United States Department of Homeland Security|Homeland Security]] have had two; the [[United States Department of Agriculture|Department of Agriculture]] has had one.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/lifestyle/style/sally-jewell-at-a-different-kind-of-summit-head-of-the-department-of-the-interior/2013/12/25/ec42aa32-5613-11e3-835d-e7173847c7cc_story.html|title=Sally Jewell at a different kind of summit: Head of the Department of the Interior|last=Bernstein|first=Lenny|date=December 25, 2013|newspaper=The Washington Post|access-date=February 19, 2017|last2=Bernstein|first2=Lenny|language=en-US|issn=0190-8286}}</ref><ref name="women"/> The defunct Department of Health, Education, and Welfare also had two female Secretaries.<ref name="women"/> The three departments of [[United States Department of Defense|Defense]], [[United States Department of the Treasury|Treasury]] and [[United States Department of Veterans Affairs|Veterans Affairs]] are the only existing Cabinet departments that have not had women Secretaries,<ref name="treasury"/><ref name="defense"/><ref name="va"/> though President-elect [[Joe Biden]] has named former Chair of the Federal Reserve [[Janet Yellen]] to be the first woman Secretary of the Treasury.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/janet-yellen-is-bidens-pick-for-treasury-secretary-11606161637|title=Biden Picks Janet Yellen for Treasury Secretary|last=Davidson|first=Kate|date=November 23, 2020|access-date=November 24, 2020|newspaper=Wall Street Journal}}</ref>

==Notes==
*{{note label|Note1|a|a}} [[Elaine Chao]] became the second foreign-born woman to serve in the Cabinet when she was appointed Secretary of Labor in 2001.<ref name="chao"/>
*{{note label|Note2|b|b}} The Secretary of State, as the most senior Cabinet position, is the first Cabinet member in the line of succession and the fourth overall.<ref name="succession">{{cite web |url=https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/3/19.html |title=U.S. Code: Title 3, 19. Vacancy In Offices of Both President and Vice President; Officers Eligible to Act |publisher=[[Cornell Law School]] |accessdate=November 16, 2008}}</ref> Albright was ineligible to serve in the line of succession due to her foreign birth.<ref name="succession"/><ref name="constitution">{{cite web |url=https://www.archives.gov/exhibits/charters/constitution_transcript.html |title=The Constitution of the United States: A Transcription |publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]] |accessdate=November 16, 2008}}</ref>
*{{note label|Note3|c|c}} The Department of the Treasury was established in 1789; no woman has served yet.<ref name="treasury">{{cite web |url=http://www.treasury.gov/about/history/Pages/edu_history_secretary_index.aspx |title=History of the Treasury: Secretaries of the Treasury |publisher=[[United States Department of the Treasury]] |accessdate=November 8, 2008}}</ref>
*{{note label|Note4|d|d}} The Department of Defense was established in 1947; no woman has served yet.<ref name="defense">{{cite web |url=http://www.defenselink.mil/specials/secdef_histories/ |title=Histories of the Secretaries of Defense |publisher=[[United States Department of Defense]] |accessdate=November 8, 2008}}</ref>
*{{note label|Note5|e|e}} The Department of Veterans Affairs was established in 1989; no woman has served yet.<ref name="va">{{cite web |url=http://www1.va.gov/opa/fact/vafacts.asp |title=Fact Sheet: Facts About the Department of Veterans Affairs |publisher=[[United States Department of Veterans Affairs]] |accessdate=November 9, 2008 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080430091112/http://www1.va.gov/opa/fact/vafacts.asp |archive-date=April 30, 2008 }}</ref>
*{{note label|Note6|f|f}} The Postmaster General ceased to be a member of the Cabinet when the [[United States Post Office Department|Post Office Department]] was re-organized into the [[United States Postal Service]], a special agency independent of the executive branch, by the 1970 Postal Reorganization Act. No woman had ever served while it was a Cabinet post. Following the creation of the independent United States Postal Service, [[Megan Brennan]] became the first woman to serve as Postmaster General in 2015.<ref name="Linns20141114">{{cite web|url=http://linns.com/news/breaking-stamp-news/1020/First-woman-postmaster-general-starts-Feb-1 |title=First woman postmaster general starts Feb. 1 |last=Bigalke |first=Jay |date=November 14, 2014 |work=[[Linn's Stamp News]] |accessdate=November 15, 2014 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141117113652/http://linns.com/news/breaking-stamp-news/1020/First-woman-postmaster-general-starts-Feb-1 |archive-date=November 17, 2014 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://about.usps.com/publications/pub100.pdf |title=The United States Postal Service: An American History 1775– 2002 |date=September 2003 |publisher=[[United States Postal Service]] |accessdate=May 30, 2014}}</ref>
*{{note label|Note7|g|g}} The Secretary of the Navy ceased to be a member of the Cabinet when the [[United States Department of the Navy|Department of the Navy]] was absorbed into the Department of Defense in 1947. No woman had ever served while it was a Cabinet post. [[Susan Livingstone]] was the first woman to serve in that post from 2001 to 2003 after it became a position beneath the Secretary of Defense.<ref name="DOD">{{cite web |url=https://www.archives.gov/research/guide-fed-records/groups/330.html |title=Records of the Office of the Secretary of Defense |publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]] |accessdate=November 15, 2008}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.history.navy.mil/faqs/faq37-1.htm |title=Secretaries of the Navy |publisher=Department of the Navy |accessdate=November 16, 2008 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140801164903/http://www.history.navy.mil/faqs/faq37-1.htm |archive-date=August 1, 2014 }}</ref>
*{{note label|Note8|h|h}} The position of Secretary of War became defunct when the [[United States Department of War|Department of War]] was split between the Department of the Army and the Department of the Air Force and both absorbed into the Department of Defense in 1947. No woman had ever served while it was a Cabinet post.<ref name="DOD"/>
*{{note label|Note9|i|i}} The position of Secretary of Commerce and Labor became defunct when the Department of Commerce and Labor was subdivided into two separate entities in 1913. No woman had ever served while it was a Cabinet post.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.archives.gov/research/guide-fed-records/groups/040.html |title=General Records of the Department of Commerce |publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]] |accessdate=November 15, 2008}}</ref>
