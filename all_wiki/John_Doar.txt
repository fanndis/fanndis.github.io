'''John Michael Doar''' (December 3, 1921 – November 11, 2014) was an American [[lawyer]] and senior counsel with the [[law firm]] Doar Rieck Kaley & Mack in [[New York City]]. During the administrations of presidents [[John F. Kennedy]] and [[Lyndon B. Johnson]], he served first as [[United States Department of Justice Civil Rights Division|Deputy Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights]] from 1961 to 1965, and then as head of the division from 1965 until 1967. He led the government's response to events such as the admission and protection of [[James Meredith]], the first black student admitted to the [[University of Mississippi]], as well as the evolving response to the civil rights movement promoting integration and voter registration in the South. Additionally, in 1973–74, he served as the lead special counsel for the [[United States House Committee on the Judiciary|U.S. House Judiciary Committee's]] impeachment inquiry staff during the [[impeachment process against Richard Nixon]].

==Early life==
Doar was born in [[New Richmond, Wisconsin]], the son of Mae and William Doar.<ref name="NYTimesObit">{{cite news |author=Roy Reed |date=November 11, 2014 |title=John Doar, Federal Lawyer on Front Lines Against Segregation, Dies at 92 |newspaper=The New York Times |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2014/11/12/us/john-doar-federal-lawyer-in-battle-against-segregation-dies-at-92.html |page=A25}}</ref> In 1940, Doar graduated from [[St. Paul Academy and Summit School]] in [[Saint Paul, Minnesota]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.spa.edu/page.cfm?p=795|title=St. Paul Academy and Summit School: In Memoriam Archive|website=www.spa.edu|accessdate=25 April 2019}}</ref> He served in the [[United States Army Air Forces]] during [[World War II]] and was a pilot. He graduated with an A.B. from the School of Public and International Affairs (now [[Princeton School of Public and International Affairs]]) at [[Princeton University]] in 1944 after completing a senior thesis titled "An Analysis of Farmer Cooperatives, 1918-1946."<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Doar|first=John Michael|date=1944|title=An Analysis of Farmer Cooperatives, 1918-1946|url=http://dataspace.princeton.edu/jspui/handle/88435/dsp011z40kt641}}</ref> He then received an LL.B. from the [[University of California-Berkeley]] [[Boalt Hall School of Law]]. From 1950 to 1960, Doar then worked in his family's law firm in [[New Richmond, Wisconsin]].<ref name= "NYTimesObit" />

==Civil rights career==
A Republican,<ref name= "NYTimesObit" /> Doar served as Deputy Assistant and then [[United States Assistant Attorney General|Assistant Attorney General]] for [[United States Department of Justice Civil Rights Division|Civil Rights]] in the [[United States Department of Justice|U.S. Dept. of Justice]], from 1960 to 1967,<ref name=bio /> during which time he was involved in several of the most significant events of the [[American civil rights movement]]. In 1961 he operated in [[Montgomery, Alabama]], along with his assistant, [[John Seigenthaler]], to protect the [[Freedom Riders]].<ref name=breslin />  In 1962, he confronted [[Ross Barnett]] over Barnett's attempts to prevent [[James Meredith]] from entering the [[racial segregation|segregated]] [[University of Mississippi]]. He also prosecuted [[Collie Leroy Wilkins]] for federal civil rights violations in the [[murder]] of [[Viola Liuzzo]],<ref>{{cite web | url = http://library.wustl.edu/units/spec/filmandmedia/collections/henry-hampton-collection/eyes1/doar.htm | title = Introduction to John Doar Oral History | publisher = Washington University at St. Louis | accessdate = 20 July 2012 | url-status = dead | archiveurl = https://archive.li/20121215120155/http://library.wustl.edu/units/spec/filmandmedia/collections/henry-hampton-collection/eyes1/doar.htm | archivedate = 15 December 2012 }}</ref> gaining conviction by an [[all-white jury]] in [[Alabama]]. In 1963, he calmed an [[angry mob]] after the [[assassination]] of civil rights leader [[Medgar Evers]], murdered outside his home.<ref name=anniston />

Doar prosecuted the federal case for civil rights violations against [[Murders of Chaney, Goodman, and Schwerner|the people who were accused of murdering]] [[Andrew Goodman (activist)|Andrew Goodman]], [[James Chaney]] and [[Michael Schwerner]],<ref name=anniston>{{cite news | title = The quiet authority of John Doar, a towering figure of the civil rights movement | newspaper = The Anniston Star | author = John Fleming | date = 21 November 2010 | url = http://www.annistonstar.com/view/full_story/10386877/article-The-quiet-authority-of-John-Doar--a-towering-figure--of-the-civil-rights-movement | url-status = dead | archiveurl = https://web.archive.org/web/20110928033235/http://www.annistonstar.com/view/full_story/10386877/article-The-quiet-authority-of-John-Doar--a-towering-figure--of-the-civil-rights-movement | archivedate = 28 September 2011 }}</ref> young civil rights workers in Mississippi. He had earlier contributed to drafting the [[Civil Rights Act of 1964]], which [[Lyndon Johnson]] signed to try to enforce constitutional rights for all citizens. In March 1965, Doar was the first to arrive in [[Montgomery, Alabama]], during the third of the [[Selma to Montgomery marches]]. He walked into Montgomery half a block ahead of the march in his capacity as Assistant Attorney General.<ref name=breslin>{{cite news |author=Jimmy Breslin |date=March 26, 1965 |newspaper=New York Herald-Tribune |title=Changing the South}} reprinted in {{cite book |editor=Clayborne Carson |title=Reporting Civil Rights: American journalism, 1963-1973 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=E8HtAAAAMAAJ |accessdate=20 July 2012 |year=2003 |publisher=Library of America |pages=361–366 |display-editors=etal}}</ref>

Doar left the government in 1967. He went into private practice and worked for [[Bedford Stuyvesant Restoration Corporation|Bedford Stuyvesant Development Corporation]].<ref>{{cite web |last1=Hunter |first1=Charlayne |title=Doar Resigns Post at Restoration Corporation After Six Years |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1973/12/14/archives/doar-resigns-post-at-restoration-corporation-after-six-years.html |website=The New York Times |accessdate=17 August 2019 |date=14 December 1973}}</ref> From late 1968 to 1969 he was president of the New York City Board of Education.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1973/12/21/archives/a-hardworking-legal-adviser-john-michael-doar-phone-call-from-laird.html|title=A Hard‐Working Legal Adviser|last=Doar|first=John Michael|date=1973-12-21|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-01-30|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> During his tenure he supported gender discrimination, opposing the entry of girls to all-boy high schools.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.newyorker.com/culture/culture-desk/how-a-thirteen-year-old-girl-smashed-the-gender-divide-in-american-high-schools|title=How a Thirteen-Year-Old Girl Smashed the Gender Divide in American High Schools|last=Shapiro|first=Laurie Gwen|date=2019-01-26|access-date=2019-01-30|language=en|issn=0028-792X}}</ref> 

==Nixon impeachment inquiry==
Doar was hired in December 1973 to be the lead special counsel for the [[United States House Committee on the Judiciary|House Judiciary Committee's]] impeachment inquiry staff during the [[impeachment process against Richard Nixon]].<ref>{{cite news| url=https://www.nytimes.com/1973/12/21/archives/a-hardworking-legal-adviser-john-michael-doar-phone-call-from-laird.html| title=Man in the News: A Hard‐Working Legal Adviser: John Michael Doar| newspaper=The New York Times| date=December 21, 1973 |page=20}}</ref> He supervised a team which in due course grew to 100 persons, including lawyers, investigators, clerks and stenographers. To minimize leaks he established strict rules of conduct that included this directive: "The staff of the impeachment inquiry shall not discuss with anyone outside the staff either the substance or procedure of their work or that of the committee<ref name=NYT1973BK>{{cite news| title=Big Impeachment Inquiry Staff Is Quietly Writing U.S. History| last=Kovach| first=Bill| date=March 10, 1974| newspaper=The New York Times| url=https://www.nytimes.com/1974/03/10/archives/big-impeachment-inquiry-staff-is-quietly-writing-u-s-history-rules.html| accessdate=October 28, 2019| via=The New York Times Archives}}</ref>

==Later life and death==
He then started a law firm in New York City: Doar, Rieck, Kaley, & Mack.<ref name= "NYTimesObit" /><ref name=bio>{{cite web|url=http://www.doarlaw.com/bios/john-doar.html |publisher=Doar, Rieck, Kaley, & Mack |title=biography |accessdate=July 20, 2012 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20120601084141/http://www.doarlaw.com/bios/john-doar.html |archivedate=June 1, 2012 }}</ref>

Doar died in [[New York City]] from congestive heart failure, aged 92.<ref name="NYTimesObit"/> He is survived by his daughter Gael Walsh, Michael Doar, [[Robert Doar]] a former Commissioner of the [[New York City Human Resources Administration]] and Burke Doar.

==Representation in film and television==
*The 1988 film ''[[Mississippi Burning]]'' drew from the murders of the three civil rights workers.
*Episode from the [[Discovery Channel]] series, "US Marshals: Mission in Mississippi" (1997). Interview with Doar about his role in Justice Department effort to enroll James Meredith at University of Mississippi in 1962. Doar and Chief US Marshal James McShane were given the job of enrolling Meredith by Attorney General Robert Kennedy.
*In the 2014 film ''[[Selma (film)|Selma]]'', he is portrayed by [[Alessandro Nivola]].
