'''Jean-Marc Vallée''' {{Post-nominals|country=CAN|OC}} (born March 9, 1963) is a Canadian filmmaker, film editor and screenwriter. After studying film at the [[Université de Montréal]], Vallée went on to make a number of critically acclaimed [[short film]]s, including ''Stéréotypes'' (1991), ''Les Fleurs magiques'' (1995), and ''Les Mots magiques'' (1998).

His debut feature, ''[[Black List (1995 film)|Black List]]'' (1995), was nominated for nine [[Genie Awards]], including nods for Vallée's direction and editing. His fourth feature film, ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' (2005), received further critical acclaim and was a financial success.  Vallée's followup, ''[[The Young Victoria]]'' (2009), garnered strong reviews and received three [[Academy Award]] nominations, while his sixth film, ''[[Café de Flore (film)|Café de Flore]]'' (2011), was the most nominated film at the [[32nd Genie Awards]]. Vallée's next films, the American dramas ''[[Dallas Buyers Club]]'' (2013) and ''[[Wild (2014 film)|Wild]]'' (2014) continued this acclaim and the former earned him a nomination for the [[Academy Award for Best Film Editing]].

Vallée ventured into television by executive producing and directing two projects for [[HBO]], the drama series ''[[Big Little Lies (TV series)|Big Little Lies]]'' (2017) and the thriller miniseries ''[[Sharp Objects (miniseries)|Sharp Objects]]'' (2018). For the former, he won the [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Directing for a Limited Series, Movie, or Dramatic Special]].

==Early life==
Vallée was born and raised in [[Montreal]], [[Quebec]].<ref name="allocine"/> He studied [[filmmaking]] at the [[Collège Ahuntsic]] and the [[Université de Montréal]].<ref name="allocine"/>

==Career==
===Early work===
Vallée's earliest-known works include five [[Music video|music videos]] written and directed in August 1985.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Jean-Marc Vallée|url=http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0885249/|website=IMDb|access-date=2020-05-03}}</ref> The music videos were part of a project by Les Productions Perfo 30 to produce 30 music videos in 30 days for a total budget of not over $50,000 [[Canadian dollar|CAD]]. Les Productions Perfo 30 had been founded earlier that year in May 1985 by [[Dédé Fortin|André Fortin]], Martin-Éric Ouellette and Martin Saint-Pierre, and wound up producing a total of 32 music videos, all directed during the month of August, with editing spanning September and October.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|title=With Les Productions Perfo 30 (Sorted by Popularity Ascending)|url=http://www.imdb.com/search/title/?companies=co0793190|website=IMDb|access-date=2020-05-03}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=André Fortin|url=http://www.imdb.com/name/nm3968934/bio|website=IMDb|access-date=2020-05-03}}</ref> Vallée was one of four directors (along with Fortin, Ouellette and Claude Grégoire) to helm directorial duties. His music videos included Wild Touch's ''My Chick Is In My Bed'', Glockenspiel's ''Odeline'', Park Avenue's ''Don't Talk To Strangers'', Angel's ''Angel's Evolution'' and New News' ''The Splice Of Life''.<ref name=":0" /> The music videos premiered theatrically at the [[Spectrum (Montreal)|Spectrum]] in Montreal on 1 November 1985 for a limited press screening, then officially on 9 November 1985 for the general public.<ref>{{Citation|title=Wild Touch: My Chick Is in My Bed (Video 1985) - IMDb|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt12083080/releaseinfo|access-date=2020-05-03}}</ref> The music videos later aired on Canadian television through [[MuchMusic]].<ref name=":0" />

In the 1990s, Vallée produced a number of short films that aroused considerable [[film criticism|critical]] interest.<ref name="tiff" /> In 1991, ''Stereotypes'', a [[fantastique]] comedy inspired by some American classic films, received numerous prizes at several events, including Best Promising Director for Vallée at the [[Rendez-vous du cinéma québécois]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://gpa-films.com/fr/fiche_ster.html|title=Stéréotypes|publisher=GPA Films|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140327221119/http://gpa-films.com/fr/fiche_ster.html|archive-date=March 27, 2014|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Vallée later adopted a more personal and autobiographical tone with ''[[Magical Flowers]] (Les Fleurs magiques)'' (1995) and ''[[Magical Words]] (Les Mots magiques)'' (1998), awarded respectively Best Short Film at the [[16th Genie Awards]] and the [[Jutra Award|1st Jutra Awards]], in which the director explored the relationship between father and son.<ref name="tiff">{{cite web|url=http://tiff.net/CANADIANFILMENCYCLOPEDIA/content/bios/jean-marc-vallee |archive-url=https://archive.is/20130222091838/http://tiff.net/CANADIANFILMENCYCLOPEDIA/content/bios/jean-marc-vallee |url-status=dead |archive-date=February 22, 2013 |title=Jean-Marc Vallée |first=Liz |last=Czach |work=Canadian Film Encyclopedia |publisher=[[Toronto International Film Festival]] |accessdate=December 9, 2012 }}</ref>

Vallée made his feature-length debut in 1995 with ''[[Black List (1995 film)|Liste noire]]'' (''Black List''), which became the highest-grossing film in Quebec that year and received nine [[Genie Award]] notimations, including [[Genie Award for Best Motion Picture|Best Motion Picture]] and [[Genie Award for Best Achievement in Direction|Best Achievement in Direction]].<ref name="telequebec"/> In the wake of this success, Vallée moved to [[Los Angeles]] where he directed ''[[Los Locos]]'' (1998), a Western film written by and starring [[Mario Van Peebles]], and ''Loser Love'' (1999).<ref name="telequebec">{{cite web|url=http://cinemaquebecois.telequebec.tv/#/Artisans/88/Clips/1026/Default.aspx|title=Jean-Marc Vallée|publisher=[[Télé-Québec]]|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French}}</ref> After these two low-budget productions, he directed two episodes of the television series ''[[The Secret Adventures of Jules Verne]]'' (2000).

===''C.R.A.Z.Y.''===
During the mid-1990s, Vallée was preparing ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' from a screenplay inspired by his own youth and that of his co-writer, François Boulay. Vallée wanted to shoot the film in the United States, but his friend [[Michel Côté (actor)|Michel Côté]], who also starred in ''Black List'', convinced him to shoot in Quebec.<ref name="allocine">{{cite web|url=http://www.allocine.fr/personne/fichepersonne-17171/biographie|title=Jean-Marc Vallée : Sa biographie|work=[[AlloCiné]]|publisher=Tiger Global|first=Alexandre|last=Jourdain|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French}}</ref> After ten years in production, ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'' was finally released in 2005 and became one of the most successful films in Quebec history, both financially and critically.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.variety.com/article/VR1117940032/|title=Quebec kudos just 'C.R.A.Z.Y.' for pic|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|publisher=[[Penske Business Media]]|first=Brendan|last=Kelly|date=March 20, 2006|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

It tells the story of Zachary Beaulieu, a young man dealing with [[homophobia]] and [[heterosexism]] while growing up with four brothers and a [[conservative]] father in 1960s and 1970s Quebec. The role of Zachary Beaulieu was portrayed by [[Marc-André Grondin]], while Michel Côté and [[Danielle Proulx]] starred as Zachary's parents. ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'' had its world premiere at the [[2005 Toronto International Film Festival]] and was awarded Best Canadian Feature Film.<ref name="canada.com"/> It received unanimous praise from film critics, with the film aggregator website, [[Rotten Tomatoes]], giving the film a 100% "Certified Fresh" rating, based on reviews from 17 critics.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/crazy/|title=C.R.A.Z.Y. (2005)|website=[[Rotten Tomatoes]]|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> It received several accolades, including eleven [[Genie Awards]] and thirteen [[Jutra Awards]].<ref name="canada.com">{{cite news|url=http://www.canada.com/montrealgazette/story.html?id=d0a4367a-b29a-4b8b-b658-c474d982c6c2&k=75110 |title=Jutra judges wild about C.R.A.Z.Y. |work=Canada.com |publisher=[[Postmedia News]] |date=March 20, 2006 |accessdate=December 9, 2012 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20150622034914/http://www.canada.com/montrealgazette/story.html?id=d0a4367a-b29a-4b8b-b658-c474d982c6c2&k=75110 |archivedate=June 22, 2015 }}</ref> ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'' was also selected as Canada's official submission for the 2005 [[Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.indiewire.com/article/dispatch_from_l.a._hoping_for_oscar_attention_a_trio_of_foreign_language_ti|title=Hoping for Oscar Attention, A Trio of Foreign Language Titles Win Over Audiences|work=[[IndieWire]]|date=November 9, 2005|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

===''The Young Victoria''===
After the success of ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'', [[Graham King]] and [[Martin Scorsese]] hired Jean-Marc Vallée to direct the period drama ''[[The Young Victoria]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/film/4344459/The-Young-Victoria-we-were-amused.html|title=The Young Victoria: we were amused|work=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|first=Chloe|last=Fox|date=February 4, 2009|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> Written by [[Julian Fellowes]], the film is based on the early life and reign of [[Queen Victoria]], and her marriage to [[Albert, Prince Consort|Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha]]. The film stars [[Emily Blunt]], [[Rupert Friend]], [[Paul Bettany]], [[Miranda Richardson]], and [[Jim Broadbent]] among a large ensemble cast. Critical reception was generally positive and the film was nominated for three [[82nd Academy Awards|Academy Awards]], winning the 2009 [[Academy Award for Best Costume Design]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://latimesblogs.latimes.com/alltherage/2010/03/academy-award-for-costume-design-goes-to-sandy-powell-for-young-victoria.html|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|title='Young Victoria' earns Sandy Powell a third Oscar for costume design|first=Adam|last=Tschorn|date=March 7, 2010|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

===''Café de Flore''===
In 2011, Vallée wrote, directed, and edited ''[[Café de Flore (film)|Café de Flore]]'', a love story which connects a man and woman living in present-day Montreal with a mother and her son in 1960s Paris.<ref>[https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1550312/ "Café de Flore"] at [[IMDb]]</ref> The film starred French popstar [[Vanessa Paradis]] and [[Québécois people|Québécois]] actors [[Kevin Parent]], [[Hélène Florent]], and [[Evelyne Brochu]]. It was received generally positive reviews from Canadian film critics and garnered thirteen nominations at the [[32nd Genie Awards]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.toronto.com/article/710366?bn=1 |title='Café de Flore', 'A Dangerous Method' lead Genie Awards race |first=Linda |last=Barnard |work=[[Toronto Star]] |date=January 17, 2012 |accessdate=December 9, 2012 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20120319082501/http://www.toronto.com/article/710366?bn=1 |archivedate=March 19, 2012 }}</ref> American reviews were more mixed; [[Variety (magazine)|''Variety'']]'s Boyd van Hoeij salutes the film's casting, but deems Café de Flore unoriginal, noting that "Vallée has taken what made ''C.R.A.Z.Y'' so successful, and simply tried to replicate it on a slightly larger scale. [Occasionally] similarities between the films... are so striking it almost feels like Vallée's ripping himself off".<ref>van Hoeij, Boyd (2011). ''Cafe de Flore''. Variety: Vol.424(6), pp. 83.</ref>

===''Dallas Buyers Club''===
Vallée's next film, ''[[Dallas Buyers Club]]'', starred [[Matthew McConaughey]], [[Jared Leto]], and [[Jennifer Garner]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/jared-leto-returning-acting-dallas-386911|title=Jared Leto Returning to Acting with 'Dallas Buyer's Club'|work=[[The Hollywood Reporter]]|last=Kit|first=Borys|date=November 6, 2012|accessdate=November 27, 2012}}</ref> The film is based on the true-life tale of [[Ron Woodroof]], a Texas electrician diagnosed with AIDS and given 30 days to live, who began smuggling [[alternative medicine]] into the United States to help himself and other AIDS patients.

The film was released in 2013 to critical acclaim, earning Matthew McConaughey the [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actor – Motion Picture Drama|Golden Globe Award for Best Actor]] and Jared Leto a [[Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actor – Motion Picture|Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actor]]. The film was nominated for six [[Academy Awards]], including [[Academy Award for Best Picture|Best Picture]] and [[Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay|Best Original Screenplay]], and won the awards for [[Academy Award for Best Actor|Best Actor]] for McConaughey, and [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor|Best Supporting Actor]] for Leto, repeating the Golden Globes. Vallée also received a nomination for the [[Academy Award for Best Film Editing]] under his alias, John Mac McMurphy.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://plus.lapresse.ca/screens/4486-34d4-530b7b82-a74c-2426ac1c606a%257CEnsCKfjs6GGN|title=Jean-Marc Vallée aux Oscars alias John Mac McMurphy|work=[[La Presse (Canadian newspaper)|La Presse]]|last=Lussier|first=Marc-André|date=February 25, 2014|accessdate=June 3, 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151003071448/http://plus.lapresse.ca/screens/4486-34d4-530b7b82-a74c-2426ac1c606a%257CEnsCKfjs6GGN|archive-date=October 3, 2015|url-status=dead|df=mdy-all}}</ref>

===''Wild''===
Vallée's film ''[[Wild (2014 film)|Wild]]'', starring [[Reese Witherspoon]], premiered on August 29, 2014 at the [[Telluride Film Festival]], and was also featured at the [[Toronto International Film Festival]] on September 8 and the [[San Diego Film Festival]] on September 24.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://variety.com/2014/film/news/reese-witherspoon-wild-san-diego-film-festival-1201294101/|title=Reese Witherspoon's 'Wild' to Open San Diego Film Festival (EXCLUSIVE)|last=McNary|first=Dave|work=Variety|date=August 29, 2014|accessdate=August 29, 2014}}</ref> It was released in North America on December 5, 2014.<ref>{{cite web|last=Sperling|first=Nicole|title=Reese Witherspoon-starrer 'Wild' gets a release date|url=http://insidemovies.ew.com/2014/05/12/reese-witherspoon-wild-release-date|accessdate=May 13, 2014|work=Entertainment Weekly|date=May 12, 2014}}</ref>

Vallée's next film, ''[[Demolition (2015 film)|Demolition]]'' (2015), starred [[Jake Gyllenhaal]] and [[Naomi Watts]], and opened the [[Toronto International Film Festival]] in September 2015.<ref>{{cite web|title=Demolition - Gala Presentations|url=http://tiff.net/festivals/festival15/galapresentations/demolition|website=TIFF|publisher=Toronto International Film Festival|accessdate=20 August 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150815120729/http://tiff.net/festivals/festival15/galapresentations/demolition|archive-date=2015-08-15|url-status=dead}}</ref>

In May 2015, Vallée received the [[National Arts Centre]] Award, a companion award of the [[Governor General's Performing Arts Awards]], given to an artist in recognition of work of an extraordinary nature over the previous performance year.<ref>{{cite web|title=Jean-Marc Vallée biography|url=http://ggpaa.ca/award-recipients/2015/vallee,-jean-marc.aspx|website=Governor General's Performing Arts Awards|publisher=Governor General's Performing Arts Awards Foundation|accessdate=20 August 2015}}</ref> The film was nominated for two Academy Awards, Best Actress for Reese Witherspoon, and Best Supporting Actress for [[Laura Dern]].{{citation needed|date=March 2017}}

=== ''Big Little Lies'' ===
In 2017, he directed and executive-produced the acclaimed [[HBO]] miniseries ''[[Big Little Lies (TV series)|Big Little Lies]]'', winning a [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Directing for a Limited Series, Movie, or Dramatic Special]].

==Personal life==
Vallée has two children, Alex and Émilie.<ref>{{cite web|title=Jean-Marc Vallée Biography|url=https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0885249/bio|website=IMDB}}</ref>
