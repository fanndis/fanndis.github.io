'''Marina Nicole Sturdza''' (25 April 1944– 24 October 2017) was a former [[Romania]]n princess and [[humanitarian]] activist.

==Early life==
Princess Marina Nicole Sturdza was born in [[Romania]] on 25 April 1944, in [[Brasov]], [[Transylvania]]. Both of her parents came from Romanian aristocracy;<ref name="Fitterman1">{{cite news |last1=Fitterman |first1=Lisa |title=Romanian princess reinvented herself |url=http://v1.theglobeandmail.com/servlet/story/LAC.20171107.OBSTURDZA/BDAStory/BDA/deaths/?pageRequested=all |accessdate=31 August 2019 |work=Globe and Mail |date=7 November 2017}}</ref> her birth father was [[Prince]] Ion Sturdza (of the [[aristocrat]]ic [[Sturdza family]], who had been the ruling family of [[Moldavia]]) and her mother Princess Ioana [[Soutzos family|Soutzo]]. They divorced when the princess Marina was very young.<ref name="Roberts">{{cite news |last1=Roberts |first1=Sam |title=Marina Sturdza, Romanian princess and humanitarian, dies at 73 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/24/obituaries/marina-sturdza-dead-romanian-princess-and-humanitarian.html |accessdate=31 August 2019 |work=The New York Times |date=24 October 2017}}</ref>

One year after [[Michael I of Romania#Forced abdication|King Michael of Romania's abdication]] in 1947, Sturdza's parents fled the country under [[pseudonym]]s. Marina's grandmother (with whom she stayed for a while) put her on a train to [[Switzerland]]. She was sedated so as not to speak Romanian and thus betray her national origin meeting a diplomat by the Swiss border, where she was reunited with her parents in [[Zürich]]. The family later moved to Canada;<ref name="Roberts"/> her mother and stepfather worked on a piggery in [[Saskatchewan]],<ref>{{cite book |last1=MacLaren |first1=Sherrill |title=Invisible Power |date=1992 |publisher=McClelland-Bantam |isbn=9780770425265 |page=31 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Lor4zbh3JOwC |accessdate=31 August 2019}}</ref> where Marina resided in a single-room schoolhouse. The family moved to [[Toronto]], Canada, eight months later.<ref name="Roberts"/>

==Journalism, activism and awards==
Sturdza became a fashion and culture as columnist for two American newspapers, ''[[The Globe and Mail]]'' and ''[[The Toronto Star]]'' and briefly worked for [[Oscar de la Renta]]. Later on, she became a marketing manager for [[United Nations Children's Fund|UNICEF]] greeting cards in [[Geneva]], Switzerland. In the 1990s, Sturdza returned to Romania alongside interior designer [[Ioana Ertegun]] (wife of the president of [[Atlantic Records]], [[Ahmet Ertegun]]). Sturdza supported a number of child welfare and [[palliative care]] programs for children in her home country. 

She was president of [[strategic planning]] agencies Marina Sturdza Enterprises and Summits International, which specialised in Eastern Europe. She was patron of a number of charities and humanitarian causes concerning the region, including [[Hope and Homes for Children]]. She was also on the board of [[Pro Patrimonio]], the Romanian [[national trust]]. In 2005, she received the Women of Achievement Humanitarian Award from the [[European Union]].<ref name="Roberts"/>

==Personal life==
In 1970, Sturdza married journalist [[Denis Harvey]]; the couple later divorced.<ref name="Roberts"/> Sturdza was [[multilingual]] and spoke at least 6 different languages.<ref name="Fitterman1"/>

Sturdza died in New York City on 22 October 2017 from [[cancer]]-related causes.<ref name="Fitterman">{{cite news |last1=Fitterman |first1=Lisa |title=Romanian princess Marina Sturdza went from journalist to fashion exec to humanitarian |url=https://www.theglobeandmail.com/news/world/marina-sturdza-went-from-romanian-princess-to-journalist-to-humanitarian/article36851392/ |work=Globe and Mail |accessdate=31 August 2019 |date=6 November 2017}}</ref>

She has one stepdaughter and two step-grandchildren.<ref name="Roberts"/>
