'''Angelo Rizzoli''', [[Order of Merit for Labour|OML]] ({{IPA-it|ˈandʒelo ritˈtsɔːli}}; 31 October 1889 – 24 September 1970) was an Italian publisher and film producer.

==Early life==
Rizzoli was born in Milan on 31 October 1889.<ref name=pir>{{cite journal|author=Deirdre Pirro|title=Angelo Rizzoli. From magazines to movies|journal=The Florentine|date=29 September 2011|issue=149|url=http://www.theflorentine.net/articles/article-view.asp?issuetocId=7171|accessdate=24 July 2015}}</ref> Orphaned at a young age and raised in poverty, he rose to prosperity. He apprenticed in the printer trade and later became an entrepreneur in his twenties.<ref name=gran>{{cite web|url=http://www.truncellito.com/2000/angelo-rizzoli|title=Le grandi famiglie: i Rizzoli|work=Mariateresa Truncellito|year=2000|accessdate=10 January 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150206164115/http://www.truncellito.com/2000/angelo-rizzoli|archive-date=6 February 2015|url-status=dead}}</ref>

==Career==
In 1927, Rizzoli founded company ''A. Rizzoli & Co.'' (later [[RCS MediaGroup]]). In 1927 Rizzoli acquired ''[[Novella 2000|Novella]]'' magazine, a bi-weekly primarily for women that reached a circulation of 130,000 copies,<ref name=pir/> from ''[[Mondadori]]'' an independent publisher who specialized in books and magazines.<ref name="google1">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=68nbtqst-CsC&q=Novella+of+Mondadori+Rizzoli&pg=PA242|title=European Media Governance: National and Regional Dimensions|isbn=9781841501925|accessdate=10 January 2014|last1=Terzis|first1=Georgios|year=2007}}</ref> After his initial purchase, he added several new publications including ''Annabella'', ''[[Bertoldo]]'', ''[[Candido (magazine)|Candido]]'', ''Omnibus'', and ''Oggi e L'Europeo''.<ref name=gran/> In 1949 he began publishing books including both classics and popular novels. He purchased ''Cartiera di Lama di Reno'' in 1954, the foundation for what would become an Italian publishing empire.<ref name="incampania1">{{cite web|url=http://www.incampania.com/en/beniculturali.cfm?s=5&Menu_ID=211&Sub_ID=216&Info_ID=4174 |title=Museo Angelo Rizzoli – Museums – Ischia – Napoli |work=InCampania |accessdate=10 January 2014 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20130921064919/http://www.incampania.com/en/beniculturali.cfm?s=5&Menu_ID=211&Sub_ID=216&Info_ID=4174 |archivedate=21 September 2013 }}</ref> His operations were moved in 1960 to a complex on Via Civitavecchia in Milan.

He was amongst the first producers of daily newspapers within the relatively newly established nation<ref>{{cite web|url=https://docs.google.com/viewer?a=v&q=cache:_bQZ26kIQzAJ:www.ferpi.it/admin/resources/2765+italy+a+young+nation&hl=en&gl=uk&pid=bl&srcid=ADGEEShGhOX_27RFn7ePRUBUM4IEtOkFjMyX7SbzNv6tRqq5KhPZNRELYFEWhTBMiiS2zXK9IMS3TUwmLQQ594bgrm41f7TJKGqCH0wd1Sj3YnDrdakFXBZhHZfH0gJ0-FFUeYmsCvVq&sig=AHIEtbRYCTs0yOEx6w-R-YjztGIyZp4adw|title=Google Drive Viewer|accessdate=10 January 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://hcl.harvard.edu/hfa/films/2011julsep/risorgimento.html|title=Viva l'Italia! The Risorgimento on Screen|work=Harvard Film Archive|date=September 2011|accessdate=10 January 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140106235250/http://hcl.harvard.edu/hfa/films/2011julsep/risorgimento.html|archive-date=6 January 2014|url-status=dead}}</ref> of Italy.<ref name="google1"/>

Active in film, he produced [[Federico Fellini]]'s ''[[La Dolce Vita]]'' (1960) and ''[[8½]]'' (1963), as well as French films such as ''[[Une Parisienne]]'' (1957). In 1964 Rizzoli opened the original Rizzoli International Bookstore<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rizzoliusa.com/bookstore.html|title=Rizzoli Bookstore|work=Rizzoli USA|date=21 November 2013|accessdate=10 January 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100829021116/http://www.rizzoliusa.com/bookstore.html|archive-date=29 August 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref> in New York City at [[712 Fifth Avenue]], designed by architect [[Ferdinand Gottlieb]]. The bookstore was featured in various Hollywood films, most notably, [[Woody Allen]]'s ''[[Manhattan (film)|Manhattan]]'' and ''[[Falling in Love (1984 film)|Falling in Love]]'' with [[Robert De Niro]] and [[Meryl Streep]]. He also produced the controversial documentary film ''[[Africa Addio]]''.

A museum about Rizzoli's life and career is located at Villa Arbusto within the guest house of that place,<ref>[http://sit.provincia.napoli.it/museodiffuso/mdb2Viewmuse.asp?key=0166 ] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120318203720/http://sit.provincia.napoli.it/museodiffuso/mdb2Viewmuse.asp?key=0166 |date=18 March 2012 }}</ref> at Lacco Ameno. Within this are held 500 photographic-records or evidence of his activities taken on-set during production. The museum also holds the [[Pithekoussai]]<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.penn.museum/documents/publications/expedition/PDFs/8-4/Pithekoussai.pdf|title=Pithekoussai: Oldest Greek Colony in the West |author=Giorgio Buchner|work=Penn museum|accessdate=9 February 2014}}</ref> Archaeological Museum.<ref name="incampania1"/>

==Personal life==
Rizzoli married Anna Marzorati with whom he had two children, Andrea and Giuseppina. Rizzoli died aged 81 years of age.
