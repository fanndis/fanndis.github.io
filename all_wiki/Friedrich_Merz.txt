'''Friedrich Merz''' (born 11 November 1955) is a German lawyer and politician. A member of the [[Christian Democratic Union of Germany|Christian Democratic Union]] (CDU), he served as a [[Member of the European Parliament]] (MEP) from 1989 to 1994 and was elected to the [[Bundestag]] from 1994 until 2009, where he chaired the [[CDU/CSU]] parliamentary group from 2000 to 2002. Merz was a candidate in the [[2018 Christian Democratic Union of Germany leadership election|2018 CDU leadership election]], placing second; he is running again in the [[2021 Christian Democratic Union of Germany leadership election|2021 leadership election]].<ref name="suedd">{{Cite news|url=https://www.sueddeutsche.de/politik/merkel-cdu-bundesregierung-1.4189532|title=Die große Zeitenwende ist eine Chance für die CDU|last=Berlin|first=Kommentar von Stefan Braun|date=2018|work=sueddeutsche.de|access-date=2018-10-29|language=de|issn=0174-4917}}</ref>

Merz joined the CDU's youth wing in 1972 and is reputed to be a member of the Andean Pact, a powerful network formed by members of the CDU youth wing in 1979 during a trip to the [[Andes]]. After finishing law school in 1985 he worked as a judge and [[corporate lawyer]] before entering full-time politics in 1989 when he was elected to the [[European Parliament]]. After serving one term he was elected to the [[Bundestag]], where he established himself as the leading financial policy expert in the CDU. He was elected chairman of the [[CDU/CSU]] group in the same year as [[Angela Merkel]] was elected chairwoman of the CDU, and at the time they were rivals for the leadership of the party.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2018/10/31/merkel-rival-friedrich-merz-emerges-surprise-early-frontrunner/|title=Merkel rival Friedrich Merz emerges as surprise early frontrunner to succeed chancellor|first=Justin|last=Huggler|date=31 October 2018|via=www.telegraph.co.uk|newspaper=The Telegraph}}</ref> In 2002, he stepped down as leader of the opposition in favour of Merkel and gradually withdrew from politics, focusing on his legal career and leaving parliament entirely in 2009. In 2004 he became a senior counsel with [[Mayer Brown]], where he has focused on mergers and acquisitions, banking and finance, and compliance. He has served on the boards of numerous companies. In 2018 he announced his return to politics.

Merz has described himself as socially conservative and economically liberal, and is seen as a representative of the conservative and pro-business wings of the CDU.<ref name="auto1">{{cite news|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-germany-politics/conservative-contenders-vie-to-overturn-merkels-centrism-idUSKCN1N51CT|title=Conservative contenders vie to overturn Merkel's centrism|first=Thomas|last=Escritt|newspaper=Reuters|date=2018-10-31}}</ref> He has been chairman of the [[Atlantik-Brücke]] association which promotes German-American understanding and [[Atlanticism]], and is a staunch supporter of the [[European Union]] and [[NATO]], having described himself as "a truly convinced European, a convinced transatlanticist."<ref name="auto2">{{cite news|url=https://www.welt.de/politik/deutschland/article183054050/Merz-will-CDU-Chef-werden-Wir-brauchen-Aufbruch-und-Erneuerung-keinen-Umsturz.html|title=Merz will CDU-Chef werden: "Wir brauchen Aufbruch und Erneuerung, keinen Umsturz"|last=WELT|date=31 October 2018|via=www.welt.de|newspaper=Die Welt}}</ref> Merz advocates a closer union and "an army for Europe."<ref name="handelsblatt2018" />

Merz is [[Roman Catholic]] and of French [[Huguenots|Huguenot]] descent on his mother's side. He is married to the judge Charlotte Merz, and they have three children. The work of Merz as a corporate lawyer has made him a multimillionaire. He is also a licensed [[private pilot]] and owns two airplanes.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.manager-magazin.de/politik/deutschland/ex-cdu-star-friedrich-merz-und-der-karriereknick-a-970377.html|title=Ex-CDU-Star Friedrich Merz und der Karriereknick - manager magazin}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.stern.de/politik/deutschland/hat-friedrich-merz-wirklich-zwei-flugzeuge--8467946.html|title=Hat Friedrich Merz wirklich zwei Flugzeuge?|date=November 28, 2018|website=stern.de}}</ref>

== Background and early life ==
Friedrich Merz was born in [[Brilon]] in the state of [[North Rhine-Westphalia]] in then-[[West Germany]] to [[Roman Catholic]] parents Joachim Merz and Paula née Sauvigny.<ref name="faz18">{{cite news|url=https://www.faz.net/1.5867546|title=Friedrich Merz: Dieser Kandidat passt nicht auf einen Bierdeckel|first1=Eckart|last1=Lohse|last2=Berlin|via=www.faz.net|newspaper=Faz.net}}</ref> His father was a judge and a CDU member until he left the party in 2007.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.welt.de/politik/article698654/Friedrich-Merz-Vater-verlaesst-die-CDU-im-Groll.html|title=Parteien: Friedrich Merz' Vater verlässt die CDU im Groll|date=February 12, 2007|via=www.welt.de|newspaper=Die Welt}}</ref> The Sauvigny family was a prominent [[patrician (post-Roman Europe)|patrician]] family in Brilon, of French [[Huguenots|Huguenot]] ancestry; his grandfather Josef Paul Sauvigny was a lawyer and served as mayor of Brilon from 1917 to 1937.<ref>"Sauvigny," ''[[Deutsches Geschlechterbuch]]'', Vol. 38</ref><ref>Claus Jacobi, ''Im Rad der Geschichte: Deutsche Verhältnisse'', p. 166, Herbig, 2002</ref><ref>"[http://www.munzinger.de/document/00000022889 Merz, Friedrich]," in Munzinger Online</ref>

After finishing his [[Abitur]] exam in 1975 Merz served his [[military service]] as a [[soldier]] with a [[self-propelled artillery]] unit of the [[German Army]]. From 1976 he studied [[law]] with a scholarship from the [[Konrad Adenauer Foundation]], first at the [[University of Bonn]], later at the [[University of Marburg]]. At Bonn he was a member of {{Interlanguage link multi|KDStV Bavaria Bonn|de}}, a Roman Catholic student fraternity founded in 1844 that is part of the ''[[Cartellverband]]''. After finishing law school in 1985, he became a [[judge]] in [[Saarbrücken]]. In 1986 he left his position as a judge in order to work as an in-house [[Lawyer|attorney-at-law]] at the German Chemical Industry Association in [[Bonn]] and [[Frankfurt]] from 1986 to 1989.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.atlantik-bruecke.org/die-atlantik-bruecke/vorstand/friedrich-merz/|title=Friedrich Merz - Atlantik-Brücke e.V.|work=Atlantik-Brücke e.V.|access-date=2018-10-30|language=de-DE|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181031052330/https://www.atlantik-bruecke.org/die-atlantik-bruecke/vorstand/friedrich-merz/|archive-date=2018-10-31|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Merz speaks German, French and English.<ref>[https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/people/m/merz-friedrich?tab=overview Friedrich Merz, Senior Counsel], Mayer Brown</ref>

== Political career prior to 2009 ==
In 1972, at the age of seventeen, he became a member of the CDU's youth wing, the [[Young Union]],<ref name="faz18" /> and he has been described by German media as a member of the "Andean Pact," a supposed network of influential CDU members formed by members of the Young Union during a trip to the South American [[Andes]] region in 1979.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.spiegel.de/politik/deutschland/union-anden-pakt-nimmt-friedrich-merz-auf-a-382963.html|title=Union: "Anden-Pakt" nimmt Friedrich Merz auf|first=Hajo|last=Schumacher|date=3 November 2005|via=Spiegel Online}}</ref> He became President of the Brilon branch of the Young Union in 1980.

=== Member of the European Parliament, 1989–1994 ===
Merz successfully ran as a candidate in the [[1989 European Parliament election]] and served one term as a [[Member of the European Parliament]] until 1994. He was a member of the [[Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs]] and of the parliament's delegation for relations with [[Malta]].

=== Member of the German Bundestag, 1994–2009 ===
From the [[1994 German federal election|1994 German elections]], he served as member of the [[Bundestag]] for his constituency, the [[Hochsauerland]]. In his first term, he was a member of the Finance Committee.

In October 1998 Merz became vice-chairman and in February 2000 Chairman of the CDU/[[Christian Social Union of Bavaria|CSU]] parliamentary group (alongside [[Michael Glos]]), succeeding [[Wolfgang Schäuble]]. In this capacity, he was the opposition leader in the Bundestag during [[Chancellor of Germany|Chancellor]] [[Gerhard Schröder]]'s first term.

Ahead of the [[2002 German federal election|2002 elections]], [[Edmund Stoiber]] included Merz in his [[shadow cabinet]] for the Christian Democrats’ campaign to unseat incumbent Schröder as chancellor. During the campaign, Merz served as Stoiber's expert for financial markets and the national budget.<ref>[http://www.spiegel.de/politik/deutschland/wahlkampf-stoiber-team-ohne-kompetenz-bei-den-staatsfinanzen-a-178384.html Wahlkampf: Stoiber-Team ohne Kompetenz bei den Staatsfinanzen] ''[[Spiegel Online]]'', January 22, 2002.</ref> After Stoiber's electoral defeat, [[Angela Merkel]] assumed the leadership of the parliamentary group; Merz again served as vice-chairman until 2004. From 2002 to 2004, he was also a member of the executive board of the CDU, again under the leadership of Merkel.

Between [[2005 German federal election|2005]] and 2009, Merz was a member of the Committee on Legal Affairs. By 2007, he announced he would not be running for political office in the [[2009 German federal election|2009 elections]].

Upon leaving politics, Merz has worked as a corporate lawyer. ==Return to politics==
After [[Angela Merkel]] announced her intention to step down as [[Leader of the Christian Democratic Union of Germany|Leader of the CDU party]], Merz announced he would run in the subsequent party [[2018 Christian Democratic Union of Germany leadership election|leadership election]] in December 2018.<ref name="suedd" /> His candidacy was promoted by the former CDU chairman and "crown prince" of the Kohl era, [[Wolfgang Schäuble]] (current President of the Bundestag, ranked second in federal precedence).<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.faz.net/1.5870575|title=Merkel-Nachfolge: Schäuble beförderte Merz' Kandidatur für CDU-Vorsitz|via=www.faz.net|newspaper=Faz.net}}</ref> In the second round of the [[2018 Christian Democratic Union of Germany leadership election|leadership election]], Merz was defeated by [[Annegret Kramp-Karrenbauer]]. On February 25, 2020, he announced his candidacy in the [[2021 Christian Democratic Union of Germany leadership election|CDU leadership election]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.morgenpost.de/politik/inland/article228535207/Friedrich-Merz-kandidiert-fuer-CDU-Parteivorsitz.html|title=Friedrich Merz kandidiert - und schießt gegen Laschet und Spahn|last=Berlin|first=Berliner Morgenpost-|date=2020-02-25|website=www.morgenpost.de|language=de-DE|access-date=2020-02-26}}</ref>

==Political positions==
Merz has focused on economic policy, foreign and security policy and family policy. He has described himself as socially conservative and economically liberal, and is seen as a representative of the conservative and pro-business wings of the CDU.<ref name="auto1"/> He has been chairman of the [[Atlantik-Brücke]] association which promotes German-American understanding and [[Atlanticism]], and is a staunch supporter of the [[European Union]] and [[NATO]]. As a young politician in the 1970s and 1980s, he was a staunch supporter of [[anticommunism]], the dominant state [[doctrine]] of West Germany and a core tenet of the CDU. In 2018, he described himself as "a truly convinced European, a convinced transatlanticist" and said that "I stand for a cosmopolitan Germany whose roots lie in [[Christian ethics]] and the European Enlightenment and whose most important political allies are the democracies of [[Western Bloc|the West]]. I gladly use this expression again: The democracies of the West."<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.blick.ch/news/ausland/wird-er-merkels-nachfolger-friedrich-merz-kandidiert-fuer-den-cdu-parteivorsitz-id9051960.html|title=Wer ist Friedrich Merz? Merkels Nachfolger für den CDU-Parteivorsitz|last=Blick|website=www.blick.ch|date=2018-10-31}}</ref><ref name="auto2"/> He especially advocates closer relations between Germany and France. Merz has criticized Donald Trump more harshly than Angela Merkel did and has especially criticized Trump's trade war against Europe.<ref>{{cite web|date=2018-11-01|title=Merkel's Would-Be Successor Is a Real Conservative|url=https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2018-11-01/merkel-s-would-be-successor-merz-is-a-real-german-conservative|access-date=8 December 2018|website=Bloomberg.com}}</ref> In 2018, he co-authored an article in defence of the European project, which among other things called for "an army for Europe."<ref name="handelsblatt2018">{{cite web|url=https://www.handelsblatt.com/today/opinion/time-to-wake-up-we-are-deeply-concerned-about-the-future-of-europe-and-germany/23583722.html|title=Time to wake up: We are deeply concerned about the future of Europe and Germany|website=www.handelsblatt.com|access-date=8 December 2018}}</ref> In July 2018, Merz rejected the Ludwig Erhard Prize, citing objections to publications by the chairman of the [[Ludwig Erhard Foundation]], Roland Tichy, considered by some to be on the extreme right.<ref name="faz-aerger">{{cite news |url=https://www.faz.net/aktuell/wirtschaft/aerger-fuer-die-ludwig-erhard-stiftung-15694317.html |title=Ärger für die Ludwig-Erhard-Stiftung |first=Philip |last=Plickert |date=16 July 2018 |publisher=Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung}}</ref> In November 2018 Merz said, that the introduction of [[same-sex marriage in Germany]] is correct.<ref>[https://www.wallstreet-online.de/nachricht/11013733-friedrich-merz-merz-haelt-einfuehrung-ehe-alle-richtig Wallstreet Online: Merz hält Einführung der Ehe für alle for richtig] (german)], 8 December 2018</ref>

==Personal life==
Friedrich Merz is married to the [[judge]] Charlotte Merz. He has three children and resides in [[Arnsberg]] in the [[Sauerland]] region. In 2005, the couple established the Friedrich und Charlotte Merz Stiftung, a foundation supporting projects in the education sector.<ref>[http://www.merz-stiftung.de/index.php?id=4 About] Friedrich und Charlotte Merz Stiftung.</ref> 

On 17 March 2020 Merz was tested positive for [[Coronavirus disease 2019|COVID-19]].<ref>[https://www.welt.de/politik/deutschland/article206609441/Coronavirus-Friedrich-Merz-infiziert.html Welt.de: Friedrich Merz mit Coronavirus infiziert], March 17, 2020</ref>
