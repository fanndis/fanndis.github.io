'''Frances Louise McDormand''' (born '''Cynthia Ann Smith''', June 23, 1957) is an American actress. She is the recipient of [[list of awards and nominations received by Frances McDormand|numerous accolades]], including two [[Academy Awards]], two [[Primetime Emmy Award]]s, and a [[Tony Award]], making her one of the few performers to achieve the "[[Triple Crown of Acting]]." One of the most acclaimed actresses of her generation, she is known for her portrayals of unique, quirky, and headstrong female characters.

McDormand was educated at [[Bethany College (West Virginia)|Bethany College]] and [[Yale University]]. She has starred in a number of films by the [[Coen brothers]], including ''[[Blood Simple]]'' (1984), ''[[Raising Arizona]]'' (1987), ''[[Fargo (film)|Fargo]]'' (1996), ''[[The Man Who Wasn't There (2001 film)|The Man Who Wasn't There]]'' (2001), ''[[Burn After Reading]]'' (2008), and ''[[Hail, Caesar!]]'' (2016). For playing a police chief in ''Fargo'', she won the [[Academy Award for Best Actress]]. Her other film roles include ''[[Mississippi Burning]]'' (1988), ''[[Almost Famous]]'' (2000), and ''[[North Country (film)|North Country]]'' (2005), all of which earned her nominations for the [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress]]. In 2017, she starred as a hardened woman seeking justice for her daughter's murder in the crime-drama film ''[[Three Billboards Outside Ebbing, Missouri]]'', which won her a second Academy Award for Best Actress.

McDormand made her Broadway debut in a 1984 revival of the drama ''[[Awake and Sing!]]'', and received a nomination for the [[Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play]] for her acclaimed performance as [[Stella Kowalski]] in a 1988 revival of ''[[A Streetcar Named Desire]]''. She returned to Broadway in 2008 with a revival of ''[[The Country Girl (1950 play)|The Country Girl]]'', leading to a [[Drama Desk Award]] nomination for [[Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actress in a Play|Outstanding Actress in a Play]]. In 2011, she won the Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play for playing a troubled single mother in ''[[Good People (play)|Good People]]''. On television, McDormand played the titular protagonist in the [[HBO]] miniseries ''[[Olive Kitteridge (miniseries)|Olive Kitteridge]]'' (2014), which won her the [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Limited Series or Movie]].

==Early life==
McDormand was born Cynthia Ann Smith in [[Gibson City, Illinois]].<ref name="NYT">{{cite web | url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/03/magazine/frances-mcdormand-difficult-women-career-surge.html | title=Frances McDormand's Difficult Women | last=Kisner | first=Jordan | date=October 3, 2017 | work=[[The New York Times]] | accessdate=January 17, 2018 | archive-date=February 13, 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180213135712/https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/03/magazine/frances-mcdormand-difficult-women-career-surge.html | url-status=live }}</ref> She was adopted at one and a half years of age by Noreen (Nickelson) and Vernon McDormand and renamed Frances Louise McDormand.<ref name="NYT"/> Her adoptive mother was a nurse and receptionist while her adoptive father was a [[Disciples of Christ]] pastor; both were originally from Canada.<ref name="adoptive-mother">{{cite news | url=https://www.theage.com.au/entertainment/movies/naked-ambition-20031025-gdwlg8.html | title=Naked ambition | work=[[The Age]] | location=Melbourne, Australia | date=October 25, 2003 | accessdate=May 25, 2019 | archive-date=December 29, 2019 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191229132305/https://www.theage.com.au/entertainment/movies/naked-ambition-20031025-gdwlg8.html | url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/67846899|title=Rev Vernon Weir McDormand (1922 - 2011)|website=www.findagrave.com|access-date=February 20, 2018|archive-date=March 10, 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180310123434/https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/67846899|url-status=live}}</ref> McDormand has said that her biological mother, to whom she proudly referred, along with herself, as "[[white trash]]," may have been one of the parishioners at Vernon's church.<ref name="NYT"/><ref name="adoptive-mother" /> She has a sister, Dorothy A. "Dot" McDormand, who is an ordained Disciples of Christ minister and chaplain,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.disciples.org/dns/releases97/97b21.htm |title=Disciples "PK" wins best actress' award. Disciples News Service Release. 31 March 2007 |publisher=Disciples.org |accessdate=July 29, 2010 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080302090821/http://www.disciples.org/dns/releases97/97b21.htm |archivedate=March 2, 2008 |df=mdy-all}}</ref> as well as another sibling, both of whom were adopted by the McDormands, who had no biological children.

Because her father specialized in restoring congregations,<ref name="adoptive-mother" /> he frequently moved their family, and they lived in several small towns in [[Illinois]], [[Georgia (U.S. state)|Georgia]], [[Kentucky]], and [[Tennessee]],<ref>{{cite news |url=http://film.guardian.co.uk/oscars2001/storynav/0,7677,437576,00.html |title=I'd love to play a psycho killer. film.guardian.co.uk. 26 January 2001 |work=The Guardian |date=February 14, 2001 |accessdate=July 29, 2010 |location=London |archive-date=December 2, 2008 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081202064726/http://film.guardian.co.uk/oscars2001/storynav/0,7677,437576,00.html |url-status=live }}</ref> before settling in [[Monessen, Pennsylvania]], where McDormand graduated from [[Monessen High School]] in 1975. She attended [[Bethany College (West Virginia)|Bethany College]] in [[West Virginia]], earning a [[Bachelor of Arts]] degree in theater in 1979. In 1982, she earned a [[Master of Fine Arts]] degree from the [[Yale School of Drama]]. She was a roommate of actress [[Holly Hunter]] while living in New York City.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://docslide.net/documents/newsday-fast-chat-holly-hunter.html|title=Fast Chat: Holly Hunter|publisher=Newsday|date=July 13, 2008|accessdate=August 13, 2018|archive-date=August 13, 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180813210401/https://docslide.net/documents/newsday-fast-chat-holly-hunter.html|url-status=live}}</ref>

==Career==
McDormand's first professional acting job was in [[Derek Walcott]]'s play ''In a Fine Castle'' also known as ''The Last Carnival'', which was funded by the [[MacArthur Foundation]] and performed in [[Trinidad]]. In 1984 she made her film debut in ''[[Blood Simple]]'', the first film by the [[Coen brothers]]. In 1987, she appeared as wacky friend Dot in ''[[Raising Arizona]]'', starring [[Holly Hunter]] and [[Nicolas Cage]]. In addition to her early film roles, McDormand played Connie Chapman in the fifth season of the television police drama ''[[Hill Street Blues]]''. In 1988, she played Stella Kowalski in a stage production of [[Tennessee Williams]]' ''[[A Streetcar Named Desire]]'', for which she was nominated for a [[Tony Award]]. McDormand is an associate member of the [[experimental theater]] company [[The Wooster Group]]. In 2002, "the game and talented" McDormand performed as Oenone in the Wooster Group's production of an "exhilarating dissection" of Racine's tragedy ''[[Phèdre]]'' entitled ''To You, the Birdie!'', at Ann's Warehouse in Brooklyn, New York.<ref>Ben Brantley (2002). "Theater Review: Racine's Pale Queen, Struggling With Racket Sports". ''The New York Times''. February 19, 2002.</ref>

After appearing in several theatrical and television roles during the 1980s, McDormand gradually gained renown and critical acclaim for her dramatic work in film.<ref name="ImagineFashionStoryMedium">{{cite news|url=http://www.imaginefashion.com/ladies-gentlemen/story-medium-frances-mcdormand|title=''Story Medium''|access-date=April 20, 2011|archive-date=May 14, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110514183637/http://www.imaginefashion.com/ladies-gentlemen/story-medium-frances-mcdormand|url-status=live}} Imagine Fashion. Interview with Frances McDormand (2011).</ref> In 1988, she was nominated for the [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress]] for ''[[Mississippi Burning]].'' Cast alongside [[Gene Hackman]] and [[Willem Dafoe]], McDormand was singled out for praise, with [[Sheila Benson]] in her review for the ''[[Los Angeles Times]]'' writing, "Hackman's mastery reaches a peak here, but McDormand soars right with him. And since she is the film's sole voice of morality, it's right that she is so memorable."<ref>{{cite web|url=https://articles.latimes.com/1988-12-18/entertainment/ca-766_1_mississippi-burning|title=RCritic's Notebook: Some 'Burning' Questions|first=Sheila|last=Benson|date=December 18, 1988|work=Los Angeles Times|publisher=Los Angeles Times|accessdate=April 30, 2016|archive-date=June 1, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160601230046/http://articles.latimes.com/1988-12-18/entertainment/ca-766_1_mississippi-burning|url-status=live}}</ref> In 1996, she won her first [[Academy Award for Best Actress]] for her performance as pregnant police Chief Marge Gunderson in ''[[Fargo (film)|Fargo]]'', written and directed by the Coen brothers.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.theuncool.com/journalism/frances-mcdormand-interview-magazine/ |title=Cameron Crowe, Frances McDormand interview, ''Interview Magazine'', October 2000. |access-date=October 19, 2014 |archive-date=April 1, 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160401082914/http://www.theuncool.com/journalism/frances-mcdormand-interview-magazine/ |url-status=live }}</ref> Writing for the ''[[Chicago Sun-Times]]'', [[Roger Ebert]] called ''Fargo'' "one of the best films I've ever seen" and felt that McDormand "should have a lock on an Academy Award nomination with this performance, which is true in every individual moment, and yet slyly, quietly, over the top in its cumulative effect."<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.rogerebert.com/reviews/fargo-1996|title=Fargo|work=RogerEbert.com|publisher=Ebert Digital LLC|first=Roger|last=Ebert|authorlink=Roger Ebert|date=March 8, 1996|accessdate=March 20, 2018|archive-date=December 7, 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171207144410/https://www.rogerebert.com/reviews/fargo-1996|url-status=live}}</ref>

In 2000, she was nominated for the Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress and the [[Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Motion Picture|Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress in a Motion Picture]] for her portrayal of an overbearing mother in ''[[Almost Famous]].'' For her role in ''[[Wonder Boys (film)|Wonder Boys]]'' (2000), she won Best Supporting Actress from the [[Broadcast Film Critics Association]], the [[Florida Film Critics Circle]], and the [[Los Angeles Film Critics Association]]. In 2006, McDormand received her fourth nomination for the Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress for her performance in ''[[North Country (film)|North Country]]'' (2005). The same year she won an [[Independent Spirit Awards|Independent Spirit Award]] for her supporting role in [[Nicole Holofcener]]'s dark comedy ''[[Friends with Money]]'' (2006).<ref>{{cite web |url=http://filmindependent.org/spiritawards/sa_2007_winners_press_release.php |title="Little Miss Sunshine" pulls up to take the crown at 2007 Film Independent's Spirit Awards |work=Film Independent Spirit Awards |date=February 24, 2007 |accessdate=August 8, 2015|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070227155904/http://filmindependent.org/spiritawards/sa_2007_winners_press_release.php |archivedate=February 27, 2007}}</ref> She also voiced the role of the principal Melanie Upfoot in ''[[The Simpsons]]'' episode "[[Girls Just Want to Have Sums]]", which aired on April 30, 2006.

In 2008, McDormand starred in the films ''[[Burn After Reading]]'' and ''[[Miss Pettigrew Lives for a Day]]''. She then appeared in the action movie ''[[Transformers: Dark of the Moon]]'', playing the US government's National Intelligence Director, alongside her ''Burn After Reading'' co-star [[John Malkovich]]. She returned to the stage in the [[David Lindsay-Abaire]] play ''[[Good People (play)|Good People]]'', in a limited engagement on Broadway from February 8, 2011 to May 29, 2011.<ref>Jones, Kenneth.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/147422-Good-People-Play-of-Aspiration-and-Escape-With-Frances-McDormand-and-Tate-Donovan-Begins-on-Broadway " 'Good People', Play of Aspiration and Escape, With Frances McDormand and Tate Donovan, Begins on Broadway"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110213013810/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/147422-Good-People-Play-of-Aspiration-and-Escape-With-Frances-McDormand-and-Tate-Donovan-Begins-on-Broadway |date=February 13, 2011}} ''Playbill'', February 8, 2011</ref><ref>Jones, Kenneth.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/149046-Broadways-Good-People-Gets-Final-Extension-Shifting-Dates-of-Master-Class "Broadway's 'Good People' Gets Final Extension, Shifting Dates of 'Master Class{{'"}}] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110501011225/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/149046-Broadways-Good-People-Gets-Final-Extension-Shifting-Dates-of-Master-Class |date=May 1, 2011}} ''Playbill'', March 22, 2011</ref> Her performance earned her the [[Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play]].<ref>Jones, Kenneth and Gans, Andrew.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/150437-2011-Tony-Nominations-Announced-Book-of-Mormon-Earns-14-Nominations "2011 Tony Nominations Announced; 'Book of Mormon' Earns 14 Nominations"] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110914233343/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/150437-2011-Tony-Nominations-Announced-Book-of-Mormon-Earns-14-Nominations |date=September 14, 2011}} playbill.com, May 3, 2011</ref> In the animated film ''[[Madagascar 3: Europe's Most Wanted]]'' (2012), she voiced Captain Chantel Dubois and also sang a version of the French song "[[Non, je ne regrette rien]]".

McDormand starred in ''[[Promised Land (2012 film)|Promised Land]]'' with [[Matt Damon]], released in December 2012.<ref>{{cite news|last=Gerhardt|first=Tina|date=December 31, 2012|title=Matt Damon Exposes Fracking in Promised Land|url=http://www.progressive.org/matt-damon-exposes-fracking-in-promised-land|work=[[The Progressive]]|access-date=December 31, 2012|archive-date=August 26, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160826055310/http://www.progressive.org/matt-damon-exposes-fracking-in-promised-land|url-status=live}}</ref> In November 2014, [[HBO]] telecast a four-part mini-series based upon the series of short stories by [[Elizabeth Strout]], ''[[Olive Kitteridge (miniseries)|Olive Kitteridge]]'', co-produced by and starring McDormand.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2014/10/19/arts/frances-mcdormand-true-to-herself-in-hbos-olive-kitteridge.html|title=Frances McDormand, True to Herself in HBO's ''Olive Kitteridge''|first=Frank|last=Bruni|date=October 15, 2014|work=The New York Times|access-date=March 1, 2017|archive-date=March 8, 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180308203448/https://www.nytimes.com/2014/10/19/arts/frances-mcdormand-true-to-herself-in-hbos-olive-kitteridge.html|url-status=live}}</ref> The role won her the [[Primetime Emmy Award]] and [[Screen Actors Guild Award]] for Best Actress. With her Emmy win, McDormand became the 12th actress in history to achieve the "[[Triple Crown of Acting]]", for competitive Oscar, Emmy, and Tony Award wins in acting categories.

McDormand starred in the 2017 film ''[[Three Billboards Outside Ebbing, Missouri]]'', for which she won her second [[Academy Award for Best Actress]] (her statuette was stolen briefly following the awards ceremony),<ref>{{cite news |last=Melas |first=Chloe |author-link=Chloe Melas |title=Frances McDormand's Oscar stolen (and returned) |url=https://edition.cnn.com/2018/03/05/entertainment/frances-mcdormand-stolen-oscars/index.html |date=March 5, 2018 |accessdate=March 5, 2018 |newspaper=[[CNN]] |publisher=[[Turner Broadcasting System]] |archive-date=March 7, 2018 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180307115926/https://edition.cnn.com/2018/03/05/entertainment/frances-mcdormand-stolen-oscars/index.html |url-status=live }}</ref> the [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress in a Motion Picture – Drama]],<ref>{{cite news|last1=Grobar|first1=Matt|title='Three Billboards' Star Frances McDormand Expresses Gratitude For Tectonic Shift In Entertainment Industry|url=https://deadline.com/2018/01/frances-mcdormand-golden-globe-best-actress-drama-1202237657/|date=January 7, 2018|work=Deadline|accessdate=January 7, 2018|archive-date=January 8, 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180108070910/http://deadline.com/2018/01/frances-mcdormand-golden-globe-best-actress-drama-1202237657/|url-status=live}}</ref> and the [[Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by a Female Actor in a Leading Role]]. During that year's awards season, she received significant media attention for her feminist provoking speeches which came with the advent of the [[Time's Up (movement)|Time's Up]] and [[Me Too movement]]s.

In 2019, McDormand played [[God]] in ''[[Good Omens (TV series)|Good Omens]]''.

==Personal life==
McDormand has been married to director [[Coen brothers|Joel Coen]] since 1984. In 1995, they adopted a son from [[Paraguay]], Pedro McDormand Coen, when he was six months old.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2003/03/02/movies/the-prime-of-frances-mcdormand.html|title=The Prime Of Frances McDormand|first=Karen|last=Durbin|date=March 2, 2003|work=The New York Times|accessdate=July 21, 2017|archive-date=December 30, 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191230013046/https://www.nytimes.com/2003/03/02/movies/the-prime-of-frances-mcdormand.html|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.popsugar.com/node/44636563|title=Everything We Know About Frances McDormand and Joel Coen's Son, Pedro|last=Nesa|first=Kamrun|date=2018-03-11|website=POPSUGAR Celebrity|language=en-US|accessdate=2019-09-18}}</ref> They reside in New York City and also own a home in [[Bolinas, California]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.sfgate.com/bayarea/article/Going-coastal-Is-the-elusive-bohemian-enclave-2653492.php#item-85307-tbla-5|title=Going coastal / Is the elusive bohemian enclave of Bolinas getting a bit glossier?|last=Britt|first=Aaron|date=January 28, 2007|website=[[San Francisco Chronicle|SFGate]]|publisher=[[Hearst Corporation]]|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180221100105/https://www.sfgate.com/bayarea/article/Going-coastal-Is-the-elusive-bohemian-enclave-2653492.php#item-85307-tbla-5|archive-date=February 21, 2018|accessdate=March 5, 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2014/10/19/arts/frances-mcdormand-true-to-herself-in-hbos-olive-kitteridge.html|title=A Star Who Has No Time for Vanity|first=Frank|last=Bruni|date=October 15, 2014|work=The New York Times|accessdate=July 21, 2017|archive-date=March 8, 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180308203448/https://www.nytimes.com/2014/10/19/arts/frances-mcdormand-true-to-herself-in-hbos-olive-kitteridge.html|url-status=live}}</ref>
