The following is a list, ordered by length of reign, of the monarchs of the [[United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland]] (1927–present), the [[United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland]] (1801–1927), the [[Kingdom of Great Britain]] (1707–1801), the [[Kingdom of England]] (871–1707), the [[Kingdom of Scotland]] (878–1707), the [[Kingdom of Ireland]] (1542–1800), and the [[Principality of Wales]] (1216–1542).

Queen [[Elizabeth II]] became the longest-reigning British monarch on 9 September 2015 when she surpassed the reign of her great-great-grandmother [[Queen Victoria|Victoria]].<ref name=macleans>{{cite news |url=http://www.macleans.ca/society/palace-calculations-queen-elizabeth-ii-set-to-lap-victoria/ |title=Palace calculations: Queen Elizabeth II set to lap Victoria |date=30 December 2014 |accessdate=4 September 2015 |work=Maclean's |author=Patricia Treble}}</ref><ref name=royal>{{cite web|url=http://www.royal.gov.uk/ |title=Official Website of the British Monarchy|accessdate=5 September 2015|quote=On 9 September 2015, The Queen will become the longest reigning British Monarch, surpassing Queen Victoria.}}</ref> On 6 February 2017 she became the first British monarch to celebrate a [[Sapphire Jubilee of Elizabeth II|Sapphire Jubilee]], commemorating 65 years on the throne. She has now reigned for {{Age in years, months and days|year=1952 |month=2 |day=6 }}.

==Overall==
These are the ten longest-reigning monarchs in the British Isles for whom there is reliable recorded evidence.

The longest claim by a [[pretender]] was that of [[James Francis Edward Stuart]] (the "Old Pretender"), who was the [[Jacobite succession|Jacobite pretender]] to the thrones of England, Scotland and Ireland for 64 years, 3 months, and 16 days (17 September 1701 – 1 January 1766).

=== Elizabeth II: the longest-reigning monarch ===
On 9 September 2015 (at the age of {{age in years and days|1926|4|21|2015|9|9}}), [[Elizabeth II]] became the longest-reigning British monarch and the longest-reigning female monarch in world history.<ref>{{cite web|author=Warren Gaebel |url=http://warren.gaebel.ca/?p=22 |title=Longest Reigning British Monarch |publisher=Warren Gaebel |accessdate=2015-06-15}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.spectator.co.uk/features/9404472/elizabeth-is-about-to-become-britains-longest-reigning-queen-heres-how-shes-changed-monarchy/ |title=Elizabeth is about to become Britain’s longest-reigning queen. Here’s how she’s changed monarchy |publisher=The Spectator |date=2015-01-03 |accessdate=2015-03-08}}</ref> On 23 May 2016 (at the age of {{age in years and days|1926|4|21|2016|5|23}}), her reign surpassed the claimed reign of [[James Francis Edward Stuart]] (the "Old Pretender").<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.stewartsociety.org/history-of-the-stewarts.cfm?section=famous-stewarts&subcatid=19&histid=178|title=Famous Stewarts|publisher=www.stewartsociety.org|accessdate=May 20, 2016}}</ref><!--If James were an official monarch, his reign would have surpassed Victoria's--> On 13 October 2016 (at the age of {{age in years and days|1926|4|21|2016|10|13}}), she became the world's longest-reigning current monarch (and the world's [[List of current state leaders by date of assumption of office|longest-serving current head of state]]) after the death of [[Bhumibol Adulyadej|Bhumibol Adulyadej (Rama IX)]], [[King of Thailand]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-37643326|title=Thailand's King Bhumibol Adulyadej dies at 88|date=2016-10-13|newspaper=BBC News|language=en-GB|access-date=2016-10-13}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author=By PA &nbsp;Oct 13, 2016 |url=http://www.aol.co.uk/news/2016/10/13/queen-takes-over-longest-reign-mantle-after-thailands-king-bhumibol-dies/ |title=Queen takes over longest reign mantle after Thailand's King Bhumibol dies |publisher=Aol.co.uk |accessdate=2016-10-13}}</ref>

If she is still reigning on:

* 6 February 2022 (at age {{age in years and days|1926|4|21|2022|2|6}}), she will celebrate her [[Platinum Jubilee of Elizabeth II|platinum jubilee]], marking 70 years on the throne.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.cnn.com/2015/09/08/europe/arbiter-queen-elizabeth-longest-reign/|title=Queen Elizabeth II: The platinum monarch?|author=Victoria Arbiter|publisher=CNN|date=September 9, 2015|accessdate=May 20, 2016}}</ref>
* 27 May 2024 (at age {{age in years and days|1926|4|21|2024|5|27}}), she will be the longest-reigning monarch of any sovereign state, surpassing [[Louis XIV of France]], who reigned for 72 years, 110 days.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.newstatesman.com/politics/uk/2015/09/queen-elizabeth-ii-about-become-britains-longest-reigning-monarch-so-here-are|title=Queen Elizabeth II is about to become Britain's longest reigning monarch so here are some charts|publisher=New Statesman|last=Elledge|first=Jonn|date=9 September 2015}}</ref>

===Ireland===
{{See also|Monarchy of Ireland}}

The [[Monarchy_of_Ireland#Gaelic_kings_and_kingdoms|High King of Ireland (846–1198)]] was primarily a titular title (with the exception of [[Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair]] who was regarded as the first "King of Ireland"). The later [[Kingdom of Ireland]] (1542–1800) came into being under the [[Crown of Ireland Act 1542]], the [[long title]] of which was "An Act that the King of England, his Heirs and Successors, be Kings of Ireland". In 1801 the Irish crown became part of the [[United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland]].

==Principalities==
===Gwynedd===
{{See also|List of rulers of Gwynedd}}

The [[Kingdom of Gwynedd|Principality (or Kingdom) of Gwynedd]] (5th century–1216) was based in northwest [[Wales]], its rulers were repeatedly acclaimed as "[[King of the Britons]]" before losing their power in civil wars or Saxon and Norman invasions. In 1216 it was superseded by the title [[Principality of Wales]], although the new title was not first used until the 1240s.

===Wales===
{{See also|List of Princes of Wales}}

The [[Principality of Wales]] (1216–1542) was a client state of England for much of its history, except for brief periods when it was ''[[de facto]]'' independent under a Welsh [[Prince of Wales]] (see [[House of Aberffraw]]). From 1301 it was first used as a title of the English (and later British) heir apparent. The [[Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542]] formally incorporated all of Wales within the [[Kingdom of England]].
