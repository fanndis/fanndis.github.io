'''Herlinde Koelbl''' (born 31 October 1939) is a [[Germany|German]] [[Photography|photographic]] artist, [[author]] and [[documentary film]]er.

Her comprehensive work is characterized above all by long-term photographic projects, often complemented by in-depth interviews. She is particularly interested in creating portraits of milieus and people. Herlinde Koelbl has received a number of awards for her photographic work, for example the [[Dr. Erich Salomon Award|Dr Erich Salomon Prize]] in 2001.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.dgph.de/preise/salomon|title=Dr.-Erich-Salomon-Preis der DGPh {{!}} Deutsche Gesellschaft für Photographie e.V.|website=www.dgph.de|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref> Since 2009, she has regularly worked as an author and photographer for [[Die Zeit|ZEIT]] magazine,<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.zeit.de/autoren/K/Herlinde_Koelbl|title=Herlinde Koelbl|work=ZEIT ONLINE|access-date=2019-08-21|language=de}}</ref> in the column “What saved me”.

== Biography ==
Herlinde Koelbl was born in [[Lindau]] on [[Lake Constance]], Germany, 1939, and grew up there. She studied fashion design in Munich and worked in the field, while becoming a mother of four. In 1975, she discovered her love for photography and taught herself all the necessary techniques.

She taught and gave lectures at the [[Parsons School of Design|Parsons School]] in New York, the [[University of Shanghai]], China, the [[Sydney College of the Arts|College of the Arts]], Sydney, the [[Höhere Graphische Bundes-Lehr- und Versuchsanstalt|Art School for Photography]], Vienna, and [[Rencontres d'Arles|Rencontres Internationales de la Photographie]], Arles.

Her works are in several private and permanent collections, such as  the [[Museum of Fine Arts, Houston|Museum of Fine Arts]], Houston, [[Bibliothèque nationale de France|Bibliothèque Nationale]], Paris, [[Museum Ludwig]], Cologne, [[Huis Marseille, Museum for Photography|huis Marseille]], [[stichting voor fotografie]], Amsterdam, and [[Jewish Museum Frankfurt|Jewish Museum]], Frankfurt a.M.

Koelbl lives in Munich and Berlin. She is an honorary member of the [[Deutsche Gesellschaft für Photography|DGPh]], BFF hall of fame.

== Career ==
In 1976 she started working as a freelance photographer, for newspapers like [[The New York Times]],<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1990/06/10/travel/what-s-doing-in-zurich.html|title=What's Doing In; Zurich|last=Hofmann|first=Paul|date=1990-06-10|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-08-21|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1995/11/19/travel/l-munich-memorial-046612.html|title=Munich Memorial|date=1995-11-19|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-08-21|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> [[Stern (magazine)|Stern]], [[Die Zeit]] and others. Already 1980 she published her first photobook ''The German Living Room''. She created her typical working approach by photographing methodically a whole series of pictures, displaying a broad spectre of society. Her first internationally noticed success was the photographic book ''Jewish Portraits'' in 1989. She photographed and talked to 80 German-speaking Jews, who survived the [[The Holocaust|Shoa]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://forward.com/schmooze/128926/old-jews-not-telling-jokes/|title=Old Jews Not Telling Jokes|last=Ivry|first=Benjamin|website=The Forward|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref> With this book the established her personal style, which she kept in most of the books that followed. She not only took portraits, but also interviewed the portrayed and added large interviews in the book. ''Traces of Power'' may be her best-known work so far. She photographed, filmed and interviewed 15 personalities from politics and business from 1991 to 1998, among them Chancellor [[Angela Merkel]], ex-Chancellor [[Gerhard Schröder]] and ex-Foreign Minister [[Joschka Fischer]]. The project was published in 1999, the documentary film with the same title was awarded the [[Deutscher Kritikerpreis]]<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.kritikerverband.de/Die_Preistraeger.htm|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090307002628/http://www.kritikerverband.de/Die_Preistraeger.htm|url-status=dead|archive-date=2009-03-07|title=Verband der deutschen Kritiker e.V.|date=2009-03-07|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref> and was nominated for the [[Grimme-Preis|Grimme Preis]]. The exhibition was shown at numerous museums, among them the [[Deutsches Historisches Museum]] in Berlin,<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.dhm.de/archiv/ausstellungen/spuren_der_macht/|title=Spuren der Macht|website=www.dhm.de|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref> the [[Haus der Kunst]] in Munich and the [[Haus der Geschichte]] in Bonn,<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.hdg.de/haus-der-geschichte/ausstellungen/spuren-der-macht-die-verwandlung-des-menschen-durch-das-amt/|title=Spuren der Macht|last=|first=|date=2000|website=Haus der Geschichte|access-date=}}</ref> as well as at Art Frankfurt 2002. Chancellor Angela Merkel gave the opening speech at the premiere of the show at the Haus der Kunst in Munich.

Herlinde Koelbl published more than 20 books, several documentary films and was awarded numerous prizes. 2009 her first large retrospective was shown at the [[Martin-Gropius-Bau|Martin Gropius Bau]] in Berlin.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://artdaily.com/news/32135/First-Exhibition-Covering-Entire-Oeuvre-of-Herlinde-Koelbl-Opens-in-Berlin|title=First Exhibition Covering Entire Oeuvre of Herlinde Koelbl Opens in Berlin|website=artdaily.com|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref>
