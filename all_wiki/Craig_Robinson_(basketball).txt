'''Craig Malcolm Robinson''' (born April 21, 1962) is an American [[college basketball]] coach, basketball executive, and broadcaster. He is a former head men's basketball coach at [[Oregon State University]] and [[Brown University]]. He was a star forward as a player at [[Princeton University]] in the early 1980s and a bond trader during the 1990s. He currently is the vice president of player and organizational development for the [[New York Knicks]].

Robinson is the older brother of former [[First Lady of the United States|U.S. First Lady]] [[Michelle Obama]] and the [[brother-in-law]] of former [[President of the United States|U.S. President]] [[Barack Obama]].  Robinson has been married twice and has four children.

==Playing career==
Robinson, who stands {{convert|6|ft|6|in}} and played [[Forward (basketball position)|forward]], was a two-time [[Ivy League]] Player of the Year at [[Princeton University]], in 1981–1982 and 1982–1983,<ref name="nyt021607"/> leading the league in [[field goal percentage]] both years.<ref name=OSUprofile/> He is the fourth highest scorer in school history.<ref name="womanbehind"/> He graduated in 1983 with a [[Bachelor's degree|B.A.]] in [[Sociology]].<ref name=OSUprofile/> His senior thesis was on [[social stratification]] in prisons.<ref name="nytm-qf">{{Cite news| url=https://www.nytimes.com/2010/04/25/magazine/25fob-q4-t.html?scp=1&sq=robinson%20%22a%20game%20of%20character%22&st=cse | title=The First Coach | author=Solomon, Deborah | magazine=[[The New York Times Magazine]] | date=April 19, 2010 | authorlink=Deborah Solomon}}</ref> Robinson and former teammate [[John W. Rogers, Jr.]] were among those invited to practice with [[Michael Jordan]] as he prepared for his comeback.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.princeton.edu/~paw/archive_new/PAW01-02/06-1205/sports.html|title=Playin' pickup with His Airness: Alumni helped Michael Jordan back into playing shape|accessdate=December 17, 2008|date=December 5, 2001|work=[[Princeton Alumni Weekly]]|author=Bernstein, Mark F.}}</ref>

Robinson was drafted in the fourth round of the [[1983 NBA Draft]] by the [[Philadelphia 76ers]],<ref name="bb-coach"/> but never played in the league. He played professionally for the [[Manchester Giants]] in the [[British Basketball League]] for two seasons<ref name="nyt021607"/> and returned to the U.S. in 1988 to become an assistant coach at the [[Illinois Institute of Technology]], a position he held until 1990.<ref name=OSUprofile/>

==Business career, marriage and family==

Robinson left basketball partly on the advice of his Princeton coach [[Pete Carril]]<ref name="nyt021607"/> and pursued a business degree, earning an [[M.B.A.]] in [[Finance]] from the [[University of Chicago Graduate School of Business]] in 1992.<ref name=OSUprofile>{{Cite web| url=http://www.osubeavers.com/ViewArticle.dbml?SPSID=106239&SPID=1954&DB_OEM_ID=4700&ATCLID=1436883&Q_SEASON=2008 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140308023833/http://www.osubeavers.com/ViewArticle.dbml?SPSID=106239&SPID=1954&DB_OEM_ID=4700&ATCLID=1436883&Q_SEASON=2008 | url-status=dead | archive-date=March 8, 2014 | title=Men's Basketball: Craig Robinson | publisher=[[Oregon State Beavers]] | accessdate=November 9, 2008}}</ref> Robinson worked in the 1990s as a [[Bond (finance)|bond]] [[Trader (finance)|trader]]. He became a vice president at [[Continental Illinois]] Bank and worked there from 1990 to 1992.<ref name="OSUprofile" /><ref name="espn-katz" /> He was then a vice president, from 1992 to 1999, at [[Morgan Stanley|Morgan Stanley Dean Witter]].<ref name="nyt021607" /> Later, he was a [[managing director]] and [[Partner (business rank)|partner]] at Loop Capital Markets, a minority-owned boutique [[investment banking]] firm.<ref name="nyt021607" /><ref name="espn-katz" />

That's how I could tell."<ref name="esq-bkbl"/> The story of this [[pick-up game]] and of a "test" being passed became a key part of the Obama narrative.<ref name="esq-bkbl"/>

==Basketball career==
Robinson returned to coaching in 1999, making one-tenth his former salary.<ref name="nyt110808"/> He was an assistant for six years to [[Bill Carmody]] at [[Northwestern University]],<ref name="bb-coach"/><ref name="nyt110808">{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/11/09/sports/ncaabasketball/09coach.html|title=He Helped Elect a President; Now Comes a Harder Job|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|date=November 8, 2008|accessdate=November 9, 2008|author=Thamel, Pete}}</ref> where he was an effective recruiter.<ref name="espn-katz"/> He then became a head coach at [[Brown University]] in 2006, where he ran a variation of the [[Princeton offense]] which he learned from [[Pete Carril]] during his years at Princeton. In improving a mid-level basketball program,<ref name=tucker/> he stressed [[work ethic]], used [[tough love]], and tried to improve the players' [[vocabulary]].<ref name="nyt110808"/> A fifth-place placing with a strong finish to the season garnered Robinson the [[Ivy League]] men's basketball Coach of the Year for the 2006–2007 season by Basketball-U.com.<ref name="bb-coach">{{Cite news | title=Craig Robinson named Ivy League Men's Basketball Coach of the Year by Basketball-U | url=http://brownbears.cstv.com/sports/m-baskbl/spec-rel/031407aaa.html | publisher=[[Brown Bears]] | date=March 14, 2007 | accessdate=November 9, 2008 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080210003650/http://brownbears.cstv.com/sports/m-baskbl/spec-rel/031407aaa.html | archive-date=February 10, 2008 | url-status=dead }}</ref> The following year, the [[Brown Bears]] finished second in the league, and their 19 wins for the season was a team record.<ref name="nyt110808"/>

[[Image:20080825 Craig Robinson Introducing his sister at Democratic National Convention.png|thumb|Robinson introducing his sister, [[Michelle Obama]], at the [[2008 Democratic National Convention]].]]
Robinson assisted his brother-in-law throughout [[Barack Obama 2008 presidential campaign|the latter's 2008 presidential campaign]], including campaigning for him during the [[Iowa Democratic caucuses, 2008|Iowa caucuses]] and campaigning and giving speeches for him in a number of other states, sometimes combining campaigning with recruiting visits.<ref name="espn-katz"/><ref name="nyt110808"/> He introduced his sister Michelle before her speech on August 25, 2008, the first night of the [[2008 Democratic National Convention]],<ref>{{Cite news| url=http://www.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,1836039_1836038_1836032,00.html| title=Scorecard: First-Night Speeches: Craig Robinson: Grade: B+| work=[[Time (magazine)|Time]]|accessdate=April 4, 2009| date=August 2008|author=Halperin, Mark}}</ref> which gave him his largest national exposure.<ref>{{cite news| url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/world/americas/an-obama-family-affair-in-denver-908323.html | title=An Obama family affair in Denver |author=Doyle, Leonard | newspaper=[[The Independent]] | date=August 26, 2008 | location=London}}</ref> He was also on stage following [[Barack Obama presidential acceptance speech, 2008|Obama's victory speech]] in [[Grant Park (Chicago)|Grant Park]] after his election as president on November 4, 2008.<ref name="nyt110808"/>

On April 7, 2008, Robinson was hired as the [[Oregon State Beavers]]' head basketball coach<ref name=robinsonera>{{Cite news|url=http://www.osubeavers.com/ViewArticle.dbml?SPSID=24839&SPID=1954&DB_OEM_ID=4700&ATCLID=1435076|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303214641/http://www.osubeavers.com/ViewArticle.dbml?SPSID=24839&SPID=1954&DB_OEM_ID=4700&ATCLID=1435076|url-status=dead|archive-date=March 3, 2016|title=Craig Robinson Era Begins at Oregon State|publisher=[[Oregon State Beavers]]|date=April 7, 2008|accessdate=April 7, 2008}}</ref> following the team's winless [[Pacific-10 Conference]] record and overall 6–25 mark the year before.<ref name="nyt110808"/> ([[Jesse Agel]], Robinson's assistant of two years, took over Robinson's former position at Brown.)<ref name="nyt110808"/>

In July 2009, President Obama alluded to the possibility of Robinson coaching elsewhere by saying: "Craig Robinson is an outstanding coach. ... Anybody in Oregon and anybody who knows sports knows he turned it around. He loves Corvallis, and I'm sure that as a young, successful coach, he's going to start getting offers."<ref>{{cite news| url=http://blog.oregonlive.com/behindbeaversbeat/2009/07/obama_on_oregon_states_robinso.html | title=Obama on Oregon State's Robinson: 'He's going to start getting offers' | author=Pope, Charlie | newspaper=[[The Oregonian]] | date=July 1, 2009 | accessdate=July 9, 2009}}</ref> Oregon State's 2009–10 season featured an inconsistent level of play in a conference dominated by parity, leading to an 8–10 regular season conference record for a tie for fifth place. They then lost in the first round of the [[Pacific-10 Conference Men's Basketball Tournament|Pac-10 Tournament]]. Despite the losing record, the team was invited to defend their championship at the [[2010 College Basketball Invitational]],<ref>{{Cite news| url=http://sports.espn.go.com/ncb/tournament/2010/news/story?id=4996134 | title=Oregon State to open CBI vs. BU | agency=[[Associated Press]] | publisher=[[ESPN]] | date=March 15, 2010 | accessdate=March 20, 2010}}</ref> but lacked focus and energy and were beaten easily in the first round,<ref>{{Cite news |url=http://kezi.com/news/local/166634 |title=Beavs Slammed Out of CBI by Boston U. |author=Andress, Steve |publisher=[[KEZI-TV]] |date=March 17, 2010 |accessdate=March 20, 2010 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100322221141/http://kezi.com/news/local/166634 |archivedate=March 22, 2010 |df=mdy }}</ref> leaving them with an overall season record of 14–18.

In March 2010, shortly before the end of the season, the university and Robinson agreed on a two-year contract extension that would keep him in place through the 2015–16 season.<ref>{{Cite news| url=http://sports.espn.go.com/ncb/news/story?id=4960122 | title=Robinson agrees to contract extension | agency=[[Associated Press]] | publisher=[[ESPN]] | date=March 2, 2010 | accessdate=March 9, 2010}}</ref> In April 2010, Robinson published his memoir, ''A Game of Character: A Family Journey from Chicago's Southside to the Ivy League and Beyond''.<ref>{{Cite news| url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=126308946 | title='First Brother-In-Law' Craig Robinson Talks Family, Character | author=Martin, Michel | work=[[Tell Me More]] | publisher=[[NPR]] | date=April 27, 2010 | accessdate=May 2, 2010 | authorlink=Michel Martin}}</ref>

The 2010–2011 season was one of regression for Oregon State, with the team falling to a 5–13, ninth-place finish in the conference and a sharply losing record overall. However, Robinson professed to not being overly worried or disappointed, saying he was encouraged by the development of some of the underclassmen.<ref>{{cite news | url=http://blog.oregonlive.com/behindbeaversbeat/2011/02/oregon_state_sunday_rundown.html | title=Oregon State Sunday rundown: Craig Robinson says all remaining games for the Beavers are winnable | author=Buker, Paul | newspaper=[[The Oregonian]] | date=February 27, 2011 | accessdate=March 6, 2011}}</ref> The Obamas showed their support for Robinson by attending an Oregon State game against [[Towson]] in November 2011.<ref>{{cite news | url=http://mrs-o.com/newdata/2011/11/26/have-a-heart.html | title=Have a Heart | publisher=Mrs-O.com | date=November 26, 2011 | accessdate=January 29, 2012}}</ref> The 2011–2012 season saw a Robinson-era best for overall wins, 21, including a pair in the [[2012 College Basketball Invitational]], but a fourth consecutive losing record within the conference. It was the highest win total for the team since 1990,<ref name="ore-good">{{cite news | url=http://www.oregonlive.com/beavers/index.ssf/2012/06/oregon_state_beavers_basketbal_110.html | title=OSU finally gets shovels ready for practice facility | author=Hunt, John | newspaper=[[The Oregonian]] | date=June 25, 2012 | accessdate=April 6, 2013}}</ref> and the completion of it saw star guard [[Jared Cunningham]] leaving after his junior season and being selected in the [[2012 NBA Draft|NBA Draft]],<ref>{{cite news | url=http://newyork.cbslocal.com/2012/08/01/rothstein-files-oregon-state-ready-to-move-on-without-cunningham/ | title=Oregon State Ready To Move On Without Cunningham | author=Rothstein, Jon | publisher=[[WCBS-TV]] | date=August 1, 2012 | accessdate=April 6, 2013}}</ref> the first Beaver to be drafted in over a decade. In June 2012, construction began on a $15 million basketball practice facility that Robinson and previous coaches had long been campaigning for.<ref name="ore-good"/>

Prior to the 2012–2013 season, Robinson characterized his squad as "probably the best team I've had."<ref name="ore-bad">{{cite news | url=http://www.oregonlive.com/beavers/index.ssf/2013/03/osu_basketball_craig_robinson_2.html | title=Craig Robinson is banking on a turnaround | author=Hunt, John | newspaper=[[The Oregonian]] | date=March 19, 2013 | accessdate=March 24, 2013}}</ref> But the Beavers went in the opposite direction by suffering one of their worst seasons ever, with a penchant for second-half collapses and end-of-game failures.<ref name="ore-bad"/> Attendance at [[Gill Coliseum]] fell to half capacity, and the [[student section]] lost enthusiasm.<ref name="ore-bad"/> The team finished with a dead-last 4–14 record in the conference and a 14–18 mark overall. Fans began calling for a change in coaches, but the university athletic director said, "Coach Robinson is our coach, and this administration is in full support of him."<ref name="ore-bad"/> Following the season, Robinson appeared on the [[CBS Sports Network]] as a studio analyst during the [[NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship]] tournament.<ref>{{cite news | url=https://www.sbnation.com/puck-the-media/2013/3/22/4137090/cbs-sports-network-adds-craig-robinson-ncaa-tournament-coverage | title=CBS Sports Network adds Craig Robinson to NCAA Tournament coverage | author=Lepore, Steve | publisher=[[SB Nation]] | date=March 22, 2013 | accessdate=March 24, 2013}}</ref>

The 2013–2014 season saw the team improve to a 16–16 mark overall but have its sixth consecutive losing season in the conference. The team faced the loss of all five of its starters and its five top scorers following the season, due to graduation, transfers, and the like.<ref name="fox-fired"/><ref name="espn-fired"/> In March 2014, the [[Pac-12 Conference|Pac-12]] announced that Robinson would coach an all-star basketball team that would tour China in August.<ref>http://www.oregonlive.com/beavers/index.ssf/2014/03/craig_robinson_to_coach_pac-12.html</ref> On May 5, 2014, Robinson was fired from his position as the Oregon State men's head basketball coach.<ref name="fox-fired">{{cite news | url=http://www.foxnews.com/sports/2014/05/05/brother-michelle-obama-fired-as-basketball-coach-at-oregon-state/ | title=Brother of Michelle Obama fired as basketball coach at Oregon State | author= | publisher=[[Fox News]] | date=May 5, 2014 | accessdate=May 5, 2014}}</ref> He was given a $4 million buyout of the three remaining years on his contract.<ref name="espn-fired">{{cite news | url=http://espn.go.com/mens-college-basketball/story/_/id/10884778/craig-robinson-fired-oregon-state-beavers | title=Oregon State fires Craig Robinson | author=Goodman, Jeff | publisher=[[ESPN]] | date=May 5, 2014 | accessdate=May 11, 2014}}</ref> The team had failed to make the [[NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship]] or the [[National Invitation Tournament]] during Robinson's tenure.<ref name="espn-fired"/> His overall 93–104 record did make him the fourth-winningest coach in team history.<ref name="espn-fired"/>

The [[Milwaukee Bucks]] hired Robinson as vice president of player and organizational development on August 15, 2016.<ref>{{cite web|author=Gardner, Charles F.|title=Bucks hire Michelle Obama's brother as player mentor|url=http://www.jsonline.com/story/sports/nba/bucks/2016/08/15/craig-robinson-join-bucks-mentor-role/88761458/|work=Milwaukee Journal Sentinel|date=August 15, 2016|accessdate=August 15, 2016}}</ref> After one season, he left the Bucks to join the [[New York Knicks]] in a similar position.<ref>{{cite web|title=Knicks hire Obama's brother-in-law to fill front-office job|url=http://scores.nbcsports.com/nba/story.asp?i=20170808174330636913008|website=scores.nbcsports.com|accessdate=August 9, 2017|date=August 8, 2017}}</ref>

==Broadcasting career==
On October 7, 2014, [[ESPN]] announced the hiring of Robinson as a college basketball analyst.<ref>{{cite press release | url=http://espnmediazone.com/us/press-releases/2014/10/former-college-basketball-head-coaches-stan-heath-craig-robinson-join-espn/ | title=Former College Basketball Head Coaches Stan Heath & Craig Robinson Join ESPN | publisher=[[ESPN]] | date=October 7, 2014 | accessdate=October 7, 2014}}</ref> There, he began to work broadcasting games and doing studio work on [[ESPNU]].<ref>{{cite news | url=https://www.chicagotribune.com/sports/smackblog/chi-espn-hires-michelle-obama-brother-craig-robinson-20141007-story.html | title=ESPN hires Michelle Obama's brother, Craig Robinson | first=Ed | last=Sherman | newspaper=[[Chicago Tribune]] | date=October 7, 2014}}</ref>

==After coaching==
On July 13, 2020, Robinson was named the Executive Director of the [[National Association of Basketball Coaches]].
