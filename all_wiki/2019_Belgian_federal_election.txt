{{politics of Belgium}}
'''Federal elections''' were held in [[Belgium]] on 26 May 2019, alongside the country's [[2019 European Parliament election in Belgium|European]] and [[2019 Belgian regional elections|regional elections]]. All 150 members of the [[Chamber of Representatives (Belgium)|Chamber of Representatives]] were elected from eleven multi-member constituencies.

The far-right [[Vlaams Belang]] (VB) saw a resurgence in [[Flanders]], and with the [[New Flemish Alliance]] (NVA), parties subscribing to [[Flemish separatism]] and [[Flemish nationalism|nationalism]] obtained nearly 50% of the vote in [[Flanders]].

In addition, gains for the far-left [[Workers' Party of Belgium]] (PVDA-PTB), and the green [[Ecolo]] party in [[Wallonia]] occurred. Overall, [[Political parties in Belgium|traditional parties]] suffered losses in both regions. 

==Background==
{{Main|2014 Belgian government formation|Michel I Government}}
Following the [[2014 Belgian federal election|2014 elections]], a centre-right government consisting of [[N-VA]], [[CD&V]], [[Open Vld]] and [[Mouvement Réformateur|MR]] was formed, led by Prime Minister [[Charles Michel]] (MR). The government coalition is unique in several aspects: the N-VA participates for the first time, the MR is the only French-speaking party, and the French-speaking [[Socialist Party (Belgium)|Socialist Party]] is not part of the government for the first time in 25 years.

Local elections were [[2018 Belgian local elections|held on 14 October 2018]]. As such, the 2019 simultaneous regional, federal and European elections were held only several months after the local elections.

In early December 2018, a political crisis emerged regarding the [[Global Compact for Migration]], which was supposed to be signed but sparked instead opposition from government party [[N-VA]]. As the three other government parties as well as a large majority in parliament supported the compact, N-VA left the government and the three other parties continued briefly as a minority cabinet ([[Michel II Government|Michel II]]) with an unclear status. Prime Minister Michel ultimately offered his resignation to the King on 18 December 2018, who accepted it three days later. As regular elections were scheduled for May 2019 anyway, snap elections were only favoured by N-VA and Vlaams Belang and did not happen, and the minority cabinet continued as a [[caretaker government]] until the elections.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-belgium-politics-idUSKCN1OK17R|title=Belgian PM to stay on heading caretaker government|date=2018-12-21|work=Reuters|access-date=2019-06-28|language=en}}</ref>

The 150 members of the [[Chamber of Representatives (Belgium)|Chamber of Representatives]] were elected in 11 multi-member constituencies, being the ten [[Provinces of Belgium|provinces]] and Brussels, with between 4 and 24 seats. Seats are allocated using the [[D'Hondt method]], with an [[electoral threshold]] of 5% per constituency.<ref>[http://www.ipu.org/parline-e/reports/2029_B.htm Electoral system] IPU</ref>

Representatives elected from the five Flemish provinces, [[Antwerp (province)|Antwerp]] (24), [[East Flanders]] (20), [[Flemish Brabant]] (15), [[Limburg (Belgium)|Limburg]] (12) and [[West Flanders]] (16), automatically belonged to the Dutch-speaking language group in parliament, whereas those elected from the five Walloon provinces, [[Hainaut (province)|Hainaut]] (18), [[Liège (province)|Liège]] (15), [[Luxembourg (Belgium)|Luxembourg]] (4), [[Namur (province)|Namur]] (6) and [[Walloon Brabant]] (5), formed the French-speaking language group. The 15 members elected in [[Brussels]] could choose to join either group. Apportionment of seats is done every ten years in accordance with population data, last by royal order of 31 January 2013.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.ejustice.just.fgov.be/cgi/article_body.pl?language=nl&caller=summary&pub_date=2013-02-14&numac=2013000095|title=Moniteur Belge - Belgisch Staatsblad|website=www.ejustice.just.fgov.be|access-date=2019-06-28}}</ref>

The 60-member [[Senate (Belgium)|Senate]] is composed of 50 representatives from the regional and community parliaments, plus 10 co-opted senators proportionally divided among parties based on the result of the federal election.

All Belgian citizens aged 18 or over were [[Compulsory voting|obligated to participate in the election]]. Non-Belgian citizens residing in Belgium (regardless of EU citizenship) couldn't vote, whereas Belgian citizens living abroad could register to vote.

==Date==
The [[Sixth Belgian state reform|2011–14 state reform]] changed several aspects regarding federal elections. Starting with the May 2014 election, which coincided with European Parliament and regional elections, the federal parliament is elected for a five-year term rather than a four-year term.

The federal elections would from then on always coincide with the European Parliament elections;<ref>Article 65 of the Belgian Constitution</ref> snap federal elections would trigger a parliamentary term lasting until the next European Parliament elections.<ref>Article 46 of the Belgian Constitution</ref> However, as of 2017, a law needed for this to take effect has not been enacted. Given the five-year term for all three elections, they all coincided in 2019 regardless, as no snap federal elections were called.

Additionally, de facto practice is to formally [[Dissolution of parliament|dissolve parliament]] and trigger new elections by [[Declaration of Revision of the Constitution]] shortly before regular expiry of the parliamentary term.

==Campaign==
Despite leaving the government coalition in late 2018, it was the explicit ambition of N-VA to continue governing after the May 2019 election. In January 2019, the party put forward ex-minister [[Jan Jambon]] as candidate for Prime Minister should the party enter a coalition.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2019/01/14/n-va-maakt-lijsttrekkers-bekend/|title=De Wever is kandidaat om Vlaams minister-president te worden, Jambon kandidaat-premier, Bourgeois gaat Europees|date=14 January 2019|publisher=VRTNWS}}</ref> A continuation of the "Swedish coalition" (N-VA, MR, CD&V and Open Vld), potentially expanded with cdH, was a likely scenario. Outgoing Prime Minister [[Charles Michel]] (MR) was candidate to continue in his position.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.7sur7.be/7s7/fr/18101/Elections-2019/article/detail/3526110/2019/04/17/Charles-Michel-est-le-candidat-Premier-ministre-du-MR.dhtml|title=Charles Michel est le candidat Premier ministre du MR|date=17 April 2019|publisher=7sur7}}</ref>

Meanwhile, the French-speaking Socialist Party (PS) had the ambition to re-enter government as well, or "re-conquer" as formulated by leader [[Elio Di Rupo]], after being in the opposition at federal level and being ousted from the Walloon government.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.hln.be/nieuws/binnenland/di-rupo-ps-het-is-tijd-voor-de-herovering-we-zijn-het-enige-geloofwaardige-linkse-alternatief~a1b444bc/|title=Di Rupo (PS): "Het is tijd voor de herovering. We zijn het enige geloofwaardige linkse alternatief"|date=11 January 2019|publisher=Het Laatste Nieuws}}</ref>

Additionally, there was speculation that the Flemish liberal and green parties (Open Vld and Groen) would favour cooperating, given Groen's recent rise and their local governing coalitions in cities like Mechelen, Gent and Oostende. However, their French-speaking counterparts, MR and Ecolo, are politically and ideologically further apart.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.standaard.be/cnt/dmf20181207_04016534|title=Blauw en groen kunnen elkaar vinden in 2019|date=8 December 2018|publisher=De Standaard}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2019/01/11/de-blauw-groene-motor-loopt-in-vlaanderen-misschien-warm-maar-i/|title=De blauw-groene motor loopt in Vlaanderen misschien warm, maar in Franstalig België helemaal niet|date=11 January 2019|publisher=VRTNWS}}</ref>

==Aftermath==
{{Main|2019–20 Belgian government formation}}

The election once again exposed the deep linguistic, ethnic and regional divisions of Belgium, with the Dutch-speaking region of Flanders strongly voting for right-wing Flemish nationalist and separatist parties, and the French-speaking region of Wallonia strongly voting left.<ref>[https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2019/05/29/belgian-king-meets-flemish-far-right-first-time-since-1930s/ Belgian king meets Flemish far right for first time since 1930s, amid fears of 'Vlexit' breakaway] The Telegraph, 29 May 2019</ref><ref>[https://www.politico.eu/article/belgium-election-results-analysis/ Election treble spells trouble for Belgium] Politico, 27 May 2019</ref>

In response to [[Vlaams Belang]]'s surge in support there has been some speculation that the [[New Flemish Alliance|N-VA]] leader [[Bart De Wever]] may break the ''[[Cordon sanitaire (politics)|Cordon sanitaire]]'' imposed on the party, resulting in the two Flemish nationalist parties joining forces, by refusing to rule out talks with the VB, as their strong results could make forming a coalition more difficult.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.euractiv.com/section/eu-elections-2019/news/belgian-black-sunday-sees-far-right-surge-threatening-new-government-crisis/|title=Belgium's 'Black Sunday' sees far-right surge, threatens new government crisis|work=Euractive|access-date=28 May 2019|language=en-GB}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.brusselstimes.com/all-news/belgium-all-news/57328/belgiums-far-right-not-ruled-out-of-potential-coalition/|title=Belgium's far-right not ruled out of potential coalition|work=The Brussels Times|access-date=28 May 2019|language=en-GB}}</ref>

The [[People's Party (Belgium)|People's Party]], a small right-wing French-speaking party, dissolved on 18 June through an internal party vote in response to losing their only seat in the elections. Former party leader [[Mischaël Modrikamen]] blamed the "systematic exclusion of the People's Party from the political and media debate."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.lalibre.be/belgique/politique-belge/modrikamen-l-a-annonce-le-pp-c-est-termine-5d0a384f9978e2779656502c|title=Modrikamen l'a annoncé: le PP, c'est terminé|last=|first=|date=19 June 2019|website=La Libre|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=}}</ref>

In the days following the election, [[Philippe of Belgium|King Philippe]] held consultations with all main party leaders, including [[Vlaams Belang]] party leader [[Tom Van Grieken]]. According to British newspaper ''[[The Guardian]]'', 1936 was the last time a far-right leader had an official meeting with the king.<ref>[https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/may/29/belgian-king-meeting-far-right-leader-sparks-controversy Belgian king's meeting with far-right leader sparks controversy] The Guardian, 29 May 2019</ref>

On 30 May, King Philippe appointed [[Johan Vande Lanotte]] (sp.a) and [[Didier Reynders]] (MR), two experienced politicians with a long period of service, as [[informateur]]s. They reported on their progress by 6 June as expected,<ref>[https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2019/05/30/koning-ontvangt/ Koning Filip stelt Johan Vande Lanotte en Didier Reynders aan als informateur] VRT, 30 May 2019</ref> after which they were given some more time, with a new deadline of 17 June.<ref>[https://vrtnws.be/p.lWR6V4PKq Reynders en Vande Lanotte blijven informateur tot 17 juni: "Lange formatie van 2010 overdoen, is niet mogelijk"] VRT, 6 June 2019</ref> After this deadline passed, another extension was granted until 1 July,<ref>[http://www.xinhuanet.com/english/2019-06/18/c_138151220.htm Mission to form Belgian government extended by King Philippe] Xinhua, 18 June 2019</ref> and again until 29 July<ref>[https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2019/07/01/informateurs-vande-lanotte-en-reynders-werken-verder-aan-preform/ Informateurs Vande Lanotte en Reynders werken verder aan preformatienota] VRT, 1 July 2019</ref> and one more extension until 9 September.<ref>[https://nieuws.vtm.be/politiek/vande-lanotte-en-reynders-nog-tot-9-september-informateur Vande Lanotte en Reynders nog tot 9 september informateur] VTM, 29 July 2019</ref>

On 24 August the Belgian government decided to appoint Didier Reynders as [[European Commissioner]].<ref>[https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2019/08/24/reynders-eurocommissaris/ Didier Reynders (MR) voorgedragen als Belgische Eurocommissaris: "Franstaligen waren eensgezind, Vlamingen niet"] VRT, 24 August 2019</ref> On 4 October 2019, King Philippe announced the end of the first phase of federal government formation talks, with the informateurs advising that a government of the N-VA, the Socialist Party and the Greens was the best option to pursue. The informateurs will report back to the king of the 4 November, following which a formateur will be appointed to introduce the third stage of government formation.<ref>[https://royalcentral.co.uk/europe/belgium/king-philippe-introduces-new-phase-in-government-formation-131716/] Royal Central, 9 October 2019</ref>

On 4 November, the two new informateurs, Rudy Demotte and Geert Bourgeois, offered their resignations to the King, as they could not break the political deadlock.<ref>https://euobserver.com/political/146520</ref>

On 17 March 2020, the successor of [[Michel II Government]], the Wilmès Government, [[Wilmès II Government|had its mandate extended]]. Most parliamentary parties provided them external support in order to manage the [[COVID-19 pandemic in Belgium|coronavirus pandemic]]. Informal talks on forming a new government began in June of 2020.<ref>https://crisisinbelgium.blogspot.com/2020/06/mission-impossible.html</ref> Wilmès II government was eventually replaced by the permanent seven-party coalition — [[De Croo Government]] in October, with Wilmès becoming one of the deputy prime ministers.
