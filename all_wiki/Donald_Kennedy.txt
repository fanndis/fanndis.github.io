'''Donald Kennedy''' (August 18, 1931&nbsp;– April 21, 2020) was an American scientist, public administrator, and academic. He served as [[Commissioner of Food and Drugs|Commissioner]] of the U.S. [[Food and Drug Administration]] (1977–79), President of [[Stanford University]] (1980–92), and Editor-in-Chief of ''[[Science (journal)|Science]]'' (2000–08). Following this, he was named president emeritus of Stanford University; Bing Professor of Environmental Science and Policy, emeritus; and senior fellow of the [[Freeman Spogli Institute for International Studies]].

==Early life and education==
Donald Kennedy was born on August 18, 1931 in [[New York City]], the son of Barbara Bean and William Dorsey Kennedy.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://news.stanford.edu/pr/91/910729Arc1239.html |title=Kennedy chronology |work= news.stanford.edu| publisher= Stanford University |date=July 29, 1991 |access-date=September 10, 2011}}</ref> He attended [[Dublin School]] through high school<ref name="Kennedy2018">{{cite book|author=Donald Kennedy|title=A Place in the Sun: A Memoir|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=aVlHDwAAQBAJ&pg=PT30|date=9 January 2018|publisher=Stanford University Press|isbn=978-0-911221-61-9|pages=30–}}</ref> and went on to attend [[Harvard University]], where he received an [[Bachelor of Arts|A.B.]], M.S., and [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]] in [[Biology]], in 1956.<ref name='FSI'>{{cite web |url= http://fsi.stanford.edu/mediaguide/donaldkennedy/ |work= FSI Stanford Media Guide| title= Donald Kennedy, PhD | publisher= Stanford University |access-date=January 28, 2010 |url-status=dead |archive-url= https://archive.is/20121215012430/http://fsi.stanford.edu/mediaguide/donaldkennedy/ |archive-date=December 15, 2012 }}</ref><ref name='FDA'>{{cite web |url= https://www.fda.gov/AboutFDA/CommissionersPage/PastCommissioners/ucm113407.htm |title=Donald Kennedy, Ph.D. |access-date=January 28, 2010 |publisher= US Food and Drug Administration }}</ref> His doctoral dissertation was titled ''Studies on the Frog [[Electroretinogram]]''.<ref>{{cite dissertation| url= http://id.lib.harvard.edu/alma/990013471010203941/catalog|last=Kennedy|first=Donald|title=Studies on the frog electroretinogram|type=PhD|publisher=Harvard University|date=1956| via= lib.harvard.edu| access-date= April 22, 2020}}</ref>

==Career==

=== Teacher ===
From 1956 to 1960, Kennedy taught biology at [[Syracuse University]], receiving tenure by 1960.<ref name= brodie-banner>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=l8KSkEC8sGcC&pg=PA139|title=The Research University Presidency in the Late Twentieth Century: A Life Cycle/case History Approach| first1= Harlow Keith Hammond| last1= Brodie| first2= Leslie| last2= Banner| date=June 30, 2019| publisher= Greenwood Publishing Group| access-date=June 30, 2019| via= Google Books| page= 139|isbn=9780275985608}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=Donald Kennedy, Former Syracuse University Professor and Stanford University President, Passes of COVID-19 |url=https://news.syr.edu/blog/2020/04/24/donald-kennedy-former-syracuse-university-professor-and-stanford-university-president-passes-of-covid-19/ |access-date=28 April 2020 |work=SU News |date=24 April 2020}}</ref> His research included the patterns of neural action in crayfish, demonstrating some of the connection principles among nerve cells that impose the sequences underlying a behavioral event. Kennedy showed that some single neurons, which he termed “command” neurons, could produce a complex, fixed-action pattern of locomotory behavior.<ref>{{cite journal| url= https://mcb.berkeley.edu/labs/zucker/PDFs/Zucker_JNeurophysiol35,599.pdf| title= Crayfish Escape Behavior and Central Synapses. I. Neural Circuit Exciting Lateral Giant Fiber| first= Robert S. | last= Zucker | journal= Journal of Neurophysiology| year= 1972| volume= 35| issue= 5| pages= 599–620| doi= 10.1152/jn.1972.35.5.599| pmid= 5054506| via= mcb.berkeley.edu| access-date= April 22, 2020}}</ref>

Arriving at Stanford University as an assistant professor in 1960, Kennedy was granted [[tenure]] in 1962.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/fdas-evolving-regulatory-powers/donald-kennedy-phd|title=Donald Kennedy, Ph.D. |date=February 9, 2019| publisher=US Food and Drug Administration|access-date=June 30, 2019}}</ref> In 1967 he was appointed chairman of the Department of Biology in the School of Humanities and Sciences.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=9qpmDJQPEZEC&pg=PA294|title=Holding the Center: Memoirs of a Life in Higher Education|first=Howard Wesley|last=Johnson|date=August 24, 2001|publisher=MIT Press|isbn=9780262600446|access-date=June 30, 2019| via=Google Books}}</ref> He was one of the founding faculty in the Program in Human Biology, Kennedy served ten years on the board of directors of the [[David and Lucile Packard Foundation]].<ref name="auto">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=l8KSkEC8sGcC&pg=PA140|title=The Research University Presidency in the Late Twentieth Century: A Life Cycle/case History Approach |first1= Harlow Keith Hammond |last1= Brodie |first2= Leslie| last2= Banner| date=June 30, 2019| page= 140 |publisher=Greenwood Publishing Group |isbn=9780275985608 |access-date= June 30, 2019|via=Google Books}}</ref> where he served as director from 1973 to 1977.<ref name="FSI" /><ref name="FDA" />

=== FDA Commissioner ===
For 26 months he served as Commissioner of the United States [[Food and Drug Administration]] during the [[Jimmy Carter|Carter Administration]], appointed by the [[United States Secretary of Health, Education, and Welfare|Secretary of Health, Education, and Welfare]], [[Joseph Califano]], in April 1977. In the next two-plus years Kennedy and the FDA dealt with issues such as the fallout from the attempt to ban [[saccharin]], and the risks of [[antimicrobial resistance|antibiotic resistance in humans]] from agricultural antibiotic use<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/frontline/film/trouble-with-antibiotics/|title=The Trouble with Antibiotics|website=FRONTLINE}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/frontline/article/inside-an-early-attempt-to-restrict-antibiotic-use-on-farms/|title=Inside an Early Attempt to Restrict Antibiotic Use on Farms|website=FRONTLINE}}</ref> and worked on provisions of the proposed ''Drug Regulation Reform Act of 1978''.<ref name='FDA' />

=== Stanford presidency ===
After stepping down from the FDA in June 1979, Kennedy returned to Stanford, where he served as provost.<ref name="FDA" /> In 1980 he became president of Stanford University and served in that position until 1992.<ref name="FSI" /> While president, he inaugurated overseas campuses in Kyoto, Japan, and Oxford, England, the Institute for International Studies,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://news.stanford.edu/pr/91/910729Arc1241.html|title=Stanford President Kennedy to step down next year|website=news.Stanford.edu|access-date=June 30, 2019}}</ref> the Stanford Center for the Humanities,<ref name=Dailyobit>{{cite news |first1=Julia |last1=Ingram |first2=Anastasia |last2=Malenko |url= https://www.stanforddaily.com/2020/04/21/former-stanford-president-donald-kennedy-dies-of-covid-19/ |title=Former Stanford President Donald Kennedy dies of COVID-19 |newspaper=The Stanford Daily |date=April 21, 2020 }}</ref> the Haas Public Service Center, and the Stanford-in-Washington campus. One of his focuses was on improving the quality of undergraduate education.<ref name="nyt" /> In the mid-1980s, he led a $1.1&nbsp;billion fundraising effort to improve the facilities of the university,<ref>{{cite web |url= https://books.google.com/books?id=J2Q36p345CAC&pg=PA56|title= Buddy, can you spare a billion?| work= [[New Scientist]] |date=February 19, 1987|publisher=Reed Business Information| pages= 56–57| access-date=June 30, 2019|via=Google Books}}</ref> and the total raised was $1.2&nbsp;billion.<ref name="auto" /> In 1990 Kennedy hosted [[Mikhail Gorbachev]] on an international visit to Stanford.<ref name="nyt">{{cite news| url= https://www.nytimes.com/1991/07/30/us/stanford-chief-quits-amid-furor-on-use-of-federal-money.html|title=Stanford Chief Quits Amid Furor on Use Of Federal Money| work= The New York Times| first= Jane|last=Gross|date=July 30, 1991|access-date=June 30, 2019}}</ref> Over his tenure, Kennedy fostered the growth of the university's endowment to $2&nbsp;billion, which was the fifth-largest in the United States.<ref>{{cite web|last=Richards |first=Evelyn |url= https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1991/07/31/stanford-sees-end-of-era-in-kennedy-resignation/7196b1e8-4c90-407b-a5b6-8de57f18600a/ |title=Stanford Sees End Of Era In Kennedy Resignation |newspaper=The Washington Post |date=July 31, 1991 |access-date=April 22, 2020}}</ref> He also led Stanford to divest all investments in [[South Africa]] during [[Apartheid]] after student protests,<ref>{{cite journal| url= https://www.yesmagazine.org/issues/the-end-of-poverty/stanford-divestors-we-love|title=Stanford's Coal Divestment: Meet 2 Students—And 1 President—Who Made It Happen| first=Dana|last=Drugmand| website= yesmagazine.org|access-date=June 30, 2019}}</ref> and changed the “Western Culture" credit requirements to “Cultures, Ideas, and Values” in an attempt to encompass non-Western cultures.<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1988/04/19/bennett-assails-new-stanford-program/68ca775f-f95c-499b-9e1d-cfc219d8b7ea/ |title=Bennett Assails New Stanford Program |newspaper=The Washington Post |date=April 19, 1988 |access-date=April 22, 2020}}</ref>

=== Later career ===
He remained at Stanford after resigning from the presidency. In 1997 Kennedy published the book ''Academic Duty'', which advocated for university professors to pay more attention to the teaching part of their duties, and to make an effort to connect their research with the wider public.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.emory.edu/EMORY_REPORT/erarchive/1999/June/erjune.1/6_1_99duty.html|title=Academic Duty|website=Emory Report|publisher=Emory University|volume=51|issue=32|access-date=June 30, 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20010223050857/http://www.emory.edu/EMORY_REPORT/erarchive/1999/June/erjune.1/6_1_99duty.html|archive-date=February 23, 2001|url-status=live}}</ref> From 2000 until 2008, he was editor-in-chief of ''[[Science (journal)|Science]]'',<ref name="sciencemag">{{cite news |last1=Mervis |first1=Jeffrey |title=Donald Kennedy, who led Science through turbulent times, dies at 88 |url=https://www.sciencemag.org/news/2020/04/donald-kennedy-who-led-science-through-turbulent-times-dies-88 |access-date=25 May 2020 |work=Science |agency=AAAS |date=23 April 2020 |language=en |doi=10.1126/science.abc4229}}</ref><ref name="FSI" /> the weekly published by the [[American Association for the Advancement of Science]]. In 2010, he received [[Wonderfest]]'s [[Carl Sagan Prize for Science Popularization]].<ref>{{cite web |url= http://wonderfest.org/sagan-prize/sagan-prize-recipients/ |title=Sagan Prize Recipients |work=Wonderfest.org |year=2011 |access-date=September 10, 2011}}</ref> Kennedy was a fellow of the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]], the American Academy for the Advancement of Science, the [[American Philosophical Society]], and the [[California Academy of Sciences]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://islandpress.org/author/donald-kennedy|title=Donald Kennedy|website=IslandPress.org|access-date=June 30, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite book| url= https://books.google.com/books?id=ofWvCwAAQBAJ&pg=PR24|title=Arrow and the Foundations of the Theory of Economic Policy|first=George R.|last=Feiwel|date=January 1, 2016| publisher= Springer|isbn=9781349073573|access-date=June 30, 2019|via=Google Books}}</ref> According to his Stanford biography, Kennedy's research interests related to "policy on such trans-boundary environmental problems as: major land-use changes; economically-driven alterations in agricultural practice; global climate change; beyond coal; and alternative energy sources."<ref name="FSI" /> He was president emeritus of Stanford University, Bing Professor of Environmental Science and Policy, and emeritus and senior fellow of the Freeman Spogli Institute for International Studies by courtesy.<ref>{{cite web| url= http://longevity.stanford.edu/donald-kennedy/ | title= Donald Kennedy, Emeritus| publisher= Stanford University| website= stanford.edu| access-date= April 22, 2020}}</ref>

==Personal life and death==
Kennedy's first marriage, to Jeanne Dewey, ended in divorce. In 1987, Kennedy married Robin Hamill.<ref name="Washington Post obituary">{{cite web |last1=Anderson |first1=Nick |title=Donald Kennedy, who led Stanford to rising national influence, dies of coronavirus at 88 |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/obituaries/donald-kennedy-who-led-stanford-to-rising-national-influence-dies-of-coronavirus-at-88/2020/04/24/f4a69c3e-859a-11ea-a3eb-e9fc93160703_story.html |website=The Washington Post |access-date=May 18, 2020 |date=April 24, 2020}}</ref> Kennedy had two children from his first marriage and two stepchildren with Hamill.<ref>{{harvnb|Kennedy|2017|pp=52, 163}}</ref>

Kennedy had a stroke in 2015 and moved in 2018 to Gordon Manor, a residential care home in [[Redwood City, California]]. He died there on April 21, 2020, of [[coronavirus disease 2019|COVID-19]], aged 88.<ref name=Dailyobit/><ref>{{cite news |last1=Sullivan |first1=Kathleen J. |title=Donald Kennedy, Stanford's eighth president, dead at 88 |url=https://news.stanford.edu/2020/04/21/donald-kennedy-stanfords-eighth-president-dead-88/ |access-date=April 21, 2020 |publisher=Stanford University |date=April 21, 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200423022101/https://news.stanford.edu/2020/04/21/donald-kennedy-stanfords-eighth-president-dead-88/# |archive-date=2020-04-23 |url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=Former Stanford President Donald Kennedy dies at 88 of COVID-19 |url=https://paloaltoonline.com/news/2020/04/21/former-stanford-president-donald-kennedy-dies-at-88-of-covid-19 |access-date=April 21, 2020 |work=Palo Alto Weekly |date=April 21, 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200423022246/https://paloaltoonline.com/news/2020/04/21/former-stanford-president-donald-kennedy-dies-at-88-of-covid-19 |archive-date=2020-04-23 |url-status=live}}</ref>
