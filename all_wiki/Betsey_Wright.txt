'''Betsey Ross Wright''' (born July 4, 1943) is an [[United States|American]] [[Lobbying in the United States|lobbyist]], [[Activism|activist]], and [[Political consulting|political consultant]] who worked more than a decade for [[Bill Clinton]] in [[Arkansas]].<ref name="Baquet 1994">Baquet (1994)</ref><ref name="Gambrell 2009">Gambrell (2009)</ref><ref name="Linn 2009">Linn (2009)</ref>  She served as [[chief of staff]] to [[List of Governors of Arkansas|Governor]] Clinton for seven years.  As deputy chair of the [[Bill Clinton presidential campaign, 1992|1992 Clinton presidential campaign]], Wright established the rapid response system that was responsible for defending Clinton's record in Arkansas and promptly answering all personal attacks on the candidate.<ref name="Cole, Castro 1992">Cole, Castro (1992)</ref>  During the 1992 campaign, Wright coined the term "[[bimbo]] eruptions" to describe rumors alleging extramarital [[Infidelity|affairs]] by Clinton.<ref name="Gambrell 2009" />  In the 1990s, Wright was an executive for The Wexler Group, a lobbying firm in [[Washington, D.C.]]<ref name="Baquet 1994"/>  She currently resides in [[Rogers, Arkansas]].<ref name="Gambrell 2009" /><ref name="Reynolds 2009" />

==Early life==
Wright was born July 4, 1943 in [[Alpine, Texas|Alpine]], [[Texas]].<ref name="Reynolds 2009">Reynolds (2009)</ref> She attended Alpine High School and received her [[Higher education in the United States|higher education]] at the [[University of Texas at Austin]], graduating with a [[Bachelor of Arts]] in 1967.<ref name="Baquet 1994"/><ref>Partheymuller (2000), p. 16</ref>

==Career==
Wright worked for [[George McGovern]]'s unsuccessful [[1972 United States presidential election|1972 presidential campaign]].<ref>Maraniss (1996), p. 358</ref>  It was during this campaign when she met Bill Clinton and [[Hillary Clinton|Hillary Rodham]].<ref name="Baquet 1994"/>  Wright became close friends with Rodham, a woman she thought had the potential to become a [[United States Senate|U.S. senator]] or America's first female president.<ref>Bernstein (2008), p. 42</ref><ref>Bernstein (2008), p. 92</ref>  In 1973, Wright moved to Washington, D.C. and began working for the [[National Women's Political Caucus]], hoping the job would further Rodham's political viability.<ref>Bernstein (2008), p. 62</ref>  During Clinton's unsuccessful run for the [[United States House of Representatives|House of Representatives]] in 1974, Wright commuted on the weekends to Arkansas to assist his campaign.<ref>Bernstein (2008), p. 98</ref>

During the late 1970s, Wright founded and served as executive director of the now-defunct National Women's Education Fund, an organization based in Washington, D.C. which raised funds for women candidates.<ref>Shanahan (1976)</ref><ref name="Harvard 1992">Harvard (1992)</ref>  While there, she designed, organized and conducted training programs throughout the country for women candidates, [[campaign manager]]s, and officeholders.<ref name="Harvard 1992"/> in 1977, Wright became an associate of the [[Women's Institute for Freedom of the Press]] (WIFP).<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.wifp.org/who-we-are/associates/|title=Associates {{!}}  The Womenâ€™s Institute for Freedom of the Press|website=www.wifp.org|language=en-US|access-date=2017-06-21|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190327102342/http://www.wifp.org/who-we-are/associates/|archive-date=2019-03-27|url-status=live}}</ref>

In 1980, ten days after his [[Arkansas gubernatorial election, 1980|defeat for re-election]] as Governor of Arkansas, Bill Clinton asked Wright to come to [[Little Rock, Arkansas|Little Rock]] and help organize the campaign's records and files.  Bringing only her suitcase, Wright traveled to Little Rock and began formulating Clinton's political comeback.<ref>Bernstein (2008), p. 161</ref>  She organized and ran Clinton's successful [[Arkansas gubernatorial election, 1982|1982 gubernatorial campaign]], as well as his re-election campaigns in 1984 and 1986.<ref name="Baquet 1994"/>  ''[[The New York Times]]'' credited Wright as being "an architect of [Clinton's] rise to power in Arkansas."<ref name="Baquet 1994"/>  In 1982, Wright was appointed as chief of staff to Governor Clinton.  Her duties included managing public support for Clinton's controversial [[education reform]]s.  Wright resigned from her position in 1989, citing exhaustion.<ref name="Barnes 2009">Barnes (2009)</ref>

In 1990, Wright was elected chair of the [[Democratic Party of Arkansas]] and was hired as its executive director.<ref name="Harvard 1992"/>  While serving as a [[Research fellow|fellow]] at [[Harvard University]]'s [[John F. Kennedy School of Government|Kennedy School of Government]] in 1992, Wright led a seminar entitled "High Tech Politics".<ref name="Harvard 1992"/>  She resigned to return to Arkansas and assist Clinton during his run for the presidency.<ref name="Cole, Castro 1992"/>  Wright served as deputy chair of the Clinton campaign during the [[1992 United States presidential election|1992 election]], but did not work for the [[Presidency of Bill Clinton|Clinton administration]].<ref name="Barnes 2009"/>

On the eve of the 1992 election, [[Anne Wexler]] offered Wright a position as executive vice president of The Wexler Group, a lobbying firm whose parent company is the [[WPP Group]].<ref name="Baquet 1994"/><ref>Martin (2009)</ref>  As a lobbyist, Wright's clients included [[American Airlines]], the [[American Dietetic Association]], the [[American Forest & Paper Association]], and [[ARCO]], among others.<ref name="Baquet 1994"/>

===Advocacy===
Wright is a supporter of the [[feminist movement]], a [[prisoners' rights]] advocate, and strongly opposes [[Capital punishment in the United States|capital punishment]].<ref name="Gambrell 2009" /><ref>Klein (1992)</ref><ref name="CNN 2009">CNN (2009)</ref>  She often visits inmates on [[Capital punishment in Arkansas|Arkansas]]' [[death row]] at the [[Varner Unit]], a [[Supermax|high-security prison]] located near [[Grady, Arkansas|Grady]].<ref name="CNN 2009" />

While visiting a death row inmate in 2005, Wright was accused of trying to smuggle money into the prison.  After the incident, Wright lost visitation privileges for six months.<ref name="Gambrell 2009" />  In August 2009, the Arkansas [[State's Attorney]]'s office filed 51 [[felony]] charges against Wright, accusing her of attempting to smuggle a knife, [[tweezers]], a [[Utility knife|boxcutter]], and 48 [[tattoo]] needles into the Varner Unit on May 22.<ref name="Linn 2009"/><ref name="Reynolds 2009" />  During an interview with the [[Associated Press]], Wright denied any wrongdoing, saying that the needles were in a bag of chips that she got from a prison vending machine.<ref name="Gambrell 2009" />  In April 2010, Wright agreed in a [[plea bargain]] to plead [[nolo contendre|no contest]] to two [[misdemeanors]]; in exchange, 48 felony counts were dropped. She was sentenced to one year of probation and a $2000 fine.<ref>KTHV 2010</ref>

==Film==
Wright appeared in the 1993 documentary film ''[[The War Room]]'', a behind the scenes look at Clinton's 1992 presidential campaign.<ref>Kelly (1992)</ref><ref>Isikoff, Thomas (1998)</ref>  Wright also appeared in the 2004 documentary ''[[The Hunting of the President|The Hunting of the President: The Ten-Year Campaign to Destroy Bill Clinton]]'', a film adaption of the book written by [[Joe Conason]] and [[Gene Lyons]],<ref>O'Sullivan (2004)</ref> and the 2012 ''[[American Experience]]'' documentary ''[[Clinton (film)|Clinton]]''.<ref>PBS (2012)</ref>

===Cultural depictions===
The character of Libby Holden in [[Joe Klein]]'s 1996 novel ''[[Primary Colors (novel)|Primary Colors]]'' is loosely based on Wright.<ref>Shapiro (1996)</ref>  In the 1998 [[Primary Colors (film)|film adaption]], Holden's character was portrayed by [[Kathy Bates]], a role which earned Bates an [[Academy Award]] nomination for [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress|Best Supporting Actress]].<ref>Weinraub (1998)</ref><ref>BBC News (1999)</ref>
