'''Eugene Harold Robinson''' (born March 12, 1954)<!--source is Ext link LCCN--> is an American newspaper [[columnist]] and an associate editor of ''[[The Washington Post]]''. His columns are syndicated to 262 newspapers by [[The Washington Post Writers Group]]. He won a [[Pulitzer Prize]] in 2009, was elected to the Pulitzer Prize Board in 2011<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.pulitzer.org/board/2011|title=Pulitzer Prize Board 2010-2011|website=pulitzer.org}}</ref> and served as its chair from 2017 to 2018.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.pulitzer.org/board/2018|title=Pulitzer Prize Board 2017-2018 |website=pulitzer.org}}</ref>

Robinson also serves as [[NBC News]] and [[MSNBC]]'s chief political analyst.

Robinson is a member of the [[National Association of Black Journalists]] and a board member of the [[IWMF]] (International Women's Media Foundation).<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.iwmf.org/staff.aspx|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100804163048/http://www.iwmf.org/staff.aspx|url-status=dead|title=IWMF website.|archivedate=August 4, 2010}}</ref>

==Biography==

===Early years and education===
Robinson was born in [[Orangeburg, South Carolina]] and attended [[Orangeburg-Wilkinson High School]], where he "was one of a handful of black students on a previously all-white campus."<ref name="columbia.edu">{{cite web|url=http://news.columbia.edu/content/Washington-Posts-Eugene-Robinson-elected-chair-of-Pulitzer-Prize-Board|title=Washington Post's Eugene Robinson Elected Chair of Pulitzer Prize Board|date=10 May 2017|publisher=}}</ref>

Before graduating from the [[University of Michigan]] in 1974, he was the first African American co-editor-in-chief of ''[[The Michigan Daily]]''.<ref name="columbia.edu"/> During the 1987-1988 academic year, he was a mid-career [[Nieman Fellow]] at [[Harvard University]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/educational-magazines/robinson-eugene-1954|title=Robinson, Eugene 1954- - Dictionary definition of Robinson, Eugene 1954- - Encyclopedia.com: FREE online dictionary|website=encyclopedia.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://niemanreports.org/articles/eugene-robinson-nf-88/|title=Eugene Robinson, NF '88|website=niemanreports.org}}</ref>

===Career===
In 1976, he began his journalism career at the ''[[San Francisco Chronicle]]''; his early assignments included the trial of publishing heiress [[Patty Hearst]]. He joined ''The Washington Post'' in 1980. Working his way up through the ranks, he was first a [[city hall]] reporter at the paper. He then became the assistant [[city editor]]; a South America correspondent based in [[Buenos Aires, Argentina]]; [[London]] bureau chief; foreign editor; and, most recently, the assistant managing editor of the paper's Style section. He began writing columns for the opinion page of the paper in 2005, also writes a twice-a-week column on politics and culture, and conducts a weekly online conversation with readers.

Robinson appears frequently as a [[Modern liberalism in the United States|liberal]] political analyst<ref>{{cite web|url=http://stateofthemedia.org/2012/cable-cnn-ends-its-ratings-slide-fox-falls-again/|title=State of the News Media - Pew Research Center|website=stateofthemedia.org}}</ref> on [[MSNBC]] cable-TV network's programs such as ''[[Morning Joe]]'', ''[[PoliticsNation with Al Sharpton]]'', ''[[The Rachel Maddow Show (TV series)|The Rachel Maddow Show]]'', ''[[Velshi & Ruhle]]'', and ''[[Andrea Mitchell Reports]]''. In addition, he is often a panelist on [[NBC]]'s [[Public affairs (broadcasting)|public affairs]] program'' [[Meet the Press]].

Robinson was awarded the 2009 [[Pulitzer Prize for Commentary]] in recognition of his columns that focused on then-Senator [[Barack Obama]] in the context of his [[2008 United States presidential election|first presidential campaign]].<ref>{{Cite news | url = https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2009/04/20/AR2009042001981.html | title = Post's Robinson Wins Commentary Pulitzer | date=  April 20, 2009 | author = Howard Kurtz | work=The Washington Post| author-link = Howard Kurtz }}</ref>
