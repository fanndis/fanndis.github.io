'''General elections''' were held in [[Bangladesh]] on 1 October 2001. The 300 single-seat constituencies of the [[Jatiya Sangsad]] were contested by 1,935 candidates representing 54 parties and including 484 independents. The elections were the second to be held under the [[caretaker government]] concept, introduced in 1996.

The result was a win for the [[2001 18 Party Alliance#Bangladeshi general election|Four Party Alliance]] of the [[Bangladesh Nationalist Party]] (BNP), [[Jamaat-e-Islami Bangladesh]], [[Jatiya Party (Manju)]] and [[Islami Oikya Jote]]. BNP leader [[Khaleda Zia]] becoming [[Prime Minister of Bangladesh|Prime Minister]].

==Background==
The Seventh Parliament headed by [[Prime Minister of Bangladesh|Prime Minister]] [[Sheikh Hasina]] was dissolved on 13 July 2001,<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://www.parliament.gov.bd/index.php/en/about-parliament/tenure-of-parliament|title=Tenure of All Parliaments|date=12 August 2018|website=Bangladesh Parliament|access-date=2018-12-27|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180812074443/http://www.parliament.gov.bd/index.php/en/about-parliament/tenure-of-parliament|archive-date=12 August 2018}}</ref> having completed its designated 5-year term (the first parliamentary administration to ever do so)<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=http://archive.ipu.org/parline-e/reports/arc/2023_01.htm|title=IPU PARLINE database: BANGLADESH (Jatiya Sangsad), Elections in 2001|website=archive.ipu.org|access-date=2018-12-27}}</ref> and power was transferred to the [[Caretaker government of Bangladesh|caretaker government]] headed by [[Latifur Rahman|Justice Latifur Rahman]].

==Electoral system==
In 2001, the 345 members of the [[Jatiya Sangsad]] consisted of 300 seats directly elected by [[first-past-the-post voting]] in single-member constituencies,<ref>[http://archive.ipu.org/parline-e/reports/2023_B.htm Electoral system] IPU</ref> and 45 seats reserved for women. The reserved seats were distributed based on the national vote share.<ref>{{cite book |last1=Ahmed |first1=Nizam |last2=Hasan |first2=Sadik |year=2018 |chapter=''Alangkar'' or ''Ahangkar?'' Reserved-Seat Women Members in the Bangladesh Parliament |editor-last=Ahmed |editor-first=Nizam |title=Women in Governing Institions in South Asia |chapter-url=https://www.springer.com/cda/content/document/cda_downloaddocument/9783319574745-c2.pdf?SGWID=0-0-45-1615881-p180810316 |publisher=Springer |page=18 |isbn=978-3-319-57474-5 |doi=10.1007/978-3-319-57475-2_2}}</ref>

==Conduct==
The international<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.ecoi.net/en/file/local/1412745/625_tmpphpGBRYSw.pdf |title=Bangladesh parliamentary Elections 1 October 2001: Final Report |website=EU Election Observation Mission |access-date=2018-12-27}}</ref> and national monitors declared the polling free and fair even though the Awami League alleged massive vote rigging by the BNP. The accusation was denied by the Chief Election Commissioner, who declared the charges "baseless".<ref>{{Cite web| url=http://edition.cnn.com/2001/WORLD/asiapcf/south/10/05/bangla.victory/index.html|title=Zia wins power in Bangladesh |date=5 October 2001|website=CNN |access-date=2018-12-27}}</ref> International observers, from the European Union, the United Nations and the Carter Center<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.cartercenter.org/news/documents/doc92.html|title=Postelection Statement by Former U.S. President Jimmy Carter on Bangladesh Elections, Oct. 5, 2001|website=www.cartercenter.org|access-date=2018-12-27}}</ref> of former US President [[Jimmy Carter]], also praised the heavy voter turnout, which was 75%.<ref name=":2" />

The BNP were the clear winners in terms of seats, winning a secure majority with 193 (of 300) seats. BNP's allied parties [[Jamaat-e-Islami Bangladesh]], [[Jatiya Party (Manju)]] and [[Islami Oikya Jote]] also won a combined 23 seats, bringing the alliance total to 216 seats. As a result of the [[first-past-the-post voting]] system in Bangladesh, [[Bangladesh Awami League|Awami League]] only secured 62 seats, despite a difference in popular vote share of only ≈1.4%.  Voter turnout was very high at 75%.<ref name=":2" />

Of the 300 directly elected seats, only seven were won by women.<ref name="WomenRep">{{cite journal |last1=Kumar Panday |first1=Pranab |date=1 September 2008 |title=Representation without Participation: Quotas for Women in Bangladesh |journal=International Political Science Review |volume= 29|issue= 4|pages= 489–512|doi= 10.1177/0192512108095724}}</ref> This parliament marked an increase in the number of reserved seats for women (which are in addition to the 300 directly elected seats) from 30 to 45. Of these 45 reserved seats, 36 were awarded to BNP.<ref name=":2" />

==Aftermath==
There were [[2001 Bangladesh post-election violence|reports of violence]] targeting minority communities in the immediate wake of the elections.<ref>[https://bdnews24.com/bangladesh/2011/04/24/2001-violence-on-hinduscaretakers-bnp-jamaat-blamed 2001 violence on HindusCaretakers, BNP, Jamaat blamed| bdnews24, 24 April 2011]</ref>

With a clear majority BNP leader [[Khaleda Zia]] was invited to form a government and on 10 October 2001, was sworn in as Prime Minister and formed her [[Cabinet of Bangladesh|Cabinet]], which included members of her allied parties. The first sitting of the Eighth Parliament occurred on 28 October 2001<ref name=":1" /> with [[Jamiruddin Sircar]] as its new Speaker.

Zia's administration completed a full five-year term, running from 28 October 2001 to 27 October 2006. However, disputes over the selection of a [[Caretaker government of Bangladesh|caretaker government]], with disagreements between the parties over their neutrality, led to the [[2006–08 Bangladeshi political crisis]], which eventually resulted in military intervention. New elections would not be held until [[2008 Bangladeshi general election|December 2008]].
