'''Rose Farwell Chatfield-Taylor''' (March 7, 1870 – April 5, 1918) was an American sportswoman, bookbinder, suffragist, and socialite, and co-founder of a [[Onwentsia Club|golf club in Illinois named Onwentsia]].

== Early life ==
Rose Farwell was born a twin in [[Lake Forest, Illinois]], the daughter of [[Charles B. Farwell|Charles Benjamin Farwell]] and Mary Eveline Smith Farwell. Her father was a United States Senator from Illinois. She and her older sisters [[Anna de Koven]] and Grace were considered fashionable beauties in Chicago society, and all enjoyed various sports. Anna became a novelist, and married composer [[Reginald De Koven|Reginald de Koven]]. Grace became the first president of the [[Art Institute of Chicago]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=https://www.lflbhistory.org/media-gallery/detail/251/1366|title=Farwell-Winston Family|last=|first=|date=|work=Lake Forest-Lake Bluff History Center|access-date=July 10, 2019}}</ref>

Rose attended [[Ferry Hall School|Ferry Hall]] and [[Lake Forest College]] for her schooling.<ref name=":1">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=MQnhIJhYbbMC&lpg=PA173|title=Woman's Who's who of America: A Biographical Dictionary of Contemporary Women of the United States and Canada|last=Leonard|first=John William|date=1914|publisher=American Commonwealth Company|year=|isbn=|location=|pages=173–174|language=en}}</ref> Portraits of Rose Farwell as a young woman were painted by [[John Elliott (artist)|John Elliott]] and [[Adolfo Müller-Ury]].<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://www.classicchicagomagazine.com/farwells-twilight-generation-it-couple/|title=Farwells 5: Twilight Generation "It" Couple|last=Whitfield|first=Kay|website=Classic Chicago Magazine|language=en-US|access-date=2019-07-10}}</ref>

== Career ==
Because of Rose's and her new husband's interest in golf, the family arranged for [[Charles B. Macdonald|Charles B. MacDonald]] to design a golf course in 1892.<ref name=":0" /><ref name=":3">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=DrjWHvknMRIC&lpg=PA10&ots=XWZvpghv0y&dq=Rose%20Farwell%20Chatfield%20Taylor&pg=PA67|title=Classic Country Estates of Lake Forest: Architecture and Landscape Design, 1856-1940|last=Coventry|first=Kim|last2=Meyer|first2=Daniel|last3=Miller|first3=Arthur H.|date=2003|publisher=W. W. Norton & Company|year=|isbn=9780393730999|location=|pages=67–68, 269|language=en}}</ref> In 1895, Rose Farwell Chatfield-Taylor and her husband were among the founders of the Onwentsia Club, a golf club in Lake Forest.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=N0fQ1c1KVV8C&lpg=PA71|title=Creating Chicago's North Shore: A Suburban History|last=Ebner|first=Michael H.|date=1988|publisher=University of Chicago Press|year=|isbn=9780226182056|location=|pages=71–72|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.onwentsiaclub.org/Default.aspx?p=DynamicModule&pageid=383932&ssid=302711&vnf=1|title=History|last=|first=|date=|website=Onwentsia Club|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-07-10}}</ref> She won several golf events, owned a racehorse, and played lawn tennis. She was a [[Woman's club movement|clubwoman]], and served as vice president of the Northside Chicago branch of the Illinois Woman Suffrage League.<ref name=":1" />

The Chatfield-Taylors were also members of the "Little Room", a social gathering of artists, writers, and performers. They kept studios in the [[Fine Arts Building (Chicago)|Fine Arts Building]] on [[Michigan Avenue (Chicago)|Michigan Avenue]], where Rose Chatfield-Taylor ran a [[bookbinding]] business called the Rose Bindery, "a shop where appreciated and clothed in beautiful and appropriate bindings."<ref name=":1" /><ref name=":2" /><ref name=":4">{{Cite journal|last=Chatfield-Taylor|first=Mrs. H. C.|date=October 1905|title=Bookbinding|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=INE9AQAAMAAJ&lpg=PA109|journal=Sketch Book|volume=5|pages=109–116|via=}}</ref> She learned the craft in Paris, and was a member of the [[Guild of Bookworkers]] from 1906 to 1910.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ilab.org/fr/node/57547|title=The Strange Suicide of an Early 20th C. Female Rare Book Binder|last=Gertz|first=Stephen J.|date=February 12, 2014|website=International League of Antiquarian Booksellers|language=|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-07-10}}</ref> She wrote about bookbinding for the ''Sketch Book'' magazine.<ref name=":4" />

== Personal life ==
In 1890, Rose Farwell married wealthy writer, social host, and sportsman [[Hobart Chatfield-Taylor]].<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ZnuYKJSoHCMC&lpg=PA106&ots=8sJdAND-Fy&dq=Rose%20Farwell%20Chatfield%20Taylor&pg=PA106|title=Dictionary of Midwestern Literature, Volume 1: The Authors|last=Greasley|first=Philip A.|date=2001-05-30|publisher=Indiana University Press|year=|isbn=9780253108418|location=|pages=106|language=en}}</ref> Together, they were the parents of three sons and one daughter:

* Adelaide Chatfield-Taylor (1891–1982), who was awarded a [[Croix de Guerre]] for her work running a canteen in Boston during [[World War II]]. She married Hendricks Hallett Whitman in 1912. They divorced in 1932,<ref name="HHWObit1950">{{cite news |title=H.H. WHITMAN, 66, TEXTILE MAN, DEAD; Chairman of William Whitman Co., Manufacturers, Succumbs in France on World Cruise |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1950/03/19/archives/hh-whitman-66-textile-man-dead-chairman-of-william-whitman-co.html?searchResultPosition=3 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=19 March 1950}}</ref> and she married William Davies Sohier Jr. in 1940. Her granddaughter is politician and businesswoman [[Meg Whitman]].<ref name="1980Engagement">{{cite news |title=Meg Whitman to Wed June 7 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1980/04/20/archives/meg-whitman-to-wed-june-7.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=20 April 1980}}</ref>
* [[Wayne Chatfield-Taylor]] (1893–1967), who served as Under Secretary of Commerce and Assistant Secretary of the Treasury under President [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]].<ref name="WCTObit1967">{{cite news |last1=Times |first1=Special to The New York TimesThe New York |title=Wayne Chatfield Taylor Dead; Roosevelt and Truman Aide, 73; Banker Held Major Posts in Commerce, Treasury and the Export-Import Bank In Many Public Posts Envoy at Trade Meetings |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1967/11/23/archives/wayne-chatfield-taylor-dead-roosevelt-and-truman-aide-73-banker.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=23 November 1967}}</ref>
* Otis Chatfield-Taylor (1899–1948),<ref name="OCTObit1948">{{cite news |last1=TIMES |first1=Special to THE NEW YORK |title=WRITER IS KILLED WHEN AUTO SKIDS; Otis Chatfieid-Taylor, Long Known in Theatre and Press, Fatally Hurt at Croton |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1948/01/18/archives/writer-is-killed-when-auto-skids-otis-chatfieidtaylor-long-known-in.html?searchResultPosition=6 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=18 January 1948}}</ref> a writer, playwright, editor, theatrical producer who married Janet Benson in 1931. They divorced in 1934,<ref name="1934Divorce">{{cite news |title=THREE DIVORCES IN RENO.; Chatfield-Taylors, R.E. Sherwoods and J.D. Pierces Parted. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1934/06/16/archives/three-divorces-in-reno-chatfieldtaylors-re-sherwoods-and-jd-pierces.html?searchResultPosition=9 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=16 June 1934}}</ref> and he married Marochka Borisovna Anisfeld,<ref>{{cite news |title=Paid Notice: Deaths CHATFIELD TAYLOR, MAROCHKA |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1999/11/04/classified/paid-notice-deaths-chatfield-taylor-marochka.html?searchResultPosition=5 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=4 November 1999}}</ref> a daughter of [[Boris Anisfeld]], in 1936.<ref name="1936Wedding">{{cite news |title=MAROCHKA ANISFELD WED; Daughter of Chicago Artist Bride of Otis Chatfield-Taylor, |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1936/05/07/archives/marochka-anisfeld-wed-daughter-of-chicago-artist-bride-of-otis.html?searchResultPosition=1 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=7 May 1936}}</ref>
* Robert Farwell Chatfield-Taylor (1908–1980), who married Valborg Edison Palmer in 1928.<ref name="1928Wedding">{{cite news |title=VALBORG E. PALMER WED.; Becomes Bride of Robert Farwell Chatfield-Taylor. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1928/11/08/archives/valborg-e-palmer-wed-becomes-bride-of-robert-farwell.html?searchResultPosition=1 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=8 November 1928}}</ref>

She died in Santa Barbara in 1918,<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/33743437/rose_farwell_chatfield_taylor_1918/|title=Mrs. Chatfield-Taylor is Dead in California|last=|first=|date=April 6, 1918|work=Chicago Tribune|access-date=July 10, 2019|page=1|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> aged 48 years, from pneumonia after an appendectomy.<ref name=":2" /><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/33743341/rose_farwell_chatfieldtaylor_1918/|title=Estate of Woman Worth More than Million|last=|first=|date=May 7, 1918|work=Salt Lake Telegram|access-date=July 10, 2019|page=6|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> In her memory, her sisters funded a visiting nurse position in Chicago, beginning in the fall of 1918.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/t/text/idx/f/flu/2180flu.0015.812/--twenty-ninth-annual-report-for-year-ending-december-31-1918?q1=Organization+--+Visiting+Nurse+Association&view=text&seq=15&size=175|title=Twenty-Ninth Annual Report for Year Ending December 31, 1918, The Visiting Nurse Association of Chicago|last=|first=|date=1918-12-31|website=Influenza Encyclopedia|language=en|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-07-10}}</ref> Her sister's book, ''A Cloud of Witnesses'' (1920), recounts Anna de Koven's efforts to contact the spirit of the late Rose Farwell Chatfield-Taylor.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://archive.org/details/acloudwitnesses00kovegoog|quote=Anna de Koven.|title=A Cloud of Witnesses|last=Koven|first=Anna De|date=1920|publisher=Dutton|language=en}}</ref>
