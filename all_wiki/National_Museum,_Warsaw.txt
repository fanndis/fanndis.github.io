The '''National Museum in Warsaw''' ({{lang-pl|Muzeum Narodowe w Warszawie}}), popularly abbreviated as '''MNW''', is a [[national museum]] in [[Warsaw]], one of the largest museums in [[Poland]] and the largest in the capital. It comprises a rich collection of [[ancient art]] ([[Art of ancient Egypt|Egyptian]], [[Art in ancient Greece|Greek]], [[Roman art|Roman]]), counting about 11,000 pieces,<ref name="Murawska-Muthesius 2001 31">{{Harvnb|Murawska-Muthesius|2001|p=31}}</ref> an extensive gallery of [[Culture of Poland#Art|Polish painting]] since the 16th century and a collection of foreign painting ([[Art of Italy|Italian]], [[French art|French]], [[Flemish Baroque painting|Flemish]], [[Dutch School (painting)|Dutch]], [[German art|German]] and [[Russian culture|Russian]]) including some paintings from Adolf Hitler's private collection,<ref name=Schwarz /> ceded to the Museum by the [[Allied-occupied Germany|American authorities]] in post-war Germany.<ref name=Lorentz /> The museum is also home to [[Numismatics|numismatic]] collections, a gallery of [[applied art]]s and a department of oriental art, with the largest collection of [[Chinese art]] in Poland, comprising some 5,000 objects.<ref name=Oriental />

The Museum boasts the [[Faras Gallery at the National Museum in Warsaw|Faras Gallery]] with Europe's largest collection of [[Nubia]]n Christian art and the Gallery of Medieval Art with artefacts from all regions historically associated with Poland, supplemented by selected works created in other regions of Europe.<ref name=warsawtour1 />

==History==
The National Museum in Warsaw was established on 20 May 1862, as the "Museum of Fine Arts, Warsaw", and in 1916 renamed "National Museum, Warsaw"<ref>{{Harvnb|Folga-Januszewska|2008|p=9}}</ref> (with the inclusion of collections from museums and cultural institutions such as the Society of Care for Relics of the Past, the Museum of Antiquity at [[Warsaw University]], the Museum of the [[Zachęta|Society for Encouragement of the Fine Arts]], and the Museum of Industry and Agriculture).

[[File:Warsaw 1938.jpg|thumb|left|Main facade of the National Museum, 1938]]

The collection, on [[Aleje Jerozolimskie|Jerusalem Avenue]], is housed in a building designed by Tadeusz Tolwiński, developed between 1927 and 1938 (earlier the museum had been located at ''ulica Podwale 15''). In 1932 an exhibition of decorative art opened in the two earlier erected wings of the building.<ref name="Murawska-Muthesius 2001 31"/> The new building was inaugurated on 18 June 1938. The purpose-built [[Modernism|modernistic edifice]], was situated on the edge of Na Książęcem Park established between 1776&ndash;79 for Prince [[Kazimierz Poniatowski]].<ref name=warsawtour1 /> From 1935 the museum director was [[Stanisław Lorentz]], who directed an effort to save the most valuable works of art during World War II.<ref name=warsawtour1 />

During the [[invasion of Poland]] the building was damaged and after the [[Siege of Warsaw (1939)|Siege of Warsaw]] the collection was looted by the [[Gestapo]] led by Nazi historian [[Dagobert Frey]], who had already prepared a meticulous list of the most valuable artwork on official visits from Germany in 1937.<ref name=warsaw_accuses /> The Gestapo headquarters presented [[Rembrandt]]'s portrait of Maerten Soolmans as a gift to [[Hans Frank]] in occupied [[Kraków]] and packed everything else to be shipped to Berlin.<ref>{{Harvnb|Folga-Januszewska|2008|p=11}}</ref> After the war the Polish Government, under the supervision of Professor Lorentz, retrieved many of the works seized by the Germans.<ref name="Folga-Januszewska 2008 12">{{Harvnb|Folga-Januszewska|2008|p=12}}</ref> More than 5,000 artifacts are still missing.<ref name=Kuhnke />

Many works of art of, at that time unknown or of uncertain provenance<ref name="Folga-Januszewska 2008 12"/> (e.g. originating from Nazi German art repositories in Polish [[Recovered Territories]] in [[Kamieniec Ząbkowicki|Kamenz]], [[Kartuzy|Karthaus]], [[Lubomierz|Liebenthal]] and [[Roztoka, Lower Silesian Voivodeship|Rohnstock]] among others) were nationalized by the [[People's Republic of Poland|communist authorities]]<ref name=Trosborg /> using subsequent decrees and acts<ref name=Hambura /> from 1945, 1946 and 1958 and were included in the museum collection as so-called ''abandoned property''.<ref name=polskalokalna /> At present, the collection of the National Museum in Warsaw includes over 780,000 items displayed in many permanent galleries, including the Professor [[Kazimierz Michałowski]] Faras Gallery and galleries given over to ancient art, medieval art, painting, goldsmithing, decorative art and oriental art, as well as many temporary exhibitions.

In 2008-2013 the "Polish Archaeological Mission "[[Tyritake]]" of National Museum in Warsaw" conducted works at [[Tyritake]], [[Crimea]]. In 2016 the "Polish Archaeological Mission "Olbia" commenced works at [[Olbia (archaeological site)]], [[Ukraine]]. Both are headed by [[Alfred Twardecki]] curator of the Ancient Art Gallery. In 2010 the National Museum, as one of the first state institutions in the world, held an exhibition entirely dedicated to [[Homoeroticism|homoerotic]] art - ''Ars Homo Erotica''.<ref name=homoerotic /> Since the 2011&ndash;12 renovation, the museum is also considered as one of the most modern in Europe with a computer-led [[LED lamp|LED lighting]] allowing to enhance unique qualities of every painting and exhibit.<ref name=warsawtour />

In 1945 the National Museum took over the historic [[Nieborów Palace]] in the village of the same name and made them its subsidiaries. In the early perioid, the deputy curator there was the writer [[Mieczysław Smolarski]].

==Permanent galleries==
[[File:Faras Gallery National Museum in Warsaw 2016 02.JPG|thumb|[[Faras Gallery]]]]
[[File:Rome Hermes sitting on a ram.jpg|thumb|[[Hermes]] Sitting on a Ram, 2nd century BC]]
In 2012 the permanent galleries underwent revolutionary changes.<ref name=rp /> The [[curator]]s of the museum re-arranged it and supplemented it with new works from the museum's warehouses.<ref name=rp /> Paintings were not hung chronologically, but thematically: [[Genre works|genre painting]], [[still life]]s, [[Landscape art|landscapes]], [[cityscape]]s, biblical, mythological, [[Depictions of nudity|nudes]].<ref name=rp /> Works by Italian, Flemish, Dutch, German and Polish artists were hung together, making it easy to observe and compare similarities and differences.<ref name=rp /> The Gallery of Ancient Art, [[Faras Gallery at the National Museum in Warsaw|Faras Gallery]], Gallery of Medieval Art, Gallery of Old Masters, Gallery of 19th-century Art and the Gallery of 20th and 21st-century, includes the works of Polish painters and sculptors and are displayed in the context of art in other countries and in different epochs.<ref name=rp /> The galleries reflect the richness and diversity of traditions and historical experiences of individual nations, which, however, built their cultural identity on the same foundation of [[Classical antiquity|Greco-Roman antiquity]] and the [[Christianity|Christian religion]].<ref name=19century />

The Gallery of Medieval Art mainly presents objects from the late [[Middle Ages]] (14th-15th century), originating from different regions of today's Poland, as well as several examples of [[Art of Europe|western European art]].<ref name=Dziewulska /> These works were originally designed almost exclusively for churches.<ref name=Dziewulska /> The exhibition was designed to allow the audience to understand the role of art in the religious life of the Middle Ages.<ref name=Dziewulska />

The gallery presents trans-regional phenomena from the 12th–14th centuries, such as the distinction of figurative sculpture from architecture in the [[Romanesque art|Romanesque era]], [[Central Europe]]an sculpture from the circle of the ''Madonnas on the Lion'', and so-called ''[[International Gothic]]'', also referred to as a courtly style.<ref name=Dziewulska /> Many of the works included in the exhibition underline a distinct character of Central European regions such as [[Silesia]] between 1440 and 1520 (with large quartered [[polyptych]]s, [[epitaph]]s, [[Votive offering|votive]] and didactic plaques, and Ways of the Cross), [[Lesser Poland]], [[Greater Poland]] and [[Kuyavia]] between 1440 and 1520 (with [[altarpiece]]s and devotional paintings) and [[Gdańsk]] and the [[Hanseatic League|Hanseatic region]] between 1420 and 1520 (with large altars from [[Hamburg]] and [[Pomerania]]).<ref name=Dziewulska />

[[File:Carel Fabritius, Wskrzeszenie Łazarza.jpg|thumb|upright|left|''The Raising of Lazarus'' by [[Carel Fabritius]] is displayed in the Gallery of Old Masters]]

The new techniques implemented in the gallery allow the unabridged presentation of large polyptychs, such as the famous Grudziądz Polyptych, including the reverse of the wings.<ref name=Dziewulska /> The new arrangement of the exhibition was designed by WWAA.<ref name=Dziewulska />

The Gallery of Old Masters on the second floor was conceived from the former Gallery of Decorative Art, Gallery of Old European Painting and the Gallery of Old Polish and European Portrait in 2016.<ref name=old_masters/> It combines species of pictorial art - [[painting]], [[sculpture]], [[drawing]]s and [[Printmaking|print]]s with [[craft]]s in reference to the very notion of "[[art]]" which originally meant [[Workmanship|craftsmanship]].<ref name=old_masters/> Painting and sculpture with its representational character and imitation of reality (''[[mimesis]]'') was united with [[decorative arts]] in common goals and functions as well as in spaces where they were collected and exhibited.<ref name=old_masters/> These "social spaces" have provided the key to the division of the gallery: 1. palace, villa, court; 2. church, chapel and domestic altar; 3. the city. In other words: 1. court culture; 2. religious culture; 3. city culture.<ref name=old_masters/>

In the redesigned gallery, the works are presented not according to national schools, but as a confrontation of artistic circles of the South and North.<ref name=european /> The new system reflects the hierarchy of the genres created by [[Renaissance art]] theory and the former function of the paintings.<ref name=european /> The aim of this exhibition is to show, for what purpose and for which recipients, works of art were created.<ref name=european />

[[File:Muzeum Narodowe w Warszawie Galeria Sztuki XIX wieku.JPG|thumb|Gallery of 19th-century Art]]

The gallery shows a variety of effigies, reflecting the multiplicity of social, political and private functions of [[Portrait painting|portraiture]].<ref name=portrait /> The exhibition opens with monumental images of courtly and aristocratic portraits and busts compiled with some examples of traditional Polish and Western European portraits ''en pied'' and followed with smaller, less formal, or private portraits, coffin portraits and 18th-century portraits followed with miniature portraits and paintings of [[Stanisław August Poniatowski|Stanislaus Augustus]]' era on the first floor.<ref name=portrait />

The core of the new exhibition of the Gallery of 19th-century Art, presenting the main trends shaping the art in the nineteenth century, is the work of Polish painters and sculptors, which is present in the context of selected works by representatives of other nationalities.<ref name=19century /> Confrontation of works by artists from different European countries shows their artistic aspirations, universal ideas or [[symbol]]s, similar experiments carried out independently or in workshop practice.<ref name=19century /> One of Poland's largest [[canvas painting]]s, the ''[[Battle of Grunwald (painting)|Battle of Grunwald]]'' by [[Jan Matejko]] (426&nbsp;cm × 987&nbsp;cm (168 in × 389 in)), is displayed in the Gallery of 19th-century Art.<ref name=warsawtour1 />

Collections of [[Modern art|modern]] and [[contemporary art]] are among the largest in Poland.<ref name=20century /> Paintings, sculptures, prints and drawings from the 1920s and 1930s, Polish [[Experimental film|avant-garde film]], photographs, photomontages, along with selected works of the 1980s independent culture, video and performance of the last forty years are exhibited in a 700 m{{Smallsup|2}} gallery.<ref name=20century /> Information about the selected objects is available through mobile applications and selected works are visualized and described by the gallery curator thanks to [[augmented reality]].<ref name=20century />

==Collection==
[[File:Grudziądz Polyptych 15.jpg|thumb|260px|[[Grudziądz]] Polyptych, ca. 1400]]
Collections of the National Museum in Warsaw comprise about 830,000 exhibits of Polish and foreign art, from antiquity to the present, and include paintings, sculptures, drawings, engravings, photographs and coins as well as objects of applied art and design.<ref name=Muzeum/>

The Collection of Ancient and East Christian Art numbers some 24,000 exhibits, being the largest and most important of its kind in Poland.<ref name=Starozytnej/> The collection of [[fresco]]es from the Christian cathedral in Faras (ancient [[Faras|Pachoras]] in today's [[Sudan]]) and a collection of painted [[Pottery of ancient Greece|Greek vases]] are among the most important.<ref name=Starozytnej/>

The origins of the Old European Painting Collection, comprising 3,700 paintings, dates back to 1862 and the establishment of the Museum of Fine Arts, when 36 Italian, Dutch and German paintings from Johann Peter Weyer collection in Cologne were acquired.<ref name=Europejskiej/> The Museum came into possession of the works of such masters as [[Pinturicchio]], [[Cornelis van Haarlem]] and [[Jacob Jordaens]].<ref name=Europejskiej/> The collection was enlarged through purchases, donations and deposits.<ref name=Europejskiej/> The most significant acquisition was the collection of paintings of Pietro Fiorentini, donated in 1858 to the [[Academy of Fine Arts in Warsaw|School of Fine Arts in Warsaw]], and given to the Museum in 1879.<ref name=Europejskiej/> The collection was further expanded through the purchase of paintings from the collection of Wojciech Kolasiński in the years 1877–1896 and bequests by Cyprian Lachnicki in 1906 including ''Flagellation of Christ'' by [[Pedro Campaña|Pieter de Kempeneer]], ''Portrait of a man in a yellow jerkin'' by [[Hans Leonhard Schäufelein|Hans Schäufelein]], ''Expulsion from Paradise'' by [[Pier Francesco Mola]] and ''Academic study'' by [[Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres]].<ref name=Europejskiej/> 

In 1935 the Museum purchased a large collection of [[:ru:Поплавский, Иван Иванович|Jan Popławski]], including ''Portrait of Admiral'' by [[Tintoretto]],<ref name=Europejskiej/> and in 1961 a collection of [[Gabriela Zapolska]], including several paintings by [[Paul Sérusier]]. The collection of Polish modern art gained a more international context with the purchase of ''Portrait of Tadeusz Makowski'' by [[Marcel Gromaire]] in 1959<ref name=Bialostocki/> and ''Lassitude'' by an [[Art Deco]] painter [[Tamara de Lempicka]] in 1979,<ref name=delempicka/> both on permanent display. The museum also features the works of other major European artists such as [[Rembrandt]], [[Sandro Botticelli]], [[Lucas Cranach the Elder]], [[Auguste Renoir]], [[Gustave Courbet]], [[Angelica Kauffmann]], [[Luca Giordano]], [[Joos van Cleve]], [[Jan Brueghel the Elder]], [[David Teniers the Younger]] and [[Gaspare Traversi]].

On the top floor a large gallery displays Polish [[history painting]]s of the 19th century, many of which are well-known to Poles, and vividly depict a nationalist take on incidents from the nation's history.
