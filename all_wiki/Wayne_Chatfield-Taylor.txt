'''Wayne Chatfield-Taylor''' (December 19, [[1893]] – November 22, [[1967]])<ref name="WCTObit1967"/> was [[Under Secretary of Commerce]] and [[Assistant Secretary of the Treasury]] under President [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]].

==Early life==
Chatfield-Taylor was born in [[Chicago, Illinois]] on December 19, 1893 . He was the eldest of three sons and one daughter born to [[Rose Chatfield-Taylor|Rose (née Farwell) Chatfield-Taylor]] by [[Hobart Chatfield-Taylor]], an author and biographer who was considered a top authority on [[Molière]].<ref name="1909Review">{{cite news |title=Brief Reviews of Lately Published Books |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1909/04/17/archives/brief-reviews-of-lately-published-books.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=17 April 1909}}</ref> His siblings were Robert, Otis, and Adelaide Chatfield-Taylor (wife of Hendricks Hallett Whitman and William Davies Sohier and grandmother of [[Meg Whitman]]).<ref name="1980Engagement">{{cite news |title=Meg Whitman to Wed June 7 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1980/04/20/archives/meg-whitman-to-wed-june-7.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=20 April 1980}}</ref> After his mother died in 1918, his father married Estelle (née Barbour) Stillman, the widow of [[George S. Stillman]] and daughter of [[George Harrison Barbour]],<ref name="Metal1920">{{cite book |title=The Metal Worker, Plumber, and Steam Fitter |date=1920 |publisher=D. Williams Company |page=51 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=NZxCAQAAMAAJ&pg=PA51 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |language=en}}</ref> in 1920.<ref name="HCCTObit1945"/><ref name="Greasley2001">{{cite book |last1=Greasley |first1=Philip A. |title=Dictionary of Midwestern Literature, Volume 1: The Authors |date=2001 |publisher=[[Indiana University Press]] |isbn=978-0-253-10841-8 |page=106 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ZnuYKJSoHCMC&pg=PA106 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |language=en}}</ref>

His paternal grandparents were Henry Hobart Taylor and Adelaide ([[née]] Chatfield) Taylor. His father added "Chatfield" to his surname as the stipulation of a large inheritance from his maternal uncle, Wayne Chatfield.<ref name="HCCTObit1945">{{cite news  |title=H.C. Chatfield-Taylor; Author, Authority on Moliere, Dies in California at 80 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/01/17/archives/hc-chatfieldtaylor-author-authority-on-moliere-dies-in-california.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=17 January 1945}}</ref>  His maternal grandparents were [[U.S. Senator]] [[Charles B. Farwell]] (brother of [[John V. Farwell]]) and Mary Eveline (née Smith) Farwell.<ref name="Kelsey2015">{{cite book |last1=Kelsey |first1=Susan L. |last2=Miller |first2=Arthur H. |title=Legendary Locals of Lake Forest |date=2015 |publisher=Arcadia Publishing |isbn=978-1-4396-5400-2 |page=157 |url=https://www.google.com/books/edition/Legendary_Locals_of_Lake_Forest/v11UCwAAQBAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&dq=Anna+Farwell+Reginald+de+Koven.&pg=PT157 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |language=en}}</ref> His maternal aunt [[Anna de Koven|Anna Farwell]] married the composer [[Reginald de Koven]].<ref name="woodmereartmuseum">{{cite web |title=De Koven, Anna Farwell |url=https://woodmereartmuseum.org/the-violet-oakley-experience/people/de-koven-anna-farwell |website=woodmereartmuseum.org |publisher=[[Woodmere Art Museum]] |accessdate=6 June 2020 |language=en-gb}}</ref>

Wayne was a graduate of [[St. Mark's School (Massachusetts)|St. Mark's School]] in [[Southborough, Massachusetts]] and of [[Yale University]]. During [[World War I]], he served overseas as a Lieutenant in the [[U.S. Army]].<ref name="WCTObit1967"/>

==Career==
Chatfield-Taylor began his business career in 1916 with the Central Trust Company of Illinois (which had been formed in 1901 by former [[Office of the Comptroller of the Currency|Comptroller of the Currency]] [[Charles G. Dawes]] before he became [[Vice President of the United States]] under [[Calvin Coolidge]]). Later, he worked for [[Marshall Field III|Field]], Glore, Ward & Co., an investment banking house in Chicago, becoming vice president of the business in 1927. He was also vice president of the Chicago Investors corporation and a director of the People's Trust and Savings Bank.<ref name="WCTObit1967"/>

He also served the government in administrative and advisory capacities from 1933 to 1952. He first joined President [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]]'s [[New Deal]] administration in 1933 as assistant to the Administrator of the [[Agricultural Adjustment Administration]] before becoming special advisor to the President on foreign affairs. He joined the [[Export-Import Bank of the United States|Export-Import Bank]] as a vice president 1935 before succeeding [[L. W. Robert Jr.]] as the [[Assistant Secretary of the Treasury]] under Secretary [[Henry Morgenthau Jr.]] in 1936.<ref name="1936Treasury">{{cite news |title=W.C. Taylor Chosen for Treasury Post; Chicagoan to Leave Export-Import Banks for Assistant Secretaryship. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1936/02/13/archives/wc-taylor-chosen-for-treasury-post-chicagoan-to-leave-exportimport.html?searchResultPosition=14 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=13 February 1936}}</ref><ref name="1936Aide">{{cite news |title=W.C. Taylor New Treasury Aide |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1936/02/14/archives/wc-taylor-new-treasury-aide.html?searchResultPosition=11 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=14 February 1936}}</ref> He resigned in February 1939 over differences over Treasury policies with Secretary Morgenthau to become the European delegate of the [[American Red Cross]].<ref name="WCT1945">{{cite news |title=W.C. Taylor in View as Aide to Clayton |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/04/04/archives/wc-taylor-in-view-as-aide-to-clayton.html?searchResultPosition=10 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=4 April 1945}}</ref>

He returned to government service in October 1940 becoming the Under Secretary of Commerce under Secretary [[Jesse H. Jones]] in 1940,<ref>[http://chronicles.dickinson.edu/studentwork/engage/hartigan/taylor.htm Wayne-Chatfield Taylor]</ref><ref>[https://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/washingtonpost_historical/access/216824692.html?dids=216824692:216824692&FMT=ABS&FMTS=ABS:FT&date=OCT+22%2C+1940&author=&pub=The+Washington+Post&desc=Wayne+Chatfield+Taylor+Named+Undersecretary+of+Commerce&pqatl=google Wayne Chatfield Taylor Named Undersecretary of Commerce]</ref> serving until 1945.<ref name="1942Commerce">{{cite news |title=World Commerce Program is Given; Chatfield-Taylor Tells Foreign Traders Pattern of Pan-Americanism Is Key |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1942/05/19/archives/world-commerce-program-is-given-chatfieldtaylor-tells-foreign.html?searchResultPosition=8 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=19 May 1942}}</ref> In 1944, he was the federal official who took charge of [[Montgomery Ward|Montgomery Ward & Co.]]'s Chicago plant after it was ordered seized by the government and the firm's chairman, [[Sewell Avery]], was removed form the premises by soldiers.<ref>{{cite news |title=FDR seizes control of Montgomery Ward |url=http://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/fdr-seizes-control-of-montgomery-ward |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[History (American TV network)|History]] |date=November 16, 2009 |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=FDR seizes control of Montgomery Ward Dec. 27, 1944 |url=http://www.politico.com/news/stories/1207/7557.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=Politico |language=en}}</ref>  In April 1945,<ref name="1945Post">{{cite news |title=Talked of for Bank Post; W.C. Taylor's Selection for Export-Import Forecast |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/04/06/archives/talked-of-for-bank-post-wc-taylors-selection-for-exportimport.html?searchResultPosition=8 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=6 April 1945}}</ref><ref name="1945Resignation">{{cite news  |title=Taylor Resigns Commerce Post; Named Wallace Aide |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/04/12/archives/taylor-resigns-commerce-post-named-wallace-aide.html?searchResultPosition=7 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=12 April 1945}}</ref> resigned from the Commerce Department to return to [[Export-Import Bank of the United States|Export-Import Bank]] after being elected president.<ref name="1945Appointment">{{cite news  |title=Taylor Heads Trade Bank |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/04/13/archives/taylor-heads-trade-bank.html?searchResultPosition=5 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=13 April 1945}}</ref> He served in that role until the position was abolished under the [[Export–Import Bank Act of 1945]].<ref name=whoweare>{{cite web |publisher=Export Import Bank of the United States |title=EXIM History |url=http://www.exim.gov/about/whoweare/history.cfm |accessdate=August 7, 2013|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20150123095653/http://www.exim.gov/about/whoweare/history.cfm|archivedate=January 23, 2015}}</ref><ref name="1945Fed">{{cite news |last1=Austin |first1=Kenneth |title=Federal Bank Aid to Foreign Trade; Export-Import's Policies of Spreading Credit Explained by Wayne C. Taylor Will Use Business Banks  Loans Made Only to Promote United States' Commerce, With Safeguards |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1945/11/04/archives/federal-bank-aid-to-foreign-trade-exportimports-policies-of.html?searchResultPosition=4 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=4 November 1945}}</ref>

Later economic advisor to [[Paul G. Hoffman]] in setting up the [[Economic Cooperation Administration]], Chatfield-Taylor was also economic advisor to the [[European Recovery Program]] (better known as the [[Marshall Plan]]) after [[World War II]].<ref name="WCTObit1967"/>

==Personal life==
[[File:York Hall, State Route 1005 and Main Street, Yorktown, York County, VA HABS VA,100-YORK,1-2.tif|thumb|right|Chatfield-Taylor's residence, [[Thomas Nelson House (Yorktown, Virginia)|York Hall]] in [[Yorktown, Virginia]].]]
On August 22, 1917, Chatfield-Taylor was married to Adele Margaret Blow (1895–1977).<ref name="ACTObit1977"/>
He built the noted 1925 [[Lake Forest, Illinois|Lake Forest]], [[Illinois]] mansion "Bluff's Edge" located at 620 Lake Road.<ref name=":3">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=DrjWHvknMRIC&lpg=PA10|title=Classic Country Estates of Lake Forest: Architecture and Landscape Design, 1856–1940|last1=Coventry|first1=Kim|last2=Meyer|first2=Daniel|last3=Miller|first3=Arthur H.|date=2003|publisher=W. W. Norton & Company|isbn=9780393730999|location=|pages=67–68, 269|language=en}}</ref> His wife, a descendant of a family that settled in Virginia in 1609 and a member of the [[Colonial Dames of America]], restored thirty historic homes and buildings, including the [[Thomas Nelson House (Yorktown, Virginia)|Thomas Nelson House]] (also known as York Hall) in [[Yorktown, Virginia]], the Federal House, [[Nantucket, Massachusetts]], and Mantua, a classic structure in [[Heathsville, Virginia]].<ref name="ACTObit1977"/><ref>{{cite book |title=A Guidebook to Virginia's Historical Markers |date=1994 |publisher=[[University of Virginia Press]] |isbn=978-0-8139-1491-6 |page=67 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Ek75KZ26-zwC&pg=PA67 |accessdate=6 June 2020 |language=en}}</ref>

Chatfield-Taylor died at the Washington Medical Center in [[Washington, D.C.]] on November 22, 1967.<ref name="WCTObit1967">{{cite news  |title=Wayne Chatfield Taylor Dead; Roosevelt and Truman Aide, 73; Banker Held Major Posts in Commerce, Treasury and the Export-Import Bank In Many Public Posts Envoy at Trade Meetings |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1967/11/23/archives/wayne-chatfield-taylor-dead-roosevelt-and-truman-aide-73-banker.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=23 November 1967}}</ref> He was buried at [[Rock Creek Cemetery]] in Washington. His widow died on August 31, 1977 in [[Greenwich, Connecticut]] and was buried at [[Hollywood Cemetery (Richmond, Virginia)|Hollywood Cemetery]] in [[Richmond, Virginia]].<ref name="ACTObit1977">{{cite news |title=Adele Chatfield Taylor, Civic Aide |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1977/09/03/archives/adele-chatfield-taylor-civic-aide.html |accessdate=6 June 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=3 September 1977}}</ref>
