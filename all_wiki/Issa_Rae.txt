She first garnered attention for her work on the [[YouTube]] web series ''[[Awkward Black Girl]]''.<ref name=Vulture-ABG-2013>{{cite news|last1=Gopalan|first1=Nisha|title=Issa Rae on Awkward Black Girl, Her Shonda Rhimes Show, and Hating L.A. ==Early life==
Jo-Issa Rae Diop was born in Los Angeles, California.<ref name=FamilySearch-CABirthIndex-1985>{{cite news|title=Joissa Rae Diop Potomac Maryland Birth Index|url=https://familysearch.org/ark:/61903/1:1:VG62-5PG|agency=[[FamilySearch]]|date=January 12, 1985}}</ref> Her father, Abdoulaye Diop, is a [[pediatrician]] and [[neonatology|neonatologist]] from [[Senegal]], and her mother, Delyna Diop (nÃ©e Hayward), is a [[teacher]] from [[Louisiana]].<ref name=HuffPo-AGB-2013 /><ref name=ChicagoMag-Halfrican-2015>{{cite news|last1=Obaro|first1=Tomi|title=Issa Rae on Her New Memoir and Being "Halfrican"|url=http://www.chicagomag.com/arts-culture/February-2015/Issa-Rae-and-Samantha-Irby/|work=[[Chicago (magazine)|Chicago]]|date=February 16, 2015}}</ref><ref>http://www.okayafrica.com/issa-rae/</ref> Her parents met in [[France]], when they were both in school. She has four siblings. Her father has a medical practice in [[Inglewood, California]].<ref name=TMoABG-2015>{{cite book|last1=Rae|first1=Issa|title=The Misadventures of Awkward Black Girl|date=2015|publisher=37 Ink/Atria&nbsp;â€“ Simon & Schuster|location=New York, NY|isbn=9781476749051|oclc=901338241}}</ref>{{rp|xiii}}

The family lived in [[Dakar]], Senegal,<ref name=NYTimes-Magazine-2015 /> for a short period during her childhood.<ref name=NewRepublic-ABG-2015>{{cite news|last1=Brown|first1=Stacia L.|title=Meet the Black _________|url=https://newrepublic.com/article/121020/issa-raes-misadventures-awkward-black-girl-reveals-real-rae|work=[[The New Republic]]|date=February 10, 2015}}</ref> She was raised mostly in [[Potomac, Maryland]], where she grew up with "things that aren't considered 'black,' like the [[Swimming (sport)|swim team]] and [[street hockey]] and [[Passover Seder|Passover dinners]] with Jewish best friends."<ref name=StanfordAlumni-ABG-2012>{{cite news|last1=Hua|first1=Vanessa|title=Awkward Stage: A web sitcom's quirky black heroine is poised for takeoff|url=http://alumni.stanford.edu/get/page/magazine/article/?article_id=53330|work=Stanford Magazine|date=May 2012}}</ref> When Diop was in sixth grade, her family moved to the affluent [[View Parkâ€“Windsor Hills, California|View Park-Windsor Hills]] neighborhood of Los Angeles, where she attended a predominantly black [[middle school]]. Diop graduated from [[King Drew Magnet High School of Medicine and Science]], where she started acting.<ref name=NYTimes-Magazine-2015 /> Her parents divorced when she was in high school.<ref name=TMoABG-2015 />{{rp|100â€“102}} Diop is fluent in French.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.oprah.com/entertainment/issa-rae-interview-august-2017-o-magazine|title= Issa Rae's Motivational Mantra (and 5 Other Facts)|work=O, the Oprah Magazine|publisher=Harpo Productions|access-date=October 20, 2020}}</ref>

In 2007, Diop graduated from Stanford University with a Bachelor of Arts in African and [[African-American Studies]]. As a college student, she made music videos, wrote and directed plays, and created a mock reality series called ''Dorm Diaries'' for fun. At Stanford, Diop met [[Tracy Oliver]], who helped produce ''Awkward Black Girl'' and starred on the show as Nina.<ref name=StanfordAlumni-ABG-2012 />

After college, Diop received a theater fellowship at [[The Public Theater]] in New York City.<ref name=NYTimes-Magazine-2015 /> Oliver and Diop started taking classes together at the [[New York Film Academy]]. Diop worked odd jobs and at one point was struggling to decide between [[business school]] and [[law school]], but eventually abandoned both ideas when ''Awkward Black Girl'' started taking off in 2011.<ref name=HuffPo-AGB-2013>{{cite news|last1=Gray|first1=Emma|title=Issa Rae, Creator Of 'Awkward Black Girl', Felt Like Her Voice Was Missing From Pop Culture&nbsp;â€“ So Here's What She Did|url=https://www.huffingtonpost.com/2013/11/05/issa-rae-awkward-black-girl_n_4209313.html|work=[[The Huffington Post]]|date=November 5, 2013}}</ref>

==Career==
===''Awkward Black Girl''===
{{main|Awkward Black Girl}}
Rae's web series ''[[Awkward Black Girl]]'' premiered on YouTube in 2011.<ref>Brown, S Tia (September 12, 2011). "Nerdy Girls Rock". ''Jet''. Vol. 120. Iss. 11. p. 31.</ref> The show follows the life of J (played by Rae) as she interacts with co-workers and love interests who place her in uncomfortable situations. The story is told through a first-person narrative as J usually reveals how she feels about her circumstances through voice-over or dream sequence.

The series eventually went viral through word of mouth, blog posts, and social media, resulting in mainstream media coverage and attention.<ref name=CNN-ABG-2011>{{cite news|last1=Whitfield|first1=Fredricka|title='Awkward Black Girl' web hit|url=http://www.cnn.com/videos/living/2011/10/08/whitfield-issa-rae-interview.cnn|format=video interview|work=[[CNN]]|date=October 8, 2011}}</ref><ref name=PhillyTribune-ABG-2011>{{cite news|last1=Anderson|first1=Stacy A.|title=Diverse Web series grows through social media|url=http://www.phillytrib.com/entertainment/diverse-web-series-grows-through-social-media/article_7da9523e-033d-5218-aef4-4b87d7655150.html|work=[[The Philadelphia Tribune]]|agency=[[The Associated Press]]|date=September 12, 2011}}</ref><ref name=Root-ABG-2011>{{cite news|last1=Andrews|first1=Helena|title=Embracing the Awkward, One Webisode at a Time|url=http://www.theroot.com/articles/culture/2011/07/awkward_black_girl_interview_issa_rae_talks_to_the_root/|work=[[The Root (magazine)|The Root]]|date=July 6, 2011}}</ref> In an effort to fund the rest of the first season, Rae and producer Tracy Oliver decided to raise money for the series through Kickstarter. On August 11, 2011 they were awarded $56,269 from 1,960 donations and released the rest of season one on Rae's YouTube channel.<ref name=Kickstarter-ABG-2011>{{cite web|title=Update 1: Update Video: Thank You for Over $40K Raised!|url=https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1996857943/the-misadventures-of-awkward-black-girl/posts/106068|website=The Misadventures of AWKWARD Black Girl|publisher=[[Kickstarter]]|date=August 8, 2011}}</ref>

Rae eventually partnered with [[Pharrell]] and premiered season two of the series on his YouTube channel, ''iamOTHER''.<ref name="PinkNewBlog-IAmOther-2012">{{cite news|last1=Shannon|title=Pharrell Williams Teams Up With Awkward Black Girl & Launches New Brand|url=http://www.pinkisthenewblog.com/2012-06-15/pharrell-williams-teams-up-with-awkward-black-girl-launches-new-brand|work=[[Trent Vanegas|Pink is the New Blog]]|date=June 15, 2012}}</ref> Rae also began releasing other content on her original channel, predominantly created by and starring people of color.<ref name=NYT-ABG-Review-2012>{{cite news|last1=Caramanica|first1=Jon|title=Issa Rae and 'Awkward Black Girl' Are Breaking Ground|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2012/07/14/arts/television/issa-rae-and-awkward-black-girl-are-breaking-ground.htm|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=July 13, 2012}}</ref>

In 2013, ''Awkward Black Girl'' won a [[Shorty Awards|Shorty]] award for Best Web Show. Rae created ''Awkward Black Girl'' because she felt the Hollywood stereotypes of African-American women were limiting and she could not relate to them:

<blockquote>I've always had an issue with the [assumption] that people of color, and black people especially, aren't relatable. I know we are.<ref>Sherman, S.  (2015). Issa Rae, "Making The black Experience Relatable". ''Sun Reporter'', 9.</ref></blockquote>

By using YouTube as her forum, Rae was able to have autonomy of her work because she writes, films, produces, and edits most of her work. Rae's other showsâ€”''Ratchet Piece Theater'', ''The "F" Word'', ''Roomieloverfriends'', and ''[[The Choir (web series)|The Choir]]'', among othersâ€”also focus on African-American experiences that are often not portrayed in the mainstream media.<ref name="THR-CreativityRoundtable-2016">{{cite news|last1=Favreau|first1=Jon|title=Creativity Roundtable: Lin-Manuel Miranda, Donald Glover, Issa Rae and Damien Chazelle in One Epic Conversation|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/features/lin-manuel-miranda-donald-glover-issa-rae-damien-chazelle-one-epic-conversation-956697|work=[[The Hollywood Reporter]]|date=December 16, 2016|format=Video roundtable includes transcript}}</ref>

===''Insecure''===
{{main|Insecure (TV series)}}
In 2013, Rae began working on a comedy series pilot with [[Larry Wilmore]], in which she would star.<ref name=Vibe-Insecure-2013>{{cite news|title=Issa Rae & Larry Wilmore To Create 'Non-Prophet' For HBO|url=http://www.vibe.com/article/issa-rae-larry-wilmore-create-non-prophet-hbo|work=[[Vibe (magazine)|Vibe]]|date=August 6, 2013}}</ref> The series, about the awkward experiences of a contemporary African-American woman, was eventually titled ''[[Insecure (TV series)|Insecure]]''. HBO picked up the pilot in early 2015 and it was subsequently [[Green-light|greenlit]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://deadline.com/2015/10/issa-rae-insecure-comedy-series-hbo-1201584383/|title=Issa Rae Comedy 'Insecure' Gets HBO Series Order|first=Nellie| last=Andreeva|website=Deadline Hollywood|language=en-US|date= October 15, 2015|accessdate=January 9, 2016}}</ref> Since its release in 2016, the series has received critical acclaim; Eric Deggans of NPR wrote that "Rae has produced a series that feels revolutionary just by poking fun at the life of an average, twenty-something black woman."<ref>{{cite web|title=At TV Press Tour, Actors And Producers Of Color Speak Of Hollywood Struggles|url=https://www.npr.org/2016/08/02/488380575/at-tv-press-tour-actors-and-producers-of-color-speak-of-hollywood-struggles}}</ref>

In 2017, the [[American Film Institute]] selected ''Insecure'' as one of the [[American Film Institute Awards 2017#Top 10 Television Programs|top 10 Television Programs of the Year]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://www.afi.com/afiawards/AFI-Awards-2017.aspx|title=AFI Awards 2017|last=|first=|date=|website=www.afi.com|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171208174824/http://www.afi.com/afiawards/AFI-Awards-2017.aspx|archive-date=December 8, 2017|access-date=January 26, 2018|url-status=dead}}</ref> For her acting work on the show, Rae has received two Golden Globe Award nominations for [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress â€“ Television Series Musical or Comedy|Best Actress â€“ Television Series Musical or Comedy]] in 2017 and 2018,<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://www.goldenglobes.com/person/issa-rae|title=Issa Rae|website=www.goldenglobes.com|language=en|access-date=January 26, 2018}}</ref> as well as a Primetime Emmy Award nomination for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Comedy Series|Outstanding Lead Actress in a Comedy Series]] in 2018.

In 2018,  at the 77th annual [[Peabody Awards]], ''Insecure'' was honored for "creating a series that authentically captures the lives of everyday young, black people in modern society."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.peabodyawards.com/award-profile/insecure|title=Insecure (HBO)|access-date=January 18, 2019}}</ref>

On November 14, 2016, HBO renewed the show for a second season.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://deadline.com/2016/11/westworld-divorce-insecure-renewed-season-2-hbo-1201853994/|title='Westworld', 'Divorce' & 'Insecure' Renewed For Season 2 By HBO|last=Andreeva|first=Nellie|date=November 14, 2016|website=[[Deadline Hollywood]]|accessdate=November 14, 2016}}</ref> The second season premiered on July 23, 2017.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://twitter.com/IssaRae/status/841695814627938305|title=Issa Rae on Twitter|publisher=}}</ref> On August 8, 2017, it was announced that the show was renewed for a third season,<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.vulture.com/2017/08/hbo-renews-insecure-for-season-three.html|title=Insecure Is Hella Renewed for Season Three|last=Lockett|first=Dee|work=Vulture|date=August 8, 2017 |access-date=August 8, 2017|language=en}}</ref> which premiered on August 12, 2018. The fourth season premiered on HBO in 2020.

===Other work===
Rae's first book, a memoir titled ''The Misadventures of Awkward Black Girl'', was released in 2015 and became a ''New York Times'' best-seller. In the book, she chronicles her life through a series of humorous anecdotes and opens up about her personal struggle with not fitting in, and not being considered "black enough" at times.<ref name=ChicagoMag-Halfrican-2015 />

On October 11, 2019, Google announced that Rae would be an additional voice to the [[Google Assistant]]. Users can make Google Assistant speak in Rae's voice by saying "Ok Google, talk like Issa."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.instagram.com/p/B3cPBsalahR/|title=Google on Instagram: "Meet the new voice of your Google Assistant: @issarae! ðŸ¤© She's taking over our story todayâ€”follow along or say "Hey Google, #TalkLikeIssa"â€¦"|website=Instagram|language=en|access-date=2019-10-14}}</ref>

Also in 2019, Issa Rae, through her newly launched record label "Raedio," partnered with [[Atlantic Records]] to produce "Kinda Love" by singer-rapper TeaMarrr.<ref name=AmoMama>{{cite web|title=Issa Rae of 'Insecure' Launches Her Own Record Label with TeaMarrr's 'Kinda Love'|url=https://news.amomama.com/177253-issa-rae-insecure-launches-her-own-recor.html|date=October 23, 2019}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Rae's birth name, Jo-Issa, comes from a combination of the names of her grandmothers: Joyce and Isseu. Her middle name, Rae, is after an aunt, who was an artist.<ref name=StanfordAlumni-ABG-2012 />

She is engaged to her longtime boyfriend, Louis Diame, a Senegalese businessman.<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=https://www.ebony.com/entertainment/issa-rae-engaged-to-longtime-boyfriend/|title='Insecure' Creator Issa Rae Engaged to Longtime Boyfriend|last=Washington|first=Jasmine|date=2019-04-01|website=EBONY|language=en-US|access-date=2019-05-13}}</ref> Issa Rae first wore her engagement ring publicly on the cover of ''Essence'' magazine's April 2019 issue.<ref name=":3" />

===Activism===
Issa Rae has used her platform to bring attention to police violence and brutality against Black Americans. Following the police [[shooting of Alton Sterling]] in 2016, Rae raised $700,000 for the Sterling Family Trust to help pay for the Sterling children to attend college.<ref>{{Cite book |title=Modern HERstory : stories of women and nonbinary people rewriting history|last1=Blair |first1=Imani |last2=Le |first2=Monique |isbn=9780399582233|edition=First |location=California|oclc=1019616770|date=October 15, 2018 }}</ref>

==In media==
In 2012, Rae was included on the annual ''Forbes '[[Forbes 30 Under 30|30 Under 30]]''' list in the entertainment section.<ref name="Forbes30Under30-2014">{{cite web|title=Forbes 30 Under 30|url=https://www.forbes.com/special-report/2014/30-under-30/hollywood-and-entertainment.html|date=2014|work=[[Forbes]]}}</ref>

In May 2015, Rae appeared on the cover of ''Essence'' magazine's Game Changers issue, alongside [[Shonda Rhimes]], [[Ava DuVernay]], [[Debbie Allen]], and [[Mara Brock Akil]]. Rae expressed her desire for more people of color working in production behind the scenes to make a lasting impact in the television industry.<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=https://www.essence.com/2015/04/14/shonda-rhimes-ava-duvernay-debbie-allen-mara-brock-akil-issa-rae-essence-cover|title=Shonda Rhimes, Ava DuVernay, Debbie Allen, Mara Brock Akil and Issa Rae Cover ESSENCE's 'Game Changers' Issue|work=Essence.com|date= April 13, 2015|access-date=January 26, 2018|language=en}}</ref>

On the red carpet at the 2017 [[Emmy Awards]], Rae told reporters, "I'm rooting for everybody black." The quote went viral and appeared on t-shirts and in the song "Sue Me" by the rapper [[Wale (rapper)|Wale]].<ref>{{Cite news|title=Issa Rae Shares Story Behind "I'm Rooting For Everybody Black" Meme|url=https://www.okayplayer.com/news/issa-rae-rooting-for-everybody-black-meme-interview.html|access-date=July 22, 2020}}</ref>
