'''Progress Energy''', headquartered in [[Raleigh, North Carolina]], is a [[subsidiary]] of [[Duke Energy]] and prior to its merger with Duke Energy was a Fortune 500 energy company with more than 21,000 megawatts of generation capacity and $9 billion in annual revenues. Progress Energy includes two major electric utilities that serve approximately 3.1 million customers in [[the Carolinas]] and [[Florida]]. As an independent company, the last chairman and [[CEO]] of Progress Energy was [[William D. Johnson (CEO)|William D. Johnson]]; his predecessor was [[Robert McGehee]], who died on October 9, 2007 at the age of 64 of a [[stroke]] while on a business trip to London.

Progress Energy is the majority owner and operator of the [[Brunswick Nuclear Generating Station|Brunswick]], [[Crystal River 3 Nuclear Power Plant|Crystal River 3]], [[H. B. Robinson Nuclear Generating Station|Robinson]], and [[Shearon Harris Nuclear Power Plant|Shearon Harris]] [[nuclear power plants]].

On October 16, 1999, third quarter earnings of Florida Progress Corp. fell 20.5 percent as damage from Hurricane Floyd.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.lexis.com/research/retrieve?_m=5bc075e1e3a1213d5098f4f5c6cd89ea&docnum=4&_fmtstr=FULL&_startdoc=1&wchp=dGLzVzB-zSkAl&_md5=ac0a248656058bc26327e33f606ff984 |title= Florida Progress buyer suffers}}</ref>

On August 24, 1999, Carolina Power & Light Co. said that it would acquire Florida Progress Corp. for $5.3 billion, creating the nation's ninth-largest electric utility.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.referenceforbusiness.com/history/Pa-Ql/Progress-Energy-Inc.html |title= Carolina Power Set To Buy Fla. Utility; Deal Would Form No. 9 Electric Firm}}</ref>

The company's current slogan is, "People, performance, excellence."

==History==
{{main|Carolina Power & Light}}

In 2000, [[Carolina Power & Light]] bought [[Florida Power Corporation]] and changed its name to Progress Energy. Progress Energy represents a family of companies, including CP&L, Florida Power, Progress Telecom, NCNG and SRS, and an important new organization, Energy Ventures. The company built a new [[Two Progress Plaza|headquarters]] in downtown Raleigh in 2004.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.newsobserver.com/2011/01/10/910125/progress-energy-close-to-being.html|title=Merger means uncertainty for Raleigh utilityâ€™s workers|last=Murawski|first=John|work=[[News & Observer]]|date=2011-01-10|accessdate=2011-01-10|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20110111191352/http://www.newsobserver.com/2011/01/10/910125/progress-energy-close-to-being.html|archivedate=2011-01-11}}</ref>

On January 10, 2011, [[Duke Energy]] announced plans to take over Progress Energy in a $26 billion deal resulting in the country's largest electric utility with 7.1 million customers. Duke Energy plans to "maintain substantial operations in Raleigh." <ref>{{cite news|url=http://blogs.newsobserver.com/business/duke-energy-to-buy-progress-energy-in-26-billion-deal|title=Duke Energy to buy Progress Energy in $26 billion deal|last=Wolf|first=Alan M.|work=[[News & Observer]]|date=2011-01-10}}</ref> When the merger was completed on July 3, 2012, Duke Chairman James E. (Jim) Rogers became Chairman and CEO of the new combined company, while Progress CEO Bill Johnson resigned.<ref>[http://www.wral.com/business/story/11274942/ WRAL.com: Progress CEO is out as Duke, Progress complete merger]</ref><ref>[http://www.duke-energy.com/news/releases/2012070301.asp Duke Energy press release]</ref>

==Environmental record==
In 2008, the Corporate Responsibility Officer named Progress Energy to its list of 100 Best Corporate Citizens.<ref>100 Best Corporate Citizens 2008 http://www.thecro.com/node/615 {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110408230548/http://www.thecro.com/node/615 |date=2011-04-08 }}</ref> Progress Energy was named to the Dow Jones Sustainability Index in 2009, 2008, 2007, 2006 and 2005.<ref>DJSI http://www.sustainability-indexes.com/ {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070930144459/http://www.sustainability-indexes.com/ |date=2007-09-30 }}</ref>

The company is investing $300,000 in a [[UNC Chapel Hill]] study to map the offshore wind power potential of North Carolina.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.newsobserver.com/2010/07/01/560072/progress-boosts-unc-wind-study.html#storylink=misearch |title=Progress boosts UNC wind study |publisher=NewsObserver.com |date= |accessdate=2012-05-08}}</ref> Progress Energy launched its SunSense-branded solar incentive programs in 2009.<ref>Company news release: http://www.progress-energy.com/aboutus/news/article.asp?id=21723</ref>

Progress Energy has installed [[flue-gas desulfurization]] technology, or scrubbers, to remove sulfur dioxide emissions from its nine largest coal-fired power plant units.<ref>{{cite web|last=Hiers |first=Fred |url=http://www.ocala.com/article/20091208/articles/912081005 |title=Progress Energy fires up scrubbers to curb emissions |publisher=Ocala.com |date= |accessdate=2012-05-08}}</ref>

Researchers at the [[University of Massachusetts Amherst]] have identified Progress Energy as the 29th-largest corporate producer of [[Air pollution in the United States]] in 2002, when it released roughly 39 million pounds of toxic chemicals into the air. Major pollutants included [[nickel]] compounds, [[chromium]] compounds, [[sulfuric acid]], and [[hydrochloric acid]].<ref>[http://www.peri.umass.edu/Toxic-100-Table.265.0.html [[Political Economy Research Institute]] Toxic 100] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111001071549/http://www.peri.umass.edu/Toxic-100-Table.265.0.html |date=2011-10-01 }} retrieved 14 Aug 2007</ref> Progress has also been named a potentially responsible party at the Carolina Transformer Co. [[Superfund]] toxic waste site, according to the [[Center for Public Integrity]].<ref>[http://projects.publicintegrity.org/Superfund/SiteResults.aspx?act=progress%20energy Center for Public Integrity] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110718203014/http://projects.publicintegrity.org/Superfund/SiteResults.aspx?act=progress%20energy |date=July 18, 2011 }}</ref>

==Naming rights==
Progress Energy owns the naming rights to the [[University of Central Florida]]'s ''Progress Energy Welcome Center'', [[St. Petersburg, Florida|St. Petersburg]]'s ''[[Progress Energy Park]], home of Al Lang Field'', [[Mahaffey Theater|Progress Energy Center for the Arts-Mahaffey Theater]], and the "[[Progress Energy Center for the Performing Arts]]" in downtown [[Raleigh, NC]].
