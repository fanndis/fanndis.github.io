'''Martine Louise Marie Aubry''' ({{IPA-fr|maʁtin obʁi}}; ''née'' '''Delors'''; born 8 August 1950) is a French politician. She was the First Secretary of the [[French Socialist Party]] (''Parti Socialiste'', or PS) from November 2008 to April 2012, and has been the first female [[Mayor (France)|Mayor]] of [[Lille]] (Nord) since March 2001. Her father, [[Jacques Delors]], served as Minister of Finance under President [[François Mitterrand]] and was also President of the [[European Commission]].

Aubry joined the PS in 1974, and was appointed Minister of Labour by Prime Minister [[Édith Cresson]] in 1991, but lost her position in 1993 after the Right won the legislative elections. However, she became Minister of Social Affairs when [[Lionel Jospin]] was appointed [[Prime Minister of France|Prime Minister]] in 1997. She is mostly known for having pushed the popular [[35-hour workweek]] law, known as the "Loi Aubry", reducing the nominal length of the normal full-time working week from 39 to 35 hours, and the law that created [[Couverture maladie universelle]] (Universal health care coverage).

Aubry stepped down from her Cabinet post in 2001 to be elected [[Mayor (France)|Mayor]] of Lille in place of [[Pierre Mauroy]]. Aubry subsequently lost her seat in the [[National Assembly of France|National Assembly]] in the [[2002 French legislative election|general election of 2002]]. In March 2008, she was re-elected Mayor of Lille, with 66.55% of the votes.

In November 2008, Aubry was elected to lead the Socialist Party, narrowly defeating [[Ségolène Royal]]. While Royal disputed the results, the Socialist Party declared on November 25, 2008, that Aubry had won the contested election. On 28 June 2011, Martine Aubry announced she would seek the [[French Socialist Party presidential primary, 2011|Socialist nomination]] to run in the [[2012 French presidential election|2012 presidential election]], ultimately losing to [[François Hollande]], her predecessor as First Secretary.

==Biography==

===Early life and education===
Born in [[Paris]], Aubry is the daughter of [[Jacques Delors]], French Minister of Finance (1981&ndash;1985) and European Commission President (1985&ndash;1995), and his wife. Aubry was educated at the lycée Notre-Dame-des-Oiseaux<ref>it was there that she met and became friends with [[Chantal Goya]]</ref>  and the lycée Paul-Valéry (in Paris).<ref>{{cite web |url=http://lyc-paul-valery.scola.ac-paris.fr/spip.php?rubrique18 |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2010-02-05 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20091119094544/http://lyc-paul-valery.scola.ac-paris.fr/spip.php?rubrique18 |archivedate=2009-11-19 }} Site of the lycée, citing her as an alumna</ref> She holds a degree in economic science from [[Panthéon-Assas University]].<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Mathieu|first1=Béatrice|last2=Deschamps|first2=Pascale-Marie|last3=Mas|first3=Isabelle|last4=Collomp|first4=Florentin|last5=Steinmann|first5=Lionel|title=Où étaient-ils ?|journal=[[L'Expansion]]|date=18 December 1997|language=French}}</ref>

She did additional studies, gaining a diploma from the ''Institut des Sciences Sociales du Travail,'' and one from the ''[[Institut d'Études Politiques de Paris]]'' (or '''Sciences Po''') in 1972. Between 1973 and 1975, Aubry studied at the [[École nationale d'administration]] (ÉNA, National School of Administration).

===Professional career===
{{more citations needed|section|date=April 2017}}
In 1975 Aubry became a civil administrator at the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs (''Ministère du Travail et des Affaires sociales''). During this period, she was active within the [[French Democratic Confederation of Labour]] (CFDT). She became a professor at ÉNA in 1978. In addition, she was seconded to the [[Council of State (France)|State Council]] between 1980 and 1981.

Following the election of [[François Mitterrand]] to the [[President of France|French presidency]] in 1981, Aubry successively held several posts at the Ministry of Social Affairs, in the cabinets of [[Jean Auroux]] and [[Pierre Bérégovoy]]. In 1984, she investigated French [[asbestos]] policy for the Comité Permanent Amiante (Permanent Asbestos Committee, an informal public-private working group formed to manage the health problems of workers affected by asbestos). The group's deputy director, Jean-Luc Pasquier, testified before the courts to account for the group's members' actions.

After the defeat of the socialists in the [[1986 French legislative election|French legislative election of 1986]], Aubry was named [[Master of Requests (France)|Master of Requests]] at the State Council. From 1989-91 she worked as Assistant Director at [[Pechiney]], working with [[Jean Gandois]]. She was involved with the opening of a plant at [[Dunkerque]] and the closure of the aluminium works at [[Noguères]].<ref name="Aubry L'Express">[[Jean-Michel Aphatie]] et Corinne Lhaik, [http://www.lexpress.fr/info/france/dossier/aubry/dossier.asp? Une femme ambitieuse]  {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071225202644/http://www.lexpress.fr/info/france/dossier/aubry/dossier.asp |date=25 December 2007 }}, ''[[L'Express (France)|L'Express]]'', 28 August 1997 {{in lang|fr}}</ref>

== Political career ==

=== Minister of Labour, Employment and Vocational Training: 1991–1993 ===

Aubry was named Minister of Labour, Employment and Vocational Training by [[Édith Cresson]], and carried on in this capacity in the [[Pierre Bérégovoy|Bérégovoy ministry]] until March 1993. According to Jean-Luc Pasquier,<ref>Cité par François Malye</ref> she supported the controlled use of asbestos whilst all other members of the EEC supported an outright ban. She caused the French veto of a European decree against the use of asbestos.<ref>Pierre Mabut, ''[http://www.wsws.org/francais/News/2005/octobre05/261005_Amiantefr.shtml France : Amiante: manifestation de travailleurs français demandant que justice soit faite contre les empoisonneurs]'', WSWS amiante, 26 octobre 2005</ref> France did not ban asbestos until 1997.<ref>[http://www.asbestos-institute.ca/newsletters/nl-97-1/nl-97-1.html ''Newsletter of the Asbestos Institute''] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081020041000/http://www.asbestos-institute.ca/newsletters/nl-97-1/nl-97-1.html |date=October 20, 2008 }}</ref>

In January 2010, a public health judge charged with investigating former government measures on asbestos had Aubry interrogated by gendarmes in Lille.<ref>[http://sedulia.blogs.com/testblog_/2010/03/asbestos-martine-aubry-interrogated-by-gendarmes.html] Translation of article by François Malye in ''Le Point,'' Sedulia blog</ref>

When the Right came into power at the [[1986 French legislative election|French legislative election in 1986]], Aubry started the ''[[Fondation Agir Contre l'Exclusion]]'' (FACE, the Act Against Exclusion Foundation). In 1995, [[Pierre Mauroy]] named her as the first deputy to the Mayor of [[Lille]], thus giving her a foothold in the [[Nord (French department)|department of Nord]].

[[Lionel Jospin]], who became the socialist candidate as French President in 1995, made her his campaign spokesman during the [[1995 French presidential election|presidential campaign]]. Upon his defeat, Jospin became first secretary of the [[Socialist Party (France)|Socialist Party]], and offered her the number two spot, which Aubry refused.

Aubry had good relations with part of the establishment, especially with her former Pechiney boss, Jean Gandois, and the [[French Communist Party|Parti communiste francais]]. But she did not get on well with the unions, in particular with [[Nicole Notat]], the former General Secretary of the CFDT{{Citation needed|date=November 2009}}

Aubry has been described as hard and demanding. She counters, "''Je dis les choses en face, je ne suis pas faux-cul. Mais je crois être bien moins dure que beaucoup de gens en politique. Je suis même peut-être trop sensible.'' (I'm up-front, and I'm not a hypocrite. But I think I'm much less hard than many politicians. I may even be too sensitive.)<ref name="Aubry L'Express" /> "

=== Minister of Employment and Solidarity: 1997–2000 ===
{{See also|35-hour workweek|Couverture maladie universelle}}
Elected as a member of the National Assembly, Aubry was appointed in 1997 as Minister of Employment and Solidarity, the most important minister after the Prime Minister. The same year, to fight unemployment, she created a new employment contract for young people (''Emplois-jeunes'') with financial help from the government. In 1998, a law establishing the 35-hour workweek was adopted.

In 1999, the ''Couverture maladie universelle'' (CMU), a program that reimburses medical expenses through [[Social security in France|social security]] for everyone, was voted through. Furthermore, for people on low incomes, the CMU offers complementary health coverage of 100%, which is added to standard Social Security payments; this avoids the necessity for additional private (top-up) insurance.

=== 2012 Presidential candidacy ===
{{See also|Martine Aubry presidential campaign, 2012}}

On June 28, 2011, Aubry said in a televised address from the former [[Gare of Lille-Saint-Sauveur|train station of Lille-Saint-Sauveur]] : "I have decided to propose my candidacy to the presidential election".<ref>[https://online.wsj.com/article/BT-CO-20110628-703310.html "French Socialist Chief Aubry Seeks Party Backing For 2012 Election"], ''Wall Street Journal'' (WSJ){{dead link|date=June 2014}}</ref>

Following the first round of the [[Citizens primaries 2011|citizens primary]], she faced François Hollande in the second round of voting on October 16 in a two-way runoff. In the final round of voting, Hollande won the nomination with 56.6% of the vote.

After Aubry's defeat in the primaries, she became one of the main supporters of the [[François Hollande presidential campaign, 2012|Francois Hollande presidential campaign]]. Aubry's name had been mentioned as a potential [[Prime Minister of France|prime minister]] for François Hollande.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.leparisien.fr/election-presidentielle-2012/aubry-bien-placee-pour-matignon-03-02-2012-1843655.php |title=Aubry, bien placée pour Matignon ? |publisher=Leparisien.fr |date= 3 February 2012|accessdate=2014-06-23}}</ref> But, after Hollande was elected President, he chose [[Jean-Marc Ayrault]] as Prime Minister; Aubry refused to join his [[Ayrault Cabinet|cabinet]].<ref name="snubbed">{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/worldnews/europe/france/9270807/Francois-Hollande-chooses-first-cabinet-as-Martine-Aubry-is-snubbed.html|title=Francois Hollande chooses first cabinet as Martine Aubry is snubbed|last=Samuel|first=Henry|date=16 May 2012|work=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|accessdate=4 September 2012}}</ref>

=== From 2014: statements of divergence ===

On several occasions, Aubry expressed criticism of the [[Manuel Valls]] government, including the fact that he was chosen.<ref>{{cite web|work=Le Figaro|date=2014-04-05|title=(Martine Aubry à la manœuvre contre Manuel Valls)|trans-title=Martine Aubry to maneuver against Manuel Valls|accessdate=2015-10-17|author=François-Xavier Bourmaud|url=http://www.lefigaro.fr/politique/2014/04/04/01002-20140404ARTFIG00270-aubry-a-la-manoeuvre-contre-valls.php}}</ref> In October 2014, she asked for a reorientation of the economic policy.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.leparisien.fr/politique/martine-aubry-partage-les-propositions-des-frondeurs-19-10-2014-4224039.php|trans-title=Aubry asks Hollande a "reorientation of the economic policy"|title=(PS : Aubry demande à Hollande une "réorientation de la politique économique")|work= Le Parisien |date=2014-10-19|accessdate=2015-10-17}}</ref>
During a press conference held September 23, 2015, where Aubry confirmed the choice of [[Pierre de Saintignon]] as the head of list for the next coming Regional Elections, she said that the bad polls of the list are partly caused by some choices of the government. She was criticising [[Emmanuel Macron]], Minister of Economy since 2014. "Macron? How to tell it... The cup is full", she said.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.liberation.fr/politiques/2015/09/23/aubry-macron-comment-vous-dire-ras-le-bol_1388925|title=(Aubry: "Macron ? Comment vous dire... Ras-le-bol")|trans-title=Aubry says: "Macron ? How to tell it... The cup is full"|date=2015-09-03|ref=Liberation-2015-09-23|work=Liberation}}</ref>
