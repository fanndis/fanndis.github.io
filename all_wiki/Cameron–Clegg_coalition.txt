The '''Cameron–Clegg coalition'''{{<!--Sources for name:-->sfnm|1a1=Adonis|1y=2013|2a1=Eccleston|2a2=Krever|2y=2017|2p=132|3a1=Jowell|3a2=Oliver |3a3=O'Cinneide|3y=2015|3p=220|4a1=Kickert|4a2=Randma-Liiv|4y=2015|4p=114|5a1=Peele|5a2=Francis|5y=2016 |5p=48}} was formed by [[David Cameron]] and [[Nick Clegg]] when the former was invited by [[Queen Elizabeth II]] to [[2010 United Kingdom government formation|form a new government]], following the resignation of Prime Minister [[Gordon Brown]] on 11 May 2010 after the [[2010 United Kingdom general election|general election on 6 May]]. The UK's first [[coalition government]] since the [[Churchill caretaker ministry]], it was led by Cameron with Clegg as [[Deputy Prime Minister of the United Kingdom|Deputy Prime Minister]], composed of members of both Cameron's [[Conservative Party (UK)|Conservative Party]] and Clegg's [[Liberal Democrats (UK)|Liberal Democrats]].

The [[Cabinet of the United Kingdom|Cabinet]] was made up of sixteen Conservatives and five Liberal Democrats, with eight other Conservatives and one other Liberal Democrat attending cabinet but not members.<ref name="coalition">{{cite press release|url=http://www.number10.gov.uk/news/latest-news/2010/05/appointment-of-nick-clegg-as-deputy-prime-minister-49926 |title=Nick Clegg appointed Deputy Prime Minister |publisher=Office of the Prime Minister |date=12 May 2010 |accessdate=3 February 2012}}</ref> The coalition was succeeded by the single-party [[Second Cameron ministry|Cameron ministry]] after the [[2015 United Kingdom general election|2015 election]].

==History==
{{main|2010 United Kingdom government formation|Premiership of David Cameron}}
{{see also|Lib–Con pact}}
The previous Parliament had been dissolved on 12 April 2010 in advance of the [[2010 United Kingdom general election|general election on 6 May]]. The election resulted in a [[hung parliament]], no single party having an overall majority in the [[House of Commons of the United Kingdom|House of Commons]], the Conservatives having the most seats but 20 short of a majority.

In the [[Conservative–Liberal Democrat coalition agreement]] of 11 May 2010, the two parties formed a coalition government.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.conservatives.com/News/News_stories/2010/05/Coalition_Agreement_published.aspx |title=Conservative Liberal Democrat Coalition Agreement |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100515024701/http://www.conservatives.com/News/News_stories/2010/05/Coalition_Agreement_published.aspx |archive-date=15 May 2010 |publisher=Conservative Party |date=12 May 2010 |accessdate=13 May 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://libdems.org.uk/latest_news_detail.aspx?title=Conservative_Liberal_Democrat_coalition_agreements&pPK=2697bcdc-7483-47a7-a517-7778979458ff |title=Conservative Liberal Democrat Coalition Agreement |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111211134147/http://libdems.org.uk//latest_news_detail.aspx?title=Conservative_Liberal_Democrat_coalition_agreements&pPK=2697bcdc-7483-47a7-a517-7778979458ff# |archive-date=11 December 2011 |publisher=Liberal Democrats |date=12 May 2010 |accessdate=13 May 2010}}</ref> The new Parliament met on 18 May for the swearing-in of Peers in the [[House of Lords]] and newly elected and returning Members of Parliament in the House of Commons, and the election for the Speakership of the House of Commons. The [[Speech from the throne|Queen's Speech]] on 25 May set out the government's legislative agenda.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/queens-speech-2010 |title=Queen's Speech 2010 |date=2010-05-25 |website=[[Gov.uk]] |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref> Of the 57 Liberal Democrat MPs, only two refused to support the Conservative Coalition agreement, with former leader [[Charles Kennedy]] and Manchester Withington MP [[John Leech (politician)|John Leech]] both rebelling.<ref>{{cite blog|last=Hampson  |first=Tom  |date=27 September 2010 |url=http://www.nextleft.org/2010/09/john-leech-voted-against-coalition-but.html|title=John Leech did not vote for the coalition &ndash; but who is the third man?|work=Next Left |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>

The Liberal Democrats had five Cabinet members including [[Nick Clegg]] as [[Deputy Prime Minister of the United Kingdom|Deputy Prime Minister]] though after the Cabinet and ministerial [[Cabinet reshuffle|reshuffle]], [[David Laws]], who was a [[minister of state]], was allowed to attend the Cabinet but was not a full member. If a Liberal Democrat minister resigned or was removed from office, another member of the same party would have had to be appointed to the Cabinet.<ref name="qanda">{{cite news | url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/newstopics/politics/7721588/Coalition-government-QandA.html | title=Coalition government: Q&A | work=The Telegraph | date=14 May 2010 | accessdate=15 July 2014}}{{dead|date=October 2020}}</ref>

Each [[United Kingdom cabinet committee|cabinet committee]] had a chair from one party and a deputy chair from the other; there was also a cabinet committee specifically overseeing the operation of the coalition. Both parties' ministers shared [[Cabinet collective responsibility|collective responsibility]] for the government's positions, although the coalition agreement detailed several issues on which the parties agreed to differ; the Liberal Democrats abstained from voting in such cases. Clegg, as Deputy Prime Minister, took [[Prime Minister's Questions]] (PMQs) when Cameron was unavailable.<ref name="qanda" />

Key decisions were made by a core group called the "Quad", made up of Cameron, Clegg, [[Chancellor of the Exchequer]] [[George Osborne]] and [[Chief Secretary to the Treasury]] [[Danny Alexander]], which decided "all major matters of policy" and resolved disputes between the two parties.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Montgomerie|first=Tim|authorlink=Tim Montgomerie |date=16 February 2012|title=Ten things you need to know about the group of four that runs the Coalition|url=https://www.conservativehome.com/thetorydiary/2012/02/ten-things-you-need-to-know-about-the-group-of-four-that-runs-the-coalition.html|access-date=2020-10-23|website=Conservative Home}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|last=Forsyth|first=James|date=18 February 2012|title=Politics: Britain's new gang of four|url=https://www.spectator.co.uk/article/politics-britain-s-new-gang-of-four|access-date=2020-10-23|website=The Spectator}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|last1=Asthana|first1=Anushka|last2=Helm|first2=Toby|date=2010-10-16|title=Behind closed doors, the coalition 'quad' thrashed out our fate|language=en-GB|work=The Guardian|url=https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2010/oct/17/comprehensive-spending-review-david-cameron-george-osborne-coalition|access-date=2020-10-23|issn=0261-3077}}</ref>

While the [[British Government frontbench|government's front benchers]] sat together in the House of Commons and the two parties acted as a bloc during PMQ,<ref name="riddell20100519">{{cite news |last=Riddell |first=Peter |url=https://www.thetimes.co.uk/article/all-change-in-the-transformed-house-of-commons-9j2k0sf9b8j |title=All change in the transformed House of Commons |newspaper=The Times |date=19 May 2010 |url-access=subscription |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref> the Liberal Democrat and Conservative [[backbencher]]s sat apart and each had their own [[Chief Whip#United Kingdom|whips]],<ref name="riddell20100519" /> and the two parties competed in [[List of United Kingdom by-elections (2010–present)|by-elections]]. On 4 September 2012, [[David Cameron]] [[2012 British cabinet reshuffle|reshuffled his cabinet]] for the first time.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Wintour |first1=Patrick |last2=Watt |first2=Nicholas |url=https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2012/sep/04/david-cameron-government-reshuffle-cabinet|title=David Cameron's right turn in cabinet reshuffle|date=4 September 2012|work=The Guardian |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref> He [[2014 British cabinet reshuffle|reshuffled his cabinet]] for the second time on 14 July 2014.<ref name="BBCres">{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-politics-28291281 | title=William Hague quits as foreign secretary in cabinet reshuffle | work=BBC News | date=14 July 2014 | accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>

On 29 August 2013, the Conservative-led coalition [[List of Government defeats in the House of Commons (1945–present)#Cameron (coalition government)|suffered a defeat]] when Parliament voted against UK military attacks on Syria.<ref name="blow">{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2013/aug/30/cameron-mps-syria |title=Blow to Cameron's authority as MPs rule out British assault on Syria|first1=Nicholas |last1=Watt |first2=Rowena |last2=Mason |first3=Nick |last3=Hopkins|work=The Guardian |date=30 August 2013 |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>

==Cabinets==

===May 2010 – September 2012===
{{David Cameron cabinet 1 vertical}}

====Changes====
* [[David Laws]] resigned as Chief Secretary to the Treasury on 29 May 2010 because of an [[United Kingdom parliamentary expenses scandal|expenses irregularity]] dating from the previous Parliament. He was replaced by [[Danny Alexander]], who was in turn replaced as Secretary of State for Scotland by [[Michael Moore (Scottish politician)|Michael Moore]]<ref name="autogenerated1">{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/10194287 | title=David Laws resignation: What next for government? | work=BBC News | date=30 May 2012 | accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>
* On 14 October 2011 [[Liam Fox]] resigned as Secretary of State for Defence following the procurement of high-level overseas meetings attendance for his friend and advisor, [[Adam Werrity]], working for a private contractor, and stated that he had "mistakenly allowed the distinction between my personal interest and my government activities to become blurred"<ref name="Fox1">{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-15300751 | title=Liam Fox quits as defence secretary | work=BBC News | date=14 October 2011 | accessdate=3 February 2012}}</ref> His successor was [[Philip Hammond]] who was replaced as Transport Secretary by [[Justine Greening]], the [[Economic Secretary to the Treasury]], who was in turn replaced by [[Chloe Smith]], an assistant government whip: she was replaced in turn by [[Greg Hands]]<ref name=Fox1 /><ref name="autogenerated3">{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-15313986 | title=As it happened: Liam Fox resigns | work=BBC News | date=9 July 2011 | accessdate=3 February 2012}}</ref>
* On 3 February 2012 [[Chris Huhne]] resigned as Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change following the decision of the Crown Prosecution Service to [[R v Huhne|prosecute him and his former wife]]. His successor was [[Ed Davey|Edward Davey]], who was replaced as a [[Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State]] in the Department of Business, Innovation and Skills by [[Norman Lamb]], replaced in his previous dual roles by [[Jenny Willott]] as an Assistant Whip and [[Jo Swinson]] as the [[Parliamentary Private Secretary]] to the Deputy Prime Minister<ref name=autogenerated2>{{cite press release |url=http://www.number10.gov.uk/news/new-ministerial-appointments/ |title=New Ministerial Appointments |date=3 February 2012 |publisher=Office of the Prime Minister |accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>

===September 2012 – July 2014===
{{see|2012 British cabinet reshuffle}}
{{David Cameron cabinet 2 vertical}}

====Changes====
* On 19 October 2012, [[Andrew Mitchell]] resigned as Government Chief Whip in the House of Commons following [[Plebgate|controversy surrounding an argument with police officers]] in [[Downing Street]]. He was replaced by [[Sir George Young]].<ref name="Mitchell1">{{cite news |last1=Wintour |first1=Patrick |last2=Syal |first2=Rajeev |url=https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2012/oct/19/andrew-mitchell-resigns-police-pleb | title=Andrew Mitchell resigns following allegations he called police 'plebs' | work=The Guardian | date=19 October 2012 | accessdate=2020-10-24}}</ref>
* On 7 January 2013, [[Thomas Galbraith, 2nd Baron Strathclyde|Tom Strathclyde]] resigned as Leader of the House of Lords and Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster. He was replaced by [[Jonathan Hill, Baron Hill of Oareford|Jonathan Hill]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Lord Strathclyde resigns from cabinet|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-20932061|date=7 January 2013|work=BBC News|accessdate=21 August 2013}}</ref>
* On 7 October 2013, [[Michael Moore (Scottish politician)|Michael Moore]] was replaced as Secretary of State for Scotland by [[Alistair Carmichael]], during a reshuffle which focused on junior ministerial ranks.
* On 9 April 2014, [[Maria Miller]] resigned as Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport and Minister for Women and Equalities. She was replaced as Culture Secretary and Minister for Equalities by [[Sajid Javid]], and by [[Nicky Morgan]] as Minister for Women. Morgan, who succeeded Javid as [[Financial Secretary to the Treasury]], was not a full cabinet member but attended meetings in her role as Minister for Women.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-26956184 |title=Sajid Javid named culture secretary after Miller exit |work=BBC News |date=9 April 2014 |accessdate=22 May 2014}}</ref>

===July 2014 – May 2015===
{{see|2014 British cabinet reshuffle}}
{{David Cameron cabinet 3 vertical}}

====Changes====
* On 5 August 2014, [[Sayeeda Warsi, Baroness Warsi|Sayeeda Warsi]] resigned as Minister of State at the Foreign and Commonwealth Office, and as Minister for Faith and Community, in protest at the Government's response to the [[2014 Israel–Gaza conflict|conflict in the Gaza Strip]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-28656874 |title=Baroness Warsi quits as Foreign Office minister over Gaza |date=5 August 2014 |work=BBC News |accessdate=17 February 2016}}</ref> She was replaced at the Foreign Office by [[Joyce Anelay, Baroness Anelay of St John's|Joyce Anelay]], with [[Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government|Communities and Local Government Secretary]] [[Eric Pickles]] taking on Warsi's former Faith and Community brief.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.gov.uk/government/news/ministerial-appointments-august-2014 |title=Ministerial appointments: August 2014 – Press releases |website=Gov.uk |date=6 August 2014 |accessdate=17 February 2016}}</ref>
