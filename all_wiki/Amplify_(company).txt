'''Amplify''' is a curriculum and assessment company launched in July 2012. Amplify Curriculum was built on the foundation of Wireless Generation, the educational company [[News Corp (2013–present)|News Corp]] bought in 2010. Amplify products and services provide assessment and analytics for [[data-driven instruction]] and next-generation digital curriculum based on the [[Common Core State Standards]].<ref>Dawson, Christopher. (2012-07-23) [http://www.zdnet.com/news-corp-unveils-amplify-new-partnership-with-at-and-t-for-digital-ed-7000001410/ News Corp unveils Amplify: New partnership with AT&T for digital ed]. ZDNet. Retrieved on 2013-07-19.</ref>

News Corp sold Amplify in 2015 for an undisclosed sum to the [[Emerson Collective]], a philanthropic organization founded by [[Laurene Powell Jobs]]. Larry Berger, co-founder of Wireless Generation, is now the chief executive of the new company.<ref>{{cite web|title=News Corp sells digital education brand Amplify|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/2015/09/30/us-news-corp-divestiture-amplify-idUSKCN0RU2YN20150930|website=Reuters|date=6 October 2015}}</ref><ref name="cavanagh">Sean Cavanagh, [https://marketbrief.edweek.org/marketplace-k-12/news_corp_announces_plans_to_wind_down_amplifys_tablet_business/ "News Corp. Announces Plans to Wind Down Amplifyís Tablet Business,"] [https://marketbrief.edweek.org/ "MarketBrief"], August 13, 2015.</ref>

== Company overview ==
Amplify was formed after the purchase of Wireless Generation, which was founded in 2000 by Larry Berger and Greg Gunn. The company sold its products and services to districts and states that used government funding for early reading and other programs. It also developed and maintained the [[New York City]] online warehouse of student data ARIS, and wrote the algorithm for the School of One, the [[New York City Department of Education]]'s math help system. Larry Berger served as the CEO of Wireless Generation until the sale of the company in 2010. At the time of the sale, the users of Wireless Generation software included three million students and 200,000 educators.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://ny.chalkbeat.org/2010/11/22/murdoch-buys-education-tech-company-wireless-generation/#.U5nxQfldVyU |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |publisher=Chalk Beat |title=Murdoch buys education tech company Wireless Generation |author=Anna Phillips |date=November 22, 2010}}</ref>

In November 2010 a 90% stake in Wireless Generation was purchased by News Corp for $360 million. News Corp changed the name of its subsidiary in 2012 to Amplify.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.highbeam.com/doc/1A1-e550f0e9795e47de8beed3b139bcfa9d.html |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150924043712/http://www.highbeam.com/doc/1A1-e550f0e9795e47de8beed3b139bcfa9d.html |url-status=dead |archive-date=September 24, 2015 |title=News Corp brands education business Amplify |publisher=AP Online |date=July 23, 2012 |accessdate=July 24, 2014}}</ref> Following the acquisition, News Corp invested about half a billion dollars into the company in order to expand its offerings to devices and digital curriculum, designed to replace hard copy textbooks, and to decrease the price-point gap between traditional textbooks and tablet-based education.<ref name=mash>{{cite web |url=http://mashable.com/2013/08/29/news-corp-education-tablets/ |author=Travis Andrews |title=Inside News Corp's $540 Million Bet on American Classrooms |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |date=August 29, 2013 |publisher=Mashable}}</ref>

[[Joel Klein]], former chancellor for the New York City Department of Education and an executive vice-president with News Corp served as Amplify's CEO until 2015.<ref name="alpert">Lukas I. Alpert and George Stahl, [https://www.wsj.com/articles/news-corp-sells-its-amplify-education-businesses-1443651233 "News Corp Sells Its Amplify Education Businesses,"] ''[[The Wall Street Journal]]'', September 30, 2015.</ref> During his time as CEO, Klein stated that the goal of Amplify is to encourage the integration of computer technology into the common educational environment, rather than a separate learning environment such as a laboratory.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://live.wsj.com/video/joel-klein-can-technology-save-education/5F72C8D2-84DA-45DD-B788-9BDE88552B8D.html#!5F72C8D2-84DA-45DD-B788-9BDE88552B8D |title=Joel Klein interview |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |date=January 18, 2013 |publisher=Wall Street Journal}}</ref>

Since 2015, Amplify has found success with its curriculum and assessment offerings. Its 2018 revenue was $125 million and it now serves nearly 4 million students.  Its science program has been adopted by several large urban districts, including Chicago, New York, and Denver and has 950,000 users.<ref name="auto">{{cite web|url=https://www.fastcompany.com/90207561/amplify-education-gets-a-new-lease-on-life-this-time-from-a-nonprofit|title=Inside Laurene Powell Jobs's big bet on digital curriculum|first=Ainsley|last=Harris|date=16 October 2018|website=Fast Company}}</ref>

== Assessment==
Amplify provides assessment tools for K-12 schools. Tools are available for Math and ELA, Pre-K to 8th Grade. Amplify also provides secure data hosting, reporting and management for educational institutions.<ref name=Chozik>{{cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/03/06/business/media/news-corp-has-a-tablet-for-schools.html?pagewanted=all&_r=1& |date=May 6, 2013 |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |work=New York Times |title=News Corp. Has a Tablet for Schools |author=Amy Chozick}}</ref> [[Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium]] signed a contract with Amplify to create a digital library of formative assessment professional learning tools designed for Common Core State Standards teachers. Amplify also signed a contract with Smarter Balanced before its purchase by News Corp to develop reporting tools for teacher assessment.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://blogs.edweek.org/edweek/marketplacek12/2013/03/amplify_insight_wins_contract_from_common_core_testing_consortium.html |publisher=Education Week |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |title=Amplify Insight Wins Contract from Common-Core Testing Consortium |author=Sean Cavanagh |date=March 14, 2013}}</ref> The library provides online access to teachers for formative test items, and assessment tools.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://thejournal.com/articles/2013/03/18/amplify-insight-creating-digital-library-of-assessment-tools-for-smarter-balanced.aspx?=THENU |publisher=The Journal |title=Amplify Insight Creating Digital Library of Assessment Tools for Smarter Balanced |author=Dian Schaffhauser |accessdate=March 18, 2013}}</ref> Amplify's software uses data analysis to plan teaching tactics and track educational results.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/digital/devices/article/55012-news-corp-s-joel-klein-outlines-amplify-education-unit.html |publisher=Publishers Weekly |title=News Corp.'s Joel Klein Outlines Plans for Amplify Education Unit |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |author=Calvin Reid |date=December 5, 2012}}</ref> This has included the provision of data coaches to teachers in Delaware.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.edweek.org/ew/articles/2011/05/25/32delaware_ep.h30.html |title=Delaware Pushes to Meet Race to Top Promises |author=Michele McNeil |date=May 25, 2011 |publisher=Education Week}}</ref>

==Curriculum==
Amplify provides print, digital, and blended core curriculum for ELA and science. ELA curricula are based upon the Common Core State Standards and the science curriculum is aligned to the Next Generation Science Standards. The science program was developed in partnership with the Lawrence Hall of Science at the University of California Berkeley.  In each lesson, students take on the role of a scientist or engineer.  The lessons focus on natural phenomena and the application of concepts to real-world problems.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://hechingerreport.org/will-new-standards-improve-elementary-science-education/|title=Will new standards improve elementary science education?|date=11 July 2018|publisher=}}</ref>

The Amplify ELA curriculum also includes educational games that can be played by students in and outside of class time,<ref name=mash/><ref name=Chozik/> and a library of 300 pre-loaded books.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/jordanshapiro/2014/03/03/amplifys-middle-school-content-makes-learning-look-beautiful/ |title=Amplify's Middle School Content Makes Learning Look Beautiful |date=March 3, 2014 |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |work=Forbes |author=Jordan Shapiro}}</ref>

In 2018, Amplify announced that it entered the digital supplemental market with two programs:  Amplify Close Reading and Amplify Fractions.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.edsurge.com/news/2018-06-28-all-the-upgrades-and-updates-from-apple-google-and-more-at-iste-2018|title=All the Upgrades and Updates From Apple, Google and More at ISTE 2018 - EdSurge News|date=28 June 2018|website=EdSurge}}</ref>

==Ostrilope==
The Ostrilope is a fictional creature created by Amplify as a part of their Natural Selection unit. It is modeled after an [[ostrich]], with some differences, notably their lack of wings, and fur instead of feathers.{{citation needed|date=May 2019}} The Ostrilope is used in many lessons regarding [[natural selection]], usually to observe how species change.<ref>{{cite web |title=Ostrilope by Amplify |url=https://www.thingiverse.com/thing:529483 |website=www.thingiverse.com |accessdate=14 May 2019 |language=en |date=4 November 2014}}</ref>

==Thornpalm==
As for plants, the thornpalm is a fictional species of plant created . The Thornpalm's closest correlation resembles a ''[[Yucca brevifolia]]'' also known as a Joshua Tree. The Thornpalm is used in many lessons regarding natural selection, usually as a food source to the Ostrilope. In Amplify's provided curriculum, the Thornpalm's height can often be changed by the user, as well as the size of its thorns and its water storage capabilities.{{citation needed|date=May 2019}}

==Tablet==
{{main|Amplify Tablet}}
In March 2013, Amplify released the [[Amplify Tablet]], a customized [[Asus]] [[Android (operating system)|Android]] [[tablet computer|tablet]] with a suite of subscription-based software, offering education-oriented features and [[mobile app|apps]] designed for K-12 learning environments.<ref name=nyt-schooltablet>{{cite web|title=News Corp. Has a Tablet for Schools|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/03/06/business/media/news-corp-has-a-tablet-for-schools.html?pagewanted=all|work=The New York Times|accessdate=9 October 2013}}</ref><ref name=nyt-nochild>{{cite web|title=No Child Left Untableted|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/09/15/magazine/no-child-left-untableted.html?pagewanted=all&_r=0|work=The New York Times|accessdate=9 October 2013}}</ref><ref name=pcmag-amplifytab>{{cite web|title=News Corp.'s Amplify Unveils Education Tablet|url=https://www.pcmag.com/article2/0,2817,2416298,00.asp|work=PC Magazine|accessdate=9 October 2013}}</ref> In March 2014, the company released a new version of the Amplify Tablet designed by Intel.<ref name=Rotella>{{cite web |date=September 12, 2013 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/09/15/magazine/no-child-left-untableted.html?pagewanted=all&_r=0 |title=No Child Left Untableted |work=New York Times |accessdate=July 24, 2014 |author=Carol Rotella}}</ref> In 2015, Amplify announced it would cease marketing the tablet to new customers but would continue to service its existing customers.<ref name="cavanagh"/> Amplify is no longer in the hardware business.  Since 2015, its focus has been exclusively on curriculum and assessment.<ref name="auto"/>
