'''Martin Neil Baily''' (born March 29, 1949) is an [[economist]] at the [[Brookings Institution]] and formerly at the [[Peterson Institute]]. He is best known for his work on productivity and competitiveness and for his tenure as a cabinet member<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.brookings.edu/experts/martin-neil-baily/|title=Martin Neil Baily|date=2016-03-31|website=Brookings|language=en-US|access-date=2020-01-20}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.piie.com/newsroom|title=Newsroom|date=2016-02-02|website=PIIE|language=en|access-date=2020-01-20}}</ref> during the [[Clinton Administration]]. He was one of three members of the [[Council of Economic Advisers]] from 1994 to 1996, and chairman of the Council from 1999  to 2001. <ref>{{Cite web|url=https://obamawhitehouse.archives.gov/node/231806|title=Former Chairs of the Council of Economic Advisers|website=The White House|language=en|access-date=2020-01-20}}</ref> He currently co-chairs the [[Bipartisan Policy Center]]'s Financial Regulatory Reform Initiative and serves as a senior advisor at [[Albright Stonebridge Group]].

Baily was a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution (1979–89) and subsequently professor of economics at the [[University of Maryland, College Park|University of Maryland]] (1989–96). He was vice chairman of a [[United States National Academy of Sciences|National Academy of Sciences]] – [[United States National Research Council|National Research Council]] panel investigating the effect of computers on productivity. Baily co-founded the microeconomics issues of the ''[[Brookings Papers on Economic Activity]]''. He was a principal at [[McKinsey & Company]]'s Global Institute (1996–99) and has been a senior adviser to McKinsey since 2002. He joined the board of [[The Phoenix Companies]] in 2005 and is an academic adviser to the [[Congressional Budget Office]] and associate editor of the ''[[Journal of Economic Perspectives]]''.{{citation needed|date=September 2012}}

Baily earned his [[Ph.D.]] in economics at the [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology]] (MIT) and  his undergraduate degree at [[Cambridge University]] (UK), and taught at MIT and [[Yale University]]. He is the author of numerous books and articles and coauthor with Jacob Kirkegaard of ''Transforming the European Economy'' (2004).{{citation needed|date=September 2012}}

Congressional testimony:
