Local elections were held on 24 November 2018<ref>{{cite news|last1=Liu|first1=Lee-jung|last2=Lin|first2=Ko|title=Local elections to be held in November|url=http://focustaiwan.tw/news/aipl/201801160021.aspx|accessdate=17 January 2018|agency=Central News Agency|date=16 January 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.taiwannews.com.tw/en/news/3342509|date=16 January 2018|first1=Sophia|last1=Yang|title=Taiwan to host nine-in-one elections on Nov. 24|publisher=Taiwan News|accessdate=17 January 2018}}</ref> in Taiwan, to elect county magistrates (city mayors), county (city) councilors, township mayors, township councilors and chiefs of village (borough) in 6 municipalities and 16 counties (cities). Elected officials would serve a four-year term. Polling stations were open from 08:00 to 16:00 on the election day.<ref name="engweb">{{cite web|url=http://engweb.cec.gov.tw/files/11-1030-5692.php|title=Taiwan Local Elections of 2014 - Central Election Commission|work=cec.gov.tw|access-date=2019-01-23|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141119062153/http://engweb.cec.gov.tw/files/11-1030-5692.php|archive-date=2014-11-19|url-status=dead}}</ref>

The elections resulted in a substantial defeat for the DPP. The DPP previously held 13 of 22 municipalities and counties, but won only 6 in this election due to widespread public distrust, a ''de facto'' [[vote of no confidence]] on President Tsai's Administration, both politically (relations with China), economically (agriculture, tourism), and socially (pollution, labor laws, wages), which were reflected in the series of [[2018 Taiwanese referendum|referendum]] results.{{Citation needed|reason=Explain the meaning of a de facto vote of no confidence|date=October 2019}} The KMT won back executive control of 7 municipalities and counties from the DPP, while Ko Wen-je won his re-election for Taipei mayor.

==Background==
This local election is seen as the first test for the incumbent President [[Tsai Ing-wen]] since assuming office in May 2016. The results may affect the next legislative and presidential election in January 2020. The [[Central Election Commission (Taiwan)|Central Election Commission]] opened election registration to candidates on 27 August 2018.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Chen |first1=Yu-fu |last2=Hetherington |first2=William |title=Election registrations open today |url=http://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2018/08/27/2003699287 |accessdate=27 August 2018 |work=Taipei Times |date=27 August 2018}}</ref>

The [[Democratic Progressive Party]] has won the popular vote against the [[Kuomintang]] in all of the last three elections.<ref name="cec">{{cite web |url=http://db.cec.gov.tw/ |script-title=zh:中選會資料庫網站]，中央選舉委員會 |publisher=Central Election Commission |language=zh-hant}}</ref> This trend has continued in the 2016 elections, where the [[Democratic Progressive Party]] won a majority in the [[Legislative Yuan]] with 68 seats and the presidency.

==Aftermath==
The Taiwan president [[Tsai Ing-wen]] has announced resignation as the party head for Democratic Progressive Party; Premier [[William Lai]] has announced resignation on Facebook, but reserved [http://news.ltn.com.tw/news/politics/breakingnews/2623422]; DPP secretary general Hung Yao-fu and Secretary-General to the President [[Chen Chu]] has also announced their resignations. Following the elections, the Taiwanese foreign minister claimed that China had meddled in the elections.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Brown |first1=Adrian |title=China accused of political meddling after Taiwan local elections |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2018/11/china-accused-political-meddling-taiwan-local-elections-181128143525100.html |website=www.aljazeera.com |publisher=Al Jazeera |accessdate=28 November 2018}}</ref>

Defector [[William Wang (defector)|William Wang]] claimed that the government of China had successfully supported candidates in the 2018 Taiwanese local elections.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.taiwannews.com.tw/en/news/3823123|title=Chinese spy confirms China's involvement in Taiwan 2020 election|date=23 November 2019|accessdate=25 November 2019|website=[[Taiwan News]]|quote=Wang said that Beijing successfully backed several pro-China politicians, who won seats in the 2018 local elections.}}</ref>
