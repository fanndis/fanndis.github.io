'''Julie Halard-Decugis''' (born 10 September 1970) is a French former professional [[tennis]] player.

==Tennis career==
Halard-Decugis lived in [[La Baule]], France during the initial stages of her career and later moved to [[Pully]], Switzerland. She turned professional in 1986. She won the [[French Open]] junior singles title in 1988 and was the [[The Championships, Wimbledon|Wimbledon]] junior singles runner-up in 1987. She retired from the [[WTA Tour]] tennis circuit at the end of the 2000 season.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Berkrot |first1=Bill |title=Halard-Decugis decides to retire |url=https://www.independent.co.uk/sport/tennis/halard-decugis-decides-to-retire-624429.html |accessdate=6 October 2019 |publisher=[[The Independent]] |date=16 November 2000 |location=[[New York City]]}}</ref> Her highest WTA Tour singles and doubles rankings was number seven and number one respectively. She had been coached by Arnaud Decugis since 1989.

Halard-Decugis won her first WTA Tour singles title in Puerto Rico. She enjoyed her best season in 1996, when she won her first WTA Tour Tier II singles title in Paris and finished the year with a career-high season-ending singles ranking of No. 15 and as the No. 1 singles player from France. This occurred despite the fact that her playing schedule in the second half of 1996 was curtailed because of a wrist injury sustained during the [[Fed Cup]] semifinal match against Spain. She only played two tournaments in late 1997 because of injuries.

By winning the singles title in [[Ordina Open|Rosmalen]] in 1998, she became the 20th player to have won singles titles on all four surfaces in the Open Era. Halard also won the singles and doubles titles in [[Pattaya Women's Open|Pattaya]] that year, and broke into the top 10 singles ranking in August 1999, becoming the fifth Frenchwoman after [[Françoise Dürr]], [[Mary Pierce]], [[Nathalie Tauziat]] and [[Amélie Mauresmo]] to do so. In 1999, she won WTA Tour singles titles in [[1999 ASB Classic|Auckland]] and [[1999 DFS Classic|Birmingham]] and was runner-up on three other occasions.<ref>{{cite web|title=Tennis — DFS Classic; Halard-Decugis beats Tauziat|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1999/06/14/sports/plus-tennis-dfs-classic-halard-decugis-beats-tauziat.html|publisher=[[The New York Times]]|date=14 June 1999}}</ref> Between 15 November 1999 and 9 January 2000, [[Julie Halard]], Nathalie Tauziat, Amélie Mauresmo and Mary Pierce were all ranked inside the singles top 10, the first time France had four players ranked among the singles top 10.

2000 was to be the final and perhaps the finest year of Halard's professional playing career. She reached the [[2000 Australian Open|Australian Open]] singles quarterfinal for the second time, captured the second WTA Tour Tier II title of her career in [[International Women's Open|Eastbourne]] and reached her career-high singles ranking of No. 7 in February. Halard was also runner-up in Tokyo's Princess Cup in October and won the doubles title with [[Ai Sugiyama]]. The following week, she won both the singles and doubles titles at the Japan Open in Tokyo, saving three match points in the final to defeat the defending champion [[Amy Frazier]].

On her 30th birthday, Halard won the [[2000 US Open (tennis)|2000 US Open]] women's doubles title with Ai Sugiyama, her only [[Grand Slam (tennis)|Grand Slam]] title as a professional. The pair also reached the final at [[The Championships, Wimbledon|Wimbledon]], the semifinal at the [[French Open]] and  the quarterfinal at the [[Australian Open]] that year. Halard-Decugis won nine other doubles titles in 2000, five of them with Sugiyama, and became the first Frenchwoman to attain the No. 1 doubles ranking in the Open Era. Halard-Decugis represented her country in the Federation Cup Fed Cup from 1990 to 2000 and in the [[Olympic Games]] in 1992 and 2000.

==Personal life==
She married her coach, Arnaud Decugis, on 22 September 1995.<ref>{{cite web|author1=Gene Frenette|title=When Love is a good game|url=http://jacksonville.com/tu-online/stories/041600/spo_2796693.html#.WiAXndKnGHs|publisher=[[jacksonville.com]]|date=16 April 2000}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author1=Iain Carter|title=Julie Halard-Decugis|url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/wimbledon2000/womens_seeds/798501.stm|publisher=[[BBC Sport]]|date=June 2000}}</ref> Arnaud Decugis is the great nephew of [[Max Decugis]], a leading tennis player from France during the early 20th century. The couple have two children{{citation needed|date=August 2015}}
