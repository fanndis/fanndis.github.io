'''Juliet Kathleen Pannett''' {{postnominals|country=GBR|size=100%|MBE|FRSA}} ([[Name at birth|née]] '''Somers'''; 15 July 1911 – 22 August 2005) was an English portrait painter.

==Background==
Born in Hove, East Sussex, Pannett started painting at three, and wanted to be a professional artist by seventeen.<ref name=NPG>{{cite web |url=http://www.npg.org.uk/collections/search/person/mp07394/juliet-pannett |title=Juliet Panett |publisher=[[National Portrait Gallery, London|National Portrait Gallery]]|accessdate=7 April 2013}}</ref><ref name=NPG1>{{cite web|url=http://www.npg.org.uk/whatson/display/2004/juliet-pannett.php|title=Juliet Pannett|publisher= [[National Portrait Gallery, London|National Portrait Gallery]]|accessdate=7 April 2013}}</ref> She trained at [[Faculty of Arts (University of Brighton)|Brighton College of Art]] in the 1920s under Louis Ginnett, and received her first artistic commission at eighteen to draw local Sussex characters for the ''Sussex County Magazine''.<ref name=NPG1/> Pannett maintained a studio in Hove and was elected a member of the Society of Graphic Artists in 1934. She was a professional artist until her marriage in 1938.<ref name=NPG1/><ref name=TelegSep05/>

Pannett married Major Rick Pannett who had been injured in the [[First World War]] when he was shot in the mouth. The bullet had pierced his cheek and missed all his bones.<ref name=BBCMar13/> Pannett gave up painting after the birth of her children. Suffering from depression, it was alleviated when she resumed painting.<ref name=BBCMar13/> In 1949 the family moved to [[Croydon]] where she built a studio in her garage.<ref name=TelegSep05/> The Pannett family later moved to [[Angmering]] in Sussex in 1964.<ref name=TelegSep05/>

==Noted portraits==
Among her subjects were [[Bernard Montgomery, 1st Viscount Montgomery of Alamein|Field-Marshal Montgomery]], film director [[Jean Cocteau]], athlete [[Chris Chataway]], [[Louis Armstrong]] and [[Leonard Bernstein]].<ref name=BBCMar13/> Pannett was a keen cricketer and drew such cricketers as [[Maurice Tate]] and [[Bob Wyatt]].<ref name=BBCMar13/> Pannett preferred to paint men rather than women, describing men as she found them "less troublesome as sitters...They never ask me not to put in their double chin or to leave out their wrinkles."<ref name=ObvOct05/>

Before the televised proceedings of the [[House of Commons of the United Kingdom|House of Commons]] Pannett was employed by the [[Illustrated London News]] from 1957 to 1964 to sketch the events of the House.<ref name=NPG/> Sitting in the gallery of the Commons Pannett drew such historically significant events as [[Winston Churchill]]'s last appearance in the house in 1964.<ref name=BBCMar13/> Pannett later formally drew several other [[List of Prime Ministers of the United Kingdom|British Prime Ministers]], including [[Alec Douglas-Home]], who told her that he had "the biggest head in London" and could never find a hat to fit him.<ref name=BBCMar13/> Later Prime Ministers painted by Pannett included [[Edward Heath]], [[James Callaghan]] and [[Margaret Thatcher]].<ref name=BBCMar13/>

For several years around 1960, Pannett supplied the [[Radio Times]] with drawings of musicians and other figures being broadcast on BBC radio. In February 1963, she was commissioned by ''The Illustrated London News'' to do several sketches of C. S. Lewis.

She painted [[Elizabeth II|Queen Elizabeth II]] in 1989 for the [[Chartered Insurance Institute]].<ref name=BBCMar13>{{cite news|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-21532151|author=Justin Parkinson|title=Painting the Queen (and her prime ministers)|newspaper=[[BBC News Online]]|location=London|date=6 March 2013}}</ref> Pannett had previously drawn Princes [[Andrew, Duke of York|Andrew]] and [[Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex|Edward]].<ref name=BBCMar13/> Upon seeing the portrait [[Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh|Prince Philip]] commissioned a pastel drawing from her of the Queen for the 25th anniversary of the [[History of Malta#History of independent Malta 1964-.|Independence of Malta]].<ref name=TelegSep05/>

==Recognition==
Due to her failing eyesight her last major portrait was of General Sir [[John Wilsey]].<ref name=TelegSep05>{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/1498184/Juliet-Pannett.html|author=Justin Parkinson|title=Obituary: Juliet Pannett|newspaper=[[the Daily Telegraph]]|location=London|date=12 September 2005}}</ref> She was appointed an [[Order of the British Empire|MBE]] in 1993, and was a member of the Pastel Society and a fellow of the [[Royal Society of Arts]] and an Honorary Freeman of the [[Worshipful Company of Painter-Stainers]], who awarded her their gold medal in 1995.<ref name=TelegSep05/>

At the time of her death in 2005 she was the oldest living artist to have work in the [[National Portrait Gallery, London|National Portrait Gallery]]'s collection.<ref name=TelegSep05/> Her husband had died in 1980, she was survived by her two children, who are both artists.<ref name=ObvOct05>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/news/2005/oct/06/guardianobituaries.artsobituaries|author=DL Libterre|title=Obituary: Juliet Pannett|newspaper=[[The Observer]]|location=London|date=16 October 2005}}</ref>

Her autobiography, ''My Colourful Life'' was published in 2006.
