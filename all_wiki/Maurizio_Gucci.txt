''' Maurizio Gucci '''(September 26, 1948 – March 27, 1995), was an [[Italy|Italian]] businessman, and the one-time head of the [[Gucci]] fashion house. He was the son of [[Maurizio D'Ancora|Rodolfo Gucci]], and grandson of [[Guccio Gucci]].

== Biography ==
Maurizio Gucci was born on September 26, 1948 in [[Florence]], to [[Maurizio D'Ancora|Rodolfo Gucci]] and [[Sandra Ravel]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last=Forden|first=Sara G.|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=EExuyvAOiNgC&pg=PA16|title=The House of Gucci: A Sensational Story of Murder, Madness, Glamour, and Greed|date=2012-05-08|publisher=Harper Collins|isbn=978-0-06-222267-1|pages=16|language=en}}</ref> 

In May 1983, [[Maurizio D'Ancora|Rodolfo Gucci]] died in Milan.<ref>{{Cite news|date=1983-05-16|title=Rodolfo Gucci Is Dead at 71; Brother in Leather Goods Firm (Published 1983)|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1983/05/16/obituaries/rodolfo-gucci-is-dead-at-71-brother-in-leather-goods-firm.html|access-date=2020-10-12|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> His son, Maurizio Gucci inherited his father’s majority stake in the company and launched a legal war against his uncle [[Aldo Gucci]] for full control of Gucci (a prosecution led by the city prosecutor [[Rudy Giuliani|Rudolph Giuliani]], and with [[Domenico De Sole|Domenico de Sole]] representing the Gucci family).<ref>{{Cite book|last=Gucci|first=Patricia | author-link = Patricia Gucci |url=https://books.google.com/books/about/In_the_Name_of_Gucci.html?id=xdNfCgAAQBAJ|title=In the Name of Gucci: A Memoir|date=2016-05-10|publisher=Crown/Archetype|isbn=978-0-8041-3894-9|language=en|chapter=chapter 21}}</ref>

In 1988, Maurizio Gucci sold almost 47.8% of Gucci to the Bahraïn-based investment fund [[Investcorp]] (owner of [[Tiffany & Co.|Tiffany]] since 1984), and withheld the other 50%.<ref>{{Cite web|title=INVESTCORP BUYS HALF OF GUCCI {{!}} JOC.com|url=https://www.joc.com/investcorp-buys-half-gucci_19880607.html|access-date=2020-08-09|website=www.joc.com}}</ref> From 1991 to 1993, Gucci's finances were in the red. Maurizio Gucci was blamed for spending extravagant amounts of money on the company's headquarters in [[Florence]] (Via delle Caldaie palazzo) and in [[Milan]].<ref>{{Cite news|last=Tagliabue|first=John|date=1995-12-14|title=INTERNATIONAL BUSINESS: Gucci Gains Ground With Revival of Style;Belt-Tightening in the Work Force And Lavish Spending on Marketing|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1995/12/14/business/international-business-gucci-gains-ground-with-revival-style-belt-tightening.html|access-date=2020-08-09|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

Maurizio Gucci went on to sell his stock in [[Gucci]] in 1993 for $170 million to the Bahrain-based investment group, [[Investcorp]]. In 1995, a year and a half after the sale of Gucci, he was gunned down by a hired [[hit man]]. His ex-wife [[Patrizia Reggiani]] was convicted of arranging the killing in 1998.<ref>[[Dana Thomas]],   ''Deluxe: How Luxury Lost Its Luster'', p. 59</ref><ref>Sara Gay Forden, ''The House of Gucci: A Sensational Story of Murder, Madness, Glamour, and Greed'', [[HarperCollins]] {{ISBN|0-688-16313-0}}, 2001.</ref><ref>{{Cite news|last=Stanley|first=Alessandra|date=1998-11-04|title=Former Wife Given 29 Years for Ordering Gucci Slaying (Published 1998)|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1998/11/04/world/former-wife-given-29-years-for-ordering-gucci-slaying.html|access-date=2020-10-12|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>
