'''David Robert Malpass''' (born March 8, 1956)<ref>{{cite web|title=Nominations Of Eric D. Hargan, David Malpass, Andrew K. Maloney and Brent James McIntosh|url=https://www.finance.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/30209.pdf|work=[[U.S. Senate Finance Committee]]|date =June 7, 2017|publisher = [[U.S. Government Publishing Office]]|accessdate=February 6, 2019}}</ref> is an American economic analyst and former government official serving as [[President of the World Bank Group]] since 2019. Malpass previously served as [[Under Secretary of the Treasury for International Affairs]] under [[Donald Trump]], Deputy Assistant Treasury Secretary under [[Ronald Reagan]], Deputy Assistant Secretary of State under [[George H. W. Bush]]. He served as Chief Economist at [[Bear Stearns]] for the six years preceding its collapse.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Katz|first1=Celeste|last2=Saltonstall|first2=David|title=Ex-finance honcho shooting for Gillibrand's Senate seat|url=http://www.nydailynews.com/news/politics/2010/04/15/2010-04-15_exfinance_honcho_shooting_for_gilly_seat.html|newspaper=[[New York Daily News]]|accessdate=November 20, 2016|date=April 15, 2010}}</ref> 

During the [[2016 United States presidential election|2016 U.S. presidential election]], Malpass served as an economic advisor to [[Donald Trump]], and in 2017, he was nominated and confirmed as [[Under Secretary of the Treasury for International Affairs]] at the [[United States Department of the Treasury|Treasury Department]]. Malpass was elected [[President of the World Bank]] on April 4, 2019, having been nominated to the position in February 2019 by the [[Trump Administration]].<ref name=NPR>{{cite news|last=Zarroli |first=Jim|title=Trump Nominates Outspoken Critic David Malpass To Head World Bank|url=https://www.npr.org/2019/02/06/691971989/trump-is-expected-to-name-outspoken-critic-to-head-world-bank|accessdate=February 6, 2019|publisher=[[NPR]]|date=February 6, 2019}}</ref><ref name = HsuNYT>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/04/05/business/david-malpass-world-bank.html|title=David Malpass, Trump’s Pick to Lead World Bank, Is Approved|last=Hsu|first=Tiffany|date=April 5, 2019|work=[[The New York Times]]|accessdate=April 6, 2019}}</ref> He formally took office on April 9, 2019.<ref name = HsuNYT />

==Education and career==
Malpass earned a B.A. in [[physics]] at [[Colorado College]] and an MBA at the [[University of Denver]].<ref name="nr"/> He studied international economics at [[Georgetown University]]'s School of Foreign Service. He speaks Spanish, Russian, and French.<ref name="announcement">{{cite news|title=David Malpass For Senate: Republican Economist To Challenge Gillibrand|first=Beth|last=Fouhy |work=[[The Huffington Post]]|date=April 14, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=David Malpass: Running For Senate|first=Stephanie |last=Dahle|work=[[Forbes]]|date=April 14, 2010|accessdate=April 22, 2019|url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/face-to-face/2010/04/14/david-malpass-senate-gillibrand-republican/}}</ref> From 1977 to 1983, he worked in Portland, Oregon for Esco Corporation and [[Arthur Andersen]]'s systems consulting group, where he became a licensed CPA.

During the administrations of Ronald Reagan and George H. W. Bush, Malpass worked on an array of economic, budget, and foreign policy issues including small business promotion throughout Latin America and the 1986 tax cut.<ref name="nr">{{cite web |url=http://www.nationalreview.com/author/david-malpass|title=David Malpass (author profile)|work=[[National Review]] |accessdate=September 1, 2016}}</ref> Malpass served as the Republican staff director of the [[United States Congress Joint Economic Committee]] from 1989 to 1990, and as a member of Congress's blue-ribbon panel on budget scoring from 2002 to 2003.<ref name="nr" />

Malpass was chief economist at [[Bear Stearns]] from 1993 to 2008.<ref name=baker>{{cite news |accessdate=February 6, 2019 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/02/04/business/world-bank-david-malpass.html |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=February 4, 2019 |title=Trump to Nominate David Malpass to Lead the World Bank|first=Peter|last=Baker}}</ref> As a result of the global financial crisis and under the prodding of the [[Federal Reserve]] and [[United States Department of the Treasury]], Bear Sterns was sold to [[JPMorgan Chase]] in March 2008 for 6% of its value twelve months prior.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Sorkin|first1=Andrew Ross|title=JPMorgan Raises Bid for Bear Stearns to $10 a Share|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/03/24/business/24deal-web.html|accessdate=November 20, 2016|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=March 24, 2008}}</ref>

In June 2008, Malpass founded Encima Global, a [[New York City]] firm providing daily analysis of global economic and political trends relevant to institutional investors. In 2010, Malpass ran for the Republican nomination for [[United States Senate]] in that year's [[United States Senate special election in New York, 2010|special election in New York]]. He placed second in the three-way primary with 38% of the vote after former Congressman [[Joe DioGuardi]]'s 42%. In 2012, he wrote a chapter entitled 'Sound Money, Sound Policy' in ''[[The 4% Solution: Unleashing the Economic Growth America Needs]]'', published by the [[George W. Bush Presidential Center]].

Malpass writes a column for ''[[Forbes]]'' and is a contributor to the op-ed section of ''The Wall Street Journal''.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/dont-blame-the-feds-interest-rate-baby-step-1455148753|title=Don’t Blame the Fed’s Interest-Rate Baby Step|last=Malpass|first=David|newspaper=[[Wall Street Journal]]|access-date=April 13, 2016|date=February 10, 2016}}</ref> He is also a frequent television commentator.

Malpass sits on the boards of UBS Funds, the New Mountain Financial Corporation,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.newmountainfinance.com/our-team/david-r-malpass |title=David R. Malpass |accessdate=January 10, 2013 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20121012011549/http://www.newmountainfinance.com/our-team/david-r-malpass |archivedate=October 12, 2012 |publisher=[[New Mountain Finance Corporation]]}}</ref> and the Gary Klinsky Children's Center. He is also a former director of the National Committee on United States–China Relations, the Council of the Americas, and the Economic Club of New York, and a former member of the board of trustees of the [[Manhattan Institute]].

===Economic forecasts===
Malpass has been noted for his forecasts before the [[financial crisis of 2007–2008]] and in the time period following the [[Great Recession]]. In 2007, before the housing market collapse, Malpass wrote for the ''Wall Street Journal'' that "Housing and debt markets are not that big a part of the U.S. economy, or of job creation...the housing- and debt-market corrections will probably add to the length of the U.S. economic expansion."<ref name="Slate">{{cite web|last=Weissmann|first=Jordan|title=Trump Taps Bear Stearns Economist Who Said Not to Worry About Credit Crisis for Key Treasury Job|date=January 5, 2017 |url=http://www.slate.com/blogs/moneybox/2017/01/05/trump_picks_ex_bear_stearns_economist_for_treasury_position.html|work=[[Slate (magazine)|Slate]]|accessdate=February 2, 2017}}</ref> He also called for the raising of interest rates in 2011 at a time when others believed this would be harmful to the economy.<ref name="motherjones">{{cite web|last1=Choma|first1=Russ|title=Trump's Economic Adviser Said the Economy Was Fine—Right Before It Imploded|url=https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2016/08/trump-economic-adviser-said-economy-fine-before-crash|work=[[Mother Jones (magazine)|Mother Jones]]|accessdate=February 2, 2017}}</ref> [[Bruce Bartlett]] cited Malpass's 2008 forecast of economic growth and his 2012 forecast of recession as specific examples of partisan bias in economic forecasts.<ref name="New York Times">{{cite news|last=Bartlett|first=Bruce|title=Partisan Bias and Economic Forecasts |url=https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2012/10/09/partisan-bias-and-economic-forecasts/|work=[[The New York Times]] |accessdate=February 1, 2017|date=October 9, 2012}}</ref>

===Trump advisor===
Malpass joined [[Donald Trump]]'s [[Donald Trump presidential campaign, 2016|presidential campaign]] in May 2016 as Senior Economic Advisor. He appeared frequently on television and radio to support Trump's message of faster growth through policy reforms. Four of his pre-election ''Forbes'' columns discussed the need for political upheaval to upend the status quo.<ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/currentevents/2016/04/26/upheaval-to-reinvent-u-s-politics/|title=Upheaval To Reinvent U.S. Politics |last=Malpass|first=David|newspaper=[[Forbes]]|accessdate=December 17, 2016|date=April 26, 2017}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/currentevents/people/dmalpass/|title=David Malpass (author profile)|work=[[Forbes]]|access-date=December 17, 2016}}</ref> On August 5, the campaign announced an economic round-table including Malpass.<ref>{{cite news |title=Jobs strong, but too many Americans still left out, new Trump economic aide says|url=https://www.cnbc.com/2016/08/05/jobs-strong-but-too-many-americans-still-left-out-new-trump-economic-aide-says.html|first=Matthew J.|last=Belvedere|accessdate=August 5, 2016|publisher=[[CNBC]]|date=August 5, 2016}}</ref> His September 1, 2016, ''New York Times'' editorial described fast growth through a policy upheaval covering taxes, trade, regulations and energy ("Why Our Economy Needs Trump").<ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/09/01/opinion/why-this-economy-needs-donald-trump.html|title=Why This Economy Needs Donald Trump |last=Malpass|first=David|date=September 1, 2016|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|access-date=December 17, 2016}}</ref> Throughout the election time period, Malpass was heading the [[Presidential transition of Donald Trump|transition team]]'s work on economic agencies including Treasury, Commerce, the Federal Reserve, USTR and independent regulatory agencies as Trump prepared to become president.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/2016/11/16/502175860/from-lobbyists-to-loyalists-see-whos-on-donald-trumps-transition-team |title=From Lobbyists To Loyalists, See Who's On Donald Trump's Transition Team|last=Naylor|first=Brian|work=[[NPR]]|date=November 16, 2016|accessdate=April 22, 2019}}</ref>

===Under Secretary of the Treasury===
[[File:David Maplass official photo.jpg|thumb|150px|Malpass's official photo as Under Secretary]]
In March 2017, the White House announced that Malpass would be President Trump's nominee for [[Under Secretary of the Treasury for International Affairs]].<ref>{{cite news|last=Beech|first=Eric|title=Trump to nominate Goldman Sachs' Donovan as deputy Treasury secretary|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-trump-treasury-idUSKBN16M00D|accessdate=March 31, 2017|publisher=[[Reuters]] |date=March 14, 2017}}</ref> Malpass was confirmed for the position by the [[United States Senate]] on August 3, 2017.<ref>{{cite web |title=PN103 — David Malpass — Department of the Treasury |url=https://www.congress.gov/nomination/115th-congress/103 |website=[[Congress.gov]]|accessdate=September 8, 2018}}</ref>

Malpass took a critical position on [[China]] during his tenure.<ref>{{cite web |title=Top US Treasury Official Slams China's 'Non-Market Behavior' |url=https://www.cnbc.com/2018/02/21/us-treasury-official-slams-chinas-economic-policies.html |date=February 21, 2018 |publisher=[[CNBC]] |via=[[Reuters]] |accessdate=September 8, 2018}}</ref> He was described as "a champion of President Donald Trump’s protectionist message."<ref>{{cite web |last=Mohsin |first=Saleha |title=Treasury Struggles to Keep Staff in Unit at Heart of Trade War |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-07-12/treasury-struggles-to-keep-staff-in-unit-at-heart-of-trade-war |date=July 12, 2018 |work=[[Bloomberg News]] |accessdate=September 8, 2018}}</ref>

=== World Bank President ===
In February 2019, President Trump announced Malpass as the nominee for [[President of the World Bank Group|President of the World Bank]], succeeding [[Jim Yong Kim]], who had announced in January 2019 that he would be stepping down three years prior to the end of his five-year term in 2022.<ref name=NPR/> Malpass was unanimously approved by the executive board on April 5, 2019,<ref name = HsuNYT /><ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/trump-nominee-malpass-selected-as-next-world-bank-president-11554482367 |title=Trump Nominee Malpass Selected as Next World Bank President |last=Zumbrun |first=Josh |date=April 5, 2019 |work=[[Wall Street Journal]] |access-date=April 5, 2019}}</ref> and began his term on April 9.<ref name= "Reuters">{{Cite news |url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-imf-worldbank-malpass-idUSKCN1RM049 |title=New World Bank boss vows to keep climate goals, evolve China relationship |last=Lawder |first=David |date=April 9, 2019 |work=[[Reuters]] |access-date=April 15, 2019}}</ref>
