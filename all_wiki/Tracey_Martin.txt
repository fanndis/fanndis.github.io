'''Tracey Anne Martin''' (born 1 July 1964) is a New Zealand politician and a former member of the [[New Zealand House of Representatives]]. She is a member of the [[New Zealand First]] Party  and served as Deputy Leader from 2013 to 2015. She served as Minister of Children, Seniors, Internal Affairs and Associate Minister of Education from 2017 to 2020. Martin lost her seat in Parliament during the [[2020 New Zealand general election]].

==Prior to entering Parliament==
Her pre-children profession was as a Credit Controller. For the next 15 years, prior to entering Parliament, Martin was a stay at home parent and was very active in the [[Warkworth, New Zealand|Warkworth]] community. She spent a significant amount of time on parent-based fundraising and volunteer committees for Mahurangi Kindergarten, Warkworth Primary School and [[Mahurangi College]].

In her own time, outside of Parliament, Martin served as the Chair of the Board of Trustees at [[Mahurangi College]] for over a decade before resigning when she became the Associate Minister for Education in 2018.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://nz.educationhq.com/news/45658/movers-shakers-policy-makers-tracey-martin-associate-minister-of-education/#|title=Movers, shakers & policy makers – Tracey Martin, Associate Minister of Education — EducationHQ New Zealand|website=Nz.educationhq.com|accessdate=30 January 2019}}</ref>

Martin has been involved with New Zealand First since the party started in 1993, and has been a member of its board of directors since 2008. 
She was selected as a candidate for the [[New Zealand general election, 2008|2008 general election]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=10783495|title=Peters' new kid plunges into fray|date=4 February 2012|accessdate=30 January 2019|work=[[New Zealand Herald]]}}</ref> She successfully stood for the Rodney Local Board during the [[2010 Auckland local elections|2010 Auckland Council elections]].<ref name="Martin Rodney Local Board">{{cite web|title=Rodney Local Board|url=http://www.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/EN/AboutCouncil/LocalBoards/rodneylocalboard/Pages/default.aspx|publisher=[[Auckland Council]]|work=Local Boards|accessdate=3 December 2011}}</ref>

===Fifth National Government, 2011–2017===
Martin was first elected to the [[New Zealand House of Representatives]] during the [[New Zealand general election, 2011|2011 general election]] and was subsequently appointed as deputy leader of [[New Zealand First]].<ref>{{cite news |title='Big boys' trying to keep me out – Peters |url=http://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=10768266 |accessdate=19 August 2019 |newspaper=[[New Zealand Herald]] |date=24 November 2011}}</ref> She was first elected to Parliament as a New Zealand First list MP based in Warkworth, in 2011.<ref>{{cite web |title=Official Count Results – Successful Candidates – 2011 general election |url=https://electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2011/successfulcand.html |publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]] |accessdate=19 August 2019}}</ref>

Along with all other New Zealand First MPs, Martin voted against the [[Marriage (Definition of Marriage) Amendment Act 2013|Marriage Amendment Act]], which permits [[same-sex marriage in New Zealand]], in 2013.<ref>{{cite news |title=Marriage equality bill: How MPs voted|url=http://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=10830383|accessdate=29 September 2014|work=[[The New Zealand Herald]]|date=29 August 2012}}</ref> New Zealand First requested that the bill become a referendum issue however the request was denied.

During the [[2014 New Zealand general election|2014 general election]], Martin was re-elected to Parliament on the New Zealand First party list.<ref>{{cite web |title=Official Count Results – Successful Candidates |url=https://archive.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2014/successfulcand.html |publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]] |accessdate=5 November 2020 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20200117211903/http://archive.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2014/successfulcand.html |archivedate=17 January 2020 |url-status=live}}</ref> The party won nine percent of the popular vote and eleven seats.<ref>{{cite web |title=2014 GENERAL ELECTION – OFFICIAL RESULT |url=https://archive.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2014/ |publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]] |accessdate=5 November 2020 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20200117211235/http://archive.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2014/ |archivedate=17 January 2020 |date=17 October 2020 |url-status=live}}</ref>

On 3 July 2015 it was announced that Martin had been replaced as deputy leader following a caucus vote and replaced by [[Ron Mark]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=Jones |first1=Nicholas |title=Ron Mark new NZ First deputy leader |url=http://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=11475210 |accessdate=3 July 2015 |work=[[The New Zealand Herald]] |date=3 July 2015}}</ref>

In 2015 Martin sponsored the Social Security (Clothing Allowances for Orphans and Unsupported Children) Amendment Bill.<ref name="www.parliament.nz">{{cite web|url=https://www.parliament.nz/en/pb/bills-and-laws/bills-proposed-laws/document/00DBHOH_BILL11958_1/social-security-clothing-allowances-for-orphans-and-unsupported|title=Social Security (Clothing Allowances for Orphans and Unsupported Children) Amendment Bill – New Zealand Parliament|publisher=[[New Zealand Parliament]]|accessdate=30 January 2019}}</ref> The bill gave unsupported child or orphan clothing allowance parity with foster children.<ref name="www.parliament.nz"/> She has also been a strong advocate for the expansion of this allowance so that it can be accessed by kin carers.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Owen |first1=Lisa |title=The Nation: Lisa Owen interviews Tracey Martin |url=http://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PO1711/S00140/the-nation-lisa-owen-interviews-tracey-martin.htm |accessdate=19 August 2019 |work=The Nation |publisher=[[Scoop (website)|Scoop]] |date=12 November 2017}}</ref>

During the {{NZ election link|2017}}, Martin was re-elected on the New Zealand First party list.<ref>{{cite web|title=2017 General Election – Successful candidates|url=http://www.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2017/successful-candidates.html|publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]]|accessdate=1 November 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200610041429/https://www.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2017/successful-candidates.html|archive-date=10 June 2020}}</ref> The party won 7.2 percent of the vote and nine seats.<ref>{{cite web|title=2017 General Election – Overall Result|url=http://www.electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2017/|publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]]|accessdate=1 November 2017}}</ref>

===Sixth Labour Government, 2017–2020===
Following the formation of a [[Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand|Labour-NZ First coalition government]], Martin was as appointed Minister for Children, [[Minister of Internal Affairs (New Zealand)|Minister of Internal Affairs]], [[Minister for Seniors]], and Associate Minister of Education.<ref>{{cite web|title=Hon Tracey Martin|url=https://www.parliament.nz/en/mps-and-electorates/members-of-parliament/martin-tracey/|publisher=[[New Zealand Parliament]]|accessdate=1 November 2017}}</ref> Martin has also been NZ First spokesperson for broadcasting, communications and IT, education and women's affairs.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.newshub.co.nz/home/politics/2017/10/four-nz-first-mps-and-winston-grab-plumb-jobs-in-government-line-up.html|title=Four NZ First MPs (and Winston) grab plumb jobs in government line-up|date=25 October 2017|accessdate=30 January 2019|work=[[Newshub]]}}</ref>

Following an attempted "uplifting" by [[Ministry for Children|Oranga Tamariki]] social workers of a child in [[Hastings, New Zealand|Hastings]] in June 2019, the Minister for Children Martin met with local iwi [[Ngāti Kahungunu]] and the [[Māori Council]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=Collins |first1=Simon |title=Children's Minister steps into Oranga Tamariki baby uplift case |url=https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=12240726 |accessdate=19 August 2019 |newspaper=[[New Zealand Herald]] |date=15 June 2019}}</ref> She also announced that the Government would be conducting a review into the Hawkes Bay attempted uplifting incident.<ref>{{cite news |title=Inquiry announced into handling of attempted uplift of a baby in Hawke's Bay last month by Oranga Tamariki |url=https://www.tvnz.co.nz/one-news/new-zealand/inquiry-announced-into-handling-attempted-uplift-baby-in-hawkes-bay-last-month-oranga-tamariki |accessdate=19 August 2019 |publisher=[[1 News]] |date=16 June 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=Martin |first1=Tracey |title=Oranga Tamariki Review |url=http://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA1906/S00149/oranga-tamariki-review.htm |accessdate=19 August 2019 |agency=[[Scoop (website)|Scoop]] |publisher=[[New Zealand Government]] |date=18 June 2019}}</ref> In early August 2019, Martin announced that the Government would be scrapping its Children's Teams task forces in response to the uplifting controversy but rejected comparisons with the Australian "[[Stolen Generations]]".<ref>{{cite news |last1=Vance |first1=Andrea |title=Tracey Martin on uplift controversy: Oranga Tamariki 'believed the child was in danger' |url=https://www.stuff.co.nz/national/politics/114724381/tracey-martin-on-uplift-controversy-oranga-tamariki-believed-the-child-was-in-danger |accessdate=19 August 2019 |work=[[Stuff (website)|Stuff]] |date=4 August 2019}}</ref>

During the [[2020 New Zealand general election]] held on 17 October, Martin contested [[Ōhāriu (New Zealand electorate)|Ōhāriu]], coming fifth place.<ref>{{cite web |title=Ōhāriu – Official Result |url=https://electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2020/electorate-details-33.html |publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]] |accessdate=6 November 2020 |url-status=live}}</ref> She and her fellow NZ First MPs lost their seats after the party's vote dropped to 2.6%, below the five percent threshold needed to enter Parliament.<ref>{{cite web |title=2020 General Election and Referendums – Official Result |url=https://electionresults.govt.nz/electionresults_2020/ |publisher=[[Electoral Commission (New Zealand)|Electoral Commission]] |accessdate=6 November 2020 |url-status=live}}</ref><ref name="The Spinoff Where to now">{{cite news |last1=Van Veen |first1=Josh |title=Where to now for Winston Peters and New Zealand First? |url=https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/19-10-2020/where-to-now-for-winston-peters-and-new-zealand-first/ |accessdate=18 October 2020 |work=[[The Spinoff]] |date=18 October 2020 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20201022044636/https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/19-10-2020/where-to-now-for-winston-peters-and-new-zealand-first/ |archivedate=22 October 2020|url-status=live}}</ref>

On 9 November 2020, Martin was granted retention of the title "[[The Honourable]]" for life, in recognition of her term as a member of the [[Executive Council of New Zealand|Executive Council]].<ref>{{cite news |url=https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2020-vr5185 |title=Retention of the title "The Honourable" |date=10 November 2020 |work=New Zealand Gazette |accessdate=10 November 2020}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Martin and her husband have three children.<ref name="Martin Mahurangi College">{{cite web|last=Mahurangi College|title=Tracey Martin|url=http://www.mahurangi.school.nz/mahu2/themewide?num=656&area=news|work=Mahurangi College News and Events|accessdate=3 December 2011|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20150125192818/http://www.mahurangi.school.nz/mahu2/themewide?num=656&area=news|archivedate=25 January 2015}}</ref>

Martin has described her mother as the New Zealander that she most admires as a brave woman who has not been afraid to stand up for her belief and opinions.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.stuff.co.nz/auckland/local-news/rodney-times/95981766/rodney-candidates-unplugged-tracey-martin-of-new-zealand-first|title=Rodney candidates unplugged: Tracey Martin of New Zealand First|work=[[Stuff.co.nz]]|accessdate=30 January 2019}}</ref>

Martin's grandfather was a guard at the [[Featherston prisoner of war camp]] during the Featherston Incident in 1943.<ref name="times-age.co.nz">{{cite news|url=https://times-age.co.nz/in-grandads-honour/|title=In grandad's honour|date=26 February 2018|work=Wairarapa Times Age|accessdate=30 January 2019}}</ref> His gun was taken by another member of staff who shot an interpreter at the camp by the name of Adachi. This incident started a riot in which 48 Japanese prisoners of war and one New Zealand guard died.<ref name="times-age.co.nz"/>

Martin has taken a [[pro-choice]] stance on [[abortion]], supporting efforts to remove it from the [[Crimes Act 1961]]. Martin's views on abortion was affected by the death of her grandmother Beverley Williams during a backstreet abortion.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Jancic |first1=Boris |title=NZ First MP Tracey Martin tells of backstreet abortion that killed her grandmother |url=https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=12257322 |accessdate=19 August 2019 |newspaper=[[New Zealand Herald]] |date=9 August 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=Bracewell-Worrall |first1=Anna |title=NZ First MP Tracey Martin shares tragic personal connection to backstreet abortion |url=https://www.newshub.co.nz/home/politics/2019/08/nz-first-mp-tracey-martin-shares-tragic-personal-connection-to-backstreet-abortion.html |accessdate=19 August 2019 |publisher=[[Newshub]] |date=9 August 2019}}</ref> In October 2020, ''[[The Spinoff]]'' online magazine described her as a liberal [[feminism|feminist]] and potential successor to Winston Peters who could broaden the party's appeal to women.<ref name="The Spinoff Where to now" />
