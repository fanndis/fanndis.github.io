{{main article|2013 French Open}}

[[Serena Williams]] defeated defending champion [[Maria Sharapova]], 6–4, 6–4, to win the Women's Singles tennis title at the [[2013 French Open]]. Williams' victory in the final earned her a [[Grand Slam (tennis)#Multiple Career Grand Slams|multiple slam set]] in Women's Singles, her 16th Grand Slam singles title, her second French Open title since her triumph in [[2002 French Open – Women's Singles|2002]].<ref name=Telegraph>{{cite news|url= https://www.telegraph.co.uk/sport/tennis/frenchopen/10107875/French-Open-2013-Serena-Williams-claims-title-with-straight-sets-win-over-Maria-Sharapova.html|title=Serena Williams claims title with straight-sets win over Maria Sharapova|date=9 June 2013|work=Daily Telegraph|accessdate=10 June 2013 }}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url= https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2013/jun/08/serena-williams-maria-sharapova-live|title=Serena Williams beats Maria Sharapova: French Open final – as it happened|date=9 June 2013|work=Guardian UK|accessdate=10 June 2013 }}</ref> She also became the oldest woman to win the French Open (a record she surpassed herself in 2015) and set a record for the longest gap between French Open titles.<ref>{{cite news|url= http://www.cbc.ca/sports/tennis/serena-williams-wins-french-open-1.1325580|title=Serena Williams wins French Open|publisher=CBC Sports|accessdate=8 June 2013 }}</ref><ref name="serena" /> With her victory, Williams expanded her season winning streak to 31 matches dating back to the [[2013 Sony Open Tennis – Women's Singles|Miami Open]].<ref name="serena">{{cite news|url= http://www.tennis.com/pro-game/2013/06/serena-williams-wins-roland-garros/47851/|title=Serena Williams wins Roland Garros|date=8 June 2013|work=Tennis Magazine|accessdate=10 June 2013 }}</ref> Williams only dropped one set the entire tournament, to [[Svetlana Kuznetsova]] in the quarterfinals.

The 2013 French Open final was the first time at any Grand Slam event since the [[2004 Australian Open – Women's Singles|2004 Australian Open]] that the top two seeded players had played each other in the final. It was the first French Open since [[1995 French Open – Women's Singles|1995]] that the top two seeded players played each other in the final.

==Road to the final==
Since winning the French Open in 2002, [[Serena Williams]] had not advanced to the final of the event, last semifinal was in 2003 (lost to [[Justine Henin|Henin]]). In 2008, she was the only former champion left in the draw, she lost to [[Katarina Srebotnik]] in the third round. In 2012, she lost in the first round to [[Virginie Razzano]], a player ranked in the 100s. In 2013, however, she made quick work of her opponents advancing to the finals with an average match length of less than one hour and fifteen minutes.<ref name=LATimes /> She had problems in just one match, in quarterfinals against [[Svetlana Kuznetsova]], when she was down 2–0 and break points in third set. However, she won six of last seven games to close out the match.

After reaching quarterfinals without dropping set, Sharapova needed two three-setters to reach final. First, she met 18th seed [[Jelena Jankovic]] and dropped first set 6–0. In that set Sharapova committed 20 errors to just two from Jankovic. However, Jankovic lost opening service game in second set, which looked to be key for Sharapova as she could cool down after horrible first set. In semifinal she faced third seed [[Victoria Azarenka]]. Sharapova won first set convincingly, but lost second in the same fashion. But she eased to 5–2 lead in final set and finished match two games later to reach second French Open final.

==Final==
First seeded Williams faced off against second seeded [[Maria Sharapova]] in the women's singles final.  Williams came out aggressive, charging the net on the first point.  Her aggression set up a triple break-point, but Sharapova fought back and held serve.  She then broke Williams for a 2–0 lead.  Williams broke back in the next game, then broke Sharapova twice more for a 6–4 first set.  In the second set, Sharapova fought off five break points to hold in her opening serve.<ref name=LATimes />  After Williams held serve, Sharapova dropped a break with two consecutive errors in the fourth game.<ref name=Telegraph />  From there, Williams went on to take a 5–4 lead with her serve coming up.  She finished off the match with her tenth ace of the day.  "She's always had a big serve," said Sharapova. "I think her second serve is better than it was in the past."<ref name=LATimes>{{cite news|title=Serena Williams wins French Open, her 16th Grand Slam event title|author=Henry Chu|date=June 9, 2013|url=http://www.latimes.com/sports/tennis/la-sp-french-open-women-20130609,0,2168506.story|accessdate=June 11, 2013|work=Los Angeles Times}}</ref>

For Williams, it was her 31st consecutive victory, a career best.  She has beaten Sharapova 13 times in a row.  When asked by a reporter if  she still wanted to retire at her peak, Williams replied "That's my goal, but have I peaked yet?"<ref name=LATimes />  Commentary by Simon Briggs published by ''[[The Daily Telegraph]]'' described Williams' performance as "almost flawless".<ref name=Telegraph />  "Sharapova actually played a fine match," said Briggs. "But it is so difficult to attack an in-form Williams because she can shut you down with the quality of her serve."<ref name=Telegraph />

By winning the 1 hour 46 minute match, Williams completed her second career [[Grand Slam (Tennis)|Grand Slam]].<ref name=LATimes />  It was her 16th major title overall, moving her within six of [[Steffi Graf]]'s modern era record of 22 such wins, and within eight of [[Margaret Court]]'s all-time record.<ref name=Telegraph />  She also became the oldest women ever to win the French Open.<ref name=Telegraph />
