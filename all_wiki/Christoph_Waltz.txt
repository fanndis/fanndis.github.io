'''Christoph Waltz''' ({{IPA-de|ˈkrɪstɔf ˈvalts|lang}}; born 4 October 1956) is a German-Austrian actor and director. Since 2009 he has mainly been active in the United States.<ref name="autogenerated1">{{cite web|url=https://www.welt.de/kultur/article9173105/Christoph-Waltz-wird-im-Eilverfahren-zum-Oesterreicher.html|title=Pass-Hickhack: Christoph Waltz wird im Eilverfahren zum Österreicher – Nachrichten Kultur|language=de|publisher=Welt.de|date=24 August 2010|access-date=26 January 2011}}</ref><ref name=dra>{{cite web |work=[[Der Standard]] |url=http://derstandard.at/1280984224170/Oesterreichische-Staatsbuergerschaft-fuer-Christoph-Waltz |title=Österreichische Staatsbürgerschaft für Christoph Waltz' |date=8 August 2010}}</ref><ref name="focus1">{{cite web |url=http://www.focus.de/panorama/boulevard/film-waltz-fuehlt-sich-definitiv-als-oesterreicher_aid_543712.html |title=Waltz fühlt sich definitiv als Österreicher – Boulevard|publisher=Focus.de|date=21 January 2011|access-date=26 January 2011}}</ref>

His breakout American role came in [[Quentin Tarantino]]'s 2009 film  ''[[Inglourious Basterds]]'', where he played [[Schutzstaffel|SS]] [[Standartenführer|officer]] [[Hans Landa]]. He would later collaborate with Tarantino once again in 2012, where he played [[bounty hunter]] Dr. King Schultz in ''[[Django Unchained]]''. For each performance, he earned an [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor|Academy Award]], [[BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role|BAFTA Award]], and [[Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actor – Motion Picture|Golden Globe Award]] for Best Supporting Actor. Waltz also received the [[Prix d'interprétation masculine|Best Actor Award]] at the [[Cannes Film Festival]] and a [[Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by a Male Actor in a Supporting Role|Screen Actors Guild Award]] for his portrayal of Landa.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://stories99.com/2013/02/25/christoph-waltz-wins-the-academy-award-for-best-actor-in-a-supporting-role/ |title=Christoph Waltz Wins The Academy Award For Best Actor In A Supporting Role |publisher=stories99.com |access-date=28 January 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130510204123/http://stories99.com/2013/02/25/christoph-waltz-wins-the-academy-award-for-best-actor-in-a-supporting-role/ |archive-date=10 May 2013 |url-status=dead }}</ref>

Waltz has also starred in [[Roman Polanski]]'s dark comedy ''[[Carnage (2011 film)|Carnage]]'' (2011), [[Terry Gilliam]]'s science fiction film ''[[The Zero Theorem]]'' (2013), [[Tim Burton]]'s biographical film ''[[Big Eyes]]'' (2014), [[Alexander Payne]]'s satire ''[[Downsizing (film)|Downsizing]]'' (2017), and [[Woody Allen]]'s comedy ''[[Rifkin's Festival]]'' (2020). Waltz also gained acclaim for his performance as [[James Bond (literary character)|James Bond]]'s nemesis [[Ernst Stavro Blofeld]] in [[Sam Mendes]]' ''[[Spectre (2015 film)|Spectre]]'' (2015) and [[Cary Joji Fukunaga]]'s  ''[[No Time to Die]]'' (2021).<ref>{{cite web|last1=Miller|first1=Ross|title=The next James Bond film is called Spectre: new car, poster, and full cast confirmed|url=https://www.theverge.com/2014/12/4/7327013/james-bond-24-full-cast-title-car-release-date|website=[[The Verge]]|access-date=4 December 2014|date=4 December 2014}}</ref> For his role as Walter Keane in ''Big Eyes'' he was nominated for a Golden Globe [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actor – Motion Picture Musical or Comedy|for Best Actor]]. 

In 2020, he starred in the web series ''[[Most Dangerous Game]]'' and garnered his first [[Primetime Emmy]] nomination, for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Actor in a Short Form Comedy or Drama Series|Outstanding Actor in a Short Form Comedy or Drama Series]]. He also provided the voice of Mandrake in ''[[Epic (2013 film)|Epic]]'' (2013) and is set to voice [[the Fox and the Cat]] in [[Guillermo Del Toro]]'s [[Pinocchio (2021 film)|''Pinocchio'' adaptation]] (2021). Waltz is also set to appear in the upcoming [[Wes Anderson]] film ''[[The French Dispatch]]''. 

==Early life==
Waltz was born in [[Vienna]],<ref name=LATimesGettell>{{cite web |last=Gettell |first=Oliver |date=2 December 2014 |title=Christoph Waltz |url=http://projects.latimes.com/hollywood/star-walk/christoph-waltz/ |newspaper=[[Los Angeles Times]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> the son of Johannes Waltz, a German set designer, and Elisabeth Urbancic, an Austrian costume designer.<ref name=WWD>{{cite web |last1=Badia |first1=Alex |last2=Windolf |first2=Jim |date=December 9, 2014 |title=M: Good Day, Christoph Waltz |url=https://wwd.com/eye/people/m-christoph-waltz-has-a-present-for-you-8057511/ |newspaper=[[Women's Wear Daily]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref><ref name=GQUK>{{cite web |last=Chalmers |first=Robert |date=15 May 2015 |title=We've been expecting you, Mr Waltz |url=https://www.gq-magazine.co.uk/article/christoph-waltz-spectre-quentin-tarantino-gq-interview |newspaper=[[GQ]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref>
 
Waltz comes from a family of theatrical heritage: his maternal grandmother was [[Burgtheater]] and silent film actress Maria Mayen, and his step-grandfather, Emmerich Reimers, and his great-grandfather, Georg Reimers, were both stage actors who also appeared in silent films.<ref name=WWD/><ref name="ref1">{{cite news|last=Lim|first=Dennis|title='Inglourious' Actor Tastes the Glory|work=New York Times|date=12 August 2009|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/08/16/movies/16lim.html?pagewanted=all|access-date=21 August 2009}}</ref> Waltz's maternal grandfather, [[Rudolf von Urban]], was a psychiatrist of [[Slovenes|Slovene]] descent{{efn|Rudolf's father was [[Viktor Urbantschitsch]], son of Alois Urbantschitsch (''Alojz Urbančič''), who was born in [[Preddvor]], today Slovenia, then part of the [[Kingdom of Illyria]], [[Austrian Empire|Austria]].<ref>{{cite web |last=K. Arnegger |first=H. Bergmann |date=7 June 2016 |title= Urbantschitsch, Viktor von (1847–1921), Otologe |url=https://www.biographien.ac.at/oebl/oebl_U/Urbantschitsch_Viktor_1847_1921.xml |website=[[Österreichisches Biographisches Lexikon 1815–1950|Austrian Biographical Lexicon]] |access-date=15 August 2020}}</ref> Through Alojz, Waltz is related to [[Josipina Turnograjska|Josipina Urbančič]], Alojz's first cousin, and one of the first Slovene female poets and composers.<ref>{{cite journal |last=Kurillo |first=Jurij |year=2020 |title=Preddvorski graščaki Urbančiči |url=https://www.zdravniskazbornica.si/docs/default-source/isis/2020/isis-08-09-20.pdf?sfvrsn=dd393736_2 |journal=Isis |issue=8–9 |pages=69–71}}</ref>}} and a student of [[Sigmund Freud]].<ref name=MrPorter>{{cite web |last=Rafanelli |first=Stephanie |date=21 December 2017 |title=A Merry Dance With Mr Christoph Waltz |url=https://www.mrporter.com/en-us/journal/the-look/a-merry-dance-with-mr-christoph-waltz/2709?setupsession=false |newspaper=[[YOOX Net-a-Porter Group|MR PORTER]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref>

Waltz's father died when he was seven years old,<ref name=WWD/> and his mother later married composer and conductor Alexander Steinbrecher.<ref name=ZeitLemkeMatwey>{{cite web |last=Lemke-Matwey |first=Christine |date=12 December 2013 |title=Christoph Waltz |url=https://www.zeit.de/2013/51/interview-christoph-waltz-oper-antwerpen-rosenkavalier |newspaper=[[Die Zeit]] |language=de |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref><ref name=NYTFonsecaWollheim>{{cite web |last=da Fonseca-Wollheim |first=Corinna |date=December 11, 2017 |title=Christoph Waltz, Directing Opera, Moves From Tarantino to Verdi |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/12/11/arts/music/christoph-waltz-verdi-falstaff.html |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> Steinbrecher was previously married to the mother of director [[Michael Haneke]]; as a result, Waltz and Haneke shared the same stepfather.<ref name=GuardianRose>{{cite web |last=Rose |first=Steve |date=13 March 2014 |title=Zero Theorem: the world according to Christoph Waltz |url=https://www.theguardian.com/film/2014/mar/13/zero-theorem-world-according-to-christoph-waltz |newspaper=[[The Guardian]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref>

Waltz had a passion for opera as a youth, having seen his first opera (''[[Turandot]]'' with [[Birgit Nilsson]] in the title role) at around the age of ten. As a teenager, Waltz would visit the opera twice a week.<ref name=NYTFonsecaWollheim/> He was uninterested in theatre<ref name=WWD/> and wished to become an opera singer.<ref name=MrPorter/>

After graduating from Vienna's [[Theresianum]],<ref name=WWD/> Waltz went to study acting at the renowned [[Max Reinhardt Seminar]].<ref name=DWHitz>{{cite web |last=Hitz |first=Julia |date=4 October 2016 |title=Hollywood's favorite bad guy Christoph Waltz turns 60 |url=https://www.dw.com/en/hollywoods-favorite-bad-guy-christoph-waltz-turns-60/a-35952043 |newspaper=[[Deutsche Welle]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> At the same time, he also studied singing and opera at the [[University of Music and Performing Arts Vienna]], but eventually decided that his voice was not good enough for an opera career.<ref name="ref1"/><ref name=NPRGross2012>{{cite web |last=Gross |first=Terry |date=December 18, 2012 |title='Unchained' Admiration Between Actor And Director |url=https://www.npr.org/transcripts/167062665 |newspaper=[[NPR]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> In the late 1970s, Waltz spent some time in New York City where he trained with [[Lee Strasberg]] and [[Stella Adler]]. He studied script interpretation under Adler, and credits his analytical approach to her teaching.<ref name="ref1"/>

==Career==
[[File:Christoph Waltz.jpg|thumb|left|upright=0.9|Waltz in 2012]]
On his return to Europe, Waltz found work as a stage actor, making his debut at the [[Schauspielhaus Zürich|Schauspielhaus]] in [[Zurich]].<ref name=NZZ>{{cite web |date=27 September 2015 |title=Christoph Waltz und der Sprayer von Zürich |url=https://www.nzz.ch/feuilleton/zurich-film-festival/christoph-waltz-und-der-sprayer-von-zuerich-ld.2201 |newspaper=[[Neue Zürcher Zeitung]] |language=de |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> He also performed in Vienna, Salzburg, Cologne and Hamburg.<ref name="ref1"/><ref name=MrPorter/> He became a prolific television actor in the years 1980 to 2000. In 2000, he made his directorial debut, with the German television production ''Wenn man sich traut''.<ref>{{cite web|title=IMDB|url=https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0910607/#Director|access-date=17 December 2011}}</ref> Before coming to the attention of a larger audience in Tarantino's ''Inglourious Basterds'', he had played Dr. Hans-Joachim Dorfmann in the British TV series ''The Gravy Train'' in 1990. The show is a story of intrigue and misdeeds set in the offices of the [[European Union]] in [[Brussels]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/4oDDrama|title=4oD Drama|access-date=27 January 2013}}</ref>

In [[Quentin Tarantino]]'s 2009 film ''[[Inglourious Basterds]]'', Waltz portrayed [[Schutzstaffel|SS]]-[[Standartenführer]] [[Hans Landa]], also known as "The Jew Hunter". Clever, courteous, multilingual—but also self-serving, cunning, implacable and murderous—the character of Landa was such that Tarantino feared he "might have written a part that was un-playable".<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.variety.com/article/VR1118003822?refCatId=3628|title=Tarantino Reflects On 'Basterds'|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|publisher=Reed Business Information|date=17 May 2009|access-date=18 January 2010|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120303182806/http://www.variety.com/article/VR1118003822?refCatId=3628|archive-date=3 March 2012|first=Michael|last=Fleming|url-status=dead}}</ref> Waltz received the [[Best Actor Award (Cannes Film Festival)|Best Actor Award]] for the performance at the [[2009 Cannes Film Festival]] and received acclaim from critics and the public. In 2009, he began sweeping critics' awards circuits, receiving awards for Best Supporting Actor from the New York Film Critics Circle, the Boston Society of Film Critics,<ref name=autogenerated2>{{cite web|url=http://www.thebsfc.org/CurrWin.html|title=BSFC Award Winners – Recent|publisher=Thebsfc.org|access-date=26 January 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090216214508/http://thebsfc.org/CurrWin.html|archive-date=16 February 2009|url-status=dead}}</ref> Los Angeles Film Critics Association,<ref name=autogenerated2/> and for Best Supporting Actor at the [[67th Golden Globe Awards]] and the [[16th Screen Actors Guild Awards]] in January 2010.

The following month, he won the [[British Academy Film Awards|BAFTA]] for Best Supporting Actor,<ref>[http://www.bafta.org/awards/film/film-awards-nominations,949,BA.html#jump11 Film Awards Winners in 2010 - Film Awards - Film - The BAFTA site] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110427041841/http://www.bafta.org/awards/film/film-awards-nominations%2C949%2CBA.html |date=27 April 2011 }}</ref> and won the [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://oscar.go.com/nominations/nominees|title=2015 Oscars: Nominees - 87th Academy Awards Nominations|work=Oscar.com}}</ref>  Tarantino acknowledged the importance of Waltz to his film by stating: "I think that Landa is one of the best characters I've ever written and ever will write, and Christoph played it to a tee. It's true that if I couldn't have found someone as good as Christoph I might not have made ''Inglourious Basterds''".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://network.nationalpost.com/np/blogs/theampersand/archive/2009/08/27/christoph-waltz-thankful-for-quot-gift-quot-role-of-hans-landa-in-inglorious-basterds.aspx|title=''Inglorious Basterds feature|website=Network.nationalpost.com|publisher=The National Post|date=27 August 2009|access-date=26 January 2011}}{{Dead link|date=July 2018 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref>

Waltz played gangster Benjamin Chudnofsky in ''[[The Green Hornet (2011 film)|The Green Hornet]]'' (2011); that same year, he starred in ''[[Water for Elephants (film)|Water for Elephants]]'' and [[Roman Polanski]]'s ''[[Carnage (2011 film)|Carnage]]''. He played German bounty hunter Dr. King Schultz in Quentin Tarantino's ''[[Django Unchained]]'' (2012), a role Tarantino wrote specifically for Waltz.<ref>{{Citation |last=Ordoña |first=Michael |date=27 December 2012 |title=Christoph Waltz admires Tarantino's to-the-heart style |work=[[Los Angeles Times]] |url=https://articles.latimes.com/2012/dec/27/news/la-en-christoph-waltz-django-unchained-20121227 |access-date=12 January 2016 }}</ref> During a training accident prior to filming, Waltz injured his [[pelvis]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/christoph-waltz-pelvic-django-unchained-242745|title=Christoph Waltz Dislocates Pelvic Bone During 'Django Unchained' Training|author=Borys Kit|work=[[The Hollywood Reporter]]|date=30 September 2011|access-date=14 January 2013}}</ref> His role garnered him acclaim once again, with Waltz winning the Golden Globe, the BAFTA, and ultimately the Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor.

Waltz has been cast as the former [[Soviet]] leader [[Mikhail Gorbachev]] in the film ''Reykjavik'', based on the [[Reykjavík Summit|1986 peace talks]] between the United States and USSR.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/christoph-waltz-signs-star-opposite-378904|title=Christoph Waltz Signs to Star Opposite Michael Douglas in Reykjavik|work=The Hollywood Reporter|date=15 October 2012|access-date=24 February 2013}}</ref> In April 2013, he was selected as a member of the main competition jury at the [[2013 Cannes Film Festival]].<ref>{{cite web | url=https://variety.com/2013/film/news/nicole-kidman-christopher-waltz-ang-lee-among-cannes-jury-members-1200410650/ | title=Nicole Kidman, Christoph Waltz, Ang Lee Among Cannes Jury Members | work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]] | publisher=[[Reed Business Information]] | date=23 April 2013 | access-date=23 April 2013 | last=Saperstein | first=Pat}}</ref> He directed a production of the opera [[Der Rosenkavalier]] at the [[Vlaamse Opera]], in [[Antwerp]] in late 2013, and in [[Ghent]] early 2014.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.vlaamseopera.be/nl/#!/producties/der-rosenkavalier-1 |title=Vlaamse Opera &#124; Vlaamse Opera |publisher=Vlaamseopera.be |date=15 January 2015 |access-date=28 January 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140803182245/https://vlaamseopera.be/nl/#!/producties/der-rosenkavalier-1 |archive-date=3 August 2014 |url-status=dead  }}</ref> In 2014, he was selected as a member of the jury for the [[64th Berlin International Film Festival]].<ref name="Berlin">{{cite web|url=http://www.berlinale.de/en/presse/pressemitteilungen/alle/Alle-Detail_21332.html |title=Berlinale 2014: International Jury |access-date=31 December 2013 |work=Berlinale}}</ref> He starred as [[Walter Keane]] in [[Tim Burton]]'s ''[[Big Eyes]]'', which opened on 25 December 2014,<ref>{{cite web|last=Sepinwall |first=Alan |url=http://www.hitfix.com/in-contention/weinstein-sets-awards-season-dates-for-big-eyes-imitation-game-and-eleanor-rigby |title=Weinstein sets awards season dates for Big Eyes, Imitation Game and Eleanor Rigby |publisher=Hitfix.com |date=2 May 2014 |access-date=28 January 2015}}</ref> and appeared as [[Ernst Stavro Blofeld]] in ''[[Spectre (2015 film)|Spectre]]'', the 24th film in the ''[[James Bond]]'' franchise.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.yahoo.com/entertainment/so-who-does-christoph-waltz-play-in-spectre-020540818.html|title=So Who Does Christoph Waltz Play in 'SPECTRE'? (Spoilers!)|last=Polowy|first=Kevin|date=7 November 2015|work=Yahoo!|access-date=1 June 2018|language=en-US}}</ref> In July 2019, it was reported that Waltz would reprise the role in ''[[No Time to Die]]'' (2020).<ref name=return>{{cite web|first=Justin|last=Kroll|url=https://variety.com/2019/film/news/christoph-waltz-bond-25-blofeld-1203265974/|title=Christoph Waltz to Return as Blofeld in 'Bond 25'|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|date=12 July 2019}}</ref>

In 2015, it was announced that Waltz would direct and star in the film ''[[Georgetown (film)|Georgetown]]'' (formerly titled ''The Worst Marriage in Georgetown''), which is based on the true crime story of the murder of [[Viola Drath]].<ref name="Actor Christoph Waltz will direct and star in ‘The Worst Marriage in Georgetown’">{{cite web | url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/reliable-source/wp/2015/05/06/actor-christoph-waltz-will-direct-and-star-in-the-worst-marriage-in-georgetown/ | title=Actor Christoph Waltz will direct and star in 'The Worst Marriage in Georgetown' | work=The Washington Post | date=6 May 2015 | access-date=7 May 2015 | last=Andrews | first=Helena}}</ref> In July 2016, he portrayed lead villain Captain Leon Rom, a corrupt Belgian captain, in the reboot ''[[The Legend of Tarzan (film)|The Legend of Tarzan]]''.

In 2017, Waltz appeared in the films ''[[Tulip Fever]]'' and ''[[Downsizing (film)|Downsizing]]''. In 2019, Waltz appeared in the action fantasy ''[[Alita: Battle Angel]]''. He directed a production of the opera ''[[Falstaff (opera)|Falstaff]]'', again at the [[Vlaamse Opera]], in [[Antwerp]] in late 2017, and in [[Ghent]] in early 2018.

In 2018, it became public that Christoph Waltz had agreed to play the leading role in a film adaptation of the novel ''[[The Nazi and the Barber|The Nazi and The Barber]]'', and had described the main role, the role of the mass murderer Max Schulz, as "juicy rôle".<ref name="Hilsenrath">{{cite web|url= https://translate.google.com/translate?hl=en&sl=de&tl=en&u=http://hilsenrath.de/nachruf/ |title=On the life and work of Edgar Hilsenrath. Obituary on the occasion of his death on December 30, 2018 |access-date= 16 September 2020}}</ref>

==Personal life==
[[File:Christoph Waltz and Judith Holste @ 2010 Academy Awards (cropped).jpg|thumb|Waltz and wife Judith Holste at the [[82nd Academy Awards]], March 2010]]
Waltz has three children with his former wife, Jacqueline (née Rauch), a dance therapist originally from New York.<ref name="ref1"/><ref name=TelegraphHusband>{{cite web |last=Husband |first=Stuart |date=28 October 2015 |title=Christoph Waltz: 'Facebook is a step toward fascism' |url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/film/james-bond-spectre/christoph-waltz-interview/ |newspaper=[[The Daily Telegraph]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> The two lived in London and their marriage lasted 17 years.<ref name="ref1"/><ref name=GQUK/> Waltz married his second wife, German costume designer Judith Holste, with whom he has a daughter. They divide their time between [[Berlin]], [[Vienna]] and [[Los Angeles]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.usatoday.com/life/movies/movieawards/2010-01-26-waltzinside26_ST_N.htm|work=[[USA Today]]|first=Donna|last=Freydkin|title=At long last, movie stardom shines on Christoph Waltz|date=26 January 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |date=8 March 2013 |title=Waltz unchained for Jerusalem wedding |url=https://www.timesofisrael.com/waltz-unchained-for-jerusalem-wedding/ |newspaper=[[The Times of Israel]] |access-date=2 February 2020}}</ref> 

Waltz's native language is German, and he also speaks both English and French fluently.<ref name="languages">{{cite news|last=Billington|first=Alex|title=Interview: Col. Hans 'The Jew Hunter' Landa – Christoph Waltz|publisher=First Showing|date=20 August 2009|url=http://www.firstshowing.net/2009/08/20/interview-hans-the-jew-hunter-landa-christoph-waltz/|access-date=28 August 2009}}</ref> He speaks all three of these in ''Inglourious Basterds'' and ''Django Unchained'', and although his character in ''Inglourious Basterds'' also spoke Italian, Waltz said on the ''[[Adam Carolla Podcast]]'' that he is not fluent in Italian.{{Citation needed|date=July 2016}}

Waltz was born in Vienna to a German father who applied for him to become a citizen of Germany after his birth.<ref name="wienerzeitung.at">{{cite web|url=http://www.wienerzeitung.at/nachrichten/english_news/38618_Waltz-to-become-Austrian-citizen.html|title=Waltz to become Austrian citizen|work=[[Wiener Zeitung]] Online|date=26 August 2010|access-date=24 February 2013}}</ref> He received Austrian citizenship in 2010, thus holding citizenships of both Austria and Germany, but considers his German passport a "legal, citizenship law banality"<ref name="focus1"/> despite the fact that he had not previously been able to vote in Austria's national elections. Asked whether he felt Viennese, he responded: "I was born in Vienna, grew up in Vienna, went to school in Vienna, graduated in Vienna, studied in Vienna, started acting in Vienna – and there would be a few further Viennese links. How much more Austrian do you want it?"<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.wienerzeitung.at/DesktopDefault.aspx?TabID=4082&Alias=wzo&cob=514287|title=Waltz to become an Austrian citizen|date=26 August 2010}}{{Dead link|date=September 2018 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref>
