The '''Public Facilities Privacy & Security Act''', commonly known as '''House Bill 2''' or '''HB2''', is a [[North Carolina]] [[statute]] passed in March 2016 and signed into law by [[Governor of North Carolina|Governor]] [[Pat McCrory]]. The bill amended state law to preempt any anti-discrimination ordinances passed by local communities and, controversially, compelled schools and public facilities containing single-gender washrooms [[Bathroom bill|to only allow people of the corresponding sex as listed on their birth certificate]] to use them; it also gave the state exclusive rights to determine the [[minimum wage]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/03/24/us/north-carolina-to-limit-bathroom-use-by-birth-gender.html|title=North Carolina Bans Local Anti-Discrimination Policies|last=Philipps|first=Dave|date=2016-03-23|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-02-05|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

The bathroom portion of the bill generated immense criticism for preventing transgender people who did not or could not alter their birth certificates from using the restroom consistent with their [[gender identity]]<ref name="talkingpointsmemo.com">{{cite web|url=http://talkingpointsmemo.com/edblog/north-carolina-anti-lgbt-bill|title=What Just Happened In North Carolina?|work=TPM|accessdate=March 27, 2016}}</ref> (in North Carolina, only people who undergo [[sex reassignment surgery]] can change the sex on their birth certificates, and outside jurisdictions have different rules, some more restrictive),<ref>{{cite news |last=Shoichet |first=Catherine E. |url= http://www.cnn.com/2016/04/03/us/north-carolina-gender-bathrooms-law-opposing-views/|title=North Carolina transgender law: Is it discriminatory? |website=[[CNN]] |date=April 5, 2016 |accessdate=April 25, 2016}}</ref> and for changing the definition of [[sex]] in the state's anti-discrimination law to "the physical condition of being male or female, which is stated on a person's birth certificate."<ref name="hb2" /><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.gp.org/north_carolina_green_party_condemns_lawmakers_on_hb2|title=North Carolina Green Party Condemns Lawmakers on HB2|work=www.gp.org|accessdate=May 10, web|url=http://www.hrc.org/blog/five-things-to-know-about-north-carolinas-newest-radical-anti-lgbt-law|title=5 Things to Know About North Carolina's Radical Anti-LGBT Law|publisher=Human Rights Campaign|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref> The removal of municipal anti-discrimination protections was also criticized, as state-level protections do not explicitly cover discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity.<ref name="North Carolina Passes Law Blocking Measures To Protect LGBT People">{{cite HB2 was met with widespread protests: state, county and city governments across the United States [[Public Facilities Privacy & Security Act#Tourism and hospitality|forbade their employees from non-essential travel to North Carolina]]; numerous corporations and firms [[Public Facilities Privacy & Security Act#Economic impact|curbed plans to hold events and create jobs]] in the state, and many performers [[Public Facilities Privacy & Security Act#Economic impact#Music and performances|canceled performances in North Carolina]] to [[boycott]] the state; North Carolina's economy lost over $400 million in investments and jobs. The bill was also criticized by several religious organizations, and [[President of the United States|President]] [[Barack Obama]] denounced it and called for its repeal. McCrory ultimately lost his bid for [[North Carolina gubernatorial election, 2016|re-election]] in 2016 to Democratic [[North Carolina Attorney General|Attorney General]] [[Roy Cooper]], a vocal critic of the law, and the portion of the law regarding bathroom use was repealed on March 30, 2017.<ref name="nytimes-30mar2017">{{cite news|last1=Fausset|first1=Richard|title=Bathroom Law Repeal Leaves Few Pleased in North Carolina|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/03/30/us/north-carolina-senate-acts-to-repeal-restrictive-bathroom-law.html|accessdate=March 30, 2017|newspaper=The New York Times|date=March 30, 2017}}</ref>

==Background and passage==
===Charlotte Ordinance 7056===
On February 22, 2016, the [[Charlotte, North Carolina|Charlotte]] [[City Council of Charlotte, North Carolina|City Council]] passed by a 7-4 vote the '''Ordinance&nbsp;7056''', a non-discrimination ordinance prohibiting discrimination on the basis of [[sexual orientation]] or [[gender identity]] in public accommodations or by passenger [[vehicles for hire]] or city contractors.<ref name="CMO2016">{{cite web|url=http://charmeck.org/city/charlotte/CityClerk/Ordinances/February%2022,%202016.pdf |title=City Clerk > City Clerk |publisher=Charmeck.org |accessdate=2017-03-31}}</ref><ref name="Council approves changes to Non-Discrimination Ordinance">{{cite web|url=http://charmeck.org/city/charlotte/Newsroom/Pages/Council-approves-changes-to-non-discrimination-ordinance.aspx|title=Council approves changes to non-discrimination ordinance|publisher=charmeck.org|accessdate=March 27, 2016}}</ref> The Council had debated a similar ordinance in 2015, which failed by a 6-5 vote because it did not include full protection of transgender people; the council first considered extending non-discrimination protection to LGBT people in 1992.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/local/article11908907.html |title=Charlotte LGBT ordinance fails 6-5 in contentious meeting|newspaper=Charlotte Observer |accessdate=May 30, 2016}}</ref> The ordinance was to take effect on April 1, 2016.<ref name="CMO2016"/><ref name="Council approves changes to Non-Discrimination Ordinance"/>

===North Carolina House Bill 2===
On March 22, 2016, upon request from three-fifths of all the members of the [[North Carolina Senate]] and the [[North Carolina House of Representatives]], its President and Speaker, respectively, called the General Assembly into [[special session]] for the following day.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ncleg.net/sessions/2015e2/2016SecondExtraSessionProclamation.pdf|title=Joint proclamation to convene the General Assembly of North Carolina in extra session|date=March 22, 2016|publisher=North Carolina General Assembly}}</ref> That day, March 23, the [[North Carolina House of Representatives]] passed House Bill 2, with 82 in favor and 26 against and 11 excused absences.<ref name="KopanScott">Tal Kopan and Eugene Scott, ''[http://www.cnn.com/2016/03/23/politics/north-carolina-gender-bathrooms-bill/ North Carolina governor signs controversial transgender bill]'', March 24, 2016, CNN</ref> About three hours later, the [[North Carolina Senate]] also passed the bill, with 32 in favor, 6 excused absences, and all 11 Democrats walking out in protest and not voting.<ref name="KopanScott"/> That evening, it was signed by North Carolina Governor Pat McCrory,<ref name="hb2">{{cite web|url=http://www.ncleg.net/gascripts/BillLookUp/BillLookUp.pl?Session=2015E2&BillID=HB2|title=North Carolina General Assembly - House Bill 2 Information/History (2016 Second Extra Session)|publisher=ncleg.net|accessdate=March 27, 2016}}</ref> taking a total of 11 hours and 10 minutes to become a law.<ref name= indy>{{cite web| url = http://www.indyweek.com/indyweek/eleven-dems-voted-for-house-bill-2-we-called-to-ask-why/Content?oid=5012684| title = Eleven Dems Voted for House Bill 2. We Called to Ask Why.| date = March 30, 2016| website = indyweek.com| publisher = Indy Week| access-date = May 10, 2016}}</ref> All Senate and House Republicans voted in favor of the bill, with the exceptions of [[Charles Jeter]], [[Chuck McGrady]], [[Gary Pendleton]], [[Bob Rucho]], and [[Dan Soucek]], who were excused absent. Eleven House Democrats voted for the bill; [[Larry M. Bell]], [[William Brisson|William D. Brisson]], [[Elmer Floyd]], [[Ken Goodman (politician)|Ken Goodman]], [[Charles Graham (American politician)|Charles Graham]], George Graham, Howard Hunter III, William O. Richardson, [[Garland Pierce]], Brad Salmon, and [[Michael H. Wray]].<ref name= indy/> Senate and House Democrats Gale Adcock, [[Becky Carney]], [[Beverly M. Earle]], [[Susan C. Fisher]], [[Paul Luebke]], [[Joe Sam Queen]], Evelyn Terry, [[Ken Waddell]], Joyce Waddell, [[Angela Bryant]], Joel D. M. Ford, and Jane W. Smith were excused absent. The independent representative [[Paul Tine]], a member of the Republican caucus, voted against the bill.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://keepmyncsafe.com/roll-call-hb2/|title=HB2 RollCall NC Special Session on Bathrooms - KeepNCSafe}}</ref><ref>[http://www.ncleg.net/Legislation/voteHistory/voteHistory.html North Carolina General Assembly - Vote History]. ncleg.net. Retrieved May 14, 2016.</ref>

Supporters of House Bill 2 said the Charlotte ordinance was sloppily written and overreaching, and that in their view its wording essentially did away with single-sex bathrooms.<ref name="Confusion Around North Carolina's Newest Law">{{cite web|url=http://wfae.org/post/confusion-around-north-carolinas-newest-law-goes-beyond-bathrooms|title=Confusion Around North Carolina's Newest Law Goes Beyond Bathrooms|publisher=wfae.org|accessdate=April 13, 2016}}</ref> Representative [[Dan Bishop]], the bill's sponsor, cited this as grounds for the state to override local ordinances.<ref name="Confusion Around North Carolina's Newest Law"/>

==Enforcement==
House Bill 2 does not contain any guidance on how it is supposed to be enforced, and does not name any specific crimes or penalties.<ref name= ACLU/><ref name=motherjones>Samantha Michaels, [https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2016/04/north-carolina-lgbt-bathrooms-hb2-enforcement We Asked Cops How They Plan to Enforce North Carolina's Bathroom Law], Mother Jones, April 7, 2016</ref> Police departments in [[Raleigh, North Carolina|Raleigh]], [[Greensboro, North Carolina|Greensboro]], [[Wilmington, North Carolina|Wilmington]], and [[Asheville, North Carolina|Asheville]] have expressed a lack of clarity on how the law should be enforced and an unwillingness to devote police resources to monitor bathrooms.<ref name=motherjones/> A number of departments indicated a willingness to respond to complaints of violations, but said none had been received.<ref>NPR All Things Considered, [https://www.npr.org/2016/05/10/477529266/north-carolina-police-struggle-to-enforce-transgender-bathroom-law North Carolina Police Say They Can't Enforce Transgender Bathroom Law], May 10, 2016</ref>

Republican State Representative [[Dan Bishop]], a co-sponsor of the law, acknowledged that "there are no enforcement provisions or penalties in HB2."<ref>Diedrick Russell, ''[http://www.wbtv.com/story/31615248/are-there-any-teeth-to-house-bill-2 Are there any teeth to House Bill 2?]'', WBTV, April 30, 2016</ref> Democratic State Representative [[Rodney W. Moore|Rodney Moore]] was more emphatic, saying: "There is absolutely no way to enforce this law, as it relates to the enforcement of the bathroom provisions. It is an utterly ridiculous law."<ref name=motherjones/>

==Economic impact==
Although the full economic impact of House Bill 2 on North Carolina's economy is largely unrealized and difficult to fully quantify, some early economic consequences have been noted.<ref>Lin, Tom C. W., [https://ssrn.com/abstract=3294317 Incorporating Social Activism] (December 1, 2018). 98 Boston University Law Review 1535 (2018)</ref> As of September 2016, rough estimates put North Carolina's full economic loss due to the law at around 0.1% of the state's [[gross domestic product]].<ref>{{cite web| url = http://www.wral.com/cancellations-over-hb2-make-headlines-but-barely-dent-nc-economy/16035660/| title = Cancellations over HB2 make headlines but barely dent NC economy| last = Leslie| first = Laura| date = 21 September 2016| website = WRAL News| publisher = Capitol Broadcasting Company, Inc.| access-date = 22 September 2016}}</ref> The [[Associated Press]] estimated that House Bill 2 would cost the state US$3.76&nbsp;billion over twelve years.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://bigstory.ap.org/article/fa4528580f3e4a01bb68bcb272f1f0f8/ap-exclusive-bathroom-bill-cost-north-carolina-376b |title=AP Exclusive: Price tag of North Carolina's LGBT law: $3.76B |publisher=Bigstory.ap.org |date=2016-05-12 |accessdate=2017-03-31}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://apnews.com/ec6e9845827f47e89f40f33bb7024f61|title=How AP tallied the cost of North Carolina's "bathroom bill"|first=The Associated|last=Press|date=March 27, 2017|website=AP NEWS}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last=Berman |first=Mark |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-nation/wp/2017/03/27/north-carolinas-bathroom-bill-cost-the-state-at-least-3-7-billion-new-analysis-finds/ |title=North Carolina's bathroom bill cost the state at least $3.7 billion, new analysis finds |newspaper=The Washington Post |accessdate=2017-03-31}}</ref>

Some companies have halted or are reconsidering their plans to expand to North Carolina as a result of the passage of the law:
* [[PayPal]] announced they would no longer move forward with their expansion into Charlotte, which would have created over 400 jobs with a US$20 million annual payroll impact.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.paypal.com/stories/us/paypal-withdraws-plan-for-charlotte-expansion|title=PayPal Withdraws Plan for Charlotte Expansion|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/business/article70001502.html|title=PayPal withdraws plans for Charlotte expansion over HB2|work=charlotteobserver|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref>
* [[Red Ventures]] is re-evaluating plans to expand into North Carolina and bring 500 people in 2016 and thousands after that.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dukechronicle.com/article/2016/04/n-c-house-bill-2-prompts-backlash-in-durham-nationally|title=N.C. House Bill 2 prompts backlash in Durham, nationally|work=The Chronicle|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref><ref>Ely Portillo, ''[http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/business/article70026277.html Red Ventures expansion at risk, CEO tells McCrory]'', April 5, 2016, ''The Charlotte Observer''</ref>
* German global banking and financial services company [[Deutsche Bank]] announced plans to halt a planned expansion of their Cary, NC offices which would have employed 250 people.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/04/13/business/dealbook/deutsche-bank-freezes-north-carolina-expansion-in-protest-of-bias-law.html|title=Deutsche Bank Freezes North Carolina Expansion, in Protest of Bias Law|last=Eavis|first=Peter|date=April 12, 2016|newspaper=The New York Times|issn=0362-4331|access-date=April 12, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/deutsche-bank-freezes-north-carolina-expansion-citing-transgender-law-1460469042|title=Deutsche Bank Freezes North Carolina Expansion, Citing Transgender Law|last1=Kamp|first1=Jon|last2=Bauerlein|first2=Valerie|newspaper=Wall Street Journal|issn=0099-9660|access-date=April 12, 2016}}</ref>
* Real estate research company [[CoStar Group]] decided against a 730-job expansion into Charlotte, with House Bill 2 playing a deciding role.<ref>{{cite news|last=Rothackerrrothacker |first=Rick |url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/business/article110349597.html |title=Charlotte loses 730-job operations center over House Bill 2 |newspaper=The Charlotte Observer |date=2016-10-25 |accessdate=2017-03-31}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=Charlotte loses out on 730-job headquarters because of HB2|url=https://www.bizjournals.com/charlotte/news/2016/10/25/charlotte-loses-out-on-730-job-headquarters.html|access-date=2020-06-26|website=www.bizjournals.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |author=Joe Bruno |url=http://www.wsoctv.com/news/local/charlotte-loses-out-on-250-million-due-to-house-bill-2/460635324 |title=Charlotte loses out on $250 million due to House Bill 2 |publisher=Wsoc-Tv |accessdate=2017-03-31 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161108134728/http://www.wsoctv.com/news/local/charlotte-loses-out-on-250-million-due-to-house-bill-2/460635324 |archive-date=November 8, 2016 |url-status=dead }}</ref>

During an appearance by McCrory on [[Meet the Press]] on April 17, 2016, host [[Chuck Todd]] said that, by his conservative estimate, North Carolina had lost at least US$39.7 million in revenue as a result of House Bill 2.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nbcnews.com/meet-the-press/video/full-interview-nc-gov-pat-mccrory-discusses-his-state-s-bathroom-law-667905091935|title=Full Interview: NC Gov. On April 22, 2016, [[Time Warner Cable News North Carolina]] estimated that House Bill 2 had to date cost North Carolina more than 1750 jobs and more than $77 million of investments and visitor spending, including:<ref name="TWCN"/>

* $46.2 million and 500 jobs in Charlotte
* $5 million in Greensboro
* $3.2 million in the Triangle area of Raleigh, Durham, and Chapel Hill

On May 24, 2016, Charlotte Chamber of Commerce estimated that Mecklenburg County had lost $285 million and 1,300 jobs from the loss of PayPal and the estimated 908 spinoff jobs they estimated that it would have produced.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/politics-government/article79925922.html |title=Did losing PayPal over HB2 really cost Charlotte $285 million?|newspaper=Charlotte Observer |date=May 25, 2016}}</ref>

McCrory and North Carolina lawmakers said the Department of Justice's intervention was orchestrated by the [[Executive Office of the President|Obama Administration]], but [[White House]] [[White House Press Secretary|Press Secretary]] [[Josh Earnest]] said, "These kinds of enforcement actions are made independent of any sort of political interference or direction from the White House. Those are decisions that are made entirely by attorneys at the Department of Justice."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/politics-government/article75797387.html|title=NC leaders divided on whether they'll meet Obama administration's deadline on House Bill 2|work=charlotteobserver|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref>

"[https://medium.com/@PatMcCroryNC/affirming-and-improving-north-carolina-s-commitment-to-privacy-and-equality-d674f94bf815#.sossbswx8 Affirming and Improving North Carolina's Commitment to Privacy and Equality]". ''[[Medium (website)|Medium]]''. ====Presidential candidates====
Some [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] 2016 [[United States presidential election|U.S. presidential]] candidates have publicly stated they would not have signed House Bill 2, including [[Ohio Governor]] [[John Kasich]] and businessman [[Donald Trump]].

Kasich defended his position against the law by saying, "Why do we have to write a law every time we turn around in this country? Can't we figure out just how to get along a little bit better and respect one another?"<ref>{{cite web|title=John Kasich: I wouldn't sign North Carolina's House Bill 2|url=http://myfox8.com/2016/04/10/john-kasich-i-wouldnt-sign-north-carolinas-house-bill-2/}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://talkingpointsmemo.com/livewire/kasich-not-sign-anti-lgbt-bill|title=John Kasich: I Wouldn't Have Signed The Anti-LGBT Bill That Passed In N Carolina|work=TPM|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref>

Speaking about the economic impact that the law has had on North Carolina, Trump said, "North Carolina did something that was very strong and they're paying a big price and there's a lot of problems"; and, when asked which restroom a transgender person should use in one of his [[List of things named after Donald Trump|buildings]], he said that they should use whichever one they're most comfortable using.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://abc11.com/politics/donald-trump-comes-out-against-north-carolinas-hb2/1302289/|title=Donald Trump comes out against North Carolina's HB2|work=ABC11 Raleigh-Durham|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref> After he became the presumptive nominee of the Republican Party, Trump altered his stance and supported the law on July 6 in [[Raleigh, North Carolina|Raleigh]].<ref name= trumphb2>{{cite news|last = Campbell|first = Colin|title = LGBT groups blast Trump for voicing support for HB2 in Raleigh|newspaper = [[The Charlotte Observer]]|date = 6 July 2016|url = http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/politics-government/article88026562.html|access-date = 12 July 2016}}</ref> Later on, he emphasized that the states should be allowed to decide on the matter, despite his personal stance.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.newsobserver.com/news/politics-government/election/article87877837.html|title=Backstage with Donald Trump before his Raleigh speech|newspaper=The News & Observer|accessdate=July 7, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url = https://www.cbsnews.com/news/donald-trump-north-carolina-transgender-bathroom-law-election-2016/ |title = Donald Trump amends stance on North Carolina transgender bathroom law |website = CBS News |date = 22 April 2016 |access-date = 17 December 2016}}</ref>

The [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] 2016 presidential candidates, former [[United States Secretary of State|Secretary of State]] [[Hillary Clinton]] and [[Vermont]] [[United States Senate|Senator]] [[Bernie Sanders]], both condemned the law.<ref>Michael Walsh, ''[https://www.yahoo.com/news/transgender-rights-where-2016-candidates-stand-1415405411418166.html Transgender rights: Where 2016 candidates stand on bathroom bills]'', April 25, 2016, Yahoo! News</ref>

====Appointment of Chris Sgro====
Approximately two weeks after the passage of House Bill 2, [[Chris Sgro]], the leader of [[Equality NC]], the largest LGBT advocacy organization in North Carolina, was selected to fill a vacancy in the [[North Carolina House of Representatives]], making him the only openly LGBT member of the [[North Carolina General Assembly]] at the time. Pursuant to state law, the local Guilford County Democratic Party Executive Committee had the responsibility for filling the vacancy.<ref>{{cite web|title=Leader of LGBT rights organization named to state Legislature|url=http://myfox8.com/2016/04/09/leader-of-lgbt-rights-organization-named-to-state-legislature/}}</ref> [[Chris Sgro]] quickly became the most vocal opponent of House Bill 2 in the General Assembly, often publicly clashing with the governor.<ref>{{cite web|title=Pat McCrory Has Lost It|url=http://www.indyweek.com/news/archives/2016/07/22/pat-mccrory-has-lost-it}}</ref>

LGBT travellers may be affected by legislation passed recently in the states of North Carolina and Mississippi."<ref name="gov.uk">{{cite web|url=https://www.gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/usa/local-laws-and-customs|title=USA|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/worldviews/wp/2016/04/20/britain-issues-warning-for-lgbt-travelers-visiting-north-carolina-and-mississippi/|title=Britain issues warning for LGBT travelers visiting North Carolina and Mississippi|author=Peter Holley|date=April 20, 2016|work=Washington Post|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-us-canada-36104879|title=UK warns gay travellers about US anti-LGBT laws|work=BBC News|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref> The [[Human Rights Campaign]] called it "both frightening and embarrassing that one of our nation's staunchest allies has warned its citizens of the risks" of travel.

In a [[Question time|parliamentary answer]] to [[Labour Party (UK)|Labour]] [[Member of Parliament|MP]] [[Cat Smith]], Foreign Office Minister [[Hugo Swire]] said the British government had expressed concerns to the authorities in North Carolina; stating, "Our Consul General in [[Atlanta]] raised our concerns with the [[North Carolina Cabinet|North Carolina Commerce Secretary]] on 19 April ... This Government is opposed to all forms of discrimination. We are committed to ensuring that all LGBT people are free to live their lives in a safe and just environment."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.pinknews.co.uk/2016/05/03/uk-government-has-raised-concerns-with-north-carolina-over-anti-lgbt-law/|title=UK government has 'raised concerns' with North Carolina over anti-LGBT law|work=PinkNews|accessdate=May 10, 2016}}</ref>

====European Union====
On May 12, 2016, the [[European Union]] criticized North Carolina for House Bill 2, as well as Mississippi for the [[Religious Liberty Accommodations Act]] and Tennessee for SB 1556.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.hrc.org/blog/european-union-criticizes-anti-lgbt-laws-in-north-carolina-tennessee-and-mi|title=European Union Criticizes Anti-LGBT Laws - Human Rights Campaign|first=Human Rights|last=Campaign}}</ref> The official statement read:

{{quote|The recently adopted laws including in the states of Mississippi, North Carolina and Tennessee, which discriminate against lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender and intersex persons in the United States contravene the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, to which the US is a State party, and which states that the law shall prohibit any discrimination and guarantee to all persons equal and effective protection.

As a consequence, cultural, traditional or religious values cannot be invoked to justify any form of discrimination, including discrimination against LGBTI persons. These laws should be reconsidered as soon as possible.

The European Union reaffirms its commitment to the equality and dignity of all human beings irrespective of their sexual orientation and gender identity. We will continue to work to end all forms of discrimination and to counter attempts to embed or enhance discrimination wherever it occurs around the world.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://eeas.europa.eu/statements-eeas/2016/160512_02_en.htm|title=European Union - EEAS (European External Action Service) - Statement by the Spokesperson on LGBTI rights in the United States}}</ref>}}

===Advocacy groups===
On April 21, the National Task Force to End Sexual and Domestic Violence Against Women, a coalition of over two hundred national, state, and local organizations against [[sexual assault]] and [[domestic violence]] against women, issued a statement opposing anti-transgender initiatives, writing that "discriminating against transgender people does nothing to decrease the risk of sexual assault" and noting that of the 18 states and more than 200 municipalities with anti-discrimination laws "protecting transgender people's access to facilities consistent with the gender they live every day," none "have seen a rise in sexual violence or other public safety issues due to nondiscrimination laws."<ref>[http://endsexualviolence.org/files/NTFNationalConsensusStmtTransAccessWithSignatories.pdf National Consensus Statement of Anti-Sexual Assault and Domestic Violence Organizations in Support of Full and Equal Access for the Transgender Community] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170202001115/http://endsexualviolence.org/files/NTFNationalConsensusStmtTransAccessWithSignatories.pdf# |date=February 2, 2017 }}, National Task Force to End Sexual and Domestic Violence Against Women (April 21, 2016).</ref><ref>Stevie Borrello, [https://abcnews.go.com/US/sexual-assault-domestic-violence-organizations-debunk-bathroom-predator/story?id=38604019 Sexual Assault and Domestic Violence Organizations Debunk 'Bathroom Predator Myth'], ABC News (April 22, 2016).</ref>

===Religious organizations===
On March 28, 2016, two religious organizations, the NC Values Coalition and the Keep NC Safe Coalition, released a list of forty-one businesses which the organizations said had signed a letter of support for House Bill 2. The organizations said their list included over 300 businesses but could only publicly name forty-one of them because others were afraid of retaliation.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Jarvis|first1=Craig|title=Major companies oppose LGBT law; supporters line up smaller firms|url=http://www.newsobserver.com/news/politics-government/politics-columns-blogs/under-the-dome/article68814277.html}}</ref> The only national company listed, [[Hanesbrands]], was subsequently removed after it was learned a single employee had listed the company without permission. At least one other company has been removed from the public list for similar reasons.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Hyland|first1=Michael|title=Businesses on both sides of House Bill 2 weigh in on issue|url=http://wncn.com/2016/03/29/businesses-on-both-sides-of-house-bill-2-weigh-in-on-issue/|accessdate=March 29, 2016}}</ref>

===Public demonstrations===
On April 25, 2016, hundreds of supporters gathered at the Halifax Mall in downtown Raleigh. Speakers focused on the section of the law that requires people to use bathrooms in public facilities that correspond to the sex on their birth certificate and quoted biblical scripture in defense of the law. The crowd was encouraged to visit legislators' offices in show of support and to boycott [[Target Corporation|Target]], which had recently announced that employees and customers could use the restroom and fitting room that correspond to their gender identity.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Bonner|first1=Lynn|title=Hundreds rally near NC Legislative Building to support HB2|url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/news/politics-government/article73842452.html|newspaper=The Charlotte Observer}}</ref> The organizations held a series of prayer vigils in Greensboro, Raleigh, Charlotte, Havelock, Greenville, New Bern, Fayetteville, Marshville and Jacksonville to show public support for HB2.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Alvarez |first1=Angelica |title=Prayer Vigil Shows Support for Governor, House Bill 2 |url=http://abc11.com/politics/prayer-vigil-shows-support-for-governor-house-bill-2/1271401/|publisher=WTVD-TV}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Funkr |first1=Tim |title=Supporters of HB2 gather for prayer vigil in Charlotte |url=http://www.charlotteobserver.com/living/religion/article70621332.html |newspaper=Charlotte Observer}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=Lawrence |first1=Beth |title=House Bill 2 supporters hold prayer vigil for McCrory|url=http://www.wcti12.com/news/house-bill-2-supporters-hold-prayer-vigil-for-mccrory/38795086 |publisher=WCTI-TV}}</ref>
