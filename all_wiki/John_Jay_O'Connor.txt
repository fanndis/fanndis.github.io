'''John Jay O'Connor III''' (January 10, 1930 &ndash; November 11, 2009) was an American [[lawyer]] and the husband of [[United States Supreme Court]] Associate Justice [[Sandra Day O'Connor]], the first woman to serve on the court.<ref name=nyt>{{cite news |first=Dennis |last=Hevesi |title=John J. O’Connor III, Husband of Former Justice, Is Dead at 79 |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/11/12/us/12oconnor.html?hp |work=[[New York Times]]|publisher= |date=2009-11-11 |accessdate=2009-12-07}}</ref> O'Connor, a prominent lawyer in [[Arizona]], suffered from [[Alzheimer's disease]] during his later life.  His illness played a significant role in Sandra Day O'Connor's decision in 2005 to retire from the Supreme Court.<ref name=nyt/>

==Life and career==
O'Connor was born on January 10, 1930, in [[San Francisco, California]].<ref name=nyt/> His parents were John Jay O'Connor II and Sally Flynn O'Connor.<ref name=nyt/> He was of [[Irish Catholic]] descent and attended Saint Ignatius High School in San Francisco.  He obtained his [[bachelor's degree]] from [[Stanford University]] in 1951, and later received a [[law degree]], also from Stanford, in 1953.<ref name=nyt/>

O'Connor met his future wife, [[Sandra Day]] of [[Arizona]], while both were law review editors and students at Stanford Law School.<ref name=nyt/> The couple married in 1952.<ref name=nyt/>

O'Connor served within the U.S. Army Judge Advocate General's Corp following his graduation from law school. He was stationed in [[Frankfurt am Main|Frankfurt]], [[West Germany]], from 1954 until 1956. Mrs O'Connor was employed in the [[Quartermaster Corps]] as a [[civilian]] lawyer.<ref name=nyt/>

O'Connor returned to the United States and moved to [[Phoenix, Arizona]].  There he joined the [[law firm]] of Fennemore, Craig, von Ammon, McClennen & Udall.<ref name=nyt/> Following Sandra Day O'Connor's appointment to the United States Supreme Court by President [[Ronald Reagan]], O'Connor moved with his family to [[Washington, D.C.]]  He continued to practice law with two firms, [[Miller & Chevalier]] and [[Bryan Cave]], while living in Washington.<ref name=nyt/>

O'Connor was diagnosed with Alzheimer's nearly twenty years before his death.<ref name=nyt/> His deteriorating health played a significant role in Sandra Day O'Connor's decision to retire from the Supreme Court in 2005.<ref name=nyt/>

O'Connor died of Alzheimer's disease on November 11, 2009, in [[Phoenix, Arizona]], at the age of 79.<ref name=nyt/>
