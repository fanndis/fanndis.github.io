'''Elvira Sakhipzadovna Nabiullina''' ({{lang-ru|Эльвира Сахипзадовна Набиуллина}}; {{lang-tt|Эльвира Сәхипзәде кызы Нәбиуллина}}; {{lang-ba|Эльвира Сәхипзада ҡыҙы Нәбиуллина}}; born 29 October 1963) is a Russian economist and head of the [[Central Bank of Russia]]. She was Russian President [[Vladimir Putin]]'s economic adviser between May 2012 to June 2013<ref>[http://en.rian.ru/russia/20120523/173627458.html  ''New Kremlin Aides Allotted Responsibilities''], [[RIA Novosti]], 23 May 2012. Retrieved: 27 June 2012.</ref> after serving as [[Ministry of Economic Development (Russia)|minister of economic development and trade]] from September 2007 to May 2012.<ref name=Bloom/><ref name=interfax>{{cite news|title=Andrei Belousov appointed Economic Development Minister |url=http://www.interfax.com/newsinf.asp?id=333482 |accessdate=28 March 2013 |work=Interfax |date=21 May 2012 |location=Moscow |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140219033024/http://www.interfax.com/newsinf.asp?id=333482 |archivedate=19 February 2014 |df=dmy }}</ref> As of 2019, she is listed as the 53rd most powerful woman in the world by ''Forbes''.<ref name=Forbes14>{{cite web|title=The World's 100 Most Powerful Women|url=https://www.forbes.com/power-women/list/2/#tab:overall|website=Forbes|publisher=Forbes|accessdate=31 March 2020}}</ref>

==Early life and education==
Nabiullina was born in [[Ufa]], [[Bashkortostan]], on 29 October 1963<ref>{{cite web|title=Elvira Nabiullina|url=http://www.ece.ust.hk/ece/fck_upload/file/SPIEF%202010%20Speakers.pdf|work=ECE|accessdate=25 August 2013|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150923232947/http://www.ece.ust.hk/ece/fck_upload/file/SPIEF%202010%20Speakers.pdf|archive-date=23 September 2015|url-status=dead}}</ref> in a [[Volga Tatars|Tatar]] family.<ref>https://www.kommersant.ru/doc/1330151</ref><ref>https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2017-02-14/putin-s-central-banker-purges-100-banks-a-year-in-epic-crackdown</ref> Her father Sakhipzada Saitzadayevich was a driver, while her mother Zuleikha Khamatnurovna was a manager at a factory. Elvira graduated from school No. 31 of Ufa as an excellent pupil. She graduated from the [[MSU Faculty of Economics|Moscow State University]] in 1986.<ref name=RIAN>{{cite news|language=Russian| url=http://www.rian.ru/spravka/20070924/80699235.html|title=Elvira Nabiullina. Biography|newspaper= [[RIA Novosti]]}}</ref><ref>[http://russiaprofile.org/bg_people/resources_whoiswho_alphabet_n_nabiullina.html  ''BackGround People: Nabiullina, Elvira Sakhipzadovna'', Russia Profile 28 July 2008] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120627012837/http://russiaprofile.org/bg_people/resources_whoiswho_alphabet_n_nabiullina.html |date=27 June 2012 }}. Retrieved: 27 June 2012.</ref> In subsequent years, she was also selected for the 2007 [[Yale]] World Fellows Program.<ref>{{cite web|title=Yale University President Levin Announces Selection of 2007 Yale World Fellows|url=http://news.yale.edu/2007/05/03/yale-university-president-levin-announces-selection-2007-yale-world-fellows|website=Yale News|accessdate=24 March 2017}}</ref>

==Career==
Between 1991 and 1994 Nabiullina worked at the USSR Science and Industry Union and its successor, the [[Russian Union of Industrialists and Entrepreneurs]].<ref name=RIAN/> In 1994 she moved to the Ministry for Economic Development and Trade, where she rose to the level of deputy minister by 1997; she left the ministry in 1998.<ref name=RIAN/> She spent the next two years with [[Sberbank]] as its chief executive and with former Economic Development and Trade Minister [[German Gref]]'s non-governmental [[think tank]], the Center for Strategic Development, before returning to the Ministry for Economic Development and Trade as first deputy in 2000.<ref name=RIAN/> Between 2003 and her September 2007 appointment as minister, she chaired the Center for Strategic Development as well as an advisory committee preparing for Russia's 2006 presidency of the [[G8]] group of nations.

Russian President Putin appointed Nabiullina to the post of minister of economic development and trade on 24 September 2007, replacing Gref.<ref name=Bloom>{{cite news|url=https://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=20601087&sid=aRvXcj3Zx2Ks&refer=home|title=Putin Replaces Russian Economy Minister Gref With Nabiullina|author=Levitov, Maria|work=Bloomberg|date=September 2007}}</ref><ref name=RIAN/> She found working with then-deputy premier and finance minister [[Alexei Kudrin]] "difficult but always interesting"<ref>[http://themoscownews.com/politics/20110930/189081106.html ''Policy behind Kudrin ouster''] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111017135055/http://themoscownews.com/politics/20110930/189081106.html |date=17 October 2011 }}. Anna Arutunyan, ''Moscow News'', 30 September 2011 Retrieved 27 June 2012.</ref> and remained in that position until 21 May 2012.<ref name=interfax/> In 2012 she was one of six senior government figures to accompany [[Vladimir Putin|Putin]] back to the Kremlin administration after Putin was elected president of Russia for a third term.

In 2013 Nabiullina was appointed head of the [[Central Bank of Russia]], becoming the second woman after Tatiana Paramonova to hold that position, and thus the first Russian woman in the [[G8]].<ref name=MT>{{cite news|title=Russian Central Banker Nabiullina Among Forbes' Most Powerful Women|url=http://www.themoscowtimes.com/business/article/russian-central-banker-nabiullina-among-forbes-most-powerful-women/501147.html|accessdate=27 August 2014|work=MoscowTimes.com|date=29 May 2014}}</ref> In May 2014 she was named one of the world's most powerful women by [[Forbes]], which noted that she "has been given the difficult task of managing the ruble exchange rate during Ukraine's political [[Crimean crisis|crisis]] and facilitating growth for an economy trying to avoid a recession".<ref name=MT/> In an effort to stop the ruble's slide, the [[Central Bank of Russia]], under her leadership, hiked interest rates, free-floated the exchange rate, and kept a cap on inflation,<ref name=PKirby>{{cite news|last1=Kirby|first1=Paul|title=Russia economy: What is the risk of meltdown?|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-30492505|accessdate=17 December 2014|work=BBC News|date=16 December 2014}}</ref> thus stabilizing the financial system and boosting foreign-investor confidence.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.euromoney.com/GatewayAd.aspx?Redirect=http%3a%2f%2fwww.euromoney.com%2fArticle%2f3488368%2fCentral-bank-governor-of-the-year-2015-Nabiullina-displays-crisis-fighting-skills.html|title=Welcome to Euromoney|publisher=euromoney.com}}</ref> [[Euromoney]] magazine named her their 2015 Central Bank Governor of the Year.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.euromoney.com/GatewayAd.aspx?Redirect=http%3a%2f%2fwww.euromoney.com%2fArticle%2f3488964%2fNabiullina-named-Euromoney-Central-Bank-Governor-of-the.html|title=Welcome to Euromoney|publisher=euromoney.com}}</ref>

In 2017 British magazine [[The Banker]] chose Nabiullina as "Central Banker of the Year, Europe".<ref>{{cite news |title=Central Banker of the Year 2017 |url=http://www.thebanker.com/Awards/Central-Bank-Governor-of-the-Year/Central-Banker-of-the-Year-2017 |newspaper=TheBanker |location=UK |date=3 January 2017 |accessdate=16 January 2017 }}</ref>
