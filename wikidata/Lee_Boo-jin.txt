'''Lee Boo-jin''' (born 1969/1970) is a South Korean billionaire businesswoman, and the president and chief executive of [[Hotel Shilla]], one of Seoul's leading hotels and conference centres.

==Early life==
She is the daughter of [[Lee Kun-hee]], the late billionaire chairman of [[Samsung Group]].<ref name="forbes.com"/> She earned a bachelor's degree from [[Yonsei University]], major in children's studies.<ref name="forbes.com"/> 

==Career==
Lee began her career at Samsung Foundation in 1995. 

Lee joined Hotel Shilla 2001 and became CEO of Hotel Shilla in 2010.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.businesspost.co.kr/BP?command=article_view&num=111993|title=[Who Is ?] 이부진 호텔신라 대표이사 사장|last=이지혜|date=2019-01-28|website=[Who Is ?] 이부진 호텔신라 대표이사 사장|access-date=2020-03-01}}</ref>

As of September 2020, her net worth was estimated at US$1.6&nbsp;billion.<ref name="forbes.com" />

In November 2017, she was assessed by ''[[Forbes]]'' as being the 93rd most powerful woman in the world.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.forbes.com/power-women/list/2/#tab:overall|title=World's Most Powerful Women|author=|date=|website=forbes.com|accessdate=2 November 2017}}</ref>

==Personal life==
She lives in [[Seoul]], South Korea.<ref name="forbes.com"/>

In 1999, she married Im Woo-jae, known in the South Korean press as "Mr. Cinderella" because of his humble background, as an "employee of a security service affiliate of Samsung Group".<ref name="reuters.com">{{cite web|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-samsung-heiress-divorce/samsung-heiress-ordered-to-pay-7-6-mln-in-divorce-ruling-idUSKBN1A50QT|title=Samsung heiress ordered to pay $7.6 mln in divorce ruling|author=|date=20 July 2017|publisher=|via=Reuters|accessdate=3 November 2017}}</ref> They separated in 2012, and have one son together.<ref name="forbes.com"/>

In a court ruling in July 2017, Lee was ordered to pay her ex-husband Im Woo-jae $7.6&nbsp;million, as part of their divorce settlement, with Lee receiving sole custody of their son.<ref name="reuters.com"/> Im is counter-suing, seeking $1.1&nbsp;billion as a division of assets.<ref name="reuters.com"/>
