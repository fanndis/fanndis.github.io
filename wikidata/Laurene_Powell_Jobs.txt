==Early life and career==
Powell Jobs grew up in [[West Milford, New Jersey]].<ref name=NYT17>{{cite news|title=Steve Jobs's Widow Steps Onto Philanthropic Stage|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/05/17/business/steve-jobss-widow-sets-philanthropy-goals.html?_r=0&pagewanted=all|accessdate=May 18, 2013|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|date=May 17, 2013|author=Peter Lattman|author2=Claire Cain Miller}}</ref> She earned a [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] in political science from the [[University of Pennsylvania School of Arts and Sciences]] and a [[Bachelor of Science|B.S.]] degree in economics from the [[Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania]] in 1985.<ref name="Emerson Collective"/><ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.alumni.upenn.edu/groups/tcpw/membership.html |title= Trustees' Council of Penn Women |publisher= University of Pennsylvania |quote= Laurene Powell Jobs, CW'85}}</ref><ref name="Forbes">{{cite magazine|url=https://www.forbes.com/profile/laurene-powell-jobs/|title=Laurene Powell Jobs|magazine=Forbes|accessdate=September 17, 2013}}</ref> She received her [[Master of Business Administration|M.B.A.]] degree from the [[Stanford Graduate School of Business]] in 1991.<ref name="Emerson Collective"/><ref name="Forbes"/><ref name=autogenerated1>{{cite web | url= http://www.whitehouse.gov/the-press-office/2010/12/14/president-obama-announces-members-white-house-council-community-solution |title= President Obama Announces Members of the White House Council for Community Solutions |work= Press Release |publisher= The White House |date= December 14, 2010}}</ref>

In October 1989, Steve Jobs gave a "View from the Top" lecture at Stanford Business School. Laurene Powell was a new MBA student and sneaked to the front of the lecture and started up a conversation with Jobs, who was seated next to her. They ended up having dinner together that night.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Love|first=Dylan|title=Steve Jobs Skipped A Business Meeting To Take His Wife On Their First Date|url=https://www.businessinsider.com/steve-jobs-wife-2011-10|access-date=2020-06-30|website=Business Insider}}</ref> A year and a half later, on March 18, 1991, they married in a ceremony at the [[Ahwahnee Hotel]] in [[Yosemite National Park]].<ref name="Apple Confidential">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=mXnw5tM8QRwC&pg=PA81|title=Apple Confidential 2.0: The Definitive History of the World's Most Colorful|author=Owen W. Linzmayer|year=2004|isbn=9781593270100|accessdate=September 17, 2013}}</ref> Presiding over the wedding was [[Kōbun Chino Otogawa]], a Zen Buddhist monk.<ref name="Apple Confidential"/><ref name="CNN Money">{{cite web|first=Peter|last=Elkind|url=https://money.cnn.com/2008/03/02/news/companies/elkind_jobs.fortune/index2.htm|title=America's Most Admired Companies: Steve Jobs (pg 2)|work=[[CNNMoney]]|date=March 5, 2008|accessdate=September 17, 2013|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5ndYsXQmU?url=https://money.cnn.com/2008/03/02/news/companies/elkind_jobs.fortune/index2.htm|archivedate=February 18, 2010|df=mdy-all}}</ref> Their son, Reed, was born in September 1991, followed by daughters Erin in 1995 and Eve in 1998. Laurene is also the stepmother of [[Lisa Brennan-Jobs]], Steve's daughter from a previous relationship, who was born in 1978.

Powell Jobs co-founded Terravera, a [[natural food]]s company that sold to retailers throughout [[Northern California]].<ref name="Emerson Collective"/><ref name="Parsa"/> She also served on the [[board of directors]] of Achieva, which created online tools to help students study  more effectively at standardized testing.<ref name="Parsa"/> Before business school, Powell Jobs worked for Merrill Lynch Asset Management and spent three years at [[Goldman Sachs]] as a fixed-income trading strategist.<ref name="Emerson Collective"/><ref name="Parsa"/>

On October 3, 2017, reports indicated Powell Jobs had purchased a stake in the [[Monumental Sports & Entertainment|ownership group Monumental Sports & Entertainment]] that includes the NBA's [[Washington Wizards]], NHL's [[Washington Capitals]], and [[Capital One Arena]]. Her approximately twenty percent stake makes her the second largest shareholder behind chairman [[Ted Leonsis]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://bleacherreport.com/articles/2736639-steve-jobs-widow-laurene-reportedly-purchased-20-stake-in-wizards-capitals|title=Steve Jobs' Widow, Laurene, Reportedly Purchased 20% Stake in Wizards, Capitals|last=Zucker|first=Joseph|work=Bleacher Report|access-date=2017-10-03|language=en-US}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/capitalbusiness/laurene-powell-jobs-is-buying-a-big-stake-in-wizards-capitals-sports-empire/2017/10/02/065d37ca-a39a-11e7-8cfe-d5b912fabc99_story.html|title=Laurene Powell Jobs is buying a big stake in Wizards, Capitals sports empire|last=Heath|first=Thomas|date=2017-10-03|work=Washington Post|access-date=2017-10-03|language=en-US|issn=0190-8286}}</ref>

==Steve Jobs' death==
On October 5, 2011, at the age of 56, [[Steve Jobs]], the CEO of Apple, died due to complications from a relapse of his previously treated islet-cell neuroendocrine [[pancreatic cancer]].<ref name="Rare Pancreatic Cancer Caused Steve Jobs' Death">{{cite press release|publisher=Voice of America| title=Rare Pancreatic Cancer Caused Steve Jobs' Death| accessdate=October 7, 2011|date=October 7, 2011|url=http://www.voanews.com/english/news/Rare-Pancreatic-Cancer-Caused-Steve-Jobs-Death--131317684.html | archiveurl = https://web.archive.org/web/20120124162555/http://www.voanews.com/english/news/Rare-Pancreatic-Cancer-Caused-Steve-Jobs-Death--131317684.html | archivedate = January 24, 2012| url-status=live}}</ref><ref name="Forbes 2">{{cite magazine|url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/briancaulfield/2011/10/05/apple-co-founder-steve-jobs-dies-at-56/|title=Apple Co-Founder Steve Jobs Dies At Age 56|magazine=Forbes|accessdate=September 17, 2013}}</ref> Powell Jobs inherited the Steven P. Jobs Trust, which as of May 2013 had a 7.3% stake in [[The Walt Disney Company]] worth approximately $11.1 billion, and 38.5 million shares of Apple Inc.<ref name=Bloomberg/><ref name=NYT17/><ref name="Forbes"/>

{{As of|July 2020}}, Powell Jobs and her family are ranked 59th in the ''[[Forbes]]''' annual list of the world's billionaires<ref>https://www.forbes.com/profile/laurene-powell-jobs/?list=billionaires#1583804d704f Retrieved July 23, 2020</ref><ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.forbes.com/profile/laurene-powell-jobs/?list=billionaires|title=The World's Billionaires: Laurene Powell Jobs & family|magazine=Forbes|access-date=March 5, 2016}}</ref> and 30th in Forbes 400.<ref name="Forbes 400">{{cite web |title=#30 Laurene Powell Jobs & family |url=https://www.forbes.com/profile/laurene-powell-jobs/#a1a667b704fa |work=Forbes |accessdate=9 September 2020}}</ref> According to the same list, she is the richest woman in the technology industry.

==Activism==
In 2004, Powell Jobs founded the [[Emerson Collective]], a private company structured as a Limited Liability Company,<ref name="EdSurge: Arne Duncan Joins Emerson Collective">{{cite web |title=Arne Duncan Joins Emerson Collective |url=https://www.edsurge.com/news/2016-03-20-arne-duncan-joins-emerson-collective |publisher=EdSurge |accessdate=15 August 2018 |date=20 March 2016|quote="The Emerson Collective is a Limited Liability Company (LLC)"}}</ref> that supports social entrepreneurs and organizations working in education and immigration reform, social justice, media, and journalism and conservation through partnerships, grants, and investments.<ref name="Emerson Collective"/><ref name="Yahoo Finance">{{cite web|url=https://finance.yahoo.com/news/steve-jobs-widow-debuts-philanthropic-044821683.html|title=Steve Jobs' Widow Debuts Philanthropic|accessdate=September 18, 2013}}</ref> Through Emerson, Powell Jobs owns ''[[The Atlantic]]'' and [[Axios (website)|Axios]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=Theodore Schleifer |title=Laurene Powell Jobs's charitable group is going to give away almost all of its money |url=https://www.vox.com/recode/2020/2/28/21157049/laurene-powell-jobs-philanthropy-children |accessdate=5 September 2020 |work=Vox |date=28 February 2020 |quote=As an LLC, Emerson also invests in for-profit companies, meaning that it may be hard for Emerson to ever wind down completely and entirely. (After all, if she died tomorrow, Emerson might still own a majority stake in the Atlantic.)}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=MICHAEL CALDERONE |title=Laurene Powell Jobs solidifies control of The Atlantic as Bradley relinquishes duties |url=https://www.politico.com/news/2019/11/20/laurene-jobs-the-atlantic-072210 |accessdate=6 September 2020 |work=Politico |date=20 November 2019 |quote=Emerson has invested in media start-ups such as Axios}}</ref>

In the 2016 U.S. presidential election, Powell Jobs donated $2 million to [[Hillary Clinton]] and raised a further $4 million for her.<ref>https://www.businessinsider.com/laurene-powell-jobs-net-worth-life-career-spending-habits-2018-10?op=1</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=Megan Henney |title=Steve Jobs' widow vows Apple co-founder's fortune will be given away |url=https://www.foxbusiness.com/lifestyle/steve-jobs-widow-vows-apple-co-founders-fortune-will-end-with-her |accessdate=5 September 2020 |work=Fox Business |date=27 February 2020 |quote=In 2016, she backed Hillary Clinton, donating $2 million to her super PAC via her nonprofit and hosting a $200,000-a-plate fundraiser that raised more than $4 million}}</ref>

In 2017, she backed the founding of the political organization [[Acronym (political organization)|ACRONYM]],<ref>{{cite news |last1=Jim Rutenberg |last2=Matthew Rosenberg |title=Trump Won the Internet. Democrats Are Scrambling to Take It Back |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/03/30/us/politics/democrats-digital-strategy.html |accessdate=31 August 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=30 March 2020 |language=en |quote=Another initiative went more smoothly, at least at first. It was called Acronym; among its backers were the Dollar Shave Club founder Michael Dubin, Mr. Hoffman and Ms. Powell Jobs.}}</ref> which raised ethical questions for Powell Jobs for its creation of [[Courier Newsroom]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=ALEX THOMPSON |title=Newsroom or PAC? Liberal group muddies online information wars |url=https://www.politico.com/news/2020/07/14/newsroom-pac-liberal-info-wars-356800 |accessdate=5 September 2020 |work=Politico |date=14 July 2020 |quote=Acronym — a sprawling digital organization whose programs include millions of dollars in traditional political advertising and voter engagement efforts, with financing from some of the deepest pockets in progressive politics, such as liberal billionaires Reid Hoffman, the co-founder of LinkedIn, and Laurene Powell Jobs, the majority owner of The Atlantic — has stirred outrage and provoked debate about the ethics of such political tactics}}</ref>

In the 2020 U.S. presidential primaries, Powell Jobs donated to the campaigns of Democratic candidates [[Amy Klobuchar]], [[Joe Biden]], [[Pete Buttigieg]], [[Kamala Harris]], [[Cory Booker]], and [[Michael Bennet]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=Michela Tindera |title=Here Are The Billionaires Funding The Democratic Presidential Candidates, As Of December 2019 |url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/michelatindera/2020/02/18/here-are-the-billionaires-funding-the-democratic-presidential-candidates/#4ede6c4a33f7 |accessdate=5 September 2020 |work=[[Forbes]] |date=18 February 2020 |language=en |quote=Powell Jobs—who sits atop a $25 billion fortune she inherited from her late husband, Steve, the cofounder of Apple—has also contributed to the campaigns of Biden, Buttigieg, Kamala Harris, Cory Booker and Michael Bennet.}}</ref> After Joe Biden became the presumptive Democratic presidential nominee, she went on to donate over $600,000 to his campaign.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Shane Goldmacher |title=Biden Banks $242 Million as Big-Name Donors Write Huge Checks |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/16/us/politics/joe-biden-fund-raising.html |accessdate=5 September 2020 |work=The New York Times |date=16 July 2020 |quote=Among those who gave at least $500,000 were Laurene Powell Jobs, the philanthropist and widow of Steve Jobs}}</ref><ref>https://www.fec.gov/data/receipts/individual-contributions/?committee_id=C00703975&committee_id=C00744946&contributor_name=Jobs%2C+Laurene&contributor_employer=Emerson+collective&two_year_transaction_period=2020&min_date=01%2F01%2F2019&max_date=12%2F31%2F2020</ref>

==Philanthropy==

In 1997, Powell Jobs and [[Carlos Watson (journalist)|Carlos Watson]] co-founded College Track, a nonprofit organization in [[East Palo Alto, California|East Palo Alto]] to improve high school graduation, college enrollment, and college graduation rates for "underserved" students.<ref>{{cite news | url = https://www.nytimes.com/2013/05/17/business/steve-jobss-widow-sets-philanthropy-goals.html?pagewanted=all&_r=0 | title = Steve Jobs's Widow Steps Onto Philanthropic Stage | newspaper = [[The New York Times]] | date = May 17, 2013 | accessdate = February 10, 2015 | author1 = Lattman, Peter | author2 = Miller, Claire Cain}}</ref><ref name="College Track Vision">{{cite web|title=Our Vision|url=https://collegetrack.org/where-we-work|publisher=College Track|accessdate=March 6, 2017}}</ref><ref name=Philanthropy>{{cite news|last=Sparks|first=Evan|title=The Old College Try|url=http://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/topic/excellence_in_philanthropy/the_old_college_try|accessdate=October 12, 2011|newspaper=Philanthropy|date=Spring 2010}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=http://hollywoodlife.com/2017/07/28/who-is-laurene-powell-jobs-steve-wife-widow-atlantic-owner/|title=Laurene Powell Jobs: 5 Things About Steve Jobs' Widow & New Owner Of 'The Atlantic'|last=Brow|first=Jason|date=2017-07-28|website=Hollywood Life|access-date=2017-08-24}}</ref>

Of College Track's high school graduates, many of whom are first-generation college students, approximately 90 percent attend four-year colleges and 70 percent finish college in six years, whereas the national average for first-generation college students is 24 percent.<ref name="Philanthropy" /> College Track has facilities in [[East Palo Alto, California|East Palo Alto]], [[Sacramento, California|Sacramento]], [[San Francisco]], [[Oakland, California|Oakland]], [[Watts, Los Angeles|Watts]], [[Boyle Heights, Los Angeles|Boyle Heights]], [[New Orleans]], [[Aurora, Colorado]], [[Denver]], and the [[Washington, D.C.]], area.<ref name="College Track Vision" /><ref name="Philanthropy" /><ref name="Milken Institute">{{cite web|title=2013 Global Conference Speakers|url=http://www.milkeninstitute.org/events/gcprogram.taf?function=bio&EventID=gc13&SPID=11719|accessdate=September 18, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.latimes.com/local/lanow/la-me-ln-laurene-powell-jobs-program-in-watts-20151115-story.html| title=Laurene Powell Jobs launches college-support program in Watts| author=Howard Blume| date=November 15, 2015| newspaper=The Los Angeles Times| access-date=December 29, 2015}}</ref> "We have a wait list of five cities where we'd like to open up centers," Powell Jobs has said. "We want to keep our standards high, though, and are reluctant to grow through franchising or through dissemination of our curriculum and training."<ref name="Philanthropy" />

In September 2015, Powell Jobs launched a $50 million project to create high schools with new approaches to education. Called XQ: The Super School Project, the initiative aims to inspire teams of educators, students, and community leaders to create and implement new plans for high schools. Efforts include altering school schedules, curriculums and technologies in order to replace the country's century-old high school education model. Funding for XQ comes from Powell Jobs' Emerson Collective. Following an initial $50 million financial contribution,<ref name="school">{{cite web|last1=Medina|first1=Jennifer|title=Laurene Powell Jobs Commits $50 Million to Create New High Schools|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/09/14/us/laurene-powell-jobs-commits-dollar50-million-to-create-new-high-schools.html?_r=0|website=NY Times.com|publisher=New York Times|accessdate=23 November 2015}}</ref><ref name="school2">{{cite web| url=http://www.mlive.com/news/grand-rapids/index.ssf/2015/11/how_grand_rapids_could_get_10.html| title=How Grand Rapids could get $10M for Museum School| author=Monica Scott| date=November 16, 2015| publisher=Michigan Live| access-date=December 29, 2015}}</ref> XQ announced an additional contribution, awarding ten schools $10 million each, for a total financial contribution of $100 million.<ref>{{cite magazine| url=http://time.com/4495705/xq-super-school-grant-american-high-school/| title=These 10 Ideas Are Each Getting $10 Million to Change High School| author=Katie Reilly| date=September 15, 2016| magazine=Time}}</ref><ref>{{cite news| url=https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/2016/09/14/100-million-jobs-widow-aims-remake-schools-high-tech-age/90353636/| title=$100M from Laurene Powell Jobs to remake schools for high tech age| author=Greg Toppo| date=September 16, 2016| newspaper=USA Today}}</ref><ref>{{cite web| url=http://mobile.nytimes.com/2016/09/15/nyregion/100-million-awarded-in-contest-to-rethink-us-high-schools.html?_r=0| title=$100 Million Awarded in Contest to Rethink U.S. High Schools| author=Elizabeth A. Harris}}</ref> The schools were chosen from approximately 700 submissions nationwide.<ref>{{cite web| url=http://www.cbsnews.com/news/laurene-powell-jobs-xq-the-super-school-project-ten-million-grant-ten-schools/| title=Ten U.S. "Super Schools" awarded $10M each for reimagining education| date=September 15, 2016| publisher=CBS News}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web| url=http://www.delawareonline.com/story/news/education/2016/09/15/delaware-schools-10-million-innovation/90263636/| title=Delaware school's $10 million innovation| author=Saranac Hale Spencer| date=September 15, 2016| publisher=Delaware Online}}</ref> Powell Jobs' team of advisors is led by Russlynn H. Ali.<ref name="school" /><ref name="school2" />

Powell Jobs is a founding member of the [[Climate Leadership Council]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.clcouncil.org/founding-members/|title=Founding Members {{!}} Climate Leadership Council|work=Climate Leadership Council|access-date=2017-09-12|language=en-US}}</ref>

As of 2018, Powell Jobs sits on the board of directors of College Track, Conservation International, and Stanford University.<ref name="Emerson Collective" /><ref name="Forbes" /><ref name="Wall Street Journal 2">{{cite web|title=Jobs's Wife Backs Education Causes|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB10001424052970204450804576621262729373784|accessdate=September 18, 2013}}</ref> She is chair of the board of directors of XQ<ref>{{cite news|author=Emma Brown|date=September 15, 2015|title=Laurene Powell Jobs donates $50 million to redesign high school|newspaper=The Washington Post|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/education/laurene-powell-jobs-donates-50-million-to-redesign-high-school/2015/09/15/9d234458-5b01-11e5-b38e-06883aacba64_story.html|access-date=December 29, 2015}}</ref> and also sits on the chairman's advisory board of the [[Council on Foreign Relations]].<ref name="Emerson Collective" /><ref name="Wall Street Journal 2" /> In 2014, she was ranked as the 29th most powerful woman in the world by ''Forbes''. Her ranking rose from #39 in 2013.<ref name="Forbes14">{{cite web|title=The World's 100 Most Powerful Women|url=https://www.forbes.com/power-women/list/#tab:overall|website=Forbes|publisher=Forbes|accessdate=June 24, 2014}}</ref>

Powell Jobs' philanthropy has been described as of limited "transparency and accountability."<ref>https://www.vox.com/recode/2020/2/28/21157049/laurene-powell-jobs-philanthropy-children</ref>  In fact, in 2019, Powell Jobs was named the "Least Transparent Mega-Giver" of 2019 by ''[[Inside Philanthropy]]''.<ref>https://www.worth.com/how-laurene-powell-jobs-is-putting-her-wealth-to-work/</ref><ref>https://www.influencewatch.org/non-profit/emerson-collective/</ref><ref>https://www.insidephilanthropy.com/home/2019/philanthropy-awards</ref>
