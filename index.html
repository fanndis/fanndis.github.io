Forbes Most Powerful Women

Each year Forbes creates a list of the top 100 most powerful women.
These women come from all around the world - they are politcians, musicians, CEOs,

Project overview

This is the the final project in the course Social Graphs and Interactions, taught at DTU in the fall of 2020.
It is all about the network of the world's most powerful women.
To get a quick overview of the project, please check out this introduction video below.
Links to an explainer notebook and a Github repository can be found at the bottom of the page.

Project motivation

The goal of this project is to delve deep into the most powerful women of the world by figuring out their most immediate connections, using data found online, and trying to come up with a recipe to become more powerful. Do these women have something in common?

The data

The world is filled with data and for this project, the data could be endless. However, to limit the scope of
the project, the data that will be used is from three sources. The original data comes from Forbes, which makes
a list of the 100 most powerful women in the world every year. Forbes only gives access to the most recent year's
list, so the list from 2019 is the basis for this project. The list is extracted from Little Sis, a site containing
databases describing top people in business and politics. Additionally, lists of the top 10 most powerful women
from the years 2004-2018 is gathered from Wikipedia. Data from both websites is obtained to gather
information about the relationships the women have with other people, organizations and more. Finally, the Twitter
feeds from the women are gathered to be able to do an analysis on text written by them. To gain even more
insights into the data exploration press here.

The two networks

Since the network data is gathered from two different sources, it is initially split into two. The two networks, one containing data from Little Sis and the other from Wikipedia, are visualized using the Force Atlas 2 algorithm. Each node in each network represents either a powerful woman or a person, organization, etc. obtained from the women's connections and relationships. How the networks are constructed is further explained in the data exploration chapter.



The left graph is the one containing information gathered from the Little Sis dataset. That graph consists of around 1.300 nodes with 2.000 links between them. The node with the most links has 163 connections, but the largest portion only has around 1 to 5 links.

The right graph then contains information gathered from the women's Wikipedia pages and is obviously musch larger and denser. This results in a whole lot of new nodes and connections. The nodes are around 12.700 and the links between them around 16.300. The node with the most links has 825 connections, but as with the other network, the largest portion only has around 1 to 5 links.

In both networks, the size of the node depends on it's number of connections, or the node's so-called degree. The more links a node has, the bigger it is on the graph.

Most connected nodes

With a network of this size, it can be interesting and informative to see who the most connected nodes are. The network consists of two different types of nodes; the first type represents the powerful women themselves and the second the one the industries and people they have relations to. For a person to become powerful, it's a known fact that much importance lies in having the right connections.

Below are displayed four top 10 lists, each showing nodes with the highest degree. They are of nodes of each type, women and non-women. They are also split between the Little Sis dataset on one hand and then the combined dataset on the other hand. After having looked at the data, it was decided that this presentation would be the most informative. The reason for that is the size difference of the networks - the results for the combined network are practically the same as for the Wikipedia network, since it's so much larger than the Little Sis dataset. However, the Little Sis does have some interesting insights which Wikipedia doesn't, other kinds of relationships. As is known, Wikipedia tends to write large and informative articles on world famous people, but some of these most powerful women don't fall into that category. Doing a top 10 analysis only on the combined network would mean that the women who don't have many connections on their Wikipedia pages, simply because they aren't very known to the public, wouldn't show up. This, however, doesn't mean that they don't have important connections that should to be looked at.

By calculating the number of links that either go into or out of a node, the most
connected nodes in the networks can be found.



Here, it's interesting to see how much the top 10 lists differ from each other, the Little Sis network vs. the combined one. These are two rather different datasources, so they give different results. Ultimately, because of the considerably larger Wikipedia network, Little Sis isn't a big representative of the total data.

When examining the top 10 women, Hillary Clinton is the only one present on both lists, having the highest number of connections in the combined network. This isn't a huge surprise - if a woman were the president of the United States then she would definitely be the most powerful woman in the world and Hillary has direct ties to the presidency. Her husband, Bill Clinton, was the president of the US from 1993 to 2001 and more importantly, Hillary herself has been very prevalent in US politics, even being the first woman to run for president of the US in 2016. Sadly, she lost narrowly to the mean orange man.

For the Little Sis network, Laurene Powell Jobs, Steve Jobs' widow, is the most connected woman. This is due to the many positions she holds and many, many donations and grants she has given, which all are seperate nodes in the Little Sis dataset. Other women on the lists are tied to politics, business and arts, the artists being prevalent because of their extreme fame.

Now, looking at the top 10 lists which don't include the women. The combined graph list contains a lot of magazines and publications, which is understandable since all these women have appeared on Forbes' (Forbes at no. 1) list of the 100 most powerful women in the world. On that list appear also two US presidents, Barack Obama and Donald Trump, as being in that position makes one extremely powerful. Notably is also "Bachelor of Arts", an educational degree, which might indicate that the most powerful women are rather educated in arts than sciences - interesting!

For the Little Sis top 10 non-women, it's obvious that a lot of ties are to the US - there is also probably most data available from there. Barack Obama sits at the top following various political committees. The three other men appearing on the list are also American politicians. Lastly, Harvard business school makes an appearance with 11 connections, one of the most esteemed universities in the world.

Connections between the women

Now, to get a good overview of which women this project revolves around, a labelled network graph is drawn below. As on the network graphs above, the node size increases with increased node degree. The middle of the graph contains all the women who have one or more connections to some of the other women. Women who don't have an internal connection are then arranged around the middle cluster. In total, the graph contains 129 women which have 114 undirected links between them.


It's quite easy to see who has the most internal connections - it is Hillary Clinton, the largest node of the network, with a connection to 32 other powerful women. The largest one behind her is Angela Merkel, chancellor of Germany since 2005, with 13 connections. She is also the most powerful woman in the world right now, having sat at the top of the list consistently since 2006, with an exception of then first lady of the USA Michelle Obama topping it in 2010 (source). Her dominance even continues, with her sitting at the top spot once again in the newly released 2020 list. Other notable women in the network graph are for example Beyoncé, Oprah Winfrey and Condoleezza Rice. Beyoncé is one of the most famous singers in the world, Winfrey one of the most prevalent women in the entertainment industry and Rice a US politician, most notably a former US Secretary of State. Delving even further into this network and observing the links between the women is fascinating.

Age and birth places

To gain some further insight into who these women are, the birth year and birth place of each one will be obtained for visualizations.

  • This age distribution doesn't come as much of a surprise. Most of the powerful women are in the mid- to upper age range, from around 50 to 70 years old. These are women who have been working in their field for a long time and established their place. The oldest woman on the list is Queen Elizabeth II, turning 94 years old this year, and the youngest one, hardly a woman, is the 17-year-old Swedish global warming activist, Greta Thunberg.

  • These results aren't really shocking - most of the powerful women are born in, or come from, the USA, or 44 of those who have available data on this. Other countries have just a few to one women being born in them. After the USA comes England, with 6 women being born there.

Similarity between the most connected women

The most powerful women come from all over the world, and they all work in very different fields - but do they have any similarities? This needs to be analysed by using text analysis. The Wikipedia data for the top 10 most connected women is extracted, TF-IDF rankings made for each word in their pages and then a cosine similarity is used on all the words between the top 10 women. The results can be seen in the matrix graph to the left, which mainly implies that the women don't seem to have many similarities. However, there seem to be some minor similarities which are expected, such as between the musicians and politicians. This can be seen by for example looking at the similarity of Beyoncé in regards to the other women. She has the highest similarity with Lady Gaga, Rihanna and Taylor Swift - all famous singers.

Community detection

The Louvain method for community detection is used to detect communities within the network. A total of 43 communities are found within the whole network using this method and will the word clouds representing the top 6 communities, i.e. those with the biggest number of nodes, be listed here below. Can you see who forms the clouds?

  • The first community, and the biggest one, centers around technology and finance. In the community there are around 40 of the most powerful women. To name a few: Abigail Johnson, Aileen Lee, Amy Hood, Julie Sweet, Lynn Good and Ruth Porat are all in this community. The women have one noticable thing in common - they are successful businesswomen in the financial sector.

  • The second community revolves around the game of tennis. Serena Williams is the only powerful woman in this community which is understandable since there are no other female athletes on the most powerful women lists. She stands alone! Phrases such as Wimbledon, doubles, singles and even game scores all appear in the word cloud, everything related to tennis.

  • The third community centers around the music industry. In the community there are 3 powerful, and very famous, women: Beyoncé, Lady Gaga and Rihanna. These women have an obvious connection - they are all famous musicians. Many recognisable words can be found in the cloud, even some other artists!

  • The fourth community revolves around the entertainment and arts industries. In the word cloud there are words such as Dreamgirls, Presley and Hurston, which all relate to the industry mentioned. In the community there are three powerful women: Ava Duvernay, Oprah Winfrey and Bonnie Hammer, all a part of the entertainment industry.

  • The fifth community revolves around Great Britain. In the word cloud there are words such as Salmond, Farage and Corbyn, who are all British politicans. In the community there are five powerful women: Ana Botin, Judith McKenna, Kaherine Viener, Nichola Sturgeon and Theresa May, who all have in common that they are either of British descent or live and work in Britain.

  • The sixth community revolves only around Hillary Clinton. Not surprisingly, since she is the biggest node in the whole network. In the word cloud there are words that revolve around her directly: Rodham, for example, which is her maiden name. Fresnel, which is the type of glass used in here eyeglasses since she has a particularly bad vision. Sarin, which is a lethal gas that she was accused of approving delivery of to Syrian Rebels. And other equally random words which all have some relation to her!

Sentiment Analysis

Being powerful doesn't automatically make you a good person (in fact, it seems to be quite the contrary in many cases). By using sentiment analysis, the Wikipedia pages of each of the women is inspected to try and detect if the women have a positive or negative impact on society. However, since Wikipedia pages often tend to be quite neutrally written, the Twitter feeds of the women is also gathered to analyse text that they have written themselves and not only text that has been written about them. Are the two sentiments completely different?

First, the sentiments from the Wikipedia pages are calculated and displayed.


The sentiments for the most powerful women are quite similar, which is to be expected. According to the calculations, Jane Jie Sun is the most positive woman. Jane is a Chinese businesswoman and the CEO of the largest online travel agency in China. Jane's Wikipedia page is quite short, which means that when a positive word occurs in her article, it has a much higher value than when a positive word appearas on a longer page, which might be the reason for her being at the top of the positivity list. On the other end, Maria Macapagal Arroyo has been deemed the most negative woman. Maria is the former president of the Philippines who was accused of corruption. Those charges have since been dismissed but might explain her low score.

Now, the sentiments from the Twitter feeds of the women are calculated. Unfortunately, only 58 of the women had a verified Twitter account, but the analysis could deliver some results none the less.


How interesting! Once again, the average sentiments are quite similar. This is interesting to see since the women presumably write their own tweets and therefore have free reign over what they write. However, this is understandable to quite an extent, since these are public figures who want to keep up a good reputation. They seem to be, as a result, quite composed in their tweets, not using particularly strong words.


According to the calculations, Lady Gaga is the most positive powerful woman. When looking at her twitter feed she tends to be quite positive in her tweets and doesn’t tweet about negative things. The reason for her sentiment score not being higher than it already is could be because she calls her fans “her little monsters”, so monster often appears on her feed, a word which has a negative score.
Sophie Wilmés has the most negative sentiment score. Sophie is the Belgian deputy prime minister. When looking at her twitter profile it is noticeable that many of her tweets are in French and unfortunately the sentiment analysis only takes into account English words - so her sentiment score could be a little skewed. The reason for her even having a score is that she retweets a lot of English tweets that are quite political and do contain generally quite neutral words.
